ABOUT THE FRED HOLLOWS FOUNDATION
The Fred Hollows Foundation is an international development organisation working to prevent blindness and restore sight. We continue to carry on the legacy of Professor Fred Hollows, who believed every person had the right to quality eye care, no matter where they live. The Foundation is known around the world as an organisation that works with purpose and determination. With a reputation for excellence, we are well known for making change happen.

The Foundation has grown to work in more than 25+ countries throughout Africa, South Asia, Southeast Asia, the Middle East and the Pacific, as well as Australia. The Foundation has restored sight to more than 3 million people worldwide. We have an ambitious five-year strategic plan that aims to take us even closer to realising our vision of a world in which no person is needlessly blind, or vision impaired and Indigenous Australians exercise their right to sight and good health. To find out more about our work, please visit our website https://www.hollows.org

THE OPPORTUNITY
The Finance Lead is accountable for the effective coordination and delivery of financial operations, partner financial management, and finance support for the country program. The role ensures that financial processes, reporting, and controls are accurate, compliant, and timely, while acting as the primary finance focal point for program teams and partners to support effective program delivery and decision-making.

KEY RESPONSIBILITIES
Finance Lead will own the outcomes of:

Financial Operations & Controls: Lead the day-to-day financial operations, including payments, reconciliations, and financial records. You’ll ensure transactions are accurate, compliant with internal policies and Indonesian regulations, and supported by clear, audit-ready documentation.

Program Finance & Business Partnering: Work closely with program teams to support budgeting, forecasting, and expenditure tracking. You’ll provide timely financial insights, flag variances and risks early, and help teams make informed decisions to keep programs on track.

Partner Financial Management: Coordinate financial management of implementing partners, including fund disbursements, acquittals, and documentation. You’ll ensure compliance with donor and organisational requirements and follow up on any issues to maintain strong financial accountability.

Reporting, Audit & Process Improvement: Prepare financial inputs for internal and donor reporting, and support internal and external audits by ensuring complete and well-organised records. You’ll also identify opportunities to improve systems, processes, and controls to strengthen overall efficiency.

WHAT YOU’LL NEED TO SUCCEED
Bachelor’s degree in Accounting, Finance, or related field
Around 4–6 years’ experience in finance, ideally in an INGO or development setting

Strong understanding of:
- Financial operations and controls
- Budgeting and cash flow management
- Donor/grant financial reporting
- Working knowledge of Indonesian financial regulations and compliance requirements
- Strong Excel skills and confidence working with financial systems
- Ability to work across teams and cultures
- Clear and practical communication skills in English.

The Fred Hollows Foundation — Finance Lead interview master guide
1. What this interview is really testing
This meeting is likely designed to test four things at once: whether you understand the role, whether you can communicate clearly, whether you can think like a finance partner rather than only a technical accountant, and whether you can work calmly with program and partner stakeholders. The job description is not asking for a narrow bookkeeping profile. It is asking for someone who can manage financial operations, partner finance, reporting support, and process improvement in a country-program setting. That means your answers should sound practical, structured, and collaborative, not theoretical or overly technical.
2. The role in one sentence
The Finance Lead is the country-program finance focal point responsible for accurate and compliant financial operations, budget support, partner financial management, reporting inputs, audit readiness, and improvement of systems and controls.
3. What the Foundation seems to value
From the advertisement, the Foundation seems to value reliability, accountability, mission alignment, and practical collaboration. It wants someone who can keep finance clean, support program delivery, follow through on partner issues, and help strengthen process quality over time. The role sits at the intersection of finance and delivery, so they are likely looking for a person who can be both disciplined and helpful.
4. Best opening introduction
Use a short and grounded introduction like this:

“I am a finance professional with experience in financial operations, reconciliations, reporting, and support to program teams. I work in a structured and detail-oriented way, but I also try to keep finance practical for non-finance colleagues. What interests me in this role is the combination of compliance, partner oversight, and program support in an organization with a strong mission. I would like to bring reliability, clarity, and process discipline to the finance function.”

This works because it is clear, relevant, and does not oversell.
5. Best answer to: Why do you want this role?
“I am interested in a role where finance supports real impact. The combination of operations, controls, reporting, and partner management is a good fit for how I work. I also value the chance to contribute to an organization with a clear mission and a strong sense of accountability.”

This answer is strong because it connects motivation to both purpose and function.
6. Best answer to: What do you understand about the role?
Say that this is the main finance focal point for the country program. The role ensures financial operations are timely, accurate, and compliant, while also helping the program team with budgeting, forecasting, expenditure tracking, partner financial management, donor and internal reporting, audits, and process improvement. The key point is that finance in this role is not isolated; it actively supports delivery and decision-making.
7. What they will likely ask first
They may begin with a simple “Tell us about yourself” or “Walk us through your background.” In that answer, do not list your full career history in detail. Instead, connect your background to the job’s core needs: finance operations, reporting, controls, partner support, and cross-team coordination. The best answer is short, confident, and directly relevant to the role.
8. The best six questions to ask in a 30-minute meeting
Use these six only:

1. What are the top 2–3 priorities for this Finance Lead role in the first few months?
2. What would success look like after 3 months and after 1 year?
3. What are the main finance, compliance, or operational challenges the country program is facing right now?
4. How many implementing partners does the team currently manage, and what level of oversight is expected?
5. How is the finance function structured in Indonesia, and who would this role work with most closely?
6. What are the next steps after this meeting?

This set is optimal because it covers priorities, success measures, risks, partner load, team structure, and process clarity.
9. What they are likely to answer for priorities
If you ask about first priorities, they may say they want someone to stabilize finance operations, understand the current rhythm quickly, support the team without delay, and strengthen partner and reporting discipline. They may also mention process improvements or backlog reduction. If they say this, it means they value a person who can settle in fast and take ownership of recurring work.
10. What they are likely to answer for success
If you ask what success looks like after 3 months and after 1 year, they may say that 3-month success means dependable deadlines, clean handover, quick understanding of the finance calendar, good relationships with stakeholders, and control of routine work. One-year success may mean clean reporting, strong partner accountability, better controls, and useful support to program decisions. That answer tells you what kind of finance lead they need.
11. What they are likely to answer for challenges
If you ask about current challenges, they may mention partner reporting delays, budget tracking issues, audit preparation, documentation quality, cash-flow timing, or coordination between finance and program teams. They may also point to system limitations or recurring process inconsistency. This question is useful because it reveals where your experience may matter most.
12. What they are likely to answer for partner oversight
If you ask how many implementing partners they manage and what level of oversight is expected, they should give you a number or rough range and explain the expected level of review for fund disbursements, acquittals, supporting evidence, and issue follow-up. This helps you understand whether the role is highly hands-on or more supervisory and analytical.
13. What they are likely to answer for team structure
If you ask how the finance function is structured in Indonesia, they may explain the reporting line, team size, and who handles operations, grants, reporting, or partner support. They may also describe who the role works with most closely across program, operations, leadership, or regional finance. This helps you judge the level of independence and coordination required.
14. What they are likely to answer for next steps
If you ask about next steps, they may explain the next interview stage, timeline, assessment, reference check, or final decision process. This is a useful closing question because it shows professionalism and keeps the conversation structured.
15. Strong answer to: How would you handle day-to-day financial operations?
“I would focus on accuracy, compliance, and traceability. That means reviewing payments carefully, checking approvals, keeping reconciliations current, ensuring supporting documents are complete, and maintaining organized records that can stand up to audit. If recurring errors appear, I would look for the root cause rather than only correcting the same issue repeatedly.”

This answer is strong because it shows control, discipline, and process thinking.
16. Strong answer to: How would you support budgeting and forecasting?
“I would treat budgeting and forecasting as a working partnership with program teams. My role would be to help translate activities into realistic budgets, monitor actual spend against plan, flag variances early, and adjust forecasts when implementation changes. Finance should help the team make decisions before issues become budget overruns or underspends.”

This matches the job description’s emphasis on practical financial insight.
17. Strong answer to: How would you support decision-making?
“I would not just report numbers. I would interpret them. For example, I would explain whether a variance is timing-related, structural, or caused by implementation changes, and then recommend practical next steps.”

This shows that you understand finance as support for action, not just reporting.
18. Strong answer to: How would you manage partner financial processes?
“I would set clear expectations from the beginning: fund disbursement rules, reporting deadlines, acquittal requirements, and documentation standards. I would review partner submissions for completeness, accuracy, compliance, and reasonableness. If problems appear, I would follow up early and document the issue clearly. The tone should be firm but collaborative.”

That answer reflects accountability and relationship management at the same time.
19. Strong answer to: What would you do if a partner’s acquittal was late or incomplete?
“I would first confirm whether it is a timing issue or a pattern. Then I would define exactly what is missing, what the deadline is, and what the impact will be. I would communicate directly, keep a record of follow-up, and escalate if the issue affects compliance or future disbursement.”

This sounds measured and practical.
20. Strong answer to: How do you handle audit readiness?
“I see audit readiness as part of normal finance discipline, not a separate emergency. I would keep records complete, organized, and easy to trace from source document to report and ledger entry. I would also keep recurring issues visible so they are fixed before audit time.”

This is consistent with the job description’s focus on complete and well-organized records.
21. Strong answer to: How do you manage financial controls?
“I try to make controls practical and repeatable. I check that approvals, coding, documentation, and reconciliations all line up, and I improve the process if the same control failure keeps happening. The goal is not to create extra friction, but to make the control system reliable and usable by the team.”

This is the kind of answer that sounds like a finance lead, not only a processor.
22. Strong answer to: How do you work with program teams?
“I communicate in a clear, respectful, and practical way. Finance and program teams usually have different priorities, so my role is to bridge them. I listen carefully, explain financial requirements simply, and stay solution-oriented.”

This matters because the role explicitly requires working across teams and cultures.
23. Strong answer to: How do you handle pressure?
“When several deadlines come at once, I prioritize by risk, deadline, and dependency. I focus first on what cannot slip, keep stakeholders informed, and maintain a clear task list so the work stays controlled. I do not try to solve everything at once; I work in order of impact.”

This is one of the safest and strongest answers you can give.
24. Strong answer to: How do you handle disagreement with program staff?
“I focus on facts, policy, and the shared objective. First I understand the program team’s perspective and the operational reality behind the request. Then I explain the financial implications clearly and refer to the approved budget, donor rules, or internal policy. The goal is not to say no quickly, but to find the most compliant and workable solution.”

This shows maturity and partnership.
25. Strong answer to: What is your biggest challenge in the role?
“The biggest challenge is balancing strong financial control with responsive support to program delivery and partner needs. In a country program, finance cannot be isolated from operations. The role has to keep processes compliant while still being practical, timely, and helpful to colleagues.”

This answer is strong because it demonstrates that you understand the real tension in the job.
26. Strong answer to: What are your Excel and systems skills?
“I am comfortable using Excel for reconciliations, budget tracking, reporting checks, and data analysis. I work carefully with formulas, lookup logic, clean file structure, and version control. I value clean data and disciplined file management because they reduce errors and make reporting more reliable.”

Do not overstate technical ability; just sound solid and dependable.
27. Strong answer to: How do you approach process improvement?
“I like improving recurring pain points because small process fixes often create big gains in reliability and speed. If a process is causing repeat errors, I look at the root cause, simplify where possible, and create a repeatable control that reduces errors rather than just correcting individual cases.”

This directly matches the role’s expectation to improve systems, processes, and controls.
28. Strong answer to: How would you work with compliance requirements?
“I understand the role requires good working knowledge of local financial regulations and compliance requirements, and I would learn the local rules carefully while making sure internal controls are followed consistently. My approach is to understand the policy framework, align the process to it, and keep documentation strong so the organization is protected.”

This is careful and credible even if you are still learning local details.
29. Strong answer to: Why should we consider you?
“I can bring a steady, structured, and collaborative approach to this finance function. I am comfortable with operations, controls, reporting, and budget support, and I understand that the role also needs clear communication with program teams and partners. I would aim to be reliable on the basics, responsive to the team, and proactive in improving the process where needed.”

This is a strong closing-style answer because it combines technical and interpersonal value.
30. Strong answer to: What kind of environment do you work best in?
“I work best in an environment where expectations are clear, deadlines matter, and collaboration across teams is valued. I like roles where I can balance discipline with practicality, and where finance is seen as a support to delivery and decision-making.”

This aligns well with the Foundation’s needs.
31. Strong answer to: How do you build trust with non-finance teams?
“I build trust by being clear, reliable, and helpful. I try to explain financial issues in practical language, respond early when risks appear, and make sure finance feels like a partner in delivery rather than a separate control function.”

That is exactly the kind of answer that fits a finance lead in a program environment.
32. Strong answer to: What do you see as the role of finance here?
“Finance should protect compliance, support good decisions, and make implementation smoother. It is not only about recording what happened. It is also about helping the country program use its resources responsibly and effectively.”

This is a good answer because it shows you understand finance as mission support.

Once I understand the rhythm of the program, I would focus on strengthening the areas that need it most.”

This answer is strong because it shows orientation, discipline, and patience.

This shows that you understand reporting as an end-to-end discipline, not just a submission task.

This is a strong leadership answer for a finance lead role.

This is concise and meaningful.

38. Best answer if they ask about weaknesses
Choose a safe, controlled weakness, then show how you manage it. For example: “I can be very thorough, so I manage that by prioritizing early and using checklists to keep deadlines under control.”

Do not choose a weakness that would damage confidence in a finance role, such as disorganization or dislike of deadlines.
39. Best answer if they ask about working with unclear information

This is useful in finance because many problems come from assumptions rather than technical difficulty.

This is a very practical answer.

Say that you would first learn where the biggest pain points are, then prioritize quick wins that improve reliability. That might mean strengthening tracking, improving filing, clarifying handoffs, standardizing recurring tasks, or simplifying templates. The goal is to improve quality without disrupting operations.
47. If they ask how you would manage recurring reporting deadlines
Say that you would keep a forward calendar, identify dependencies early, and make sure the work is broken into stages rather than left to the end. You would also keep stakeholders informed so no one is surprised by a delay or missing input. This shows operational discipline.
48. If they ask how you would support audits and donor reviews
Say that you would keep records easy to retrieve, ensure supporting documents are complete, and make sure the financial story is consistent from source to report. You would also maintain a clear trail for any exceptions. That gives confidence to auditors and donors alike.
49. If they ask what you see as the biggest risk in the role
Say that the biggest risk is the gap between financial control and operational reality. If finance becomes too rigid, it may slow delivery. If it becomes too loose, it may create compliance problems. Your approach is to keep controls strong while making them workable for the team.
50. If they ask how you would support partner accountability without harming relationships
Say that accountability and relationships are not opposites. You can be firm on requirements while still being respectful and helpful. The key is to set expectations early, follow through consistently, and communicate clearly when something is missing or delayed.
51. If they ask what you expect from managers
Say that you work best when expectations are clear, priorities are stable, and feedback is direct. You value managers who support ownership, trust the process, and are open to practical improvements. This answer is useful because it suggests you can work with structure and accountability.
52. If they ask what you expect from program colleagues
Say that you expect openness, timely communication, and willingness to engage on the financial implications of program decisions. The best cross-functional relationships happen when both sides understand that finance and delivery need to work together.
53. If they ask how you handle incomplete documents
Say that you do not let incomplete support pass silently. You would return it, explain what is missing, and keep the follow-up visible until it is fixed. That is important because poor document discipline becomes audit risk later.
54. If they ask what you think the team needs most
Say that the team probably needs someone who can combine technical finance discipline with calm coordination and practical follow-up. Do not make it sound like the team is weak; just say that a role like this needs a person who can keep operations stable while helping improve how finance works across teams.
55. If they ask about communication style
Say that you prefer direct, respectful, and simple communication. In finance, clarity matters more than style. You aim to be easy to understand, precise enough to avoid confusion, and practical enough that colleagues can act on your advice.

Avoid sounding too narrow, too theoretical, or too focused only on bookkeeping. Avoid giving long answers that do not connect finance to delivery. Avoid saying you can do everything if you cannot. It is better to sound measured and credible than overconfident. Also avoid asking too many questions at once. Keep the conversation natural and controlled.

Your tone should be calm, practical, respectful, and thoughtful. You do not need to sound dramatic or overly enthusiastic. You need to sound like someone who is dependable, organized, and easy to work with. In a finance lead role, that is usually more persuasive than trying to sound impressive.
