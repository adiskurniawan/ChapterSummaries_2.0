ABOUT THE FRED HOLLOWS FOUNDATION
The Fred Hollows Foundation is an international development organisation working to prevent blindness and restore sight. We continue to carry on the legacy of Professor Fred Hollows, who believed every person had the right to quality eye care, no matter where they live. The Foundation is known around the world as an organisation that works with purpose and determination. With a reputation for excellence, we are well known for making change happen.

The Foundation has grown to work in more than 25+ countries throughout Africa, South Asia, Southeast Asia, the Middle East and the Pacific, as well as Australia. The Foundation has restored sight to more than 3 million people worldwide. We have an ambitious five-year strategic plan that aims to take us even closer to realising our vision of a world in which no person is needlessly blind, or vision impaired and Indigenous Australians exercise their right to sight and good health. To find out more about our work, please visit our website https://www.hollows.org

THE OPPORTUNITY
The Finance Lead is accountable for the effective coordination and delivery of financial operations, partner financial management, and finance support for the country program. The role ensures that financial processes, reporting, and controls are accurate, compliant, and timely, while acting as the primary finance focal point for program teams and partners to support effective program delivery and decision-making.

KEY RESPONSIBILITIES
Finance Lead will own the outcomes of:

Financial Operations & Controls: Lead the day-to-day financial operations, including payments, reconciliations, and financial records. You’ll ensure transactions are accurate, compliant with internal policies and Indonesian regulations, and supported by clear, audit-ready documentation.

Program Finance & Business Partnering: Work closely with program teams to support budgeting, forecasting, and expenditure tracking. You’ll provide timely financial insights, flag variances and risks early, and help teams make informed decisions to keep programs on track.

Partner Financial Management: Coordinate financial management of implementing partners, including fund disbursements, acquittals, and documentation. You’ll ensure compliance with donor and organisational requirements and follow up on any issues to maintain strong financial accountability.

Reporting, Audit & Process Improvement: Prepare financial inputs for internal and donor reporting, and support internal and external audits by ensuring complete and well-organised records. You’ll also identify opportunities to improve systems, processes, and controls to strengthen overall efficiency.

WHAT YOU’LL NEED TO SUCCEED
Bachelor’s degree in Accounting, Finance, or related field
Around 4–6 years’ experience in finance, ideally in an INGO or development setting

Strong understanding of:
- Financial operations and controls
- Budgeting and cash flow management
- Donor/grant financial reporting
- Working knowledge of Indonesian financial regulations and compliance requirements
- Strong Excel skills and confidence working with financial systems
- Ability to work across teams and cultures
- Clear and practical communication skills in English.

The Fred Hollows Foundation — Finance Lead interview master guide
1. What this interview is really testing

This interview is likely testing four things at once: whether you understand the role, whether you can communicate clearly, whether you can think like a finance partner rather than only a technical accountant, and whether you can work calmly with program and partner stakeholders. The job description is not asking for a narrow bookkeeping profile. It is asking for someone who can manage financial operations, partner finance, reporting support, and process improvement in a country-program setting. That means your answers should sound practical, structured, and collaborative, not theoretical or overly technical. The interviewers will probably be checking whether you can stay steady when there are deadlines, competing needs, and compliance pressure. They will also want to see whether you can make finance useful to the program, not just correct on paper.
2. The role in one sentence

The Finance Lead is the country-program finance focal point responsible for accurate and compliant financial operations, budget support, partner financial management, reporting inputs, audit readiness, and improvement of systems and controls. Another way to say it is that the role protects financial integrity while helping the program move forward.
3. What the Foundation seems to value

From the advertisement, the Foundation seems to value reliability, accountability, mission alignment, and practical collaboration. It wants someone who can keep finance clean, support program delivery, follow through on partner issues, and help strengthen process quality over time. The role sits at the intersection of finance and delivery, so they are likely looking for a person who can be both disciplined and helpful. They will probably prefer someone who is precise without being rigid, and supportive without being vague.
4. How to think about the role

Think of the job as a bridge role. One side is finance control: payments, reconciliations, documentation, audit trail, reporting accuracy, compliance, and controls. The other side is program support: budgets, forecasts, partner oversight, problem-solving, and helping people understand numbers. Your answers should show that you are comfortable on both sides of that bridge. If you talk as though finance is only about checking or only about helping, you will sound incomplete.
5. Best way to frame yourself

Position yourself as someone who is accurate, calm, and practical. The strongest tone is: I understand finance, I can manage detail, I communicate well with non-finance colleagues, and I care about making the process work for the program. Avoid sounding like someone who only wants to do back-office work. This role is about finance leadership in service of the country program. If you sound like someone who only checks numbers, you will undersell yourself.
6. Best opening introduction

Use a short and grounded introduction like this:

“I am a finance professional with experience in financial operations, reconciliations, reporting, and support to program teams. I work in a structured and detail-oriented way, but I also try to keep finance practical for non-finance colleagues. What interests me in this role is the combination of compliance, partner oversight, and program support in an organization with a strong mission. I would like to bring reliability, clarity, and process discipline to the finance function.”

This works because it is clear, relevant, and does not oversell. It says enough to show competence while leaving room for the conversation.
7. Best answer to: Why do you want this role?

“I am interested in a role where finance supports real impact. The combination of operations, controls, reporting, and partner management is a good fit for how I work. I also value the chance to contribute to an organization with a clear mission and a strong sense of accountability.”

This answer is strong because it connects motivation to both purpose and function. It also shows that you understand the job is not only technical but mission-driven.
8. Best answer to: What do you understand about the role?

Say that this is the main finance focal point for the country program. The role ensures financial operations are timely, accurate, and compliant, while also helping the program team with budgeting, forecasting, expenditure tracking, partner financial management, donor and internal reporting, audits, and process improvement. The key point is that finance in this role is not isolated; it actively supports delivery and decision-making. It is helpful to say that the role also builds confidence in systems and accountability.
9. What they will likely ask first

They may begin with a simple “Tell us about yourself” or “Walk us through your background.” In that answer, do not list your full career history in detail. Instead, connect your background to the job’s core needs: finance operations, reporting, controls, partner support, and cross-team coordination. The best answer is short, confident, and directly relevant to the role. If you talk too much about early career history, you risk losing them before you reach the useful part.
10. Best structure for “Tell us about yourself”

Use this order: present role or recent experience, most relevant finance responsibilities, cross-functional work, and why this role fits now. Finish with one sentence showing alignment to the Foundation’s mission and the country-program environment. Keep it to about 60–90 seconds. The aim is not completeness; it is relevance. A good structure is: who you are, what you do, how you work, and why this role makes sense.
11. The best six questions to ask in a 30-minute meeting

Use these six only:
1. What are the top 2–3 priorities for this Finance Lead role in the first few months?
2. What would success look like after 3 months and after 1 year?
3. What are the main finance, compliance, or operational challenges the country program is facing right now?
4. How many implementing partners does the team currently manage, and what level of oversight is expected?
5. How is the finance function structured in Indonesia, and who would this role work with most closely?
6. What are the next steps after this meeting?

This set is optimal because it covers priorities, success measures, risks, partner load, team structure, and process clarity.
12. What they are likely to answer for priorities

If you ask about first priorities, they may say they want someone to stabilize finance operations, understand the current rhythm quickly, support the team without delay, and strengthen partner and reporting discipline. They may also mention process improvements or backlog reduction. If they say this, it means they value a person who can settle in fast and take ownership of recurring work. That is a strong signal that practical maturity matters more than polished jargon.
13. What they are likely to answer for success

If you ask what success looks like after 3 months and after 1 year, they may say that 3-month success means dependable deadlines, clean handover, quick understanding of the finance calendar, good relationships with stakeholders, and control of routine work. One-year success may mean clean reporting, strong partner accountability, better controls, and useful support to program decisions. That answer tells you what kind of finance lead they need. It also tells you where to focus your examples.
14. What they are likely to answer for challenges

If you ask about current challenges, they may mention partner reporting delays, budget tracking issues, audit preparation, documentation quality, cash-flow timing, or coordination between finance and program teams. They may also point to system limitations or recurring process inconsistency. This question is useful because it reveals where your experience may matter most. It also helps you decide whether to emphasise controls, relationship management, or process improvement.
15. What they are likely to answer for partner oversight

If you ask how many implementing partners they manage and what level of oversight is expected, they should give you a number or rough range and explain the expected level of review for fund disbursements, acquittals, supporting evidence, and issue follow-up. This helps you understand whether the role is highly hands-on or more supervisory and analytical. It also shows whether you will be expected to do a lot of review work directly or more coordination work.
16. What they are likely to answer for team structure

If you ask how the finance function is structured in Indonesia, they may explain the reporting line, team size, and who handles operations, grants, reporting, or partner support. They may also describe who the role works with most closely across program, operations, leadership, or regional finance. This helps you judge the level of independence and coordination required. It also tells you whether the role is expected to influence across functions or mainly execute within finance.
17. What they are likely to answer for next steps

If you ask about next steps, they may explain the next interview stage, timeline, assessment, reference check, or final decision process. This is a useful closing question because it shows professionalism and keeps the conversation structured. It also gives you a chance to end with a calm and businesslike tone rather than leaving the call open-ended.

“Is there anything in my background that you would like me to clarify before we close?”

This is a good closing line because it gives them a direct opportunity to raise concerns and gives you one last chance to reinforce fit. It is also calm and professional, which matters in a finance interview.
72. What they are probably evaluating during the call

They are likely checking five things: whether you understand finance in a program environment, whether you can manage partner accountability, whether you communicate clearly, whether you are practical under pressure, and whether you can work across teams with maturity. It is better to sound measured and credible than overconfident. Also avoid asking too many questions at once. Keep the conversation natural and controlled. You want to sound useful, not rehearsed to the point of stiffness.

Your tone should be calm, practical, respectful, and thoughtful. You do not need to sound dramatic or overly enthusiastic. You need to sound like someone who is dependable, organized, and easy to work with. In a finance lead role, that is usually more persuasive than trying to sound impressive. Tone matters because this role is as much about steady judgment as it is about technical skill.

Have your questions selected in advance. Keep your answers short enough to stay conversational. If you can, rehearse out loud once so the wording sounds natural and not memorized.

Use language such as: “I would focus on,” “I would first understand,” “I would look for the root cause,” and “I would keep the process workable.” That wording makes you sound thoughtful and mature. It also avoids sounding like you are pretending to know everything already. Seniority in this interview is about judgment, not grand claims.

Strong finance leadership here means more than being accurate. Do not think of partner financial management as policing. Think of it as structured stewardship. The role has to help partners understand expectations, submit proper acquittals, and use funds responsibly. The goal is not to catch errors for its own sake. Budgeting is not only a planning exercise. It is a translation exercise: turning activities, timelines, and implementation assumptions into financial reality. When you talk about budgeting, emphasize that it must stay connected to program changes, partner delivery, and actual spend patterns. That sounds much more useful than treating the budget as a static document.
85. How to think about reporting

Reporting should be seen as a discipline that connects records, analysis, and explanation. Good reporting is not just numbers in a template. It is numbers that can be trusted, explained, and acted on. If you say this in the interview, you will sound like someone who understands the real purpose of reporting.
86. How to think about controls

Controls are not there to make life harder. They are there to keep the organization safe, accurate, and traceable. A strong answer is one that shows you understand the balance between protection and practicality. If a control is too hard to use, people will work around it. Process improvement should focus on recurring friction. Pressure in this role will probably come from overlapping deadlines, incomplete information, and the need to support other people while still meeting finance requirements. A good answer is one that shows you do not react emotionally. You prioritize, communicate early, and keep moving in a controlled way.
90. How to think about trust

Trust is built through repetition, not speeches. If you are accurate, helpful, and consistent, people will rely on you. If you explain finance clearly and follow through on what you say, your credibility will grow. In this role, trust is especially important because finance touches many parts of the country program.

Mission fit does not mean pretending to be a program officer. It means showing that you understand how finance supports impact. If you can say that good finance protects resources, improves accountability, and helps implementation, you are showing mission fit in a credible way. That is enough; do not overdo it.

Use examples that show control, judgment, and collaboration. The best examples will involve a financial issue, how you handled it, how you communicated, and what improved afterward. Enablers

The strategy says four enablers support all the goals: resources, people, partners, and operations. These are not side issues. They are the organisational infrastructure that makes scale possible. The public website mirrors this by showing the Foundation’s work on fundraising, training, partnership-building, data, accountability, and operational support.
Enabler 1: resources

Inspire our supporters and unlock resources from around the world. The Foundation says the scale of its ambition requires a major increase in resources. Supporters are critical, and the organisation wants diverse, dynamic, and innovative funding streams to support its work and the wider sector.
Resources objective A: build a sustainable, multi-channel funding program

The strategy says private philanthropic revenue comes through multiple sources and giving patterns, which helps distribute risk and create funding stability. The Foundation will keep building that model to safeguard future revenue. The public donor pages reinforce that the organisation wants a durable support base, not only a single stream of income.
Resources objective B: mobilise a global supporter base

The Foundation wants to expand its international fundraising markets and create a more multipolar funding structure alongside its Australian base. That means broadening support and making funding more resilient.
Resources objective C: increase global institutional funding for eye health

The strategy says the Foundation will grow official development assistance and other institutional revenue by attracting grants for both core eye health work and integrated health and development work. This reflects its identity as a development organisation, not only a service provider.
Resources objective D: explore new ventures and types of funding

The Foundation says it will build on experience with the Cameroon Development Impact Bond and the Alina Vision social enterprise hospital to explore future financing innovation. It presents financial innovation as one route to scale.
Enabler 2: people

Invest in and empower our people. The strategy says people are critical to delivery. The Foundation will invest in training, development, clear delegations, positive culture, agility, and collaboration so staff can deliver the ambition of the strategy.
People objective A: strengthen the employee value proposition

The Foundation says it must attract and retain talented people in a competitive labour market that includes commercial and multilateral organisations. It wants to maintain employer of choice status by strengthening its employee value proposition.
People objective B: develop the culture and mindset

The Foundation says it needs a culture that supports bold eye health solutions, agility, and collaboration. This objective is about the conditions that let people work well together, learn, and respond quickly.
People objective C: empower the global workforce

The strategy says people at every level need systems and processes that help them strengthen their capabilities and meet the requirements of the strategy. Capability is treated as a strategic asset, not just a human resources issue.
People objective D: enable dynamic teaming and collaboration

Much of the strategy requires joint effort across the Foundation and with external stakeholders. The Foundation says its people must be able to team flexibly and collectively, using leadership behaviours that support collaboration across sectors and geographies.
People capability and culture

The website and strategy both imply that the organisation’s people must be able to work across cultures, sectors, and geographies. Capability is not only technical; it is also relational, adaptive, and collaborative.
Enabler 3: partners

Work with and for our partners. The strategy says localisation requires a shift in how the Foundation works with others. Partnerships should be more multi-directional, with stronger codesign, shared learning, and recognition of local and government partners.
Partners objective A: redefine partnerships

The Foundation says it works primarily through partners, so it needs shared understanding and commitment to the impact sought beyond inputs and outputs. The organisation therefore wants partnerships built around shared outcomes and shared responsibility.
Partners objective B: strengthen partner engagement and collective learning

The strategy says new and different ways of working with new and different partners are needed if transformation and scale are to be achieved. The Foundation wants learning to move across the partner network so good practice is shared rather than isolated.
Partners objective C: strengthen co-design and collaborative problem-solving

The Foundation says many of the solutions needed to scale quality services must be collectively driven. It will therefore deepen codesign and collaboration with partners, especially where local ownership and government leadership are essential.
Enabler 4: operations

Optimise delivery through effective and efficient operations. As the Foundation scales, it says it must also review its support model so operations remain effective, efficient, agile, and fit for purpose.
Operations objective A: build a support model that enables scale and growth

The Foundation says front-line programme work needs support that is agile and flexible while still maintaining enough rigour for compliance and governance. Operational support is therefore presented as a core enabler of growth.
Operations objective B: leverage research, evaluation, data, and analytics

The strategy says better information capture, reporting, and learning processes are needed as the organisation grows. The website’s evidence and impact pages reinforce the same message: data should improve decision-making and help the organisation work better.
Operations objective C: transform business systems and processes

The strategy says legacy systems will need upgrading and new technologies introduced to support data reporting and insight. This is part of making the organisation more agile and more able to handle complexity.
Operations objective D: evolve governance structures

The Foundation says boards will need to become more international, representative, and localised as the organisation becomes more multi-polar. Governance therefore needs to evolve alongside the organisation’s global reach.
Governance and transparency

The public website also reinforces governance through its annual reports, financial statements, complaints pathways, charity registration details, and compliance information. This supports the strategy’s emphasis on accountability, effectiveness, and sustainability.
People and partners

The Foundation says it exists to serve people in priority populations and the local partners who make implementation possible. It aims to serve those at risk of being left behind in developing countries while retaining a strong domestic commitment to Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Peoples in Australia. The public site and the strategy both present this as a core expression of equity and sustainability.
Priority populations

The strategy says the people most likely to be left behind include women and girls, people living in poverty, children, youth, people with disabilities, people living with HIV, older persons, Indigenous peoples, refugees, internally displaced people, and migrants. The Foundation says leave no one behind is central to its commitment to the Sustainable Development Goals.
Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Peoples

The website says this work is part of the organisation’s DNA. Fred Hollows began humanitarian outreach by seeking to close the eye health gap for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Peoples relative to other Australians. The strategy says the gap has narrowed but not fully closed, so the Foundation continues to support Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander organisations, communities, and people to exercise their right to sight, good health, and self-determination.
Locally led solutions

The Foundation says locally led solutions are best because local partners are best placed to understand needs and respond to them. Most of its partners are national governments and public health institutions. The strategy says sustainable quality depends on local training, procurement, systems, and procedures, not on outside control alone.
Global partnerships

The Foundation’s Global partnerships page says partnerships are key to ending avoidable blindness and improving eye health worldwide. It says the Foundation works with the United Nations, the World Health Organization, civil society, and other eye health partners to integrate eye health into global agreements, research, tools, policies, and practices. It also says the organisation has a formal relationship with WHO and a strategic partnership with Cure Blindness Project.
Partnerships as the operational heart

The website and strategy both imply that partnerships are not optional or decorative. They are how the organisation works. That includes working with ministries of health, public health institutions, local communities, academic bodies, and global networks.
Values

The Foundation’s five values are integrity, collaboration, action, resourceful, and empowerment. These values shape behaviour across all stakeholders and support the organisation’s public identity as practical, ethical, and partnership-driven.
Integrity

The Foundation says it holds itself accountable, does what it says it will do, is honest and transparent, and strives for fairness and equity.
Collaboration

The Foundation says it partners with others to achieve more, is stronger together, values and respects others’ strengths, and embraces inclusion and diversity.
Action

The Foundation says it gets the job done, learns from successes and mistakes, strives to be innovative, agile, and flexible, and takes bold but considered risks to achieve its vision.
Resourceful

The Foundation says it approaches problems with curiosity, optimism, and determination, supports each other through challenges, and treats setbacks as opportunities for growth and learning.
Empowerment

The Foundation says it enables people to act, amplifies unheard voices, shares information, and encourages people to be brave and speak up.
Implementation approaches

The strategy says it will be implemented through four linked approaches: global principles, thematic priorities, a programmatic theory of change, and alignment to the Sustainable Development Goals. These are the practical mechanisms that translate strategy into action across countries and programmes.
Global principles

The Foundation says it needs the right balance between a global strategy and local adaptation. Global principles guide where the organisation focuses, who it works with, and what it prioritises at portfolio level, while country teams adapt those principles to local context and national eye health plans.
Global principle A

Strategically target where the Foundation can make the most impact. It says it will invest where burden and prevalence are high, where the gap between access and need is large, and where the work can deliver measurable direct benefit over time.
Global principle B

Optimise programming at global portfolio level and embed locally led development. The strategy says the Foundation should maintain a balanced portfolio, manage risk, use each dollar responsibly, and tailor solutions to regional context.
Global principle C

Deliver bold solutions. The Foundation says it will identify where it can transform eye health, tackle major barriers collectively, scale proven solutions, and pilot new interventions where cost-effective proven options do not yet exist.
Thematic priorities

The strategy identifies five thematic priorities for multi-country planning and support: workforce, data, elimination of trachoma, quality, and integration. The aim is to create synergy across countries and regions, share technical skills, and improve planning and learning.
Programmatic theory of change

The theory of change explains how the Foundation’s actions and investments contribute to collective action with partners, which changes health systems and eye care in countries, which then benefits individuals and communities. The most important feature is that the Foundation wants to build sustainable system solutions rather than focus only on immediate service delivery.
Roles in the theory of change

The Foundation says it can act as an influencer, facilitator, knowledge broker, technical adviser, and funder. These roles show that the organisation sees itself as part of a wider ecosystem of change rather than as a sole actor.
Influencer

Through advocacy and technical expertise, the Foundation says it can influence policy and practice.
Facilitator

Through collective action, the Foundation says it can convene, facilitate, and lead diverse stakeholders.
Knowledge broker

Through research and innovation, the Foundation says it can generate and broker knowledge.
Technical adviser

Through applied expertise in partnerships, the Foundation says it can strengthen partners.
Funder

Through strategic investments, the Foundation says it can enable catalytic programmes to occur.
What the theory of change is trying to strengthen

The strategy says the key enablers are policies and guidelines, research and innovation, stakeholder engagement and coordination, investment, integration into health and other systems, data collection and use, and service and system improvement planning and execution. These are meant to strengthen the building blocks of integrated people-centred eye care and broader health systems.
The building blocks

The Foundation says stronger financing mechanisms, equipment, workforce, data, community engagement, leadership, and governance are needed for sustainable eye health systems. When these building blocks are stronger, more people should be able to access high-quality eye care at the right place, time, and price.
Impact for individuals and communities

For individuals, the Foundation seeks reduced blindness and vision loss for reversible or treatable conditions and improved functioning for people with ongoing vision loss. For communities, it seeks stronger health and wellbeing, better education, stronger workforce and employment participation, and improved standards of living.
Annual report and results

The 2024 annual report says the Foundation performed 562,213 eye operations and treatments, trained 66,852 health workers, supported the distribution of more than 16 million antibiotic doses for trachoma, and distributed 178,638 pairs of glasses. Those figures show that the organisation’s model combines direct service delivery with workforce development, disease control, and access support.
Annual reporting as accountability

The annual reports are not only performance documents. They function as transparency tools, showing how the Foundation uses resources and what outcomes are being produced. This supports the strategy’s emphasis on trust, sustainability, and evidence.
Research and technology

The Foundation’s public site says it uses research to improve its understanding of avoidable blindness and to support the development of strategies and advocacy for change. Its research and technology pages show that evidence is used to shape policy, advance technology, and improve eye health systems, not just to report on them.
Research as a policy tool

The strategy and website both show that research is used to do more than describe the problem. It helps build the case for investment, supports policy engagement, and improves programme design. That makes research a strategic enabler rather than a separate academic activity.
Gender equity

The Foundation says women and girls are more likely to live with vision loss and less likely to receive treatment, especially in lower- and middle-income countries. Its gender equity page says preventing and treating avoidable blindness and ensuring the full inclusion of women and girls living with vision-related disability are important to reducing gender inequities and achieving the Sustainable Development Goals.
How the Foundation describes its public role

The website says the Foundation has been at the forefront of a global eye health movement for more than 30 years. It says it works with governments, global organisations, and local communities to tackle barriers to effective eye care and build stronger systems. The public message is that direct care, training, advocacy, and system change all belong together.
Impact

The Foundation’s impact pages say it has helped restore sight or prevent blindness for more than 150 million people worldwide. It frames impact not only in terms of individual treatment, but also in terms of systems strengthening, empowerment, and community benefit. The website says the organisation has been part of a global eye health movement for more than 30 years.
Impact as system change

The Foundation’s impact framing is deliberately broader than output counting. It includes strengthened services, improved equity, greater access, and more resilient systems, alongside the direct benefit of sight restoration.
Support and accountability

The website says the Foundation is registered as a charity with the Australian Charities and Not-for-profits Commission, that donations of $2 or more may be tax deductible in Australia, and that it is a member of ACFID and compliant with the ACFID Code of Conduct. It also provides annual reports, financial statements, and donor information as part of its transparency and accountability approach.
Founding story

The website says Fred Hollows, driven by social justice and equality, dedicated his life to making sure everyone had access to quality eye care. It says Fred and Gabi founded The Foundation after his cancer diagnosis so his mission could continue after his death. That founding story is important because it explains the organisation’s enduring mix of urgency, practicality, and long-term commitment.
Gabi Hollows

The website identifies Gabi Hollows as Founding Director and a continuing Board member. She is presented as a key steward of the organisation’s original mission and continuing direction. The public story keeps her closely linked to Fred’s legacy and to the Foundation’s focus on practical, local, lasting change.
What the website adds to the strategy

The strategy gives the architecture. The website gives the evidence of what that architecture looks like on the ground: training, trachoma control, surgery, glasses, community outreach, advocacy, gender equity, local partnerships, and impact measurement. Together they present the Foundation as a direct-service organisation that is deliberately moving toward broader systems change while keeping practical service delivery at its centre.
Long-form synthesis

The most important pattern across the strategy and website is the movement from doing for people to building with people. The Foundation still delivers surgery, treatment, glasses, and outreach. But it increasingly positions those activities as the visible tip of a larger change agenda that includes policy, workforce, financing, data, accountability, gender equity, climate awareness, and local ownership. That is the deeper organisational story.
Sustainable development goals

The strategy says the Foundation will continue to align its work with the SDGs and assess its contribution against them. Eye health is not always explicitly named in the goals or indicators, but the Foundation argues that its work contributes strongly to health, education, equity, and economic development outcomes.
Global advocacy role

The website says the Foundation works through global partnerships and official relationships to place eye health in broader health and development agendas. It wants eye health to be recognised in systems where policy is made, resources are allocated, and progress is measured.
How the Foundation works with governments

The website and strategy both say governments are central duty-bearers. The Foundation’s role is to support, influence, partner, and help strengthen the systems governments use to serve their populations. That is why advocacy and technical assistance sit alongside delivery.
How the Foundation works with local partners

The Foundation repeatedly states that local partners are best placed to understand needs and respond to them. It therefore emphasises localisation, co-design, and shared learning. This is especially visible in the way the organisation talks about national ministries, public health institutions, and community-level delivery.
How the Foundation works with communities

Community engagement is not framed as outreach alone. It is part of making eye care acceptable, reachable, and trusted. The strategy says people-centred care requires better engagement with communities, while the website shows this in practice through local health workers, teachers, community leaders, and outreach staff.
Public health framing

The Foundation’s website repeatedly frames eye health as public health, not just clinical specialty care. That matters because it connects eye health to primary care, prevention, screening, referral systems, and population-level planning.
Indigenous Australian health

The Foundation’s domestic commitment to Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Peoples remains part of its identity and is presented as a continuation of Fred Hollows’ original vision. The strategy explicitly includes self-determination, which signals that the organisation sees Indigenous health not only as service delivery, but as rights-based, community-led work.
Where the Foundation works

The website says the organisation works in more than 25 countries across East Africa, the Middle East, Asia, and the Pacific. It also highlights work in Australia with Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Peoples. The geographic spread supports the strategy’s insistence on both global principles and local adaptation.
What the Foundation says about scale

The Foundation’s public materials repeatedly show that scale is not only a matter of doing more of the same. It is a matter of changing the systems that determine who gets care and whether services last. This includes financing, workforce, data, governance, and local ownership.
What the Foundation says about sustainability

Sustainability is presented as the ability of local systems to continue delivering quality care after external support changes. That is why the strategy places such emphasis on training, local partnership, government commitment, and operational systems.
Why training is central

The training page is one of the clearest examples of the Foundation’s operating philosophy. Training people builds independence, expands local capacity, and makes systems more resilient. It is therefore not only a programme activity, but a structural investment.
Why research is central

The research and technology pages show that the Foundation uses evidence to improve policy, programme design, and advocacy. Research is part of how the organisation turns clinical work into long-term systems change.
Why gender equity is central

The gender equity page shows that the Foundation sees gender not as an add-on, but as a structural determinant of access and outcome. It therefore links gender equity to service design, leadership, evidence, and advocacy.
Why partnerships are central

The global partnerships page and strategy both make clear that the Foundation does not see itself as acting alone. Partnerships are how the organisation scales influence, shares knowledge, and builds local legitimacy.
Why annual reporting matters

The annual reports show how the Foundation translates strategy into measurable action. They also signal accountability to supporters, partners, and the public.
Why the impact pages matter

The impact pages connect the organisation’s outputs to broader outcomes such as improved lives, stronger communities, and global progress toward ending avoidable blindness.
Closing summary

The strategy and website together show an organisation that still delivers direct eye care, but increasingly sees itself as a systems-change actor. It trains local people, strengthens public services, builds the investment case for eye health, supports women and girls, works with Indigenous communities, deepens partnerships, and aims to make eye health a visible social and economic development priority. That is the organising logic running through the full public material.
