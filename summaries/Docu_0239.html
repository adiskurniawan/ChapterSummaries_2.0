Q&A NGO Finance and Accounting
1. Q: What is core NGO finance and accounting?
A: Core NGO finance and accounting is the operating system that lets a nonprofit record what it receives, what it owes, what it owns, and how resources move through programs, projects, and funds. In practice, it is not only bookkeeping. It is the discipline that turns donor money, earned income, grants, and assets into a clear record that management, donors, auditors, and boards can trust. A good core system makes it possible to see whether spending followed purpose, whether balances are real, and whether the organization can explain every major transaction.

2. Q: How does nonprofit accounting differ from for-profit accounting?
A: Nonprofit accounting is built around accountability for resources, not profit for owners. A for-profit entity focuses on earnings available to shareholders, while a nonprofit focuses on whether money and other resources were used for the mission, whether donor restrictions were followed, and whether public trust is protected. The financial statements therefore answer different questions. Instead of asking how much profit was generated for owners, nonprofit reports ask what resources were received, how they were used, what remains available, and whether any balances are still restricted by donor intent.

5. Q: What is fund accounting in an NGO?

Instead of treating all money as one pool, the organization separates resources so it can see which amounts are freely available, which are tied to specific donors or projects, and which have to be used within a defined scope. This is especially important in NGOs because one grant may support a specific activity while another amount may be reserved for general operations. Fund accounting helps prevent confusion, misuse, and reporting errors by making the purpose of each balance visible.

6. Q: Why do NGOs need separate tracking for restricted and unrestricted funds?
A: Separate tracking is needed because the organization must know which money can be used for any lawful mission purpose and which money can only be spent for a particular donor-approved use. If these balances are mixed, the NGO can accidentally overspend restricted resources, misstate available cash, or report a balance that does not actually exist. Separate tracking also helps leadership make decisions about flexibility, reserves, and program continuity. It is one of the most important distinctions in nonprofit finance because it affects spending, reporting, and trust at the same time.

7. Q: What is the difference between donor restriction and internal management preference?
A: A donor restriction is an external condition attached to a contribution by the donor or grant maker. It limits how the organization may use the resources and therefore affects accounting and reporting. An internal management preference is only a decision made by leadership for planning or control purposes. It may guide budgeting, but it does not create an external accounting restriction. The distinction matters because finance staff must not treat internal preferences as if they were donor rules. Only the donor-imposed condition changes the formal treatment of the resource.

17. Q: What is the difference between cash basis and accrual basis in NGO accounting?
A: Cash basis records income and expenses when cash is received or paid. Accrual basis records them when the underlying economic event happens, even if payment happens later. In NGO finance, accrual accounting is usually more informative because grants, payables, receivables, prepayments, and deliverables often do not align neatly with cash timing. Cash basis can be simpler for very small operations, but it may hide obligations or overstate available resources. The chosen basis should be clear, consistent, and explained in the accounting policy so users know how to read the reports.

69. Q: What is the difference between income recognition and cash receipt?
A: Income recognition and cash receipt are related but not identical events. Cash receipt is simply the moment money arrives in the bank or is otherwise physically collected. Income recognition is the accounting decision about when the organization has satisfied the conditions to treat the resource as reportable income. In nonprofit finance, this difference matters constantly because grants, contributions, and restricted gifts often arrive before the organization can use them freely.
