Fixed Assets & Capitalization
Purpose & objective (complete)
Provide a single, operational reference that defines fixed-asset recognition, measurement, capitalization, depreciation, impairment, revaluation, transfer, disposal and reporting across all country offices and legal entities. Ensure consistent book treatment (financial statements) and parallel fiscal tracking (tax returns), describe controls to prevent loss/fraud, define donor-specific handling, and provide ready-to-use operational artifacts (journals, checklists, templates, reconciliations, SOP snippets and working schedules). The chapter is designed for finance, procurement, IT, programme, insurance, legal and internal audit stakeholders and must be adapted for local statutory and donor requirements. The document should be used as an actionable manual: include checklists that can be copied into ERP procedures, ready-to-use narratives for audit packs, and explicit evidence tags for each material transaction. The objective is to reduce interpretation error in field offices by providing concrete decision rules, templates and a reproducible automation approach so that routine capitalisation events are handled consistently with auditable trails and clear escalation routes for exceptions. In addition to operational completeness, the manual supports governance, training, and continuous improvement by specifying change-control, testing, and review cycles for policy, system configuration and templates. This ensures that policy updates, tax law changes or donor requirement updates are implemented with documented approvals and regression checks. The manual explains how to operationalise policy in low-connectivity settings and provides sample fallback procedures where ERP integration is not yet available.
Scope (complete)
All tangible and intangible assets capitalised on the balance sheet including: land, buildings, leasehold improvements, CIP, plant & machinery, vehicles, furniture & fittings, IT hardware & capital software, ROU assets, heritage assets (if recognised), donor-funded assets, major spare parts and components meeting capitalization criteria, and any grouped assets treated as one unit. Excludes consumables, supplies, petty purchases below threshold unless grouped or donor-required, prepayments, and AP payments that are operating expenses. Requires parallel tax asset register and donor subledgers where applicable. The scope explicitly covers assets recognised as part of program delivery where donor terms require stewardship and post-deployment monitoring — these are included even if local tax rules differ. The scope also requires that large software projects, project-specific infrastructure and major refurbishments are captured in CIP ledgers until commissioning and transfer to operating classes. The manual also covers assets held by agents or partners on behalf of the organisation where stewardship arrangements apply and where the organisation bears risk or retains ownership per donor contracts or legal agreements.
Key definitions & core recognition principles (complete)
• Fixed asset (PPE/PP&E): tangible items held for use in production/supply of goods or services, for rental to others, or for administration, expected to be used for more than one accounting period.
• Intangible asset: identifiable non-monetary asset without physical substance (e.g., purchased software, capitalised development costs) that meets recognition criteria.
• Capitalisation threshold: minimum unit cost above which expenditure is capitalised rather than expensed; set considering materiality and local tax rules.
• Cost of an asset (initial measurement): purchase price, import duties, non-refundable taxes and all directly attributable costs to bring asset to working condition (installation, site preparation, delivery), including qualifying borrowing costs where applicable.
• Useful life / residual value: estimated period/time over which asset will provide economic benefits and estimated disposal proceeds at end of useful life.
• Depreciation method: systematic allocation (straight-line, diminishing balance, units-of-production) of depreciable amount over useful life.
Core principle: capitalise expenditures that meet recognition tests; maintain parallel book and tax tracking where treatments differ; recognise deferred tax for temporary differences; maintain complete documentary evidence and a reconciled, auditable fixed asset register. Include a glossary and a short form mapping between accounting and tax terms to reduce misunderstandings among finance, tax and programme staff. Ensure that project managers and procurement are trained on the difference between operational consumption (expense) and capitalisable enhancement. Require documented rationale when departing from standard treatment and mandate senior sign-off for exceptions.
Core principles / Key policy statements (complete)
1. Apply a documented capitalisation threshold consistently; document grouping/groupable rules and donor exceptions (grouping may capitalise items individually below threshold when combined delivery serves one function).
2. Record assets at cost (or fair value on donation/acquisition) on the date control transfers; capitalise directly attributable costs and qualifying borrowing costs; expense routine repairs and maintenance.
3. Depreciate capitalised assets systematically over estimated useful lives using the chosen depreciation method; review useful lives and residual values annually and adjust prospectively when estimates change.
4. Componentisation: identify and capitalise material components separately where parts have different useful lives or replacement patterns; maintain component schedules and replacement accounting.
5. Test for impairment where indicators exist; reverse impairment only where permitted by applicable accounting standards and when reversal criteria are met.
6. Maintain a secure, reconciled fixed asset register (book and tax bases) with unique asset IDs, physical location, custodian, cost components, accumulated depreciation, net book value, tax base, donor code, insurance details, and lifecycle events (maintenance, revaluation, disposal). Add a control that flags asset records with missing or outdated critical fields (custodian, insurance, tag photo) and escalates to the CFD for action within a defined SLA. Introduce retention rules for transaction evidence and require proof-of-physical possession for high-risk items before posting capitalisation journals.

Depreciation & component accounting — detailed rules, worked examples (complete)
• Depreciable amount = Cost − Residual value. Residual values should be conservative and reviewed; many operational assets use zero residual value where market resale is uncertain.
• Method selection: Straight-line is default for predictable uniform consumption; declining balance for assets that lose value faster early in life; units-of-production for usage-linked assets (e.g., generators measured by hours or vehicles by mileage). Document method per asset class.
• Componentisation criteria: Capitalise separately when a part of the asset has a cost material relative to whole asset and a different useful life or replacement pattern (e.g., aircraft engines, building HVAC, large industrial boilers). Maintain component schedules with unique IDs mapped to parent AssetID.
Worked example — componentisation (numerical): Building purchase price USD 1,200,000. Allocate components: Structure USD 900,000 (life 40 yrs), HVAC USD 180,000 (life 12 yrs), Roof USD 120,000 (life 15 yrs). Annual depreciation: Structure 22,500; HVAC 15,000; Roof 8,000. Total annual depreciation = 45,500. Bookkeeping records components separately to match replacement cycle and to ensure correct impairment/replacement accounting. Provide guidance on how to manage component replacements financially and operationally, including treatment of the removed component and the accounting entries to ensure no double counting occurs. Encourage registering component serial numbers where available, and maintaining a replacement schedule linked to the maintenance module. Recommend rules for capitalization and derecognition of replaced components and procedures for adjusting carrying amounts and accumulated depreciation on replacement.
Fiscal (tax) treatment vs Accounting (book) treatment — conceptual framework and examples (complete)
Book accounting follows applicable accounting standards and management estimates; fiscal/tax rules are defined by statute and may prescribe different thresholds, allowable rates, or methods. These differences create temporary differences which require deferred tax accounting under accounting standards that mandate balance sheet approach for deferred taxes.
Common differences and consequences:
• Threshold differences: Tax law may permit immediate expensing under small asset allowances while book requires capitalisation — temporary difference arises until book depreciation reduces carrying amount.
• Rate/method differences: Tax authorities may allow accelerated depreciation or specific capital allowances (e.g., first year allowances) — immediate tax benefit reduces tax payable now and creates deferred tax liability to reflect reversal in future when book depreciation exceeds tax deductions.
• Revaluation differences: Book revaluation not recognised for tax until disposal — creates taxable temporary differences; deferred tax accounting required where applicable.
• Donor and VAT treatment: Donor grants may be tax exempt or VAT-exempt; tax base may therefore differ from book cost. Track such exemptions in tax register and disclose deferred tax implications. Include a process for resolving disputes with tax authorities and maintaining a provision policy when tax positions are uncertain. Provide instructions for computing deferred tax on a schedule that auditors can follow, including assumptions and the tax rate table used for each jurisdiction. Add guidance on cross-border transfer tax treatments and customs duty implications where assets move between legal entities.

Impairment & revaluation — detailed guidance, step examples and reversal (complete)
• Impairment indicators: decline in market value, obsolescence, physical damage, regulatory change, reduced utilisation, or adverse economic conditions. Trigger formal impairment test when indicators present or annually for certain classes.
• Measurement approach: Determine recoverable amount = higher of fair value less costs to sell and value in use (discounted cash flows). Use appropriate discount rate, credible forecasts and sensitivity analysis; document assumptions and obtain approval.
Worked impairment example with reversal possibility: Asset carrying amount 150,000; recoverable amount 95,000 → impairment loss 55,000 recorded. Later, product demand recovers and new recoverable amount is 110,000. Under some accounting frameworks (e.g., IAS 36 for PPE), reversal of impairment permitted up to original carrying amount net of depreciation. Reversal example: Increase in recoverable amount 15,000 → recognise reversal: Dr Accum. Impairment 15,000 — Cr Impairment reversal (profit) 15,000. Document change in circumstances and supporting evidence. Provide checklists for impairment testing including required evidence, valuation support, sensitivities, and reviewer signoffs; require a qualified valuer for material revaluations. Donor-funded & restricted assets — accounting models and examples (complete)
Treat donor-funded capital in a way that reflects donor conditions. Common approach: recognise asset at cost and recognise deferred income for donor contribution; amortise deferred income to income in line with depreciation.
Worked example: Donor funds 80% of a vehicle cost 50,000 = 40,000; organisation funds 10,000. Accounting: Dr Fixed asset 50,000 — Cr Deferred donor income 40,000 — Cr Bank 10,000. Net impact on P&L: Depreciation 10,000 offset by amortised grant income 8,000 → net expense 2,000 borne by entity (consistent with funding split). Maintenance, capital vs revenue expenditure — decision matrix and examples (complete)
Decision criteria to capitalise: (1) increases future economic benefits or capacity, (2) extends useful life beyond original estimate, (3) meets threshold or grouping rules and (4) is not routine repair.
Examples:
• Replacing engine of heavy vehicle: if new engine extends life materially, capitalise as component. Document cost vs. remaining life and obtain CFD approval.
• Routine servicing and oil change: expense as maintenance.
• Major overhaul of generator that extends useful life by 7 years: capitalise; create new component or adjust useful life prospectively. Common errors & mitigations — extended examples and controls (complete)
Errors and examples:
• Capitalising routine maintenance: leads to overstated assets and understated expenses. Control: require evidence of increased capacity/life for capitalisation approval.
• Missing disposals: asset remains on register and insured incorrectly. Control: monthly disposal reports and quarterly register vs GL reconciliations.
• Incorrect tax claims: claiming disallowed items causes tax adjustments/penalties. Control: tax register validation and pre-filing reviews.
• Double capitalisation of grouped purchases: overstates asset base. Control: three-way match, tagging before close, and independent reviewer on capex batches.
Mitigations: mandatory capitalisation checklist in ERP, automated validation blocking postings without AssetID or attachments, independent sample checks by internal audit, ongoing training and SOP updates. FAQ (extended) (complete)
Q: When is expenditure capitalised?
A: When it meets asset recognition criteria (probable future economic benefits, measurable cost, useful life > 1 period) and exceeds threshold or is groupable/material per donor rules. Obtain capitalisation approval and attach supporting evidence.

A: Choose the method that best reflects consumption of economic benefits. Straight-line is common; use reducing balance for assets losing value faster early; use units-of-production for assets with usage-based wear. Document rationale per asset class.

Glossary & definitions (extended) (complete)
• Carrying amount (NBV): cost less accumulated depreciation and impairment.
• Tax base: amount deductible for tax purposes in future periods; often cost less cumulative tax depreciation claimed.
• Temporary difference: difference between book carrying amount and tax base that will reverse in the future, creating deferred tax.
• Deferred tax liability/asset: tax payable/receivable in future because of temporary differences.
• Capital allowance: statutory tax deduction or depreciation for tax purposes.
• ROU (Right-of-Use) asset: asset representing lessee's right to use underlying asset for lease term under lease accounting. Add cross-references to where each term is used in journal examples and policy sections for easy navigation. Include synonyms and jurisdiction-specific terminology.

Policy quick reference — capitalisation decision tree
Step 1: Does the expenditure create a future economic benefit beyond current period? If no → expense. If yes → Step 2. Step 2: Does the item meet or, when grouped, exceed the capitalisation threshold? If no but donor requires capitalisation → capitalise and tag. If yes → Step 3. Step 3: Are costs directly attributable (delivery, installation, duties, qualifying borrowing costs)? If yes → include in cost base. Step 4: Is componentisation required (material part with distinct life)? If yes → create component schedule. Step 5: Record asset, tag and schedule depreciation; update tax register and donor subledger. Always attach PO, invoice, delivery/acceptance, capitalisation approval and tagging evidence. Purpose & objective
Provide comprehensive standards, practical checks, decision rules and templates to prevent, detect and remediate conflicts of interest (CoI) and misuse within travel expense reporting. Protect donor funds and organisational reputation by ensuring travel is necessary, authorised, cost-effective, documented and free from undisclosed personal or related-party benefits. Support transparent, consistent and auditable processes for pre-travel approval, booking, expense submission, vendor selection, investigation and remediation.
Scope
Applies to all travel requests, bookings and claims reimbursed or paid by the organisation for staff (local, national, international), consultants, contractors, volunteers, interns and board members. Covers advance requests, per diems, accommodation, airfare, ground transport, visas, travel insurance, conference fees, hospitality, entertainment, and any third-party arrangements paid for via organisational funds. Applies irrespective of funding source; donor-specific restrictions and allowability must be observed. Excludes routine payroll allowances unless claimed via the travel expense process.
Key definitions
• Conflict of interest (CoI): any situation where private interests (financial, familial, personal) could improperly influence, or be perceived to influence, decisions concerning travel or related procurement and claims.
• Related party: spouse/partner, immediate family, close associate, business associate, company owned/controlled by traveller, or any entity where the traveller has a material interest.
• Personal travel portion: part of travel that is non-mission-related; requires apportionment or denial.
• Red flag: an indicator suggesting potential misuse, CoI, or fraud warranting further review.
• Apportionment: documented allocation of costs between business and personal purposes or between funding sources.
Core principles
1. Necessity: travel must be justified and proportionate to expected outcomes.
2. Transparency: travellers must disclose potential CoI and provide full supporting documentation.
3. Proportionality: costs must be reasonable for purpose and location; safety-driven choices acceptable when documented.
4. Segregation of duties: request, approval, procurement and payment functions should be separated to the extent practicable.
5. Consistency: apply rules uniformly and deny claims when CoI is undeclared or unjustified.
6. Auditability: maintain a clear, indexed audit trail for each travel instance.
Governance & responsibilities (RACI)
• Traveller (R): complete request, declare CoI, provide receipts, cooperate with queries and investigations.
• Line manager (A): verify business purpose, check COI declaration, and recommend/approve per authorities.
• Budget holder (C): confirm budget availability and donor allowability; for donor-funded travel, verify compliance with grant terms.
• Procurement (C): review vendor relationships and perform market checks when related parties are involved.
• Finance / Payments (R): verify receipts, duplication, coding, and execute reimbursements; reconcile advances.
• Independent reviewer / Internal Audit (I): periodic sampling, investigation of red flags, and reporting.
Pre-travel authorisation
1. All travel requires pre-authorisation by the relevant manager and budget holder. Authorisation must state purpose, outputs, funding source and estimated costs.
2. Mandatory COI declaration: travellers must declare any known relationships with suppliers, host organisations or individuals related to the travel.
3. Approvals scaled by seniority and cost: routine staff travel approved by line manager; senior staff/board travel approved by independent authority (CD, Board Chair or delegated independent reviewer). Approvals must be in writing (email or travel system).
4. Where applicable, risk assessment (security, medical, reputational) and duty-of-care considerations must be recorded.
Booking & vendor selection
• Use approved corporate booking platforms or vetted travel agencies. Where not available, procurement should be engaged for selection.
• Vendors with known links to travellers (related vendors) are permitted only with documented procurement sign-off and independent market checks showing value for money.
• Require at least two alternative quotes for related-party vendors or above-threshold bookings, and document selection rationale.
• Personal upgrades funded by the organisation are not permitted unless justified for operational reasons and pre-approved by a senior officer, recorded with rationale.
Allowed, disallowed and split costs
Allowed: economy/approved class airfare, accommodation for business nights at reasonable standard, visa fees, conference fees, authorised per diems for working days, local transport for work activities, mandated vaccinations and travel insurance for business purpose.
Disallowed (unless pre-approved and funded personally): leisure/extension nights, family or companion costs, organisation-funded personal upgrades, gifts to personal contacts, hospitality for personal guests, vendor payments to entities owned by traveller without full procurement safeguards.
Split or apportionment required: where trips include both business and personal elements. Pre-travel apportionment must be approved and personal shares paid by traveller in advance or deducted from reimbursement.
Expense claim requirements
Claims must include: travel authorisation reference, itemised receipts/invoices (vendor name, date, description, amount), proof of payment (credit card/bank statement or petty cash voucher), boarding passes or tickets, accommodation folios showing nights/occupants, apportionment worksheet for mixed trips, COI declaration and reconciliation of any advances. Credit card convenience receipts without item detail require supporting invoice or will be queried. Missing receipts require manager justification; recurring missing receipts may lead to denial.
Red flags and detection logic
Common red flags: repeated travel to same supplier associated with traveller; frequent last-minute bookings; round-number reimbursements; repeated reimbursements just below higher approval thresholds; duplicate receipts; multiple claims for same expense across periods; travel booked with personal vendor accounts or invoices made out to third parties; large unexplained cash advances; or consistent use of cash reimbursements in contexts where card payments are feasible. Approvers must use a red-flag checklist and finance must run exception reports for patterns.
Challenge questions — required when red flags present
Approvers/finance must request written answers from traveller:
1. Describe the specific programme outcomes expected and how travel advances these outcomes (include measurable deliverables).
2. Were any companions included? If yes, provide names and indicate which costs are personal vs business (attach proof of personal payment).
3. Do you or any immediate family member have any financial or governance interest in any vendor used? Provide details and supporting disclosures.
4. Were cost-saving alternatives considered (remote meeting, different dates, alternative vendors)? Provide evidence of at least one market check.
5. Explain any late submissions or missing receipts and provide remedial evidence.
Decision rules — Approve, Query, Deny
• Approve: Complete documentation, no undeclared CoI, cost reasonable and funding eligible.
• Query: Minor documentation gaps or modest CoI disclosures that can be mitigated — require clarification and correction before payment.
• Deny: Evidence of undeclared CoI, predominant personal benefit, duplicate claims, fabricated receipts or supplier owned by traveller without proper procurement process. Denials must be recorded with clear rationale and communicated to the traveller with appeal options (if applicable).
Segregation of duties & control activities
Implement maker-checker controls: the person booking should not be the sole approver; payment execution must be independent of travel approval where possible. Finance should perform three-way match: travel authorisation ↔ claim receipts ↔ payment. Implement exception reporting for claims above preset thresholds and periodic review of frequent travellers and high-risk vendors. Maintain access controls on travel and finance systems to prevent unauthorised modifications.
Apportionment principles & worked examples
Principle: apportionment must be objective, documented and pre-approved where possible.
Worked example 1 — Mixed business/leisure: Trip 10 days, 7 business days, 3 leisure days; airfare $1,500, hotel $1,200 (10 nights), per diem $300. Business ratio 7/10 = 70%: reimbursed airfare $1,050, hotel $840, per diem $210 = $2,100. Traveller pays $900 personal share; evidence of payment required (receipt or deduction).
Worked example 2 — Companion travel: Traveller attends conference and brings spouse. Accommodation split by room-night usage and meals; spouse costs are always personal unless organisation explicitly funds spouse participation with a documented business rationale and independent approval.
Procurement due diligence for related vendors
When vendor has link to a traveller:
1. Procurement must obtain vendor registration, tax ID, ownership details and evidence of arms-length pricing.
2. Obtain at least two alternative quotes and document comparison.
3. Independent reviewer (not the traveller’s line manager) must approve vendor selection and rationale.
4. For recurrent engagements, rotate suppliers where possible or obtain periodic market benchmarking. Retain procurement file for audit.
Payment types, advances and reconciliation
• Prefer organisational payments (corporate cards, direct bookings, purchase orders) to minimise cash flows through personal accounts.
• Issue advances only with approved travel request and with signed advance terms and repayment plan. Reconcile advances within X days after travel; unreconciled advances trigger escalation and potential payroll recovery.
• Reimbursements: require submission within defined timeframe (e.g., 30 days); late claims require line manager justification. Finance to reconcile claims to bank batches and GL, tagging related-party vendors for review.
Audit sampling and monitoring approach
Adopt risk-based sampling: focus on high-value claims, frequent travellers, related-party vendors, and items with red flags. Monthly reconciliation: travel claims ↔ authorisations ↔ payments. Quarterly analytics: top 20 travellers by spend, vendor concentration analysis, frequency of missing receipts, and advances aging reports. Provide summary reports to management and internal audit, and design dashboards to surface anomalies (duplicate receipts, vendor rotations, approvals bypass).
Investigation protocol
1. Preserve evidence (emails, booking records, receipts, bank advices) and implement legal hold where necessary.
2. Assign impartial investigator (HR + Finance or Internal Audit) with documented terms of reference.
3. Conduct interviews, document statements and obtain vendor information. Maintain confidentiality and procedural fairness. 4. Recommend remedial actions (repayment, disciplinary measures, process changes) and report material findings to senior management and donors if required by agreements.
Sanctions and recovery procedures
Sanctions scaled to severity: denial of claim and repayment for minor breaches; formal warning and removal of travel privileges for repeated breaches; repayment + disciplinary action up to termination for serious or fraudulent behaviour; legal action where criminality suspected. Recover funds via payroll deduction where lawful, direct reimbursement, or invoicing staff. Document recovery plans and status in central register. Notify relevant governance bodies (CD, Board, donor) for material incidents.
Duty of care and safety exceptions
Duty-of-care exceptions (e.g., higher-cost secure hotels due to security risk) are acceptable with documented risk assessment and approver sign-off. Safety-driven costs should be treated differently from discretionary upgrades; approvers must record risk rationale in the travel approval. Do not treat safety-related costs as CoI unless a related-party vendor or other red flags exist.
Special categories: senior staff, board, consultants
• Senior staff/board: independent authoriser required (e.g., Board Chair for CEO travel) to avoid perceived conflicts.
• Consultants: travel clauses must be explicit in contracts; consultant travel claims subject to same COI and procurement rules as staff.
• Volunteers/interns: claims are subject to same documentation but may have simplified approval matrices where appropriate; maintain controls for related-party vendor use.
Remote & cash-based environments
In cash-heavy contexts, impose stricter controls: two-person cash handling, pre-numbered vouchers, independent witness signatures, and immediate reconciliation. Use mobile-money or local corporate arrangements where available. Maintain vendor lists and rotate suppliers to reduce concentration risk. For remote locations lacking conventional banking, record additional approvals and photographic evidence of payments.
Data protection & record retention
Store travel records and COI declarations securely with appropriate access controls. Apply data minimisation and anonymise reports where possible. Retention: follow donor and legal requirements (commonly 6–10 years). For investigations, retain records per legal hold. Ensure GDPR/local data law compliance where personal data crosses jurisdictions.
