“A partner’s acquittal was late or incomplete” means the partner organization did not properly submit the required accountability documents within the expected timeline.
In the context of sub-awards, partner awards, sub-grants, or partner agreements, the term “acquittal” refers to the formal financial and programmatic accountability process through which a partner organisation demonstrates, evidences, reconciles, and certifies how funds, commodities, assets, or other resources transferred by SCI were utilised in accordance with the signed sub-award agreement, approved budget, donor requirements, organisational policies, and applicable statutory or regulatory obligations.

The acquittal process is one of the core financial control mechanisms within partner management and award oversight. It is designed to ensure that all transferred resources are:

• Used strictly for authorised programme purposes
• Supported by sufficient documentary evidence
• Traceable through accounting and banking records
• Reconciled to approved budgets and workplans
• Consistent with reported programmatic outputs and deliverables
• Subject to review, monitoring, and audit verification
• Properly recorded within the General Ledger and award management systems
• Cleared, adjusted, or refunded where balances remain outstanding

Within SCI partner management procedures, acquittals are closely linked to:

• Advance management and liquidation
• Financial monitoring and desk reviews
• Programmatic monitoring and deliverable verification
• Donor compliance obligations
• Audit readiness and documentation retention
• Fraud prevention and fiduciary risk mitigation
• Sub-award close-out requirements
• Monthly reconciliation of partner advance accounts

An acquittal therefore serves as the formal evidence package that allows SCI Finance and Programme teams to determine whether:

1. Funds transferred to the partner were spent appropriately
2. Expenditures are allowable under donor and SCI rules
3. The partner complied with reporting timelines and documentation standards
4. The reported expenditures reconcile with actual activities implemented
5. Further payments may be released
6. Outstanding advances may be cleared from the GL
7. Any unspent balances, unsupported expenditures, or questioned costs must be recovered or adjusted

In practical terms, acquittal is not merely the submission of receipts. It is a structured accountability exercise involving financial reconciliation, documentary verification, management review, and programmatic confirmation.

Under SCI procedures, partner acquittals may relate to:

• Monthly reports
• Quarterly reports
• Interim liquidation of advances
• Milestone-based reporting
• Final financial reports at sub-award close-out
• Emergency response reporting cycles
• Donor-specific reporting requirements

A standard partner acquittal package commonly includes, but is not limited to:

• Detailed financial expenditure reports
• Expense summaries against approved budget lines
• General ledger extracts or cashbooks maintained by the partner
• Receipts and supporting invoices
• Procurement documentation and quotations
• Payroll records where partner staff are charged to the award
• Timesheets or attendance records where required
• Bank statements covering the reporting period
• Bank reconciliations signed and dated by authorised personnel
• Cash-on-hand statements and petty cash reconciliations
• Fixed asset records for assets purchased under the award
• Distribution records for in-kind assistance
• Activity reports or narrative progress reports
• Beneficiary data where applicable
• Variance explanations against budget
• Exchange rate calculations where foreign currency transactions occurred
• Remaining cash balance reconciliation
• Refund evidence for unused balances or interest recoveries
• Tax withholding documentation where legally required
• Prior approval documentation for exceptional expenditures
• Audit reports or spot-check reports where required by donor terms

The acquittal process is particularly important where SCI provides advance funding to a partner. Under SCI accounting guidance, advances may initially be recorded through partner advance accounts such as:

Dr Partners payments – advances 5200
Cr Bank

Those advances remain outstanding until the partner submits an acceptable acquittal package that supports the expenditures incurred. Once Finance reviews and accepts the acquittal documentation, the advance may then be cleared and recognised as expenditure through the appropriate expense accounts and donor expenditure analysis lines.

Because of this, acquittals are directly connected to:

• GL reconciliation processes
• Advance clearance controls
• Cash management oversight
• Financial reporting accuracy
• Donor expenditure reporting
• Audit sampling and verification

For this reason, SCI policies require Finance teams to reconcile partner advances regularly and ensure that acquittals are received, reviewed, approved, and cleared promptly.

Failure to obtain proper acquittals exposes SCI and donors to significant financial, compliance, operational, and reputational risks, including:

• Unsupported expenditures
• Fraud or misuse of funds
• Duplicate or fictitious payments
• Donor disallowances
• Material audit findings
• Inaccurate financial statements
• Idle cash balances held by partners
• Delayed project implementation
• Weak audit trails
• Regulatory non-compliance
• Reputational damage with donors and stakeholders

Accordingly, SCI applies a risk-based approach to partner monitoring and acquittal review. Higher-risk partners, larger sub-awards, emergency response projects, or partners operating in insecure or high-risk environments may be subject to:

• More frequent acquittal submissions
• Enhanced supporting document requirements
• Spot checks and transaction testing
• Additional desk reviews
• Partner audits
• Reduced advance levels
• Conditional disbursement arrangements
• Escalated management review

In this framework, the phrase:

“A partner’s acquittal was late or incomplete”

means that the partner failed to fully comply with the agreed accountability and reporting obligations under the sub-award agreement.

This may involve either:

• Delayed submission of required reports and supporting documents
• Submission of partial or insufficient documentation
• Failure to provide adequate supporting evidence
• Failure to reconcile expenditures properly
• Failure to clear outstanding advances within the required timeframe
• Failure to respond to queries raised during Finance review

More specifically:

Late acquittal

= the partner submitted the required financial and/or programmatic accountability documentation after the reporting deadline defined in the signed sub-award agreement, reporting schedule, donor conditions, or approved payment terms.

A late acquittal may include situations such as:

• Quarterly financial reports submitted weeks or months after the deadline
• Delayed liquidation of advance payments
• Failure to submit reports before requesting additional disbursements
• Late submission of bank reconciliations or supporting schedules
• Delayed submission of donor-required reporting templates
• Failure to submit final acquittal documentation during close-out
• Delays caused by weak financial systems or staffing gaps at the partner level
• Delayed responses to Finance clarification requests

Operationally, late acquittals create significant control concerns because they:

• Delay advance clearance
• Increase the risk of unsupported expenditures
• Reduce visibility over actual cash utilisation
• Complicate month-end and year-end reconciliations
• Delay donor reporting submissions
• Limit SCI’s ability to assess project performance accurately
• Increase audit exposure and compliance risk

Late acquittals may also indicate broader underlying issues such as:

• Weak partner financial capacity
• Poor accounting systems
• Inadequate staffing or segregation of duties
• Weak document retention practices
• Operational disruption in the field
• Cash management problems
• Fraud indicators or misuse concerns
• Poor programme oversight

Examples of late acquittals include:

• A partner receives an advance in January but only submits the financial report in April despite a February reporting deadline.
• A partner fails to provide the required quarterly liquidation before requesting additional funding.
• Final close-out reports are submitted months after the sub-award end date.
• The partner delays submission of procurement documentation needed to support major purchases.

Meanwhile:

Incomplete acquittal

= the partner submitted an acquittal package, but required information, supporting documentation, reconciliations, approvals, or explanations were missing, inconsistent, inaccurate, or insufficient to support the reported expenditures and activities.

An incomplete acquittal may involve:

• Missing receipts or invoices
• Missing bank statements
• Missing bank reconciliations
• Unsupported cash expenditures
• Incomplete procurement files
• Missing approvals or authorisations
• Inconsistent figures between reports
• Unexplained variances against budget
• Unsupported payroll charges
• Missing attendance records or timesheets
• Missing asset registers
• Missing narrative reports
• Unsupported exchange rate calculations
• Incomplete beneficiary records
• Missing tax documentation
• Missing audit reports where required
• Mathematical errors or reconciliation differences
• Financial reports that do not align with actual activities implemented

An incomplete acquittal prevents SCI Finance from confirming whether the expenditures are:

• Valid
• Allowable
• Accurate
• Properly authorised
• Properly supported
• Consistent with donor rules
• Properly allocated to the award

As a result, SCI may classify expenditures as:

• Pending clarification
• Unsupported
• Ineligible
• Questioned costs
• Recoverable amounts

Incomplete acquittals are particularly serious where they involve:

• Large cash expenditures
• Weak procurement documentation
• Missing bank evidence
• Duplicate invoices
• Round-number transactions
• Unusual vendor patterns
• Missing beneficiary evidence
• Unreconciled balances
• Significant variances against budget

These issues may trigger:

• Additional review procedures
• Enhanced monitoring visits
• Transaction testing
• Spot audits
• Payment suspension
• Escalation to Country Finance leadership
• Fraud investigations
• Donor notifications where required

Under SCI’s monitoring procedures, Finance teams are responsible for reviewing acquittals to ensure that:

• Expenditures are supported by adequate evidence
• Programmatic and financial reports reconcile
• Bank reconciliations are complete and signed
• Advances are properly liquidated
• Expenditures comply with donor restrictions
• Budget variances are reasonable and explained
• Procurement procedures were followed
• Assets are appropriately recorded and safeguarded
• Any interest earned or unused balances are identified and refunded where required

Programme teams also play an important role in the acquittal process. Programme managers are expected to confirm that:

• Reported activities actually occurred
• Deliverables were achieved
• Beneficiary targets were met where applicable
• Financial expenditures are consistent with implementation progress
• The acquittal aligns with operational realities in the field

This linkage between programmatic and financial review is critical because a technically correct financial report may still be problematic if activities were not implemented as planned.

For example:

• A partner may report full expenditure against training costs, but no evidence exists that the training actually occurred.
• A partner may claim procurement expenses without evidence that the procured items were distributed or used appropriately.
• Financial reports may indicate vehicle fuel expenses inconsistent with field movement records.

Accordingly, acquittal review is both a financial and operational control process.

In SCI practice, common reasons for late or incomplete acquittals include:

• Weak accounting capacity at partner level
• Delays in obtaining field documentation
• Poor internet or banking infrastructure
• High staff turnover
• Inadequate understanding of donor requirements
• Weak filing systems
• Emergency operating conditions
• Delays in procurement reconciliation
• Cash-based operating environments
• Insufficient partner induction or training
• Weak supervision and oversight

To address these risks, SCI may implement partner capacity-building measures such as:

• Financial reporting training
• Procurement compliance training
• Coaching on bank reconciliation preparation
• Cash management guidance
• Introduction to donor compliance requirements
• Template-based reporting tools
• Monitoring visit support
• Enhanced review checklists
• Reduced advance levels
• Increased reporting frequency

SCI policies also emphasise that no additional disbursements should normally be released where prior acquittals remain outstanding, overdue, incomplete, or unsupported unless authorised under exceptional circumstances with documented justification and approval.

Where acquittal issues persist, SCI may implement corrective measures including:

• Withholding future payments
• Suspending the sub-award
• Reducing funding levels
• Requiring repayment of unsupported costs
• Conducting spot audits or investigations
• Revising payment schedules
• Escalating the issue to Regional Finance or Legal
• Terminating the sub-award agreement

During sub-award close-out, final acquittals become especially important because SCI must confirm that:

• All advances have been cleared
• All expenditures are supported
• Final balances are reconciled
• Unspent funds are returned
• Assets are properly transferred or disposed of
• Final reports are approved
• Donor reporting obligations are complete
• The audit trail is complete and retained appropriately

If final acquittals remain late or incomplete, close-out cannot be properly completed, which may delay donor reporting, financial closure, and audit sign-off.

Example scenario 1 — Late acquittal:

A partner received USD 50,000 as an advance payment in January to implement community health activities. Under the sub-award agreement, the partner was required to submit a financial liquidation and narrative progress report by the end of February. However, the partner failed to submit the acquittal package until late April. During this period, the advance remained uncleared in the GL, donor reporting timelines were affected, and Finance suspended further disbursements pending receipt of the overdue documentation. The partner’s acquittal was therefore considered late.

Example scenario 2 — Incomplete acquittal:

A partner submitted a quarterly financial report on time, but several required supporting documents were missing, including supplier invoices, bank reconciliation statements, payroll records, and evidence supporting procurement transactions. Additionally, the expenditures reported did not fully reconcile with the approved budget. Finance therefore classified the acquittal as incomplete and requested clarification and additional documentation before the expenditures could be accepted and the advance cleared.

Example scenario 3 — Late and incomplete acquittal:

The partner received advance funding in March under a signed sub-award agreement. The agreement required monthly liquidation of expenditures and quarterly financial reporting. However, the partner submitted only a partial report in May, without complete receipts, bank statements, procurement records, or explanation for the remaining cash balance. Because the reporting package was submitted after the agreed deadline and lacked sufficient supporting documentation, the partner’s acquittal was considered both late and incomplete. SCI Finance escalated the matter, withheld additional funding, and scheduled a targeted monitoring visit to assess the underlying control weaknesses.

From an audit and compliance perspective, late or incomplete acquittals are important because auditors will expect SCI to demonstrate that:

• Funds were properly monitored
• Advances were cleared appropriately
• Unsupported expenditures were identified and addressed
• Partner monitoring procedures were followed
• Exceptions were escalated and documented
• Management responses were implemented
• Donor conditions were enforced consistently

Audit reviewers commonly request:

• Signed sub-award agreements
• Financial reports and acquittal packages
• Bank reconciliations
• Supporting vouchers and invoices
• Monitoring reports
• Approval evidence
• Corrective action plans
• Correspondence regarding overdue reports
• Evidence of follow-up actions for missing documentation

Where acquittals are persistently late or incomplete, auditors may conclude that:

• Financial controls are weak
• Partner oversight is insufficient
• Advance management procedures are ineffective
• There is elevated fraud risk
• Donor funds may not have been adequately safeguarded

As a result, timely and complete acquittal management is considered a critical internal control within sub-award management frameworks.

In simpler operational language, the phrase may also be expressed as:

• “The partner submitted the financial accountability documents late and with missing supporting documents.”
• “The partner failed to provide complete financial liquidation within the agreed timeline.”
• “The financial acquittal package was delayed and not fully supported.”
• “The partner did not complete the required financial reporting on time.”
• “The supporting documents for fund utilisation were delayed or incomplete.”
• “The partner failed to submit sufficient evidence to clear the outstanding advance.”
• “The advance reconciliation remained unsupported due to missing documentation.”
• “The partner’s reporting package was overdue and lacked the required financial evidence.”
• “The partner did not provide a complete and timely liquidation of project funds.”
• “The acquittal submission was not adequate to support the reported expenditures and activities.”

Use case — Below is a much fuller draft for Adi’s actual-case material. It is written in a neutral, audit-friendly style and can be copied into a report, presentation, management note, or case summary. The draft is intentionally detailed so it can be used both as narrative evidence and as an explanatory control note. Four collaborator examples are included: two hospitals and two universities. Each case shows the same control issue in a different form, so the wording can be reused for internal reporting without changing the substance. The main themes are late submission, incomplete submission, insufficient supporting evidence, delayed close-out, financial impact on the ledger and reporting, and follow-up actions by Finance and Programme teams. The overall aim is to show not only that a report was late or incomplete, but also how that affected financial clearance, reconciliation, and accountability.
Purpose of this draft — The purpose of this draft is not only to state that a partner was late or incomplete, but to explain what the delay meant operationally, why it may have happened, what it affected financially, and how the issue was managed. In financial control discussions, the practical question is rarely just whether the report arrived. The more important question is whether the report was complete, on time, consistent with the activity, traceable to source documents, and sufficient to clear the advance. This draft therefore gives a fuller picture of the issue so that the final write-up can support internal review, management discussion, donor communication, or audit documentation. It is written in a way that allows the reader to understand the control problem without needing to know every detail of the underlying project.
How to read the four examples — Case 1 focuses on a hospital that submitted payment proof and receipts after the agreed deadline. Case 2 focuses on a hospital that submitted an acquittal package but with missing supporting evidence. Case 3 focuses on a university that submitted a report but not enough documentation to support all costs. Case 4 focuses on a university that submitted its final acquittal late during close-out and still needed revisions before closure. Together, the four examples show the difference between being late, being incomplete, being unsupported, and being late at close-out. That distinction matters because the root cause, the financial effect, and the follow-up action may differ depending on which type of failure occurred.
Why these examples are useful — These examples are useful because they show different manifestations of the same control weakness. One partner can be late because documents were not ready in time. Another can be incomplete because documents existed but were not attached. Another can be partially complete but still not strong enough for Finance to accept. Another can be late at close-out, which is more serious because it affects the final settlement of balances and the formal ending of the award. Having all four versions makes the actual-case material stronger and more realistic. It also helps distinguish between a simple timing delay and a deeper control weakness. In practice, Finance often sees all of these patterns across different partners, so a structured narrative is more helpful than a short generic statement.
Case 1: Hospital collaborator A — The hospital received an advance or project payment from OUCRU, but the payment proof, supporting receipts, and/or acquittal documents were submitted later than the agreed timeline. In operational terms, this meant the funds had already been used for project-related activity, but Finance could not yet clear the outstanding balance because the evidence needed for verification had not arrived on time. The issue was not only the late date itself. In some reporting periods, the partner also submitted the package in stages, meaning the first submission was partial and required several rounds of clarification before the file could be accepted. This created an open item in the partner advance account and kept the transaction outside normal clearance timing. It also meant that Finance had to keep the case visible in its follow-up tracking rather than removing it from the open-item list.
Case 1: What the delay looked like — The hospital could not produce a full set of receipts immediately after the expenditure period ended. Some invoices were still being collected from internal departments, some approvals were still pending, and some transaction references did not match the report. In a practical finance review, this means the acquittal package was not ready for acceptance by the expected deadline. Finance therefore had to wait longer than planned before it could test the expenditure for allowability, authorization, completeness, and traceability. If a single supporting document was missing for a high-value line, the whole package could be delayed because Finance would not want to clear the advance prematurely. The result is that a single missing item can hold up the entire file, even if most of the package is already available.
Case 1: Operational pattern — This type of delay often follows a familiar pattern. The project team spends the funds first, the supporting evidence is then collected from several internal units, and the package is assembled near the deadline. When one unit is slower than expected, the whole process slips. In hospitals, this can happen because finance, pharmacy, procurement, administration, and clinical departments all hold different parts of the documentation. A late signature or a missing invoice from only one source can keep the entire acquittal open. The delay is therefore often the result of a chain reaction rather than one single fault.
Case 1: Possible reasons — Common reasons for this type of delay include a slow internal approval process, weak document filing, multiple layers of sign-off, and dependence on several departments to provide underlying evidence. In some cases, the finance staff at the hospital may have been handling other urgent work and could not finalize the acquittal package on time. In other cases, the person responsible for preparing acquittals may have changed during the reporting period, which created continuity gaps. There may also have been delays in obtaining evidence from procurement, pharmacy, administration, or clinical units. A further reason can be that the hospital did not have a strong internal checklist showing exactly what needed to be attached before submission. This often results in the partner thinking the file is nearly complete when Finance still sees key gaps.
Case 1: Possible reasons, expanded — Another likely reason is that the hospital may have been relying on manual file circulation. When documents move physically from desk to desk, each approval adds time, and each missing signatory can stop the process completely. Sometimes the partner has the documents but not in a single complete folder, so the finance officer must search through different records or contact several staff members before submission. Where the transaction involves vendor payments, tax documentation, or delivery confirmations, the partner may also need to wait for external parties before the file is complete. This creates a delay that is administrative rather than fraudulent, but it still has a financial control effect because the advance remains uncleared. In many real cases, the cause is simply that the hospital’s internal process is not designed to produce a complete acquittal package quickly enough.
Case 1: Additional practical indicators — Late submission is often visible through small but telling operational signs. The partner may ask for a short extension shortly before the deadline. The package may arrive with the main financial summary but without all attachments. The finance officer may see repeated versions of the same report because the partner is still correcting figures. The explanation may be given verbally first and only later documented in writing. These patterns do not automatically imply misconduct, but they do show weak process discipline and poor readiness for formal acquittal. They can also indicate that the partner is trying to finish the file under pressure rather than through a planned workflow.
Case 1: Financial impacts — The most immediate financial impact was that the advance remained uncleared in the GL longer than expected. That meant the accounting records continued to show OUCRU as holding an outstanding amount with the partner, even though the project funds had already been used. Month-end reconciliation was delayed because the open balance could not be matched to a complete acquittal package. It also delayed recognition of the expenditure in the correct period. If the next disbursement was planned, Finance had to consider whether it should be withheld until the prior acquittal was completed. The delay also increased the risk that unsupported expenditure would move into the next accounting period unresolved. It can therefore distort both the current-month and future-month financial position.
Case 1: Financial impacts, expanded — A late acquittal affects cash visibility because the transferred funds remain outstanding in the system even after they have been spent by the partner. This can make the project appear to have a larger open balance than it really should have. It can also affect donor reporting, especially where the donor expects a clear picture of fund utilization by month-end or quarter-end. If the hospital later submits an incomplete package, Finance may need to classify some items as pending clarification, questioned, or unsupported until the missing evidence is received. In long-delay situations, the item can become aged and may require escalation because aged partner advances are usually reviewed more strictly. If many such items accumulate, they can distort the project’s financial position and affect the credibility of cash forecasting. A long-open advance also makes it harder for Finance to explain the current status of the award in review meetings.
Case 1: Financial impacts, operational detail — The effect is not limited to accounting entries. A delayed advance clearance can also affect how programme teams interpret implementation pace, because the books may still show an open balance even though field activity has moved forward. It can complicate the monthly review of partner balances and can create a backlog of items that Finance must explain in reporting meetings. If the open amount is significant, management may ask whether the issue is isolated or a sign of a broader weakness in partner management. In some cases, delayed clearing can also create pressure to delay or recheck the next payment cycle so that Finance does not increase exposure before the earlier amount is resolved.
Case 1: Risk implication — The risk is not only that the money is late in being cleared. The deeper concern is that Finance cannot fully confirm whether the expenditure was allowable, accurately recorded, and properly supported until the documentation is complete. If the delay continues, the partner may be able to spend further funds while the older advance remains unresolved, which weakens the discipline around advance management. It also makes it harder to determine whether the original payment was spent correctly or whether some funds should be returned, adjusted, or reclassified. This risk is especially important when the partner handles large cash-based transactions or where supporting records are spread across multiple departments.
Case 1: Risk implication, expanded — A late acquittal can also indicate that the partner is operating with limited financial control capacity. When the same type of delay repeats, it may point to a pattern in which documents are assembled only at the last minute. That creates dependency on individual staff memory, increases the risk of errors, and weakens the evidence trail. For Finance, the issue is therefore both accounting and governance-related. It is not just a late file; it is a sign that the control environment may not be sufficiently robust. The risk is also cumulative because every delayed package increases the backlog of items that need review.
Case 1: Follow-up actions performed — Finance issued reminders before and after the deadline, requested the missing receipts, and asked the hospital to reconcile the report with bank or cash records. When the response remained incomplete, Finance held the next payment and followed up again by email or meeting. The programme team was informed so the issue could be chased at operational level as well as financial level. Once the package was received, Finance reviewed it line by line, checked the receipts against the report, and cleared the advance only after the evidence was considered acceptable. This approach ensures that the late package is not simply accepted without review, because timeliness alone is not enough for clearance.
Case 1: Follow-up actions, expanded — If the hospital continued to delay, the follow-up usually became more formal. Finance would record the issue, document each reminder, and maintain a communication trail showing when requests were sent and what was still missing. If the delay affected more than one reporting cycle, management could ask the partner for a corrective action plan, stronger internal controls, or a clearer submission timetable. In more serious cases, further disbursement would stay on hold until the outstanding acquittal was fully resolved. This process is important because it shows that Finance did not simply wait passively; it actively monitored the balance and pushed the partner toward completion. The documentation trail also protects the organisation by showing that the issue was escalated appropriately.
Case 1: Follow-up actions, further detail — The usual escalation path is incremental. The first reminder is normally friendly and operational. If no response is received, Finance sends a second request that is more specific about the missing items. If the documents are still not received, the issue may be escalated to a supervisor or programme lead. A final step can be a decision to pause additional funding until the partner provides a complete and credible package. Every step is important because it creates evidence that the control issue was chased rather than ignored. In a strong control environment, each reminder also becomes a signal to the partner that late submission is not treated as a minor administrative matter.
Case 1: Suggested wording for report — “Hospital collaborator A submitted payment proof and receipts after the agreed deadline. The delay was mainly due to internal document collection and approval processes. The late submission kept the advance uncleared in the GL and delayed reconciliation. Finance followed up through reminders, requested missing documents, and held further disbursement until the acquittal was completed.” This wording is neutral, factual, and suitable for a management report or internal note.
Case 1: Management-style summary — This case shows a routine but important control issue. The hospital did not necessarily misuse the funds; the issue is that the accountability evidence arrived too late to allow timely clearance. In finance terms, the late evidence delayed advance liquidation, kept the balance open, and postponed the reporting process. The response by Finance was standard but necessary: reminders, document requests, and disbursement hold until completion. This type of case is usually easiest to explain when it is framed as a delay in evidence rather than an allegation of wrongdoing.
Case 1: Audit-style interpretation — From an audit perspective, the concern is that the open advance remained outstanding for longer than expected, which increases the amount of time during which the funds are not fully evidenced. Auditors generally look for proof that advances are monitored, that overdue items are chased, and that unresolved balances are escalated. This case supports that control narrative because it shows that Finance actively followed up rather than ignoring the overdue package. If the communication trail is retained, the file is stronger because it demonstrates active control.
Case 1: Additional narrative detail — If the hospital’s reporting cycle is monthly or quarterly, a late acquittal can also create a knock-on effect for the next cycle. The finance team may need to split their attention between the overdue package and the new period’s transactions. That makes the close of the month more difficult because the team is working with both current expenses and prior unresolved balances. Even if the spending itself was valid, the delay still creates extra review time and increases the administrative burden on Finance. The practical effect is that one late package often creates several hours of extra work across multiple staff members.
Case 1: One-line operational summary — The hospital spent the funds, but the evidence came too late for timely clearance, so the advance stayed open and Finance had to chase until the package was complete.
Case 2: Hospital collaborator B — The hospital submitted an acquittal package, but it was incomplete. The file did not contain all supporting invoices, some payment references were unclear, and the bank reconciliation was not attached in the first submission. The hospital had attempted to submit on time, but Finance could not fully validate the expenditure because the documentary support was not complete enough to prove that the funds were properly used. This means the issue is not only about timing; it is also about whether the package is structurally sufficient for clearance. In some cases, the file looks substantial, but the missing attachments prevent Finance from treating it as a true acquittal.
Case 2: What the incompleteness looked like — The submission included a financial summary, but several critical attachments were missing. For example, some receipts were absent, some procurement documents were incomplete, and some explanations for budget variances were not detailed enough. In some lines, the amounts in the summary did not match the underlying documents. This meant the package could not yet be treated as a completed acquittal, even though something had been submitted. From a control perspective, the presence of a report alone is not enough if the supporting evidence cannot be tied back to the original transactions. A good report without evidence is still not enough for financial clearance.
Case 2: Why incomplete packages matter — Incomplete packages are risky because they create uncertainty. Finance may see numbers on a form, but without bank evidence, receipts, or reconciliations, the numbers cannot be fully trusted. Even if the spending was legitimate, the absence of evidence means Finance cannot confirm that at the required level of assurance. This is why an incomplete acquittal is not treated the same as a complete acquittal. The control objective is not simply to receive a document but to receive a document package that can support an accounting decision. If the evidence trail is weak, the accounting decision must remain open.
Case 2: Possible reasons — This type of issue often happens when the partner has a weak filing system or limited staff capacity to compile the full package. It can also happen when a staff member preparing the report does not have access to all source documents, or when the hospital assumes that a summary is enough without attaching the required evidence. Staff turnover, weak supervision, and unfamiliarity with donor requirements are also common causes. In some cases, the problem is that the finance person knows the documents exist, but they are stored in different units and cannot be gathered quickly enough. The issue may also arise because the partner has not internalized the difference between a report and an acquittal package.
Case 2: Possible reasons, expanded — Another reason is that the hospital may have internal pressure to submit on time and therefore sends the main financial schedule first, expecting to attach the backup later. That approach creates a submission that appears timely but is still incomplete. If the people approving the report are not familiar with partner reporting rules, they may underestimate the importance of the reconciliation or the source documents. In some cases, there may be no dedicated staff member following the acquittal checklist from start to finish, which leads to missing attachments at the final stage. This usually results in avoidable back-and-forth with Finance and delays the eventual clearance of the advance.
Case 2: Common signals of weak completeness — Finance often notices that the package includes a front-page summary but no supporting schedule, or that the attachments are present but not linked to individual lines. Another common signal is that the package contains totals but not transaction-level evidence. A third sign is that the narrative explanation is too general and does not explain the key differences between the report and the supporting records. These are practical indicators that the acquittal is incomplete even if it appears substantial at first glance. When these signs appear, Finance normally assumes that further checking is needed before any part of the expenditure can be accepted.
Case 2: Financial impacts — Incomplete documentation creates a risk that part of the expenditure will be classified as unsupported or questioned. That can delay recognition of the expense and prevent the advance from being cleared. It can also affect future payments because Finance may decide not to release more funds until the missing evidence is provided. If the missing documents are never produced, the amount may have to be recovered, reclassified, or adjusted. The open balance also remains on the books, which means the project’s financial position does not reflect the true status of the partner account.
