“A partner’s acquittal was late or incomplete” means the partner organization did not properly submit the required accountability documents within the expected timeline.
In the context of sub-awards, partner awards, sub-grants, or partner agreements, the term “acquittal” refers to the formal financial and programmatic accountability process through which a partner organisation demonstrates, evidences, reconciles, and certifies how funds, commodities, assets, or other resources transferred by SCI were utilised in accordance with the signed sub-award agreement, approved budget, donor requirements, organisational policies, and applicable statutory or regulatory obligations.

The acquittal process is one of the core financial control mechanisms within partner management and award oversight. It is designed to ensure that all transferred resources are:

• Used strictly for authorised programme purposes
• Supported by sufficient documentary evidence
• Traceable through accounting and banking records
• Reconciled to approved budgets and workplans
• Consistent with reported programmatic outputs and deliverables
• Subject to review, monitoring, and audit verification
• Properly recorded within the General Ledger and award management systems
• Cleared, adjusted, or refunded where balances remain outstanding

Within SCI partner management procedures, acquittals are closely linked to:

• Advance management and liquidation
• Financial monitoring and desk reviews
• Programmatic monitoring and deliverable verification
• Donor compliance obligations
• Audit readiness and documentation retention
• Fraud prevention and fiduciary risk mitigation
• Sub-award close-out requirements
• Monthly reconciliation of partner advance accounts

An acquittal therefore serves as the formal evidence package that allows SCI Finance and Programme teams to determine whether:

1. Funds transferred to the partner were spent appropriately
2. Expenditures are allowable under donor and SCI rules
3. The partner complied with reporting timelines and documentation standards
4. The reported expenditures reconcile with actual activities implemented
5. Further payments may be released
6. Outstanding advances may be cleared from the GL
7. Any unspent balances, unsupported expenditures, or questioned costs must be recovered or adjusted

In practical terms, acquittal is not merely the submission of receipts. It is a structured accountability exercise involving financial reconciliation, documentary verification, management review, and programmatic confirmation.

Under SCI procedures, partner acquittals may relate to:

• Monthly reports
• Quarterly reports
• Interim liquidation of advances
• Milestone-based reporting
• Final financial reports at sub-award close-out
• Emergency response reporting cycles
• Donor-specific reporting requirements

A standard partner acquittal package commonly includes, but is not limited to:

• Detailed financial expenditure reports
• Expense summaries against approved budget lines
• General ledger extracts or cashbooks maintained by the partner
• Receipts and supporting invoices
• Procurement documentation and quotations
• Payroll records where partner staff are charged to the award
• Timesheets or attendance records where required
• Bank statements covering the reporting period
• Bank reconciliations signed and dated by authorised personnel
• Cash-on-hand statements and petty cash reconciliations
• Fixed asset records for assets purchased under the award
• Distribution records for in-kind assistance
• Activity reports or narrative progress reports
• Beneficiary data where applicable
• Variance explanations against budget
• Exchange rate calculations where foreign currency transactions occurred
• Remaining cash balance reconciliation
• Refund evidence for unused balances or interest recoveries
• Tax withholding documentation where legally required
• Prior approval documentation for exceptional expenditures
• Audit reports or spot-check reports where required by donor terms

Failure to obtain proper acquittals exposes SCI and donors to significant financial, compliance, operational, and reputational risks, including:

• Unsupported expenditures
• Fraud or misuse of funds
• Duplicate or fictitious payments
• Donor disallowances
• Material audit findings
• Inaccurate financial statements
• Idle cash balances held by partners
• Delayed project implementation
• Weak audit trails
• Regulatory non-compliance
• Reputational damage with donors and stakeholders

Accordingly, SCI applies a risk-based approach to partner monitoring and acquittal review. Higher-risk partners, larger sub-awards, emergency response projects, or partners operating in insecure or high-risk environments may be subject to:

• More frequent acquittal submissions

• Additional desk reviews

• Escalated management review

In this framework, the phrase:

“A partner’s acquittal was late or incomplete”

means that the partner failed to fully comply with the agreed accountability and reporting obligations under the sub-award agreement.

This may involve either:

• Delayed submission of required reports and supporting documents
• Submission of partial or insufficient documentation
• Failure to provide adequate supporting evidence
• Failure to reconcile expenditures properly
• Failure to clear outstanding advances within the required timeframe
• Failure to respond to queries raised during Finance review

More specifically:

Late acquittal

= the partner submitted the required financial and/or programmatic accountability documentation after the reporting deadline defined in the signed sub-award agreement, reporting schedule, donor conditions, or approved payment terms.

A late acquittal may include situations such as:

• Quarterly financial reports submitted weeks or months after the deadline
• Delayed liquidation of advance payments
• Failure to submit reports before requesting additional disbursements
• Late submission of bank reconciliations or supporting schedules
• Delayed submission of donor-required reporting templates
• Failure to submit final acquittal documentation during close-out
• Delays caused by weak financial systems or staffing gaps at the partner level
• Delayed responses to Finance clarification requests

Operationally, late acquittals create significant control concerns because they:

• Delay advance clearance
• Increase the risk of unsupported expenditures
• Reduce visibility over actual cash utilisation
• Complicate month-end and year-end reconciliations
• Delay donor reporting submissions
• Limit SCI’s ability to assess project performance accurately
• Increase audit exposure and compliance risk

Late acquittals may also indicate broader underlying issues such as:

• Weak partner financial capacity
• Poor accounting systems
• Inadequate staffing or segregation of duties
• Weak document retention practices
• Operational disruption in the field
• Cash management problems
• Fraud indicators or misuse concerns
• Poor programme oversight

Examples of late acquittals include:

• A partner receives an advance in January but only submits the financial report in April despite a February reporting deadline.
• A partner fails to provide the required quarterly liquidation before requesting additional funding.
• Final close-out reports are submitted months after the sub-award end date.
• The partner delays submission of procurement documentation needed to support major purchases.

Under SCI’s monitoring procedures, Finance teams are responsible for reviewing acquittals to ensure that:

• Expenditures are supported by adequate evidence
• Programmatic and financial reports reconcile
• Bank reconciliations are complete and signed
• Advances are properly liquidated
• Expenditures comply with donor restrictions
• Budget variances are reasonable and explained
• Procurement procedures were followed

Programme teams also play an important role in the acquittal process. Programme managers are expected to confirm that:

• Reported activities actually occurred
• Deliverables were achieved
• Beneficiary targets were met where applicable
• Financial expenditures are consistent with implementation progress
• The acquittal aligns with operational realities in the field

This linkage between programmatic and financial review is critical because a technically correct financial report may still be problematic if activities were not implemented as planned.

For example:

• A partner may report full expenditure against training costs, but no evidence exists that the training actually occurred.
• A partner may claim procurement expenses without evidence that the procured items were distributed or used appropriately.
• Financial reports may indicate vehicle fuel expenses inconsistent with field movement records.

Accordingly, acquittal review is both a financial and operational control process.

• Weak accounting capacity at partner level

• Poor internet or banking infrastructure
• High staff turnover
• Inadequate understanding of donor requirements
• Weak filing systems
• Emergency operating conditions
• Delays in procurement reconciliation
• Cash-based operating environments

SCI policies also emphasise that no additional disbursements should normally be released where prior acquittals remain outstanding, overdue, incomplete, or unsupported unless authorised under exceptional circumstances with documented justification and approval.

Where acquittal issues persist, SCI may implement corrective measures including:

• Withholding future payments
• Suspending the sub-award
• Reducing funding levels
• Requiring repayment of unsupported costs
• Conducting spot audits or investigations
• Revising payment schedules
• Escalating the issue to Regional Finance or Legal

During sub-award close-out, final acquittals become especially important because SCI must confirm that:

• All advances have been cleared
• All expenditures are supported
• Final balances are reconciled
• Unspent funds are returned
• Assets are properly transferred or disposed of
• Final reports are approved
• Donor reporting obligations are complete
• The audit trail is complete and retained appropriately

If final acquittals remain late or incomplete, close-out cannot be properly completed, which may delay donor reporting, financial closure, and audit sign-off.

Example scenario 1 — Late acquittal:

A partner received USD 50,000 as an advance payment in January to implement community health activities. Under the sub-award agreement, the partner was required to submit a financial liquidation and narrative progress report by the end of February. However, the partner failed to submit the acquittal package until late April. During this period, the advance remained uncleared in the GL, donor reporting timelines were affected, and Finance suspended further disbursements pending receipt of the overdue documentation. The partner’s acquittal was therefore considered late.

Example scenario 2 — Incomplete acquittal:

A partner submitted a quarterly financial report on time, but several required supporting documents were missing, including supplier invoices, bank reconciliation statements, payroll records, and evidence supporting procurement transactions. Additionally, the expenditures reported did not fully reconcile with the approved budget. Finance therefore classified the acquittal as incomplete and requested clarification and additional documentation before the expenditures could be accepted and the advance cleared.

Example scenario 3 — Late and incomplete acquittal:

The partner received advance funding in March under a signed sub-award agreement. The agreement required monthly liquidation of expenditures and quarterly financial reporting. However, the partner submitted only a partial report in May, without complete receipts, bank statements, procurement records, or explanation for the remaining cash balance. Because the reporting package was submitted after the agreed deadline and lacked sufficient supporting documentation, the partner’s acquittal was considered both late and incomplete. SCI Finance escalated the matter, withheld additional funding, and scheduled a targeted monitoring visit to assess the underlying control weaknesses.

From an audit and compliance perspective, late or incomplete acquittals are important because auditors will expect SCI to demonstrate that:

• Funds were properly monitored
• Advances were cleared appropriately
• Unsupported expenditures were identified and addressed
• Partner monitoring procedures were followed
• Exceptions were escalated and documented
• Management responses were implemented
• Donor conditions were enforced consistently

Audit reviewers commonly request:

• Financial reports and acquittal packages

• Monitoring reports

Where acquittals are persistently late or incomplete, auditors may conclude that:

• Financial controls are weak
• Partner oversight is insufficient
• Advance management procedures are ineffective
• There is elevated fraud risk
• Donor funds may not have been adequately safeguarded

As a result, timely and complete acquittal management is considered a critical internal control within sub-award management frameworks.

Common reasons across collaborators, expanded — Across the cases, there is a clear relationship between internal process complexity and the risk of late or incomplete acquittal. Common reasons across collaborators, further detail — Another shared issue is that partners sometimes treat acquittal as a reporting task instead of a control task. Better practice is to collect supporting evidence throughout the spending cycle rather than at the end. When evidence is gathered continuously, the final package is much easier to assemble and more likely to pass Finance review the first time.
