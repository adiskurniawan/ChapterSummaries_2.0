Interdisciplinarity — what it is & how to practise it
Definition (short): Interdisciplinarity = purposeful collaboration between disciplines to produce insights that cannot be achieved by any single discipline alone. It combines methods, theories and epistemologies to address a shared problem.
Modes & metaphors:
Additive: disciplines work in parallel and outputs are combined at the end (A + B → report).
Interactive: disciplines iteratively shape each other’s questions and methods (A ↔ B → new method).
Holistic: the problem framing and solution emerge from integrated perspective(s), often producing new concepts or methods.
Related terms (clarify differences):
Multidisciplinary (multi): multiple disciplines work on a problem but remain epistemically distinct; results sit side-by-side.
Interdisciplinary (inter): disciplines integrate methods/frames to create shared insights (the primary focus here).
Cross-disciplinary (cross): one discipline uses tools from another without deep integration.
Transdisciplinary (trans): integration goes beyond academic disciplines to include non-academic knowledge (policy, community) aiming for societally actionable knowledge.
Intradisciplinary (intra): variety within a single discipline (subfields interacting).
Core assumptions to make explicit:
- Different disciplines have different epistemic standards (what counts as evidence).
- Methods and language are not automatically interoperable.
- Power, workload and reward systems are uneven across partners.
- Integration requires time, negotiation and translation work.
Language (practical guidance):
- Translate, don’t assume: define key terms and methods in plain language.
- Create a one-page shared glossary for recurring terms.
- When writing for mixed audiences, use layered language: one-line takeaway → one-paragraph synthesis → technical annex.
- Avoid discipline-specific abbreviations on first use; show example data/outputs in neutral formats.
Reciprocity (how to operationalise):
- Explicitly map benefits for each partner (capacity, authorship, funding, visibility).
- Share budget lines for local/admin support and capacity strengthening.
- Agree authorship and data rights up-front.
- Compensate non-academic contributors equitably (honoraria, resources).
Understanding your audience (tailoring):
- Identify what each discipline values (theory, method, data, impact).
- For academics emphasise novelty/rigour; for practitioners emphasise feasibility and outcomes; for policy actors emphasise relevance and timelines.
- Use different channels: slides + visual summaries for transdisciplinary partners; detailed methods appendix for specialist reviewers.
Practical steps to run interdisciplinary work (checklist):
1) Co-create the research question with representatives of the key disciplines.
2) Draft a short integration plan: who integrates, when, and how (workshops, joint analysis sessions).
3) Budget time for translation work (glossary, joint methods sessions).
4) Set governance that balances technical leads and integrators.
5) Run early pilots to surface mismatches and adapt methods.
Barriers & mitigations:
- Barrier: disciplinary jargon → Mitigation: one-page glossary + translation sessions.
- Barrier: unequal reward structures → Mitigation: pre-agreed authorship/credit and capacity support.
- Barrier: methodological incompatibility → Mitigation: hybrid methods, cross-training, and documented compromises.
Evaluation & success indicators (interdisciplinary focus):
- Evidence of integrated outputs (new methods, joint frameworks, co-authored papers across disciplines).
- Stakeholders can explain the integrated insight in a single sentence.
- Documented mutual benefits (training delivered, shared data, joint funding).
Quick templates (copy-ready):
One-line integration goal: "Combine [method A] and [method B] to answer [joint question] so we can [explicit outcome]."
Glossary entry format: Term | Definition (1 line) | Discipline(s) using it | Practical implication for analysis.

Language — operational guidance
Problem statement: Conversations across disciplines fail when the same words mean different things (e.g., "model", "validation", "significant", "impact"). Anticipate divergent interpretations and design messages to reduce costly misunderstandings.
Purpose: Ensure your message is heard as intended by translating discipline-specific language into shared, actionable meaning for the target audience.
Core rules:
- Assume ambiguity: never assume shared definitions.
- Translate, don’t dumb-down: paraphrase technical terms into neutral, outcome-focused language.
- Use layered explanations: 1-line takeaway → 1-paragraph synthesis → technical annex.
Common cross-discipline traps (examples):
- Model: in engineering = physical/Computational simulation; in statistics = fitted predictive model; in social sciences = conceptual framework.
- Validation: in lab sciences = experimental replication; in modelling = hold-out data; in social research = stakeholder verification.
- Significant: statisticians mean p-value; policymakers mean practical importance.
Audience mapping (how to do it):
1) List disciplines in the audience.
2) For each, note what they primarily value (theory, rigour, feasibility, impact).
3) Tailor the headline and the first sentence to the highest-priority audience, then layer detail for specialists.
Practical translation tactics:
- Provide a 1-line definition for any critical technical term on first use.
- Create a one-page glossary for recurring terms.
- Show an illustrative example (data snippet, diagram) that makes the term concrete.
- Use analogies sparingly and check they don't introduce new misconceptions.
Reciprocity & language:
- Ask partners: "Which terms do you use differently?" and record shared definitions.
- Offer to adapt your terminology in shared documents and agree on the canonical glossary entry.
Message templates (copy-ready):
One-line (mixed audience): "Summary: [one-sentence outcome]. For technical readers: [one-line method]. See Annex: [technical details]."
First-use term template: "Term — brief definition (discipline-specific meaning) — practical implication for this project."
Checks before sending (quick checklist):
- Does the message include 1-line takeaway? (Yes/No)
- Are discipline-specific terms defined at first use? (Yes/No)
- Is there a technical annex or link for specialists? (Yes/No)
- Could any common term be misread by another discipline? (Yes/No)
Tools & artefacts to produce:
- Shared glossary (one page).
- Layered summary templates (1-line → 1-paragraph → annex).
- Short explainer examples (diagram, data snippet).
- Meeting norm: 30s term-clarify slot when new terms arise.
Facilitation techniques:
- Glossary-rotation: each meeting one discipline presents 2 key terms and their real meaning/implication.
- Round-robin paraphrase: after an explanation, another discipline summarizes what they heard in one sentence.
- Translation pair: pair up members from different disciplines to co-edit the executive summary.
Measurement & success indicators:
- % messages with defined key terms.
- Reduction in clarification rounds for multi-disciplinary requests.
- Team survey: % feel they understand other disciplines' key terms.
Self-check: How many disciplinary languages do you speak? (quick self-assessment)
- List disciplines you can comfortably read/translate (e.g., epidemiology, economics, qualitative social science, remote sensing).
- For each, rate fluency 1–5: Read papers (1), Understand methods (3), Explain to others (5).
- Action: if any critical partner scores ≤3, plan a 1-hour cross-training or create a 1-page cheat-sheet before joint work.
Case prompts (practice):
- Convert a technical paragraph about "model validation" into a one-line summary and a 3-sentence explanation for non-statisticians.
- Build a 1-page glossary for your project with 8 terms that commonly cause confusion.

Reciprocity — practical analysis of two vignettes & questions to ask
Scenario A (multimorbidity spatial modelling)
Researcher: wants regional treatment approaches; proposes spatial model of multimorbidity using NHS data to identify regional patterns and target interventions.
What is interesting/novel for the mathematical modeller?
- Access to large, linked, geo-coded NHS datasets (rare and valuable for spatial inference).
- A problem framed for policy/implementation (moving from pattern detection to intervention design).
- Opportunity to build spatially-explicit predictive models that feed directly into intervention targeting and evaluation.
- Potential to develop new methods for multi-morbidity clustering and spatial heterogeneity.
Why the modeler should participate (reciprocal benefits)
- Methodological novelty & publishable outputs (new models, code, simulations).
- Access to curated data and domain expertise to validate models.
- Co-authorship, visibility in applied policy contexts, and potential for follow-on funding for methods translation.
Critical questions the modeller should ask (before committing)
- Exactly which datasets (tables/fields) and geographic resolution are available?
- What are the legal/ethical/data-sharing constraints (NHS governance, pseudonymisation)?
- Who will prepare/clean/link the data and who pays for data access/secure compute?
- What is the expected deliverable (maps, risk scores, code) and timeline?
- How will model outputs be validated and used by clinicians/policymakers?
- What resource/time commitment and authorship/credit are proposed?
Risks & mitigations (modeller perspective)
- Risk: limited or delayed data access → Mitigate: data access plan & contingency data sources.
- Risk: unclear use-case → Mitigate: co-develop decision rules with clinical partners.
- Risk: reputational risk from misuse of model → Mitigate: pre-agreed interpretation notes and governance.
Practical reciprocity checklist (modeller ↔ clinical researcher)
- Data access & compute (who, when, cost)
- Roles: model lead, data custodian, clinical validators
- Outputs: code repo, reproducible pipeline, maps, policy brief
- Credit: authorship, presentations, training sessions
- Ethics & governance: data approvals, safe outputs, release policy
Scenario B (behavioural info points + vaccination delivery)
Researcher: tests how explanations affect vaccination choice at community centres; seeks health professional collaborator to administer vaccines.
What is interesting/novel for the health professional?
- Embedded, pragmatic evaluation of communication strategies with real behaviour outcomes.
- Opportunity to link service delivery with evidence on uptake drivers in a local population.
- Potential for co-designed interventions that improve clinic workflows or outreach.
Why the health professional should participate (reciprocal benefits)
- Operational insights that can increase uptake and service efficiency.
- Training and data to support quality improvement and local targets.
- Recognition, co-authorship, possible funding for staff time and supplies.
What even is a “health professional”? (practical definition)
- Clinicians authorised to vaccinate: e.g., nurses (practice/public health), GPs, pharmacists (where pharmacist vaccination is authorised), immunisation nurses or community health teams.
- Includes those with clinical accountability, training in vaccine administration, and ability to manage adverse events per local policy.
Critical questions the health professional should ask (before agreeing)
- Is this an observational intervention or a trial/randomised study? What approvals are needed (ethics, governance)?
- Who provides vaccines, cold-chain, consumables, and liability indemnity?
- What clinical governance is in place for adverse events and record-keeping?
- What is the recruitment pathway, consent process, and data capture plan?
- What is the expected time commitment, staffing, and compensation?
- How will patient data be handled, stored and linked to research datasets?
Risks & mitigations (health professional perspective)
- Risk: clinical governance gaps → Mitigate: written SOPs, named clinical lead, indemnity confirmation.
- Risk: disruption to routine services → Mitigate: separate staffing/compensation and clear scheduling.
- Risk: informed consent failures → Mitigate: tested consent materials and training.
Asking the right questions (common checklist for both collaborations)
- Purpose & outcomes: what exact question does the study answer and how will results be used?
- Roles & deliverables: who does what, by when, and with what resources?
- Benefits: who gains what, and when (papers, training, infrastructure, payments)?
- Ethics & governance: IRB/REC, data protection, clinical approvals, indemnity.
- Data & methods: access, cleaning responsibilities, reproducibility, validation, and release rules.
- Timeline & feasibility: realistic milestones that allow equity checks.
- Credit & authorship: agreed criteria and sequence for outputs.
Short templates (copy-ready prompts for initial outreach)
- To modeller (from clinical lead): "We can provide NHS-derived, pseudonymised regional diagnosis tables and local clinical context. Are you available to scope a spatial model—deliverables: reproducible code, regional risk maps, 3 advisory notes—within 4 months? Who needs data access approvals?"
- To health professional (from behavioural lead): "We need a vaccinator for in-centre information pilots (X hours/week for 8 weeks). We will supply vaccines/consumables, SOPs, indemnity confirmation and pay honoraria. Can you confirm governance requirements and availability?"
Final reciprocity rules (keep simple and enforceable)
- Write a one-page compact before work starts: roles, resources, data access, ethics, outputs, credit, and a 2-week review point.
- Budget realistic staff time and governance costs.
- Pilot before scale to test feasibility and governance.
- Log decisions and credit contributors visibly in outputs.

Understanding your audience — operational guidance
Purpose: Tailor engagement so your message aligns with what each audience values, their professional norms, and their resourcing realities; enable equitable co-design rather than one-way requests.
Goal: For any outreach, be able to state: (a) what the audience gains, (b) what you need from them, (c) realistic time & resource expectations, and (d) agreed next steps.
Outcomes:
- Audience-specific one-line asks and benefits.
- Shared timeline and budget sketch.
- Agreement on deliverables and decision owners.
What is interesting & valuable to them? (quick map)
Academic researchers: novelty, methodological rigour, authorship, funding for time.
Clinicians / health professionals: patient impact, feasibility, low-clinical-burden workflows, CPD/recognition.
Policymakers / commissioners: actionable evidence, timelines aligned to policy windows, cost/benefit.
Funders / institutional leaders: clear outputs, value-for-money, risk management, scalability.
Community partners / practitioners: tangible local benefits, capacity, respectful engagement, reasonable compensation.
Data/tech teams: clear spec, access permissions, realistic SLAs, maintenance expectations.
Professional expectations by discipline (what they care about)
- Methods & rigor: reproducibility, validation, ethical approvals.
- Outputs & credit: authorship norms, presentation venues, reporting formats.
- Timelines & processes: review cycles, procurement, governance.
- Compliance: data protection, clinical governance, institutional sign-offs.
How much time does their work typically take? (estimates)
- Rapid consult / advice: hours–days.
- Secondary analysis / modelling: weeks–3 months (depends on data quality).
- New data collection / pilot: 3–12 months.
- Full trials or multi-site implementation: 12–36+ months.
- Funding & governance approvals can add weeks–months.
How much money does their work typically require? (estimates & caveats)
- Small pilots / workshops: £5k–£50k.
- Project-level studies (single-site, modest fieldwork): £50k–£250k.
- Multi-site studies, trials, infrastructure: £250k–£5m+.
Caveat: cost varies by field, country, and ethical/governance requirements. Money enables capacity and time but ≠ intrinsic value—low-cost outputs (policy briefs, training) can have high impact.
Develop approaches together (operational steps)
1) Co-design meeting: clarify problem, roles, realistic deliverables.
2) Produce a one-page Benefit Map: Partner | Contribution | Benefit | Timing.
3) Draft a lightweight compact (roles, budget lines, decision rules, authorship).
4) Agree minimum viable pilot before full scale.
5) Schedule check-ins and a 30-day equity review.
Trust & time (practical guidance):
- Build relationships before deadlines; include a small "trust fund" in budgets for travel/exchanges.
- Prefer initial in-person or intensive virtual workshop to align expectations.
- Record decisions and credit contributions visibly.
- Normalise early difficult conversations; set short decision SLAs.
Scripts & message templates (audience-focused)
Academic: "Ask: 10 days of modelling support to produce regional risk maps by [date]. Offer: data access, co-authorship, methods paper lead."
Clinician: "Ask: vaccinator for 8 weeks (X hrs/wk). Offer: supplies, SOPs, honoraria, service-improvement report."
Policymaker: "Ask: 30-minute input to align outcomes to policy windows. Offer: short evidence brief and tailored recommendations."
Checklist before outreach (must-have items)
- One-line ask + clear benefit for the recipient. (Yes/No)
- Realistic time estimate and required resources stated. (Yes/No)
- What you bring (data, funding, admin) explicit. (Yes/No)
- Governance/ethics/data constraints noted. (Yes/No)
- Proposed next step (20-min call or direct intro) and deadline. (Yes/No)
Measurement & signals of alignment:
- Response within SLA and named next actions.
- Partner confirms at least one tangible benefit for them.
- Equity check shows resource/time expectations met.

Project communications — comprehensive operational guide
Overview (purpose): A practical, testable playbook to set teams up for successful communication across research collaborations. Covers needs assessment, mechanisms, responsibilities, records, pathways, interdisciplinarity, language, reciprocity, audience understanding, principles (purpose, leverage, outcome), partnerships, psychological safety, timing, finding people, and trust-building. Use as a workshop handout and an operational checklist.
SETTING YOUR TEAM UP FOR SUCCESS — needs assessment & practical resourcing
Objective: ensure every team member can participate effectively from day 1.
Needs assessment steps: 1) Circulate a 1-page survey: role, tasks, access needs, constraints (calendar, internet, institutional approvals), preferred channels and available hours. 2) Consolidate responses into a Resource Matrix: Name | Role | Tasks | Access needed | Bandwidth (hrs/wk) | Preferred channel. 3) Identify gaps and cost them.
Core resourcing items to budget explicitly: Wifi bundles/dongles for field partners; laptops or device grants; software licences (Teams, Zoom, EndNote, STATA/R/Python libraries); institutional access (OneDrive/SharePoint, secure data sandboxes); data costing (access fees, secure compute); admin support for partner institutions.
Budget practicalities: include a line for a small “trust & access” fund (travel, mixers, seed work), data access & governance costs, and contingency (5–10%). Put capacity strengthening as explicit budget items (honoraria, workshops).
Procurement & timing: procure critical hardware & licences at least 8–12 weeks before fieldwork; allocate one staff day per partner for onboarding to shared platforms.
SETTING YOUR TEAM UP FOR SUCCESS — what does everyone need to engage effectively?
- Reliable internet (dongles, prepaid bundles for remote partners).
- Working device (laptop/tablet) and basic peripherals.
- Access credentials to shared drives and project tools.
- Short induction: platform walkthrough, communication norms, quick glossary.
- Clear role description with time commitment and deliverables.
- Local admin support and budget lines covering small expenses.
WHAT IS YOUR PLAN? — mechanisms & routines
Meeting cadence (recommended baseline):
- Weekly WP (work-package) 30–60 min stand-ups for operational issues.
- Bi-weekly technical working groups for methods & data (1 hour).
- Monthly leadership/steering meeting (60–90 min) for decisions and risk review.
- Quarterly all-partner review (2–4 hrs; ideally in-person once per year).
Standing agenda items (example):
- Safety & ethics updates (if relevant).
- Actions from last meeting (quick status tick).
- Blockers & resourcing needs.
- Risk & mitigation (update).
- Decisions requiring sign-off.
- Quick “what worried you?” pulse (60s per person, optional).
How will you meet?: choose based on function: face-to-face for new or trust-critical topics; video calls for iterative technical work; email for formal records and approvals; task-tracker for day-to-day assignments.
Information flow model (copy-ready): Activity supervisors → WP leads → Leadership/Steering → External stakeholders. Feedback & decisions flow down via leadership → WP leads → supervisors. Implement a 3-tier log: Decisions Register, Action Tracker (owner/due/status), Archive (minutes, versions, evidence links).
WHO IS RESPONSIBLE? — ownership & clarity
Principle: clear delineation avoids duplication and drift.
Project setup ownership:
- Project Director / PI: overall sign-off, funder liaison, escalation.
- Project Manager: day-to-day coordination, budget tracking, meeting logistics, records.
- Data Custodian: secure access, data agreements, compute environment.
- Communications Lead: templates, external comms, meeting minutes governance.
In regular meetings — "We need to…" who is "we"?
- Always specify owner when assigning actions: Owner = Name & role (e.g., "Sam — Data Custodian"). Use the phrase "Owner: [Name] — Due: YYYY-MM-DD".
Responsibilities checklist (onboarding): confirm each member can answer: "What are my deliverables? Who do I report to? Who reports to me?" Record in the shared team compact.
RECORD-KEEPING — memory, traceability & low-friction archives
Principles: memory is fallible; capture decisions and rationales.
Minimum records policy: meeting minutes (attendees, decisions, owners, due dates), action tracker entry per assigned task, follow-up emails after informal discussions ("Just to confirm we agreed..."), versioned documents in the shared archive with IndexNo and access log.
Suggested tech stack: project OneDrive/SharePoint with clear folder taxonomy: 01_ProjectDocs | 02_Data | 03_Outputs | 04_Admin | 05_Meetings. Use DOIs or persistent IDs for published datasets.
Retention & compliance: maintain a record of ethics approvals, data-use agreements, and signed compacts; store backups and note data destruction or archive dates.
EFFECTIVE USE OF COMMUNICATION PATHWAYS — choose the right channel
Principles: match channel to need (speed, traceability, formality).
- Email: formal approvals, audit trail, long-form documents.
- Video calls: ambiguity, complex negotiation, trust-building.
- Chat (Teams/Slack): short clarifications, quick coordination; summarise decisions into email or tracker.
- Task tracker (Asana/Jira/Trello): assignable actions, due dates, status.
- Shared drive: canonical documents and evidence.
Best practices to reduce noise: limit CCs to stakeholders who need to act or archive; use subject tokens (EventCode | WP | ShortIssue | Due); summarise chat decisions into the tracker within 24h.
Create leverage through pathways: only share appropriate info to avoid overload and protect reputations; use a pre-brief call for high-impact asks and follow with an email summary.
Behavioural norms: listen actively; speak up early; address communication gaps in standing agendas.
PRINCIPLES — audience, purpose, outcome, leverage
Audience: identify primary recipient (owner) explicitly; treat institutions as ORG and name individuals.
Purpose (must include): explicit ask, empathy for recipient constraints, clear motivating reason (benefit or consequence). Use the structure: Ask → Constraint → Rationale.
Outcome (must include consequences): state the result if approved, quantified consequences of action and inaction, and acceptance criteria (how success is measured).
Leverage (use sparingly): reputation, delivery mechanism, evidence, clarity. Do not exaggerate; cite verifiable references (IndexNo). Choose one dominant lever + one support lever.
AUDIENCE (practical checklist):
- Produce a stakeholder map: Name | Role | Interest | Influence | Preferred channel | Required response | SLA.
- For each primary audience, prepare: one-line message, channel, timing and success criterion.
PURPOSE (copy-ready template):
Subject: [Decision] [EventCode] — Reply by [YYYY-MM-DD].
Body: 1) Ask: "Approve X (Owner — Due)?" 2) Constraint: "I know you review budgets weekly." 3) Rationale: "Approval prevents vendor hold and a £X penalty." 4) Evidence: [IndexNo]. 5) Options: A/B + recommended.
OUTCOME (structure):
- Outcome line: "If approved, [action] will [result] by [date]."
- Action consequence: positive impact quantified.
- Inaction consequence: negative impact quantified + escalation path.
- Acceptance criteria: measurable metric by date.
LEVERAGE (practical):
- Use pre-brief calls for high-stakes approvals.
- Attach 1-page evidence brief and IndexNo.
- Provide a recommended default and one simple alternative.
- If using reputation, attach the verifiable source (prior approval dates, IndexNo).
DEVELOPING COLLABORATIONS — equitable partnerships
Core definition (UKCDR): equal value for each partner across all research stages.
Must-do items at set-up: 1) 1-page expectation statement from each partner (contributions, constraints, benefits). 2) Benefit map: Partner | Contribution | Expected benefit | Timing | Acceptance criteria. 3) Partnership compact (one page + annexes) documenting roles, budgets, authorship rules, data rights, capacity strengthening and dispute resolution.
Acknowledge structural inequalities: run an equity assessment that lists administrative burdens, legal constraints, and funding control asymmetries; attach mitigation measures (local admin support, fund-flow arrangements).

Introduction — why this matters (short narrative)
Working in international teams is rewarding but fragile: misread signals, different expectations, and unseen rules can turn small misunderstandings into missed deadlines, damaged relationships or inequitable outcomes. This expanded guide gives facilitators and teams a practical conversation map: vivid examples teams can recognise, scripts to defuse tension, workshop exercises to surface norms, and concrete project-level fixes you can trial immediately. Use it as a working document: surface one item per meeting, test changes, and iterate.
Prompt 1 — Unspoken rules in many cultures that create tension (detailed narratives & examples)
Directness vs indirectness (narrative): In low-context cultures (e.g., many Northern European contexts, North America) people say exactly what they mean: "This needs rework by Friday." In high-context cultures (some East Asian, West African, Middle Eastern contexts) people soften or imply refusal: "We will try to see what can be done." Example tension: a UK PI interprets "we will try" as yes and proceeds; local partners interpret it as polite no — result: work fails to start and blame ensues.
Concrete mitigation example & script: Pre-agreed decode: when someone says "we will try", facilitator asks: "Do you mean 'Yes, with resources', 'Yes, but later', or 'Not feasible'?" Or use the email follow-up: "Thank you. To be clear, does 'we will try' mean you can commit X person for Y days by Z date?"
Hierarchy & deference (narrative): In cultures with strong hierarchical norms, juniors rarely contradict seniors in public. When a team mixes these expectations, senior voices from egalitarian cultures may be puzzled by silence. Example: during a design review, students or junior staff sit quiet while the senior nods; Western colleagues assume agreement, later learn the juniors had serious concerns.
Mitigation & example practice: Use round-robin check-ins explicitly for junior voices ("Let's go round and each person names one risk we missed — junior contributors first"). Offer anonymous question boxes. Create a "junior voice" slot on meeting agendas.
Saving face / avoiding public correction (narrative): Some partners avoid public correction to preserve dignity. This can mean problems are only raised privately or too late. Example: a fieldworker notices an ethical consent mismatch but won't flag it publicly; later audit finds the problem.
Mitigation & practice: Normalise private flagging channels and public gratitude for raised issues; leader script: "Thanks for flagging — that was useful. We’ll treat this as a systems fix, not an individual error."
Meeting time norms (narrative): Monochronic cultures expect rigid start/stop times; polychronic cultures treat meetings as elastic. Example: A UK team waits 10 minutes for late colleagues from a polychronic culture, then proceeds without them; later that team feels excluded.
Practical rule: State meeting start time and a 5–10 minute welcome window, and publish whether the meeting will proceed at time or wait for key people. For global teams, rotate meeting times so the same people aren't always inconvenienced.
Interruptions & turn-taking (narrative): In some contexts overlapping talk = engagement; in others it's rude and disruptive. Example: a lively engineer who interjects is seen as engaged by some and rude by others.
Mitigation: Introduce a "stack" or raised-hand tool for virtual meetings and a norm: "If you overlap, briefly note the point in chat to avoid cutting someone off."
Response expectations & inbox norms (narrative): "OK" might mean "read & action" for one person and "noted" for another; "reply all" habits vary. Example: a finance email asking for documents gets several "OK" replies but no attachments — later confusion.
Mitigation: Use action tokens in subject lines: Action: Documents needed by YYYY-MM-DD — Owner: [name]. Spell out expected response type ("Please reply 'I will send' or 'I cannot' by X").
Formality / titles (narrative): First-name culture vs title culture. Example: a junior colleague calls a senior by first name in a context where titles show respect — senior feels slighted.
Mitigation: Ask partners about name preferences in onboarding and record in directory. Use "Professor X / Dr Y unless told otherwise" as default.
Use of silence (narrative): Silence can mean agreement, reflection, or discomfort depending on culture. Example: after proposing a contentious method, silence follows; some interpret this as agreement; others are deliberating.
Mitigation: After a pause, the chair should ask a specific person to summarise their view or use a thumbs-up/neutral/down poll.
Nonverbal cues (examples): Eye contact norms vary; gestures may be offensive elsewhere. Example: a western speaker's sustained eye contact felt aggressive to a partner from a culture where modesty is signalled by averting gaze.
Mitigation: Recommend avoiding reliance on nonverbal cues in mixed groups; verbalise reactions instead.
Homework & pre-reading norms (narrative): Expectation that everyone reads long pre-reads can disadvantage those with bandwidth, language or time constraints. Example: long pre-reads posted 24 hours before a meeting are unread by many, causing unequal participation.
Mitigation: Provide layered materials (one-line summary + 2-page brief + full annex) and give at least 3–5 working days for substantive pre-reads.

Intro — why Indonesia matters for project teams
Indonesia is high-context, consensus-oriented, and relationship-driven. Communication emphasises harmony (saving face), respect for hierarchy and personal relationships (gotong royong spirit). Practical consequence: partners often prefer indirect language, rely on intermediaries, and use WhatsApp/phone for fast coordination. Core cultural traits (short orientation)
- High-context & indirectness: meaning is often implied; explicit refusal is rare.
- Hierarchy & deference: status, titles, age and institutional rank shape who speaks and how.
- Saving face & harmony: public disagreement is avoided; criticism is often private or politely phrased.
- Collectivism & gotong royong: teamwork, mutual support and consensus matter more than individual assertion.
- Flexible punctuality ("jam karet"): meeting start times can be elastic, though professional settings increasingly expect punctuality.
- Channel preferences: WhatsApp/phone for fast coordination; email for formal records; in-person or video for relationship building.

Due diligence — operational guide
Definition: Due diligence is the investigation or exercise of care a reasonable person or organisation undertakes before entering an agreement or action. It identifies costs, benefits and risks and produces evidence for a go/no-go decision.
Purpose: Reduce surprise, protect reputation, confirm feasibility, ensure legal/compliance alignment, clarify costs and responsibilities, and create an auditable record that supports timely decisions.
When to run it (rules of thumb):
- Before signing contracts, MOUs or data-sharing agreements.
- Before committing significant budget, staff time or reputational capital.
- When partners, data, procurement or legal environments are new or unfamiliar.
- At major project milestones where scope, partners or funding change.
Levels of due diligence (pick appropriate depth):
Light-check: quick verification of identity, basic compliance flags, and clear yes/no red flags. Useful for low-cost, low-risk engagements.
Standard-check: document review, basic financial and governance checks, data access scoping, and reputational scan.
Comprehensive-check: full legal review, financial statements, detailed data governance, procurement review, ethics, on-site checks and reference interviews for high-value or high-risk deals.
Core domains to investigate:
- Legal & contractual: registration, authority to sign, litigation history, required approvals.
- Financial & fiduciary: budget realism, cashflow, audited accounts, funding controls, banking arrangements.
- Operational & capacity: staffing, admin systems, IT & physical security, logistics capacity.
- Data & information governance: data ownership, access, de-identification, secure compute, retention and sharing rules.
- Ethics & safeguarding: human-subject approvals, consent standards, child protection, sign-off records.
- Compliance & regulatory: tax, import/export, sector-specific regulations, funder rules.
- Reputational & sanctions: media/press history, sanctions lists, controversies, conflicts of interest.
- Insurance & liability: professional indemnity, public liability, clinical indemnity where relevant.
Process & workflow (recommended sequence):
1) Scope the check: agree depth, domains, owner and evidence list.
2) Request materials: send standard evidence checklist & deadline.
3) Desk review: documents, public records, web/reputation search, sanctions checks.
4) Interviews & references: short calls with named contacts, institutional finance/admin.
5) Spot checks (as needed): sample data access test, environment photos, site visit.
6) Risk assessment & scoring: score domains, aggregate risk, list mitigations.
7) Decision & conditions: accept, accept with conditions (mitigations/controls), defer, or decline.
8) Record & sign-off: store evidence, decisions, and owners in the project archive.
Standard evidence & documents to request (copy-ready list):
- Certificate of incorporation / registration or institutional letter.
- Authorising signatory details & specimen signature.
- Recent audited accounts or management accounts.
- Budget & bank details for fund flow.
- Sample contracts or templates used for suppliers/subgrants.
- Data protection policy, data-sharing agreements, sample consent forms.
- Ethics approvals or evidence of process for obtaining them.
- Insurance certificates and indemnity confirmation.
- CVs for named key personnel and organogram.
- Two referees or institutional contacts for verification.
Practical questions to ask partners (copy-ready):
- Who signs legally for your institution and can confirm budget lines?
- Can you provide recent financials and a sample bank remittance?
- What approvals (ethics, institutional, government) will be needed and who holds them?
- Where will data be stored and who will have access? Describe pseudonymisation and retention.
- Have you or your staff been subject to any sanctions, investigations, or adverse media in the last five years?
- What are your procurement and anti-fraud controls?
- Who is responsible for local insurance and indemnity?
Data governance mini-checklist (must-ask items):
- Data owner and legal basis for sharing.
- Types of data (personal, sensitive, health).
- Anonymisation/pseudonymisation approach.
- Secure environment for analysis (SRE, VPN, accredited cloud).
- Access control lists and auditing.
- Data retention and deletion policy.
- Data export restrictions (cross-border rules).
- Incident response & breach notification process.
Risk scoring rubric (simple, implementable):
- Score each domain 1=low, 2=medium, 3=high risk.
- Weight domains according to project (e.g., data & legal higher for research projects with sensitive data).
- Aggregate weighted mean score and map to decisions: Accept (low), Accept-with-conditions (medium), Decline/Defer (high).
- Always attach mitigations for medium risks and owners & timelines for implementation.
Decision criteria (practical standard):
- Go: no high-risk flags; medium risks have assigned mitigations; all essential approvals in train.
- Go with conditions: manageable medium/high risks only if mitigations are implemented and tracked.
- Hold: significant unresolved issues that require external approvals or further evidence.
- Stop: critical legal, ethical, sanction, or fraud findings.

- Due Diligence Lead: coordinates requests, compiles evidence, runs checks.
- Legal Reviewer: assesses contracts, registration, power to sign, regulatory risk.
- Finance Reviewer: examines accounts, banking, fund-flow mechanics.
- Data Custodian / IT Security: evaluates data and IT safeguards.
- Safeguarding/Ethics Lead: reviews participant protections and approvals.
- Project Director / Approving Officer: makes the final go/no-go decision and signs record.
