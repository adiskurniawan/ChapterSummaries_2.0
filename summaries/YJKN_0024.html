Defensible bank charge allocation
1. Core position and audit logic
Your bank charge allocation can be defended in an audit if it is presented as a reasonable, consistent, documented proxy for how bank costs arise, rather than as a perfect transaction-by-transaction tracing method.

The key audit question is not whether the method is mathematically exact in every case. The key question is whether the method is explainable, repeatable, supported by source documents, and fair in relation to the size and nature of the cost involved.

The method does not need to be perfect. It needs to be systematic, transparent, internally controlled, and applied in the same way from month to month. That is especially important where the organization is dealing with routine recurring charges that are small individually but meaningful in aggregate.

This argument becomes stronger when the charge is modest and repetitive. If the organization is dealing with a recurring banking cost that is operationally expensive to trace one by one but economically modest in total, then a simple allocation rule is usually more defensible than a highly complex approach that consumes staff time without improving the result in any meaningful way.

The audit defense is strongest when the organization can show that management made a conscious judgment, documented the basis, and reviewed it consistently rather than applying an arbitrary split.
2. Core formula
The core formula you have been using is:
Monthly bank charges × project voucher count ÷ total voucher count.

This formula is easy to explain because it has three clear parts: the total monthly bank charge pool, the voucher count for the relevant project, and the total voucher count for all projects. The project share is calculated proportionately, so the method is easy to reproduce, easy to check, and easy to explain to reviewers.

If someone asks how a project received its share, the answer is visible directly in the calculation sheet. The method is simple enough that a finance officer, manager, or auditor can recalculate it manually without needing a complicated model or specialized system logic.

The clarity of the formula is a strength in itself. A formula that is easy to trace from source record to final charge is often more auditable than a technically sophisticated model that few people can understand or verify.

The formula also creates consistency because the same inputs produce the same results every time. That predictability is valuable in month-end close and donor reporting.
3. Why the formula can be acceptable
That formula can be acceptable when voucher count is a practical stand-in for the banking activity that created the charge, and when the organization can show that the method is applied consistently every month.

The strength of the method is not that it is exact. The strength of the method is that it is systematic, explainable, easy to reproduce, and supported by source documents. A method that is approximate but disciplined is often more defensible than one that tries to be overly precise but is not practical to maintain.

Consistency matters because an auditor is usually more comfortable with a method that is approximate but stable than with a method that changes without explanation. If the same allocation rule is used every month, the project shares remain comparable over time, and the finance team can explain them in donor reports and audit files with less risk of inconsistency.

The organization should also be able to show that the allocation basis was selected deliberately, not casually, and that it reflects the real operating pattern of the bank charges as closely as practical.

That means the decision should be documented in a policy or memo, not merely applied informally by one staff member.
4. The small fee matters
In your case, there is an important real-world detail: you are aware that the bank often charges IDR 2,500 per voucher, but not every voucher necessarily creates a fee. Some transfers, especially same-bank internal transfers such as BNI to another BNI branch, may not be charged.

That means the allocation basis should be described carefully. The policy should not claim that every voucher creates an identical bank cost if that is not always true. Instead, it should say that voucher count is used because it is the closest practical monthly driver available and because most vouchers are expected to incur the standard fee, while fee-free exceptions are expected to be immaterial or limited.

This is an important distinction because it shows the method is based on a practical assumption, not on an inaccurate blanket statement. A strong policy acknowledges the exception while still explaining why the simple method remains reasonable.

It is better to say “most vouchers are feeable” than to say “all vouchers generate the same fee,” because the first statement is honest and the second statement is too absolute.

That kind of careful wording helps preserve trust in the accounting process.
5. Why the monthly total matters
Yes, IDR 2,500 is a very small amount per voucher, and the monthly bank charges of about IDR 600,000 are still relatively modest in operational terms. That supports the use of a simple, documented allocation key. A fee pool made up of many small repeated items usually does not justify a highly complicated tracing system.

If the monthly charges are around IDR 600,000, then the cost pool is essentially a bundle of many small repeated banking events. In that environment, a count-based allocation is easier to justify than it would be for a larger, more volatile, or more irregular banking cost pool. The size is large enough to require accounting treatment, but small enough that the control process should remain efficient.

That also means the organization can reasonably prioritize practicality, timeliness, and consistency over excessive precision. The cost of achieving a more exact allocation may exceed the benefit of doing so.

In a small or medium finance function, time spent reconstructing exact causation for a modest monthly fee pool may produce little extra value. An auditor will usually understand that tradeoff if it is explained clearly and documented.

The key is to avoid ignoring the cost entirely. Small does not mean irrelevant; it means a proportionate control is acceptable.
6. Cost causation
An auditor will look for cost causation. If a project generated more bank processing, it should bear more bank cost. If a project generated fewer bank transactions or less bank effort, it should bear less.

A voucher-count method can satisfy that principle when the vouchers are broadly similar and the non-feeable cases are not numerous enough to distort the allocation. The idea is not that every voucher costs exactly the same. The idea is that the voucher count is a reasonable approximation of the cost driver in the absence of a better practical monthly measure.

That is the central logic behind the method. It uses a visible operational event, the voucher, as the basis for sharing the bank cost across projects in proportion to activity.

Cost causation is easier to defend when the chosen base matches the way the bank fees are actually generated. That is why the method should be described as a proxy for bank-processing activity, not as a perfect measurement of cost causation.

The more closely the proxy reflects actual activity, the stronger the defense.
7. Feeable and non-feeable vouchers
If the feeable and non-feeable vouchers are mixed together without any judgment, the method becomes weaker. For example, if Project A has many internal BNI-to-BNI transfers that do not attract fees, and Project B has fewer but mostly external feeable transfers, a simple total voucher-count split might overcharge Project A and undercharge Project B.

That is why the best audit position is to say that management has considered the nature of the bank transactions and has determined that the number of fee-free internal transfers is not material, or that it will review the method periodically and refine it if the mix changes.

The method should be chosen deliberately, not just used because it is convenient. A reviewer should be able to see that the organization understands the difference between feeable and non-feeable activity and has made a reasonable judgment about whether the difference matters materially to the allocation.

If the mix of free and chargeable items is stable and limited, total voucher count may still be reasonable. If the mix becomes uneven, chargeable voucher count becomes more appropriate.

That judgment should be revisited, not assumed permanently.
8. Three defensible levels of sophistication
There are three defensible levels of sophistication for the method.

First level: total voucher count. This is the simplest version. It works when most vouchers incur the normal bank fee and the few fee-free vouchers do not materially affect the overall distribution. This is the easiest version to operate and explain, but it is also the least precise.

Second level: chargeable voucher count. This is stronger. It excludes vouchers that do not incur a bank charge, such as same-bank internal transfers. It better matches the true driver of the cost and is easier to justify if the organization can identify those transactions from the bank statement or payment system.

Third level: weighted voucher count or split fee pools. This is the strongest version when the bank charge structure is mixed. For example, some transactions may be free, some may cost IDR 2,500, and some may have larger fees for interbank or international transfers. In that case, the bank cost should be split into categories and allocated using the most appropriate driver for each category.

This tiered view is helpful because it shows that the organization can start with a simple basis, but has a clear upgrade path if the bank cost structure becomes more complex.

It also helps the organization avoid overengineering at the beginning while preserving a path to stronger control later.
9. How the policy should choose among the methods
The policy should say exactly how the organization chooses among these approaches. A good policy statement would explain that bank charges are allocated using the simplest reasonable basis that reflects the actual pattern of bank fees, and that the method is reviewed periodically to ensure it still matches the way charges are generated.

This is the key governance point: the method must be chosen deliberately, not just used because it is convenient. It should show that management has thought about the fee structure, the available data, the materiality of the charges, and the burden of maintaining the method.

The policy should also say what happens when the transaction pattern changes. If fee-free transfers become common, or if the bank changes its fee schedule, the organization should move to a more precise basis or split the cost pool into separate categories.

This makes the policy adaptable rather than rigid. A rigid policy can become inaccurate over time, while a flexible policy remains aligned with reality.

The document should also identify who approves changes to the method, so the allocation basis does not drift informally.
10. What should be in the monthly working paper
For audit purposes, the working papers should show the following each month:

1. Total bank charges extracted from the bank statement or bank ledger.
2. The monthly count of vouchers by project.
3. The allocation formula used.
4. The calculation sheet showing the project-by-project split.
5. Management review and approval.
6. A note on any unusual or non-feeable transactions, especially internal transfers that did not incur bank cost.

This file gives the auditor a clear trail from the source bank charge to the final project allocation. The strongest audit evidence is not just the calculation itself. It is the combination of bank statement, allocation schedule, and documented review.

If those three pieces line up, the allocation becomes much easier to defend. The auditor should be able to trace the number from the statement into the worksheet and then into the ledger without ambiguity.

The working paper should also show the date of preparation and review, so the control is visibly part of the monthly close.

Under simple voucher count, Project A receives IDR 150,000 and Project B receives IDR 100,000. If most of those vouchers are ordinary feeable payments, the allocation is likely acceptable. The auditor will see a straightforward, consistent method with a clear formula.

This example shows why the formula is attractive. It is easy to calculate, easy to verify, and easy to explain. The math is transparent, and there is no hidden decision-making beyond the selection of the allocation basis itself.

It also shows the audit advantage of a proportionate approach: the shares move in a way that is intuitive and easy to reconcile with the relative activity volumes of the projects.

The simplicity of the calculation also reduces the risk of arithmetic error and makes review faster.

That matters in monthly close cycles, where clarity and speed are both useful.
12. Example 2
Suppose Project A has 50 vouchers, but 15 are same-bank internal transfers that do not incur any fee, while Project B has 30 vouchers, all of which are feeable. If the organization allocates only by total voucher count, Project A may appear to consume more bank cost than it really does. In this case, a chargeable voucher count is more appropriate. This example is important because it shows the limit of a simple count. When one project has many non-feeable items, the raw count overstates its share of actual bank cost. The better solution is not to keep forcing the same formula. It is to refine the formula so that only chargeable vouchers drive the allocation of the feeable portion of the cost.

This strengthens the policy because it shows management is not ignoring the transaction mix. It is actively aligning the cost driver with the charges that actually arose.

It also shows that accuracy can be improved without abandoning the overall structure of a simple monthly allocation system.

Auditors usually respond positively when a method is refined to match reality rather than defended blindly.
13. Example 3
Suppose each feeable voucher attracts IDR 2,500. If Project A has 40 feeable vouchers, Project A’s direct bank-fee share is IDR 100,000. If Project B has 20 feeable vouchers, Project B’s direct share is IDR 50,000. If there is also a fixed monthly account maintenance charge of IDR 30,000, that fixed amount can be spread using a separate basis, such as total vouchers or another approved overhead key. This is often more defensible than forcing one formula to handle every type of fee.

This example highlights an important practical point. Different bank charges have different drivers. A per-voucher charge should generally follow feeable voucher count. A fixed monthly fee does not belong exclusively to the project with the most vouchers. It belongs to the banking relationship as a whole, so it can be shared using a broad overhead key.

Separating the fee types can make the policy much easier to justify because each charge is matched to the driver that best explains it.

It also reduces the risk that a single cost driver will distort a charge that behaves differently from the rest of the cost pool.

A mixed-fee environment is exactly where a layered allocation policy is strongest.
14. Best practical policy structure
That leads to the best practical policy structure: separate the bank charges into categories if needed. A monthly bank cost pool may contain fixed account charges, per-transaction charges, foreign exchange charges, interbank fees, and recovery or refund entries. Each category should be allocated using the basis that best reflects its driver.

Fixed fees can be spread pro rata using a general overhead key. Variable transaction fees can be allocated by chargeable voucher count. FX-related charges can be assigned to the fund that caused the transaction or to a shared overhead pool if the benefit is mixed. The more closely the basis matches the real cost driver, the stronger the audit defense.

This structure is especially useful because it avoids one overly broad formula being applied to all banking charges, even when the charges behave differently.

The policy becomes more resilient because it does not assume that every bank charge is caused by the same operational event.

That distinction is often what makes a policy sound thoughtful rather than generic.
15. Honest assumptions
Where the organization does not currently count only feeable vouchers, the policy can still be defensible if it is honest about the assumption. The statement should be that most vouchers are expected to attract the standard IDR 2,500 fee, and that occasional same-bank transfers are expected to be immaterial.

That is a judgment, not a mathematical certainty. Auditors generally accept judgments if they are reasonable, applied consistently, and supported by evidence. What weakens an audit position is pretending that the method is exact when it is only approximate.

So the policy should tell the truth about what is known, what is assumed, and what is being reviewed. That kind of honesty makes the method easier to trust.

It also protects the finance team, because a policy that clearly states its assumptions is easier to defend than one that implies precision it does not actually have.

Transparency about assumptions is usually better than overstatement.

That flexibility is valuable because it demonstrates management judgment and continuing oversight, rather than a rigid rule applied without thought.

That is often exactly what an auditor wants to see.

18. Month-end control process
The month-end control process should therefore be simple and repeatable. The bank statement total should be extracted first. That total should be tied to the general ledger bank charge account. Then the allocation should be prepared using the approved formula. Then each project share should be calculated and posted. Finally, the worksheet should be reviewed and signed off.

If there are any special cases, such as a month with unusual internal transfers, an FX transaction, or a fee reversal, those should be noted in the file so the reviewer understands why the normal pattern may have shifted.

This control process shows that allocation is not done casually. It is part of a routine monthly close with a reconciliation trail and supervisory review.

It also creates a clear audit sequence: statement, reconciliation, calculation, journal entry, review, and filing.

A clean sequence matters because it keeps the method understandable even when staff change.
19. Month-end checklist
A clear month-end checklist would include:

1. Reconcile bank charges in the statement to the bank charge ledger account.
2. Extract the voucher count by project for the month.
3. Confirm whether the vouchers are mostly feeable or whether fee-free transfers were material.
4. Apply the approved allocation formula.
5. Review the allocation for reasonableness against the transaction mix.
6. Approve and post the journal.
7. File the support with the monthly finance pack.

This process is important because a well-documented allocation is much easier to defend than a formula with no evidence trail. If someone later asks why Project A absorbed more bank charges than Project B, the organization should be able to show both the math and the rationale behind the math.

The checklist also helps make the process repeatable across months and across different staff members.

It reduces dependence on memory and informal practice, which are weak points in audit files.

Written routine is stronger than verbal habit.
20. Practical refinements
There are also some practical refinements worth including in the rewrite.

One refinement is to define what counts as a voucher. If a bank transaction batch contains multiple vouchers or one voucher creates multiple bank postings, the policy should state how it is counted. Without a definition, the method can become inconsistent between staff members.

Another refinement is to define whether voided vouchers, reversals, or cancelled transactions are counted. The policy should say whether only completed vouchers are included, because incomplete or reversed items could otherwise distort the monthly distribution.

Another refinement is to define how the organization treats bank fee refunds, charge reversals, or corrections. If the bank reverses a fee in a later month, the organization should either reverse the original charge or include the refund in the monthly bank charge pool and document the treatment consistently.

Another refinement is to distinguish between bank fees that arise from transaction processing and general banking overhead. Not all bank charges behave the same way. Some are directly tied to the number of transactions. Some are tied to balance maintenance. Some are one-off corrections. A single allocation key may be acceptable for a small organization, but a larger or more complex organization will usually defend the method better if it separates these components.

These refinements do not make the policy harder to defend. They usually make it easier.
21. Example of mixed bank charges
For example, a monthly bank statement might contain:

• a fixed account maintenance fee,
• IDR 2,500 per transfer fee,
• an FX conversion spread,
• a correspondent bank charge on an international payment,
• and a small reversal from a prior month.

These items do not all have the same cost driver. The fixed fee is not caused by one project alone. The per-transfer fee is tied to the number of chargeable payments. The FX charge follows the currency transaction. The correspondent fee belongs to the fund or project that initiated the foreign payment or benefited from it. The reversal should generally be netted against the related fee or explained separately. A more refined policy will sound much stronger in audit than one blanket rule for everything.

If the organization is small and the charges are mostly standard voucher fees, a monthly voucher-count allocation can remain reasonable. Audit defensibility is always a question of materiality, complexity, and consistency. If the amounts are small and the bank fee pattern is simple, a simple method is usually fine. If the amounts are large or the fee pattern is mixed, the allocation basis should become more detailed.

The policy should match the shape of the actual bank fee pattern, not an imagined one.
22. Underlying principle
It is also worth stating the underlying audit principle in plain language: the cost should follow the benefit or the activity that caused the cost. When direct tracing is not feasible, management may use a reasonable surrogate. Voucher count is one such surrogate. Chargeable voucher count is a stronger surrogate. Weighted voucher count is stronger still.

The more directly the surrogate matches the actual cost behavior, the more likely it is to withstand review. That hierarchy is useful because it gives the organization a clear upgrade path. If the current method is rough but workable, it can be improved later. If the bank charge pattern becomes more complex, the organization does not have to abandon allocation entirely. It just needs to choose a better surrogate.

This also helps management explain why the method was selected in the first place: it is the most practical choice available today, not a perfect theory.

The principle should remain stable even if the mechanics evolve. The selected basis should follow actual benefit as closely as practical, given the available records.

That is the heart of a defensible allocation policy.

27. Staff time and proportionality
The fact that staff time is limited also supports this approach. A bank-charge allocation method does not need to be perfect if making it perfect would consume disproportionate staff time, delay month-end close, and create deadline risk.

In a real NGO finance environment, the control objective is not theoretical perfection. The control objective is a method that is timely, repeatable, reviewable, and proportionate to the risk.

If staff must spend excessive time verifying every voucher individually, the organization may miss reporting deadlines, delay donor submissions, or introduce other operational problems that are worse than the minor inaccuracy created by a reasonable proxy.

That point should be stated carefully in the policy or working paper: the organization has chosen a method that balances accuracy with operational practicality. The allocation is reviewed enough to be credible, but not so heavily reviewed that the process becomes impractical. This is a legitimate management judgment.

Proportionality is especially important when the cost pool is small and the operational burden of exact tracing would be high.
28. Practical control statement

That wording makes clear that the organization is not ignoring control. It is using a proportional control designed to fit its size and resource constraints. It also shows that the control environment is active, not absent.

Sampling a few vouchers can be enough to validate that the transaction mix still matches the assumed pattern. This is especially sensible where the bank charge pool is relatively small and recurring.

The control works because it checks the basis, not just the arithmetic.

That is often the right balance for small recurring charges.
