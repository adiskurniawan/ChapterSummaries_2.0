Leadership — enabling international development research
Purpose (one-line): Create the environment, processes and relationships that let equitable, challenge-led international development research thrive at the University of Glasgow and with partners.
Overall goals:
• Improve institutional processes & policies that block international work.
• Build capacity across UofG and partner institutions for equitable research.
• Influence sector norms and funder practice on international development research.
Create an environment where teams can thrive — concrete actions
1) Build an easily discoverable project onboarding pack: templates (MOU, DSA, MTA), budget lines for admin & trust, sample SOW, risk checklist.
2) Standardise minimal resourcing for partners: connectivity stipend, admin honoraria, local procurement lines.
3) Define and publish meeting norms and communication charter for international projects (pre-reads, layered summaries, response SLAs, language accommodations).
4) Mandate a 30–60 minute cultural & governance onboarding for each new partnership.
5) Create a protected rapid-response escalation path for ethics/data/governance hotspots.
Improve processes & policies within UofG (practical levers)
• Push for a dedicated “International Research Fast-Track” checklist in Research Office approvals (ethical, contract, signatory, fund flow) to reduce admin friction.
• Propose a standard budget template with compulsory lines: local admin, connectivity, capacity-strengthening, translation.
• Recommend a “one-page compact” for subawards that flows down key clauses (DSA, IP, audit).
• Lobby for delegated signatory thresholds for low-risk operational items to reduce delay (with audit trail).
• Share a short evidence pack with Provost/Grants Office showing time/cost savings from streamlined processes.
Develop capacity — UofG and partners (practical programmes)
1) Short modular training: ethics & local approvals, secure research environments (SRE), data protection, MTAs/DSAs, grant financial management.
2) Institutional exchange / secondment scheme: 2–8 week funded placements (rotate admin + early career researchers).
3) Small pump-priming grants (seed corn) for joint pilot work and trust building.
4) Joint supervision & mentorship plan: pair senior UofG academics with partner mid-career researchers, with explicit outputs and time allocation.
5) Repository of “how-we-did-it” case studies and checklists for partners to reuse.
Influence the sector (practical routes)
• Publish short policy briefs with evidence from pilot reforms at UofG.
• Present at funder forums and learned societies, offering “UofG approach” as a reproducible model.
• Offer template compact & guidance openly (e.g., GitHub/Zenodo) and invite cross-institutional pilots.
• Convene short coalitions of peer institutions to standardise MTA/DSA language and trust funds.
Lead without line management (how to influence when you manage no staff)
• Build coalitions of the willing: name 6–8 allies across research, legal, finance, and a friendly head in College.
• Use small wins to build credibility: solve one recurring admin pain and publicise the result.
• Offer to own operational tasks (draft templates, run pilots) so colleagues can adopt without extra work.
• Use evidence & data (time-to-contract, partner complaints) to argue for change.
• Secure informal authority: be the “go-to” advisor (office hours, short clinics).
Positioning when you are outside official committees
- Document influence channels: who informs whom, which committees accept input, and which officers can raise items for committee agendas.
- Ask a committee member to sponsor or table your one-page proposals.
- Use formal channels sparingly but strategically (evidence memo + 2 minute speaking slot).
Repository of knowledge — how to build & share what “sits nowhere else”
1) Create a single, searchable project drive (OneDrive/SharePoint) with: templates, case studies, contact maps, donor rules matrix, DSA/MTA examples.
2) Assign stewardship: named owner who refreshes, curates and responses to queries.
3) Offer regular “show & tell” slots to the Research Office and College to surface repository items.
4) Publish non-sensitive templates publicly (GitHub/Zenodo) to increase credibility and sector influence.
Personal leadership behaviours you’ve modelled (and to codify)
• Hustle & network: “I know people who know people.”
• Radical transparency: admit errors, accept input regardless of seniority.
• Pragmatic delivery: focus on outputs, not prestige.
• Build coalitions: recruit volunteers for pilots and advocacy.
Operationalise these behaviours: regular open-door clinic, monthly coalition email, public after-action notes.
Know yourself — practical checklist for leader self-management
- Build a knowledge base: a short personal playbook of past interventions and outcomes.
- Be pragmatic: prioritise 2–3 systemic changes with the highest ROI.
- Pick battles: use a win-rate rule (win 2/3 of attempts) — drop or pause after 3 failed attempts.
- Bring evidence: document time/cost/partner feedback before and after pilots.
- Accept outcomes: remain evidence-based even when you lose an argument.
Identify key drivers of change in your institution (mapping exercise)
1) Map stakeholders: Research Office leads, College Deans, Finance, Legal, HR, TTO, International Office, Library/Data services.
2) Identify aligning influencers (champions) and blockers (gatekeepers).
3) For each champion, define one short info/ask you can provide that helps them upward-advocate (e.g., succinct benefit statement for the Provost).
4) Tactics: 1:1 briefings, two-page evidence packs, invite them to pilot reviews.
Operationalising influence — pragmatic projects to start now
- Project A: “Onboarding Pack” pilot — build templates + 3 partner trials.
- Project B: “Trust Fund Line” pilot — negotiate a small discretionary pot (2–5% of project budgets) to pay connectivity/admin.
- Project C: “Rapid DSA checklist” — a 5-item pre-approval checklist legal will accept for low-risk data shares.
Document & evidence — what to collect and how to use it
• Each engagement: brief case note (problem, action, result, time saved).
• Build a sector-norms spreadsheet: how other institutions handle DSAs, MTAs, subaward flows.
• Run a small audit of 10 recent international projects to quantify delays, admin costs and common blockers.
• Use these metrics in civilised “ask memos” to institutional decision-makers.
Templates & ready-to-use language (copy-ready)
One-line ask (email subject): Proposal: Pilot Onboarding Pack — support requested
One-line ask (email body): Ask: Approve pilot of standard onboarding pack for 3 partners (Budget: £X). Benefit: reduce contract time by estimated Y weeks. Owner: [name].
Short advocacy memo (one page): Problem → Evidence (3 bullets) → Proposed change → Resource ask → Expected benefit & KPI.
Metrics & KPIs to track impact
- Median time from LOI to signed contract (days).
- % projects with partner admin/honoria budget lines.
- Number of partners with completed onboarding/capacity modules.
- # of policy changes adopted by central offices as a result of pilot.
- Partner satisfaction score (quarterly pulse).
How to measure and present wins to leadership
- Use before/after snapshots (e.g., contract time reduced from 120→45 days).
- Present concrete cost savings or increased pipeline capacity (e.g., +N proposals completed).
- Tell short human stories: one partner quote + KPI. Keep slides to 3 images: problem, intervention, result.
Risk management — anticipate institutional pushback
- Likely objections: risk/compliance concerns, budget limits, precedence fears.
- Counter: pre-built mitigations (SRE, insurance, staged pilot budgets), and evidence from comparable institutions.
- If blocked, propose time-limited pilot with rigorous reporting and sunset clause.
Practical governance & escalation recipes
- For policy changes: route → supporting evidence memo → sponsor in College → Governance Committee → Provost.
- For operational pilots: PM submits short pilot plan to Research Office; steering group meets monthly.
- For urgent ethical/data problems: emergency escalation protocol with named contacts and 48h documented response.
Building capacity of partners — minimal package to offer
- Onboarding pack + 1-day virtual induction.
- Mini-grant for admin and connectivity (fixed sum).
- Short training voucher (data management / ethics).
- Co-authorship and capacity building obligations written into SOW.
Coalitions & sector engagement (practical actions)
- Host a half-day convening with 6 peer institutions to compare DSA/MTA templates and propose harmonised language.
- Publish a 2-page “Glasgow approach” and invite funder comment.
- Offer one or two pro-bono clinics to other universities to build credibility and relationships.
Action checklist — what you can do in the next 30 days
1) Draft the Onboarding Pack (SOW template, DSA checklist, budget template).
2) Run 3 short 30-min briefings with likely champion stakeholders.
3) Identify 2 pilot partners and propose a seed budget.
4) Create a short evidence memo (2 pages) summarising 3 operational bottlenecks and proposed fixes.
5) Publish a short entry in the internal newsletter about the initiative and invite volunteers.
Final operating principle (one-liner):
Use pragmatic pilots, visible small wins and well-documented evidence to convert informal influence into lasting institutional change.

Group discussion — "Key drivers of change" & next-step commitments
Purpose: Rapidly surface who/what drives institutional change, map practical connections, and produce individual, concrete next steps participants can take immediately. Use outputs to feed a short evidence memo for institutional champions.
Session length & format: 60 minutes (can compress to 40). Format: plenary opening (8 min) → small-group breakout (20 min) → plenary share (20 min) → individual commitments & close (12 min). Materials: sticky notes or virtual board, timer, shared note doc, action tracker template.
Room setup (physical or virtual):
- Tables of 4–6 people or 3 breakout rooms.
- Visible flipchart / shared board labelled: Stakeholders, Levers, Evidence, Small Steps.
- Action tracker template open for live editing (Action | Owner | Due | Support needed).
Roles: Facilitator (keeps time), Scribe (captures group output), Reporter (2-min group summary), Chair (plenary moves). Rotate roles across groups if time.
Opening script (90s): "We’re mapping the drivers of change in our institution and identifying small concrete actions we can take this week/month to move them. Work fast, be specific, and commit to one small action each."
Discussion Q1 — What are the key drivers of change in your institution? (group task)
Prompt: List stakeholders, processes, resources or incentives that move decisions (e.g., Research Office, Provost, funder conditions, standing committees, metrics, pilot evidence, audit findings, alumni donors, press coverage).
Activity (10 min): 1) 3 minutes silent brainstorm — one sticky/note per idea. 2) 5 minutes cluster similar items into 3 columns: People (names/roles), Mechanisms (committees/policies), Evidence (what persuades them). 3) 2 minutes choose top 3 drivers and rank by influence (High/Medium/Low).
Facilitator prompts for Q1:
- "Name a committee or person who can change a process."
- "What evidence moves them — cost-savings, risk reduction, reputational benefit?"
- "Who has the authority to approve small pilots?"
Discussion Q2 — How do you connect to those drivers of change? (group task)
Prompt: For each top driver, list existing lines to influence them (formal: committee seat, proposal; informal: 1:1, alumni contact, email summaries).
Activity (10 min): 1) Map present connections (who do you know?) 2) Identify one immediate contact or mechanism you can use (e.g., sponsor to table item, 1-page evidence memo, invite to pilot review). 3) Note practical constraints (time, approvals).
Facilitator prompts for Q2:
- "Who in your network can take this to a decision-maker?"
- "What existing meeting or committee could host a 5-minute pilot update?"
- "Who in central services has previously approved similar pilots?"
Plenary sharing (20 min):
- Each group: 2-minute report (top 3 drivers + 1 connection).
- Scribe collects common drivers and repeated contact names.
- Facilitator maps 1–2 low-effort, high-impact channels on flipchart (e.g., Dean’s monthly meeting, Research Office weekly slot).
Individual reflection (8–10 min):
Task: Each person writes 2 items: (A) One small concrete step they will take in the next 7 days; (B) One small step within 30 days that needs a sponsor. Use sticky notes or the shared doc. Examples to choose or adapt below.
Examples of small concrete steps (copyable ideas):
- Draft a one-page "ask memo" (Problem → Evidence → Proposed pilot) and email it to a named champion.
- Offer to draft an onboarding pack template and circulate for comment.
- Propose a 30-minute agenda slot at next departmental meeting to present the pilot evidence.
- Create a 2-slide before/after snapshot showing time-to-contract improvements and share with Research Office.
- Open a DSA/MTA template and annotate where partners typically stall; propose amendment.
- Pilot a £1,000 local admin / connectivity mini-grant for one partner and document results.
- Offer a 1:1 clinic for partner admin to complete onboarding forms.
- Volunteer as repository steward for templates and publish one item.
Sharing individual ideas (10 min):
- Round-robin: each participant states one 7-day step and who they’ll inform. Optionally place second-step (30-day) on the “sponsor needed” board.
- Group uses dot-vote (3 votes each) to select 2–3 actions that the group will pilot collectively.
Template: One-page ask (easy copy)
- Title: Problem → Proposed pilot → Ask (resource/time) → Benefit (measurable) → Owner & Due.
- Keep to one page or one slide. Use numbers (days saved, £, partners helped).
Capture & follow-up (operational):
- Enter chosen pilots into Action Tracker with Owner, Due (date), Support needed and Success metric (KPI).
- Assign a short check-in (15 min) 2 weeks after meeting and a 30-min review at 8 weeks.
- Facilitator sends meeting minutes + actions within 48 hours.
Simple measurement & evidence collection:
- For each pilot capture: Start date | Time spent | Cost | Outcome metric (e.g., days-to-contract) | Partner quote.
- Use evidence for a 2-page report to institutional champion.
Risk & obstacle planning (brief):
- Ask each participant to list one likely blocker (policy, budget, ownership) and one mitigation (e.g., pilot with small budget; use existing template).
- Record blockers centrally and escalate only the persistent ones with quantified impact.
If you’re uncomfortable sharing an idea aloud:
- Put it on a private sticky or DM facilitator; facilitator will relay anonymously in the plenary.
- Or commit to the 7-day action privately and ask for email accountability.
Closing script (30s): "Pick one 7-day action, put it in the tracker, and email one named person to confirm. We’ll capture two pilot proposals and present them to the Research Office."
Post-meeting deliverables (what facilitator sends):
- 48h: meeting minutes with Action Tracker link and owners.
- 14 days: one-page progress note on each pilot.
- 6–8 weeks: short evidence memo for institutional sponsor.
Facilitator checklist before running session:
- Prepare action tracker & one-page ask template.
- Invite named champion(s) as listeners or guest reviewers.
- Arrange recording of commitments and a follow-up slot.
- Print/prepare sticky notes (physical) or set up Miro/Jamboard (virtual).
Optional: sample prompts to seed answers in groups
- "Name one person in Finance who could approve a small admin line."
- "Which committee meets monthly that could accept a 5-min pilot update?"
- "What is the smallest budget line that would remove a major blocker for partners?"
End-state (what success looks like):
- Each participant leaves with a committed 7-day action.
- Group selects 1–3 pilots with named owners and measurable KPIs.
- Facilitator has a short evidence pack to brief an institutional champion.

Document & evidence — practical playbook (what to collect, who to ask, how to use it)
Purpose: Build a compact, auditable evidence base that shows sector norms, internal process variation, and measurable gains from small operational changes. Use this evidence to persuade sponsors, drive pilots and escalate required policy changes.
What to document every time you engage with another institution:
- Template names used (DSA, MOU, MTA, financial annex) and any bespoke clauses.
- Typical turnaround times (DSA sign-off days, contract execution days).
- Standard budget lines for partners (admin, connectivity, honoraria) and common cost levels.
- Escalation / delegated signatory thresholds and who signs.
- Any substitute fiduciary arrangements (institutional umbrella or host).
- Short note on lessons/oddities (what caused delay and how fixed).
Questions to ask peer institutions (copy-ready):
1) "Do you include a partner admin / connectivity line as standard? If yes, amount and justification?"
2) "What is your average turnaround for DSA signature and who signs it?"
3) "How do you route small rapid payments to partners (process & controls)?"
4) "Do you use a standard MTA/DSA template we can review?"
5) "Any common clauses you add to protect data and speed access?"
How to map how different departments work inside your institution (steps):
1) Request 15–30 min micro-interviews with: Grants, Legal, Finance (subaward team), Research Office, IT/Data Services, HR (visas), TTO.
2) Use a short questionnaire: inputs required, average times, common reasons for delay, one practical fix they recommend.
3) Consolidate answers into a 1-page process map: owner | step | average days | blocking reasons.
Minimal evidence pack to collect for pilots (template fields):
- Project name / partner / country.
- LOI date → Contract signed (days).
- DSA signed date (days).
- Invoice receipt → payment (days).

Payroll processing — operational guide for international projects (practical, for Accounting Manager)
Purpose (one line): Ensure people are paid correctly, on time, and compliantly while protecting the institution from tax, legal and reputational risk.
Scope: staff (employees), secondees, local hires, consultants, fieldworkers, honoraria, stipends, and short-term casual payments across jurisdictions. Applies to partner-hosted staff where University funds or project budgets are used.
Key risks to manage: tax & social-security exposure, misclassification (employee vs contractor), withholding obligations, incorrect currency/exchange handling, bank/payment fraud, data-protection breaches, donor restrictions, lack of local legal compliance, benefits entitlements, payroll record gaps.
High-level decision: payroll vs contractor vs honorarium
- Payroll (employee or secondment): required where control, regular hours and employment relationship exist. Pros: compliant for employment rights; cons: employer taxes, admin complexity.
- Contractor / consultancy: use for independent consultants with clear deliverables and invoice-based payment. Pros: simpler for short tasks; cons: risk of reclassification if control is high.
- Honoraria / stipend: small one-off payments for lectures/participation. Use only where legally permitted and with tax advice—still may trigger withholding.
Operational principle: choose form that matches legal reality, not convenience. Seek local payroll/legal confirmation if any doubt.
Practical onboarding (documents to collect before any payment):
- ID (passport/ID card), proof of bank details (bank statement), signed contract or consultancy agreement, authorised signatory for partner entity, tax residency certificate if available, payroll forms (local), timesheets or deliverable confirmation, onboarding checklist signed.
- For employees: employment contract, tax forms, local social-security enrolment docs.
- For students/academics: check institutional rules for payroll vs honorarium and thesis/publication implications.
Payroll run checklist (each pay period):
1) Confirm approved timesheets/delivery evidence.
2) Verify contract/payment type and tax treatment.
3) Calculate gross pay, statutory deductions, employer contributions, withholding tax.
4) Apply FX using approved rate and record exchange gain/loss method.
5) Prepare payroll file for payment run (file and review by Finance Lead).
6) Obtain required approvals (PI or budget owner, Finance Reviewer).
7) Execute payment (bank transfer, payroll provider) and generate payslips.
8) Reconcile payroll bank transaction to GL and update project ledger.
9) Archive payroll packet (payslip, timesheet, approval) in secure folder.
Tax & legal controls (must-check items):
- Confirm local employer tax registration requirement (payroll taxes, social security).
- Check withholding obligations for non-resident consultants (tax treaties).
- VAT considerations (not usually payroll but check fringe benefits or reimbursements).
- Permanent establishment risk if using local staff long-term—review with Legal.
- Ensure employment terms meet local minimums (hours, leave) to avoid claims.
Payment mechanics & currency:
- Prefer local currency payments to local hires to avoid bank fees and exchange timing issues.
- For cross-border payroll, use approved payroll provider or institutional finance channels.
- Document FX rate policy (source, timing) and GL posting rules for gain/loss.
- Use secure bank transfer with two-person authorisation for single high-value payments.
Data & privacy:
- Limit payroll data access (HR/Payroll/Finance only).
- Store payslips and payroll packets in secure drive with access logs.
- Include data-sharing clauses in partner agreements for payroll data transfers.
Use of local payroll providers / PEOs:
- For small numbers or complex jurisdictions, engage vetted local payroll provider or PEO.
- Requirements when using provider: contract, SLA, deliverables (payslips, filings), audit rights, insurance, confidentiality, notification of regulatory changes, and sample reports.
Accounting treatment & GL mapping (practical):
- Salary expense: Payroll Expense - Project (GL); employer taxes: Employer NIC / Social Security; reimbursements: Project Reimbursements; accruals: Accrued Payroll Liability.
- Always tag by Project Code / WP to allow funder reporting. Maintain a payroll sub-ledger for audit.
Contract clauses & controls to include in collaboration agreements:
- Who is employer of record and responsible for payroll taxes.
- Payment method, currency, invoice vs payroll route.
- Required documentation and retention period.
- Audit rights over payroll and personnel costs.
- Indemnity for misclassification and tax claims.
Small-project pragmatic rules (to reduce friction):
- Standard budget line for partner payroll/admin (template amounts by role).
- Pre-approved honoraria caps (e.g., £X per talk) with tax treatment note.
- Fast-track checklist for low-value payments (documented evidence + single reviewer).
Common problem scenarios & fixes:
- Late invoices/claims → require cut-off schedule and monthly clinics to collect.
- Partner lacks payroll system → use local payroll provider or route payments via institutional admin with signed agreement.
- Reclassification risk → perform simple IR35-like checklist: control, substitution, mutuality of obligation; if employer-like, use payroll.
Governance & approvals:
- For payroll runs, require: WP Lead sign-off (work done), Finance Reviewer approval, payroll officer execution.
- Changes to named personnel or rates require PI + HR/Finance approval and revised contract.
- Maintain Delegation Register for payroll approval limits.
Record retention & audit:
- Keep payroll packets (contract, timesheet, payslip, approval) for funder/audit-specified period (commonly 6–7 years).
- Log payments in Action Tracker with IndexNo and attach evidence.
KPIs & monitoring:
- % payroll runs with complete documentation.
- Median time invoice → payment for payroll-related invoices.
- Number of payroll-related audit findings.
- % partner payments routed through correct mechanism (payroll vs invoice).
