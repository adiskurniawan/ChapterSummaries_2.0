OUCRU Indonesia – End-of-Service Risk and Mitigation Summary
Short summary (thesis)
OUCRU Indonesia’s current practice — hiring staff on one-year fixed-term contracts through Alert Asia (AA), paying end-of-service compensation at contract end and recognising the expense on payment rather than accruing — reduces short-term cash outflows but creates legal, accounting, audit, and grant-compliance risks. Indonesian labour law and accounting standards support employer obligations for termination payments and treat some termination benefits as obligations that may require recognition earlier than cash payment. The arrangement can be defensible if carefully designed, documented, and governed. But it must be stress-tested against three vectors: (A) employment law (conversion/continuous employment and third-party employer risk), (B) accounting and audit standards (timing and disclosure), and (C) grant/donor compliance (allowability, budget control, and auditability). Below are the key analyses and mitigations.

A. Legal risk: employment status, fixed-term contracts and third-party arrangements
• Indonesian law requires termination compensation (uang pesangon, uang penghargaan masa kerja, uang penggantian hak) when employment ends under certain conditions. These rights exist for both indefinite and specified-term workers.
• PKWT (fixed-term) contracts are lawful for specific tasks but subject to strict formal rules. Multiple renewals or continuous renewals may cause reclassification to PKWTT (permanent), triggering back pay and protections.
• If OUCRU effectively controls work and supervision, authorities may treat OUCRU and AA as joint employers. Substantive control outweighs contractual form.
• The Job Creation Law (UU 11/2020) and Government Regulation No. 35/2021 revised benefit calculation rules. Outdated methods risk non-compliance.
Legal takeaway: the one-year PKWT through AA reduces exposure only if (a) the PKWT is objectively justified and not renewed to create permanence, (b) OUCRU’s control remains limited, and (c) AA acts as a genuine independent employer with HR capacity and liability.

B. Accounting and audit risk: timing of recognition and disclosure
• Indonesian PSAK mirrors IAS 19. Termination benefits are recognised when a demonstrable commitment or constructive obligation exists.
• Recognising payments only upon cash settlement is acceptable only when no present obligation exists before contract end. If benefits are routinely paid at each renewal, auditors may require accrual.
• Donor accounting requires costs to be recognised when incurred. Cash-only recognition can create timing mismatches and questioned costs.
Accounting takeaway: the practice is defensible only with written policy, legal and audit support confirming no present obligation. Otherwise auditors may demand accrual or disclosure.

C. Grant, compliance and practical controls
• Donor rules require costs to be reasonable, allocable, and consistent. Large termination payments at project end may be disallowed if unbudgeted.
• Maintain written PKWTs, evidence of AA’s independent HR role, OUCRU-AA agreements defining liability, and a register of renewals and justifications.
• Limit renewals; where continuity is essential, consider permanent employment with full statutory cost budgeting. Ensure AA has financial capacity and insurance for liabilities.

D. Specific recommended actions (high leverage)
1. Obtain legal opinion on structure, control, and exposure under GR 35/2021.
2. Obtain auditor or accounting-advisor opinion on PSAK/IAS 19 recognition requirements.
3. Strengthen PKWT drafting and ensure AA exercises HR control and maintains indemnity coverage.
4. Document accounting policy and, if using cash basis, include clear disclosure and contingent-liability register.
5. Pre-audit grant budgets, forecast termination costs, and document inter-project transfers for auditability.

E. Practical numeric checks
• Produce a renewal history table for each staff member (start date, end date, renewals, role changes).
• Compute statutory termination entitlement under current regulation (pesangon + penghargaan + penggantian hak) to estimate worst-case liability.
• Reconcile payroll, tax, and BPJS filings showing AA as employer. Keep proof of payments and filings.

Conclusion and decision points
Continue the current approach only if: (1) AA is a genuine independent employer; (2) PKWT use is legally justified; (3) legal and audit opinions support cash recognition; and (4) indemnities and reserves are robust.
If any condition fails, move to accrual recognition, strengthen indemnities, or convert to permanent employment with full statutory cost planning.
Immediate next steps: commission legal and accounting opinions and prepare the renewal/liability table for board review.
