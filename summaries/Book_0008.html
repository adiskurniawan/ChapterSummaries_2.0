WHAT GRIT IS AND WHY IT MATTERS
Grit is defined as the combination of sustained passion and long-term perseverance that drives people to pursue challenging goals over years despite failure, boredom, and adversity; it explains why talent alone does not guarantee success.
- Grit unites passion with perseverance.
- Talent and grit operate as independent factors.
- Long-term achievement depends heavily on grit.
SHOWING UP
Admission to West Point represents extraordinary prior accomplishment, yet merely arriving does not ensure persistence; many cadets who meet the highest academic, athletic, and leadership standards still leave early when confronted with sustained, unfamiliar demands.
- >14,000 applicants begin the process.
- ~4,000 receive nominations.
- ~2,500 meet standards; ~1,200 enroll.
- One in five cadets drop out before graduation.
A TYPICAL DAY AT BEAST BARRACKS
Beast Barracks compresses physical, mental, and military stress into a rigid seven-week schedule beginning at 5:00 a.m. and ending at 10:00 p.m., leaving no weekends, minimal rest, and almost no outside contact.
- Daily routine runs continuously from early morning to taps.
- Physical training, classes, and formations dominate the day.
- Purpose is rapid transition from civilian to Soldier.
WHO MAKES IT THROUGH BEAST
High early attrition during Beast demonstrates that conventional predictors of success fail under extreme conditions; cadets who leave are often fully capable, but unable or unwilling to persist through sustained hardship.
- Many dropouts occur during the first summer.
- Early psychological tests failed to predict attrition.
- Persistence separates stayers from leavers.
EARLY ATTRITION RESEARCH AT WEST POINT
Decades of research attempted to identify who would stay at West Point, including projective psychological tests, yet these methods produced no reliable predictions about real-world persistence during training.
- Projective storytelling tests showed no predictive value.
- Narratives revealed imagination, not endurance.
- Attrition remained unexplained for decades.
WHOLE CANDIDATE SCORE
The Whole Candidate Score combines SAT or ACT scores, adjusted class rank, leadership evaluations, and physical fitness to estimate overall talent, but it does not predict who will persist through Beast despite its value for predicting later performance.
- Central factor in admissions decisions.
- Predicts grades and fitness among stayers.
- Fails to predict early persistence.
TALENT VS ENDURANCE
Cadets with the highest Whole Candidate Scores are just as likely to quit Beast as those with the lowest, showing that talent does not protect against discouragement, fatigue, or loss of motivation under extreme stress.
- Talent does not ensure endurance.
- Ability and persistence are distinct.
- Endurance determines survival of training.
ORIGINS OF THE GRIT SCALE
The Grit Scale was constructed directly from language used by high achievers and observations of persistence, dividing grit into perseverance of effort and consistency of long-term interests to capture sustained goal commitment.
- Half the items assess perseverance.
- Half assess stability of passion.
- Items reflect everyday behavior and mindset.
GRIT AT WEST POINT
In July 2004, 1,218 cadets completed the Grit Scale on the second day of Beast, and grit strongly predicted who completed training while showing no relationship to Whole Candidate Scores.
- 1,218 cadets tested.
- 71 dropped out that summer.
- Grit predicted completion reliably.
REPLICATION OF FINDINGS
The following year, the same study was repeated at West Point, and once again grit predicted who stayed while Whole Candidate Scores showed no meaningful difference between stayers and leavers.
- 62 cadets dropped out the second year.
- Results replicated across cohorts.
- Findings demonstrated consistency.
GRIT IN SALES
In a vacation time-share sales company with high rejection and turnover, grit predicted which employees stayed on the job over six months, outperforming traits such as extroversion, emotional stability, and conscientiousness.
- 55 percent of salespeople left within six months.
- Grit best predicted retention.
- Rejection tolerance outweighed personality traits.
GRIT IN EDUCATION
Among Chicago public school students, grit predicted on-time graduation more effectively than conscientiousness, motivation, or perceived school safety, even though only 12 percent failed to graduate overall.
- Thousands of juniors studied.
- 12 percent did not graduate on time.
- Grit distinguished graduates from non-graduates.
FORMAL EDUCATION ATTAINMENT
Across adult samples, individuals with advanced degrees such as MBA, PhD, MD, or JD were grittier than those with four-year degrees, who were grittier than those with incomplete college education.
- Community college graduates scored especially high.
- Dropout rates can reach 80 percent.
- Completion reflects exceptional persistence.
SPECIAL FORCES SELECTION COURSE
The Special Forces Selection Course imposes extreme physical and navigational demands beyond prior training stages, and in the studied sample 42 percent voluntarily withdrew before completion.
- Long marches with heavy loads.
- Night navigation and obstacle courses.
- Grit distinguished completers from withdrawals.
CONTEXTUAL PREDICTORS VS GRIT
Experience, supportive teachers, and baseline physical fitness improve outcomes in specific domains, but when individuals are matched on these factors, grit continues to predict success across military, educational, and business settings.
- Contextual advantages matter.
- Grit adds predictive power beyond them.
- Persistence generalizes across domains.

GRIT AND PRACTICE BEHAVIOR
Grittier spellers accumulated advantage through deliberate practice and repeated competition, demonstrating how sustained motivation converts into measurable skill gains over time.
- Practice hours mediated success.
- Competition frequency increased mastery.
- Effort compounded over years.
GRIT AND VERBAL ABILITY
Verbal intelligence predicted spelling performance, but verbal IQ showed no relationship to grit, reinforcing the distinction between ability and sustained commitment.
- High verbal IQ did not imply higher grit.
- Talent did not increase practice time.
- Persistence remained independent.
IVY LEAGUE EVIDENCE
In Ivy League undergraduates, SAT scores and grit were inversely correlated, suggesting that exceptional academic talent does not automatically translate into perseverance under long-term challenge.
- Higher SAT associated with slightly lower grit.
- Talent does not ensure stamina.
- Persistence remains critical.
PASSION WITH DIRECTION
Highly successful individuals combine resilience with a deep understanding of what they want, allowing them to endure difficulty because effort is guided by long-term purpose rather than short-term reward.
- Passion provides direction.
- Perseverance supplies stamina.
- Together they define grit.
WHY GRIT MATTERS EVERYWHERE
Across military training, education, sales, and elite competition, grit consistently predicts who stays committed when challenges intensify, explaining outcomes that talent and experience alone cannot.
- High-dropout environments reveal grit clearly.
- Grit predicts long-term retention.
- Results replicate across domains.

Chapter 3 • Effort Counts Twice

EFFORT COUNTS TWICE
Effort plays a dual role in producing achievement: first it increases skill by enabling practice and learning, and second it leverages that acquired skill into outputs and accomplishments through sustained application.
When effort is invested consistently over long periods, the cumulative effect both accelerates improvement and multiplies productive output.
Viewed this way, talent shortens the slope of learning but effort determines both the height reached and the volume produced along the way.
- Effort → skill growth; effort → productive application of skill.
- Talent affects learning speed; effort affects quantity and persistence.
OVEREMPHASIS ON TALENT
Cultural narratives habitually highlight apparent instant ability while obscuring the day-to-day practices that produce excellence, which biases observers toward magical explanations instead of incremental causes.
When performances are consumed in isolation, without exposure to the years of practice behind them, the perceptual tendency is to label performers as simply "talented" rather than to trace their mastery to effortful routines.
This misattribution has consequences for how teachers, employers, and institutions identify potential and allocate resources.
- Spotlighting outcomes undervalues process.
- Misattribution can distort selection and support systems.
UNCONSCIOUS BIAS TOWARD TALENT
Empirical studies show that even experts prefer "naturals" to "strivers" when presented with identical work, revealing an implicit bias that favors perceived innate ability over documented effort.
This bias persists despite professed commitments to valuing hard work, and it systematically tilts judgments of hireability, investability, and promotability toward those presumed gifted.
Awareness of the bias is the first step; corrective institutional practices are required to counteract its powerful, often invisible effects.
- Experts favor naturals in blind comparisons.
- Labeling shapes opportunity distribution.
THE MUNDANITY OF EXCELLENCE
Dan Chambliss’s core insight is that elite performance typically consists of many ordinary component skills each practiced and integrated until, in aggregate, they yield extraordinary results.
From stroke mechanics to race strategy, the outstanding swimmer accumulates dozens of reliable micro-skills that, together, create world-class execution; none of these components appears supernatural on its own.
Appreciating mundanity reframes mastery as accessible through deliberate practice and environmental support rather than as an unfathomable gift.
- Excellence = many practiced micro-skills.
- Mundanity explains learnability of high performance.
DAN CHAMBLISS STUDY
Chambliss’s six-year qualitative study of swimmers combined interviews, ethnography, and participant observation to document how longstanding practice, family support, access to facilities, and coaching jointly produce elite outcomes.
His findings emphasize that background conditions—parental interest, financial resources, and pool access—create the scaffolding on which sustained practice can operate effectively.
Such resources do not wholly determine success, but they materially increase the probability that persistent effort will translate into world-class performance.
- Longitudinal qualitative evidence supports practice and context.
- Structural advantages interact with individual persistence.
SWIMMING AND CONTEXTUAL FACTORS
Biographical commonalities among top swimmers include early exposure to the sport, parental investment in coaching and travel, regular competition, and year-round pool time, all of which make repeated practice feasible and effective.
Even with anatomical differences, many of the critical determinants of advantage are environmental and logistical, which means interventions can expand access and opportunity.
Therefore, policy and program design that lower barriers to consistent practice can shift population-level probabilities of producing high performers.
- Access to facilities matters.
- Parental and institutional support enable sustained effort.
MINIMAL TALENT THRESHOLD
Chambliss argues that minimal biological or anatomical advantages are sometimes overemphasized and that the baseline capacity required to benefit from practice in many domains is lower than lay intuition supposes.
This does not deny that extreme physical endowments confer advantages, but it reframes greatness as largely the result of widely available, learnable actions assembled over time.
Consequently, many more people are capable of high performance than conventional talent-centric narratives imply, provided they receive the opportunity and sustain effort.
- Threshold vs. ceiling distinction clarified.
- Expandable potential with practice and access.
MARK SPITZ AND SPECTATOR AWE
Spectators and even practitioners often revert to instinctive "he's a fish" explanations when confronted with a transcendent performance, because the visible result feels disconnected from the invisible labor underlying it.
That instantaneous awe is psychologically gratifying because it preserves a comforting binary—some people have it and others do not—thereby absolving observers from emulating the hard, often tedious, pathway to mastery.
Recognizing the cognitive comfort of mystifying excellence is necessary to counteract the tendency to mystify.
- Spectator narratives favor mystique over process.
- Emotional comfort sustains talent myths.
NIETZSCHE ON GENIUS
Nietzsche earlier observed that we prefer to celebrate finished greatness rather than to attend to the often unglamorous work of becoming, and he urged thinking of great creators as craftsmen who have honed parts before assembling a sublime whole.
This philosophical perspective complements Chambliss’s sociology by explaining why cultural narratives gravitate toward genius myths: myth shelters ego and diminishes competitive pressure to imitate the labor of experts.
Reclaiming the craftsman metaphor helps reorient valuation toward observable, teachable behaviors.
- Philosophical roots of talent myth identified.
- Craft metaphor promotes emulation of process.
MARTY SELIGMAN CHALLENGE
An advisor’s blunt criticism—"You don't have a theory"—pushed the author from descriptive observation to the formalization of a mechanistic account linking talent, effort, skill, and achievement.
That academic push is an example of how mentorship and intellectual rigor convert impression into testable models, which in turn enable systematic investigation and prediction.
Responding to critique by building conceptual structure is itself a model of sustained intellectual effort.
- Academic mentorship catalyzes theory formation.
- Theory enables empirical testing.
EQUATIONS OF ACHIEVEMENT
The core theoretical formulation presented is simple and powerful: Talent × Effort = Skill, and Skill × Effort = Achievement, which together make effort a multiplicative factor in both acquiring capacity and producing outcomes.
These algebra-like equations do not capture all real-world nuances—opportunity, luck, and environment also matter—but they clarify why effort appears disproportionately important in longitudinal success comparisons.
Conceptualizing the pathway this way makes concrete predictions about interventions that increase sustained effort and thereby raise achievement.
- Two-step multiplicative model articulated.
- Model yields testable predictions about practice and output.
TALENT × EFFORT = SKILL
Talent is operationalized as the rate at which skill improves per unit of invested effort, so higher talent accelerates learning curves but does not substitute for sustained practice.
This framing acknowledges individual differences in learning speed while preserving the central role of deliberate effort in turning potential into reliable competence.
Consequently, talent optimizes the efficiency of practice but does not replace the need to practice.
- Talent modifies learning slope.
- Effort remains the engine of skill acquisition.
SKILL × EFFORT = ACHIEVEMENT
Skill without continued effort produces limited outputs, whereas continued effort on top of skill generates tangible accomplishments such as products made, performances delivered, and problems solved.
Effort organizes and amplifies skill through repetition, dissemination, iteration, and public application, converting latent competence into visible success.
This is why the person who applies a moderate skill level persistently can outproduce a more talented but less industrious peer.
- Application multiplies competence into results.
- Output accumulation defines achievement.
WARREN MACKENZIE CASE
Warren MacKenzie’s pottery practice illustrates the model: decades of daily production increased both the quality of his pots and the quantity he could reliably deliver, demonstrating how steady effort compounds into a lifetime output of enduring works.
MacKenzie’s remark that "the first 10,000 pots are difficult" encapsulates the idea that long-term repetition lowers marginal cost of quality and enables mastery to emerge from volume.
His case highlights the interplay between craft routines and the aesthetic criteria by which work is judged.
- Daily practice yields both improved skill and greater volume.
- Volume and quality co-evolve through sustained effort.
JOHN IRVING CASE
John Irving’s trajectory from dyslexic and low SAT scores to acclaimed novelist exemplifies how compensatory strategies, relentless revision, and acceptance of slow processes transform early disadvantages into durable advantages.
Irving’s extensive rewriting practice—turning weakness into a systematic strength—illustrates adaptive effort: he deliberately fashioned a workflow aligned to his personal cognitive tempo and thereby produced superior outcomes.
His example shows that structural or cognitive limitations can be accommodated and even harnessed through persistent, intelligent effort.
- Rewriting as mastery practice.
- Adaptive strategies convert weakness into competitive edge.
WILL SMITH WORK ETHIC
Will Smith’s blunt emphasis on outworking rivals captures a widespread practical truth: consistent, often unenviable labor wins many competitive races that raw potential alone cannot.
Smith’s metaphor of being willing to "die on a treadmill" signals a willingness to sustain marginal advantages day after day, which accumulates into large differences in output and reputation over long horizons.
His public framing makes the relationship between effort and success culturally salient and exemplifies the personal-level commitment that the chapter promotes.
- Work ethic as cumulative advantage.
- Marginal effort differences compound over time.
TREADMILL TEST AND LONGITUDINAL FINDINGS
The Harvard Treadmill Test and subsequent longitudinal follow-ups showed that early measures of perseverance under stress predict later life adjustment and success, even when physiological fitness is accounted for.
George Vaillant’s revival and longitudinal analysis of the cohort connected early behavioral persistence to decades of life outcomes, demonstrating predictive validity for simple endurance measures.
These findings empirically link micro-level persistence to macro-level life trajectories, supporting the theoretical emphasis on sustained application.
- Early endurance predicts adult adjustment.
- Longitudinal data validate persistence effects.
CONSISTENCY AND GETTING BACK ON THE TREADMILL
Returning repeatedly to practice after failure or plateau is arguably the single most important behavioral signature of grit; getting back on the treadmill is different from enduring one session because it indicates long-run commitment.
Many people are capable of short bursts of intense effort but fail to translate them into routines that persist across months and years, which is why inconsistent exercisers rarely reach transformative competence.
Embedding small returnable wins and rituals into daily life increases the probability of staying with a long-term craft or project.
- Repeat engagement beats one-off intensity.
- Rituals and routines sustain returns.
SYNTHESIS AND PRACTICAL IMPLICATIONS
The chapter synthesizes evidence to argue that institutions and individuals should recalibrate evaluation systems, pedagogy, and hiring practices to reward sustained effort and to create conditions that facilitate consistent practice.
Practically, this implies designing metrics that value repeated outputs, building infrastructures that reduce friction to practice, and training mentors to identify and cultivate committed trajectories rather than relying solely on initial aptitude signals.
Realigning incentives toward long-term work will expand opportunities, increase fairness, and raise aggregate human accomplishment.
- Reorient selection toward observable persistence.
- Build infrastructure to support long-term practice.

HOW GRITTY ARE YOU
The chapter opens by distinguishing stamina from short-term intensity and by asking readers to consider whether their efforts persist across years rather than weeks.
Grit is presented as loyalty to a general direction more than loyalty to any single project, and the author uses a classroom exchange to illustrate the difference between heroic bursts of work and long-run commitment.
The chapter frames grit as measurable and malleable and prepares the reader to take a structured self-assessment.
- Grit emphasizes sustained engagement over episodic effort.
- The Wharton anecdote illustrates common misconceptions about grit.
STAMINA VERSUS INTENSITY
The author clarifies that intense, short-term effort—even when heroic—does not equal grit unless it is repeated and sustained over long time horizons.
Gritty activity is ordinary on a daily basis but extraordinary in its cumulative persistence; intensity without continuity burns out or drifts.
Measuring grit therefore privileges patterns across months and years, not single episodes of high energy.
- Intensity is necessary but insufficient.
- Stamina is the core behavioral signature of grit.
LOYALTY TO A DIRECTION
Grit entails staying oriented toward a coherent domain of interest so that skills, contacts, and judgments compound rather than reset with each domain switch.
This directional loyalty allows one to convert small, repeated actions into cumulative advantage rather than dissipating effort across unrelated pursuits.
Switching projects frequently can mask industriousness but undermines the value of persistent investment.
- Domain continuity multiplies returns.
- Frequent domain switching dissipates cumulative skill.
THE GRIT SCALE
The chapter presents the ten-item Grit Scale used in multiple studies to quantify individual differences in perseverance and constancy of interests using comparative self-ratings.
Items probe both tendencies to keep working through setbacks and tendencies to maintain the same long-term aims rather than chasing novelty.
The instrument is introduced as a simple, repeatable measure for self-reflection and research.
- Ten statements, five odd items for passion, five even items for perseverance.
- Score range from 1 to 5; average indicates relative grit.
SCORING AND INTERPRETATION
Readers are instructed to average ten item scores to compute an overall grit score and to use percentile tables to contextualize their result relative to large adult samples.
The author emphasizes that scores capture current self-perception and may change over the lifespan as commitments and contexts evolve.
Separate component scores for passion and perseverance enable diagnostic insights into which facet might need development.
- Total score = average of ten items.
- Odd items → passion; even items → perseverance.
PASSION AND PERSEVERANCE
Grit is decomposed conceptually into two interrelated components: passion, defined as long-term consistency of interests, and perseverance, defined as the willingness to work hard and persist through obstacles.
Although correlated, these dimensions sometimes diverge within people, and the author notes that perseverance often slightly exceeds passion in observed samples.
Understanding each component independently helps target interventions and personal improvement strategies.
- Passion = consistency over years.
- Perseverance = sustained work and bounce-back after setbacks.
CONSISTENCY OVER TIME
Passion is reframed away from fireworks and toward steady, enduring curiosity about problems or domains that remain salient across decades rather than weeks.
Examples from chefs, investors, and mathematicians show that top performers often dwell on the same core questions for long stretches, gradually deepening their grasp.
Consistency enables the accumulation of domain-specific heuristics and the discovery of meaningful long-term opportunities.
- Enduring focus trumps episodic enthusiasm.
- Long-term curiosity produces compounding benefit.
PASSION AS ENDURANCE
High achievers describe passion not as transient excitement but as a compass that orients decisions, fuels repeated practice, and sustains attention through plateaus and setbacks.
Passion-as-endurance supports the daily drudgery of practice and the willingness to tolerate slow progress while pursuing an ultimate concern.
This conception helps explain why some people sustain an otherwise tedious regimen for years.
- Passion guides goal hierarchy alignment.
- Endurance converts interest into habit.

PRACTICE MATTERS
Gritty performers convert intention into output through sustained, structured practice rather than by relying on talent alone.
Practice is the mechanism that turns potential into measurable ability and then into productive accomplishment.
When practice is carried out with purpose, feedback, and adaptation, it reliably produces lasting improvement across domains from athletics to medicine to writing.
MORE TIME ON TASK
One immediate and robust advantage of gritty people is simply that they spend more time engaged with their chosen domain than less gritty peers.
Accumulated time increases exposure to learning opportunities, mistakes, and corrective inputs that drive skill change.
However, raw hours are only the start; how those hours are structured determines their yield.
QUALITY OVER QUANTITY
Not all hours are equal: mindless repetition, habitually performed routines, or passive experience yield little improvement over time.
Expert-level improvement depends on replacing passive time with sessions that deliberately target weaknesses and produce measurable change.
Practice that contains frequent failure without feedback tends to fossilize errors instead of eliminating them.
KAIZEN: CONTINUOUS IMPROVEMENT
Kaizen—continuous, incremental improvement—is a practical ethos for cumulative development, emphasizing small daily gains rather than occasional heroic efforts.
This approach reduces procrastination, normalizes steady work, and compounds marginal improvements into substantial long-term advantage.
DELIBERATE PRACTICE: CORE PRINCIPLES
Deliberate practice is defined by four core features: a specific stretch goal, focused attention, immediate informative feedback, and repeated refinement of the targeted skill.
These elements transform practice from busywork into learning work and are the common denominator among elite performers.
SET NARROW STRETCH GOALS
Effective practice sessions begin with a concrete objective that is narrowly specified and slightly beyond current capability.
By isolating a micro-skill—an element small enough to measure and improve—you enable precise feedback and reduce ambiguity about success.
FOCUS WITH INTENSITY
High-quality practice requires undistracted concentration and a willingness to sustain cognitive and physical effort for the length of the session.
Short bursts of very intense, mindful work produce far greater gains than long periods of unfocused activity.
SEEK IMMEDIATE, ACTIONABLE FEEDBACK
Feedback must be specific, timely, and directed at the variable that caused the error so learners can apply a precise correction in the next attempt.
Without clear signals about what to change and why, practice becomes gambling rather than engineering.
REFLECT, ADJUST, REPEAT
After each practice attempt, experts pause to reflect on the discrepancy between intended and actual performance, make a deliberate adjustment, and try again.
That iterative loop—attempt, feedback, reflection, correction, repeat—is the atomic unit of skill acquisition.
DIAGNOSE & TARGET WEAKNESSES
World-class performers invest disproportionate time on their weaknesses rather than polishing strengths that already function adequately.
Targeting weak links yields asymmetric returns because small improvements on a bottleneck skill can unlock large gains in overall performance.
SOLITARY WORK VS. GROUP PRACTICE
Solitary, focused work often accelerates technical improvements because it eliminates compensatory behavior and social masking of errors.
Group practice has distinct advantages for coordination, timing, and simulation of performance conditions, but it is seldom a substitute for individualized, diagnostic practice.
LIMITED DAILY CAPACITY
Because deliberate practice is mentally and physically demanding, most experts can only sustain a few high-quality hours per day before diminishing returns set in.
Maximizing the effectiveness of those limited hours requires careful session planning and recovery strategies.
EXAMPLES FROM SPORT & COMPETITION
Swimmers like Rowdy Gaines and Katie Ledecky logged enormous volumes of targeted training that combined endurance, technique drills, and race-pace work to build speed and resilience.
Spelling champions spent years isolating error patterns and rehearsing low-probability vocabulary under test conditions to transform recall into reflex.
APPLYING DELIBERATE PRACTICE OUTSIDE ATHLETICS
The same principles guide mastery in medicine, management, writing, programming, and courtroom advocacy by decomposing complex tasks into trainable components.
Benjamin Franklin rewrote essays, surgeons repeat procedural steps under supervision, and coders practice algorithmic problem sets with immediate debugging feedback.
FLOW VERSUS DELIBERATE PRACTICE
Flow is an experiential state aligned with full absorption and perceived effortlessness when skill and challenge are well matched, while deliberate practice is deliberately effortful and diagnostic.
Both phenomena appear in the lives of experts, but they occupy different temporal phases: practice is preparation; flow is a likely outcome of successful preparation during performance.
RECONCILING FLOW & PRACTICE
Rather than opposing models, practice and flow are complementary: structured, often unpleasant practice increases the probability of experiencing flow during high-stakes performance.
The apparent ease of a masterful performance is built on invisible hours of targeted struggle.
EMOTIONAL EXPERIENCE OF PRACTICE
Deliberate practice can feel aversive, frustrating, or even painful, but many high performers report deep satisfaction in the process of improvement itself over time.
As competence grows, the subjective experience of hard work often shifts toward a sense of meaning and identity that sustains continued effort.
MEASUREMENT & OBJECTIVE METRICS
Good practice depends on metrics that reflect the micro-skill being practiced, such as time, error rate, execution smoothness, or decision accuracy.
Without objective measures, it's difficult to determine whether practice led to real improvement or merely to the perception of progress.
THE PRACTICE DIARY
