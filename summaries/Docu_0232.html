Internal Controls, Compliance, and Risk
Internal Controls
Establish the baseline control structure for the whole finance and compliance stack.
Scope and control intent: Confirm who owns each control, what the control prevents, how often it is performed, what evidence proves completion, and what exception path applies when the normal process cannot be followed.
Primary focus: Completeness, authorization, accuracy, timeliness, safeguarding, and traceability.
Escalation rule: Escalate design gaps quickly so control weaknesses do not become recurring losses, avoidable corrections, or repeated reporting errors.

Control design and baseline expectations
Define the control objective first, then map the minimum steps needed to achieve it. The strongest control is the one that prevents the error before it can enter the ledger or the payment stream.
Use written policies, role descriptions, and documented procedures so control intent is visible even when the original owner is absent.
Assign a named control owner, reviewer, and backup owner for each critical step, especially where the process depends on a single staff member or a single system rule.

Execution and monitoring
Make the process easy to execute correctly. Controls that are too complicated get bypassed, delayed, or applied inconsistently.
Use periodic review to confirm that the control is still aligned with actual operations, not only with the original policy language.
Track exceptions by type, cause, owner, and date so recurring weaknesses are visible early.

Segregation of Duties
Prevent one person from initiating, approving, recording, and reconciling the same transaction.
Scope and control intent: Use access rights and workflow roles to make incompatible duties visible before processing starts, not after the transaction has already affected the books or cash.
Primary focus: Map compensating controls where staffing is thin or systems are simple, and make those compensating controls stronger than the normal process, not weaker.
Escalation rule: Review duty conflicts regularly because user roles, project assignments, delegated authority, and temporary cover arrangements change over time.

Duty separation logic
The core test is whether the same person could conceal an error or fraud without detection. If yes, the process is not adequately separated.
Look at the full transaction life cycle, not just the entry point. A person who can create a vendor, approve a payment, and reconcile the bank can bypass several safeguards at once.
Separate system admin rights from approval rights wherever possible so technical access does not quietly override business controls.

Approval Matrices
Define who can approve what, at which amount, under which fund, and with which conditions.
Scope and control intent: Keep matrices current by vendor class, grant restriction, program budget, transaction type, and exception category so approval rules remain operational rather than theoretical.
Primary focus: Make approval limits easy to audit by linking them to delegated authority, policy references, budget ownership, and document retention expectations.
Escalation rule: Require proof of approval before posting, payment release, purchase commitment, contract execution, or journal entry acceptance.

Matrix structure and governance
A usable matrix is specific enough to prevent ambiguity but simple enough for staff to follow without constant interpretation. Every limit should answer four questions: who, what, how much, and under what conditions.
Include backup approvers and escalation paths for absence, urgent processing, or conflicts of interest.
Keep versioning visible so a reviewer can tell which rule was in force at the time of the transaction.

Control Environment
Set the tone for integrity, discipline, competence, and accountability.
Scope and control intent: Translate leadership expectations into policies, role descriptions, performance review criteria, disciplinary standards, and escalation norms that staff can understand and apply in ordinary work.
Primary focus: Use clear lines of authority so staff know when to ask, when to escalate, when to document, and when to stop a transaction.
Escalation rule: Test whether culture matches policy by reviewing exception rates, late approvals, unresolved findings, repeated overrides, and staff awareness of the rules.

Leadership and tone at the top
The control environment begins with management behavior. If leadership ignores the rules, staff will learn that the rules are optional.
Policies should be backed by visible enforcement, consistent training, and timely correction when expectations are breached.
Promotions, performance reviews, and delegation should reflect compliance behavior, not just output volume or speed.

Fraud Prevention
Build barriers that reduce opportunity, pressure, and rationalization before misconduct occurs.
Scope and control intent: Combine prevention, detection, and response so suspicious activity is caught early, investigated consistently, and contained before it spreads across multiple accounts or periods.
Primary focus: Track warning signs such as vendor overlap, duplicate invoices, override behavior, unexplained adjustments, and unusual timing patterns.
Escalation rule: Link prevention work to hotline use, access reviews, reconciliations, surprise checks, and case escalation so red flags are not ignored.

Prevention model
Fraud prevention works best when the process makes wrongdoing difficult, risky, and visible. Controls should reduce opportunity first, because opportunity is the easiest fraud driver to influence.
Where possible, design systems so no single user can both create a condition and conceal it.
Use training to explain not only the policy but also the real-world fraud patterns the policy is meant to stop.

Operational controls and workflow
Preventive checks include vendor validation, identity verification, duplicate detection, approval thresholds, restricted access, and independent review of unusual transactions.

Effective prevention programs are continuous, not annual. Risk patterns shift too quickly for one-time review to be enough.

Anti-Fraud Controls
Use targeted controls that stop, detect, and contain fraud in high-risk processes.
Scope and control intent: Protect vendor setup, payments, cash handling, expense claims, inventory, grants, and journal entries with layered reviews that address both prevention and response.
Primary focus: Add threshold checks, duplicate tests, and exception reporting wherever the process is vulnerable, and document the business justification for each layered control.
Escalation rule: Close the loop by documenting investigations, recovery efforts, corrective action, and whether the weakness requires a policy or system change.

High-risk process points
Fraud risk is often concentrated where data can be created, changed, or overridden without independent review. Vendor master setup, bank detail changes, manual journal entries, and off-cycle payments deserve special attention.
Layer controls so one failure does not defeat the entire process. A duplicate check is more effective when it is paired with approval review and an independent exception report.
Use red-flag logic that is understandable by staff so abnormal patterns are detected by people, not only by software.

Fraud Risk Assessment
Identify where fraud could occur, who could do it, what the impact would be, and how likely it is.
Scope and control intent: Score inherent risk and residual risk so resources go to the most exposed processes, accounts, and control points.
Primary focus: Update the assessment when systems, programs, staffing, funding sources, or operating expectations change.
Escalation rule: Use the results to drive control design, monitoring, board reporting, and remediation priorities.

Assessment method
Start with the transaction stream, not the chart of accounts. Determine where assets move, where records are created, and where approval can be bypassed.
Evaluate incentives, opportunities, and concealment paths separately, then map them to the strongest available controls.
Use a simple rating scale that management can apply consistently over time. Overly complex scoring often reduces usefulness.

Whistleblowing Mechanisms
Provide safe ways to report suspected misconduct, policy breaches, or retaliation concerns.
Scope and control intent: Support anonymous and named reports with clear routing, triage, and escalation rules so concerns are handled consistently, confidentially, and without delay.
Primary focus: Separate intake, review, investigation, and resolution so cases do not depend on a single individual or an informal conversation.
Escalation rule: Retain case files and disposition notes to show that concerns were handled promptly, fairly, and with appropriate follow-up.

Channel design
A workable reporting channel is easy to find, easy to use, and protected from retaliation. Staff should know how to report, what happens next, and how their identity will be handled.
Anonymous reporting should still allow case tracking and triage. Named reporting should be protected by confidentiality rules and limited access.
Train managers not to discourage escalation or conduct informal side settlements that bypass documented review.

Compliance Monitoring
Check continuously that required controls, filings, and approvals are actually happening.
Scope and control intent: Use periodic testing, exception review, and management sign-off to surface breakdowns early and to show that the compliance program operates in practice, not just on paper.
Primary focus: Track trends by program, location, and process owner to find repeat problems and weak points that may need redesign rather than repeated correction.
Escalation rule: Report unresolved items with ownership, due dates, and status until closure.

Monitoring design
Monitoring should answer three questions: what was tested, what failed, and what was done about it. Without all three, review activity is incomplete.
Combine self-checks, supervisor checks, and independent testing so no one layer is expected to catch every failure.
Use dashboards carefully. They are useful only when the underlying data is accurate and the exception thresholds are meaningful.

Regulatory Compliance
Map each rule, grant term, tax requirement, and reporting obligation to a control owner.
Scope and control intent: Keep a living obligations register that identifies deadlines, evidence, escalation triggers, and the business process that satisfies each requirement.
Primary focus: Make compliance part of ordinary processing instead of treating it as a separate afterthought.
Escalation rule: Document correspondence with regulators, auditors, and funders in a central file so the record is complete and searchable.

Obligation management
A compliance register should identify the requirement, the owner, the due date, the evidence source, the filing method, and the retention rule.
Every obligation should be attached to a real process step. If no one owns the obligation, it will eventually be missed or duplicated.
When laws or award terms change, update the register and retrain the process owner immediately.

Risk Management
Identify, assess, treat, and monitor risks across finance, operations, and governance.
Scope and control intent: Treat risk as a portfolio rather than isolated events so one failure does not hide another.
Primary focus: Assign owners and action plans to the highest-priority risks with clear due dates and measurable mitigation steps.
Escalation rule: Use periodic review to decide whether to avoid, reduce, transfer, accept, or monitor each risk.

Risk portfolio discipline
Risk management should cover financial exposure, compliance exposure, operational fragility, reputational harm, and governance weakness together. Looking at only one category can produce a false sense of safety.
High-priority risks should have owners, deadlines, and a defined response. A risk without an action plan is only a label.
Review risk posture after major changes such as new funding, new systems, new leadership, or new geography.

Financial Risk Management
Watch liquidity, budget variance, concentration risk, and funding volatility.
Scope and control intent: Use forecasts, cash projections, reserve analysis, and sensitivity testing to avoid surprises in grant or operating cash.
Primary focus: Stress-test major assumptions and recurring revenue streams before commitments are made.
Escalation rule: Track covenant, restriction, and donor requirements that can affect spending flexibility and the timing of obligations.

Financial exposure areas
Liquidity risk appears when available cash cannot support committed spending, payroll, or restricted obligations on time. Budget variance risk appears when actual performance drifts beyond tolerable thresholds.
Concentration risk appears when the organization depends too heavily on one funder, one program, one geography, or one revenue line.
Volatility risk appears when renewals, reimbursements, or donor behavior can change rapidly with little warning.

Operational Risk Controls
Protect day-to-day service delivery from process failure, interruption, or weak oversight.
Scope and control intent: Use standard operating procedures, backups, escalation paths, and recovery steps to preserve continuity and avoid service disruption.
Primary focus: Monitor service delays, reconciliations, manual workarounds, repeat incidents, and control bypasses that can signal deeper process weakness.
Escalation rule: Correct root causes instead of only fixing the symptom that surfaced first.

Operational resilience
Operational risk is the risk that the process itself breaks down. That includes staff absence, system outage, delayed approvals, data entry defects, and unclear handoffs.
Resilience improves when procedures are documented, cross-trained, and periodically tested in real-world conditions.
Backup arrangements should cover not just systems but also authority, documentation, and communication.

Control Testing
Check whether controls are designed well and whether they work in practice.
Scope and control intent: Test samples for completeness, correctness, timely performance, reviewer evidence, and the ability of the control to detect or prevent the intended risk.
Primary focus: Retest after remediation so the same weakness does not reappear in the next cycle.
Escalation rule: Keep a clear distinction between design issues, operating issues, and isolated exceptions.

Testing discipline
A test should prove more than that a step happened. It should show that the step was done by the right person, at the right time, with the right evidence, and with the intended level of review.
Design testing asks whether the control could work. Operating testing asks whether it did work during the selected period.
Retesting matters because remediation without verification is only an assumption.

Compliance Audits
Prepare for independent review with complete support, clear narratives, and organized records.
Scope and control intent: Separate audit requests from management remediation so findings are tracked to closure and not treated as one-time paperwork.
Primary focus: Document the link between the condition, cause, effect, and corrective action.
Escalation rule: Use audit results to refine risk priorities, not just to satisfy the current audit cycle.

Audit readiness
Audit readiness means the organization can produce complete evidence quickly, explain the process clearly, and demonstrate that controls are operating consistently.
Preparation should include document indexing, ownership mapping, and pre-review of common audit areas such as procurement, payroll, grants, and journal entries.
Good audit readiness reduces disruption because the organization already knows where the support lives and who owns each topic.

Policy Exception Management
Allow controlled exceptions only when the risk is understood, approved, and time limited.
Scope and control intent: Record the business reason, compensating control, reviewer, and expiration date for each exception so deviations are visible and temporary, not informal or permanent by default.
Primary focus: Review whether exceptions are recurring, because repeated exceptions often indicate a flawed policy, weak training, or a process that no longer matches reality.
Escalation rule: Close exceptions formally and keep the approval trail available for later review.

Exception governance
Exceptions should be rare, documented, and time bound. A recurring exception should trigger a redesign discussion, not only another approval signature.
Assign an owner to monitor the expiration date and ensure closure or renewal occurs before the exception lapses.
Track exceptions by policy, process, approver, and root cause so management can see whether the same weakness repeats across departments or programs.

Scope and control intent: Tie each obligation to controls, evidence, and a named owner so gaps are visible and priority can be assigned rationally.
Primary focus: Rank issues by likelihood, impact, and detectability rather than by convenience.
Escalation rule: Update the assessment when funding terms, laws, systems, or operating models change.

Assessment structure
A compliance risk assessment should begin with the obligation, not the control. Identify what can go wrong, then decide whether the current control meaningfully reduces the exposure.
Score the impact in practical terms: loss of funds, penalties, late filing, reputational damage, recovery cost, and operational disruption.
Detectability matters because some risks are more dangerous when they are hard to notice until after the damage is done.
