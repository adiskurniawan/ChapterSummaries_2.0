Summary
Co-intelligence emphasizes collaborative thinking over individual intelligence.

Co-intelligence is the capacity of groups to solve problems collectively, leveraging diverse perspectives. It requires systems and processes that encourage dialogue, shared understanding, and the integration of multiple viewpoints. Unlike AI alignment, co-intelligence focuses on human collaboration, decision-making, and social coordination.
Rule 1 – Everyone is invited to participate.

Inclusivity is critical for co-intelligence. Every stakeholder, regardless of hierarchy or expertise, should have a voice. Broad participation increases the pool of ideas, prevents blind spots, and reduces groupthink. Methods like open forums, brainstorming sessions, and structured dialogue help ensure that all perspectives are considered.
Rule 2 – Listen first, speak last.

Effective co-intelligence requires active listening. Participants must understand others’ viewpoints before contributing their own ideas. This fosters empathy, reduces conflict, and allows the group to integrate diverse insights. Techniques such as reflective listening, summarizing others’ points, and delaying judgment enhance collective understanding.
Rule 3 – Pay attention to relationships and context.

Social dynamics and situational context shape group outcomes. Awareness of interpersonal patterns, power imbalances, and organizational culture helps participants navigate discussions effectively. Recognizing how context influences perceptions, priorities, and communication prevents misunderstandings and ensures more coherent decisions.
Rule 4 – Test ideas before settling on decisions.

Iterative evaluation strengthens group intelligence. Prototyping, simulations, pilot projects, or small-scale experiments allow ideas to be tested in practice. This reduces risks, uncovers hidden assumptions, and builds confidence in collective decisions. Groups learn faster and make better choices when decisions are treated as hypotheses to validate rather than immutable directives.
Tools and practices support co-intelligence.

Structured decision-making frameworks, facilitation techniques, and collaborative technologies enhance co-intelligence. Examples include consensus-building processes, visual mapping of ideas, real-time collaborative software, and decision logs. These tools create transparency, track reasoning, and help groups learn from outcomes.
Diversity is a key driver of co-intelligence.

Cognitive, experiential, and cultural diversity enriches problem-solving. Groups with heterogeneous perspectives can identify novel solutions and anticipate challenges that homogeneous teams might overlook. Encouraging respectful disagreement and balancing dominant voices with quieter participants enhances collective insight.
Trust and psychological safety are essential.

Co-intelligence thrives when participants feel safe to share ideas without fear of ridicule or retaliation. Establishing norms of respect, confidentiality, and constructive feedback fosters trust, which in turn enables more open dialogue, risk-taking, and creative problem-solving.
Iterative learning and feedback loops strengthen group outcomes.

Continuous reflection on process and results allows groups to refine strategies, improve collaboration, and adapt to changing circumstances. Feedback loops include after-action reviews, structured reflection sessions, and lessons learned repositories. These mechanisms reinforce knowledge retention and system-level intelligence.
Leadership in co-intelligence focuses on facilitation, not control.

Leaders act as facilitators, creating conditions for dialogue, inclusivity, and decision-making rather than imposing solutions. Their role includes guiding discussions, mediating conflicts, and ensuring that all voices are heard while maintaining progress toward shared goals.
Challenges in co-intelligence include conflict and coordination complexity.

Diverse perspectives can generate tension, miscommunication, and slower decision-making. Effective facilitation, clear norms, and iterative processes mitigate these risks. Recognizing conflict as an opportunity for learning rather than a barrier enhances group effectiveness.
Applications of co-intelligence extend to organizations and society.

Co-intelligence principles apply to corporate strategy, community problem-solving, scientific research, and governance. For instance, multi-stakeholder collaborations in urban planning or environmental policy benefit from structured dialogue, iterative testing, and inclusive participation.
Chapter 3 Summary / Key Takeaways

Co-intelligence leverages group capacity to solve complex problems collaboratively. The Four Rules — inclusive participation, listen first/speak last, attention to context and relationships, and testing ideas — provide a framework for effective collective thinking. Supplementary practices include diversity, psychological safety, iterative learning, facilitative leadership, and structured tools, all of which strengthen group intelligence and improve decision-making.

Summary
Commonsense is a mental skill that helps detect “unknown unknowns.”

The stock picker wanted a planner who could anticipate fresh opportunities, not just follow past data or take speculative risks. Commonsense allowed him to separate superficial market noise from fundamental changes.
Children outperform AI in commonsense by recognizing what they do not know.

A child entering a new house or meeting a new person hesitates: “I don’t know this place. I don’t know that face.” Logic-based AI lacks this ability, leading to confident but incorrect answers.
Story enables the brain to recognize novelty.

Story operates in the past-future temporal domain (why and what if), allowing the brain to identify environmental volatility and the limits of its knowledge. Computers process timeless logic and cannot detect unknown unknowns.
Commonsense has a negative function: doubt current plans.

When commonsense detects unknown unknowns, it warns: “There is relevant info you don’t know.” This can prompt new planning or rapid pivoting when volatility is high.
Commonsense can also be positive: confirming stability.

When no warning is sounded, the brain knows it is safe to continue current actions. Confidence comes from the absence of threats rather than a spreadsheet of proof.
Humans invert computer reasoning for effective decision-making.

Computers seek verification; humans seek falsification. AI is effective in stable situations; humans excel when conditions change. Overtraining on data can lead humans to anxiety and paralysis.
Commonsense is learned through experience, not formal logic.

Children act on intuition; as adults, experience allows accurate judgment and agility. This explains the stock picker’s ability to profit in stable and volatile markets.
Ben Franklin exemplified commonsense in practice.

Franklin switched deftly between conventional wisdom (e.g., Poor Richard's Almanack) and groundbreaking science (1752 lightning experiment). His editorial work taught him to balance routine and novelty.
Franklin’s aphorisms illustrate commonsense principles.

1. Be slow in choosing a friend, slower in changing.
2. Read much but not too many books.
3. Write injuries in dust, benefits in marble.
4. He who gives promptly gives twice as much.
5. You may delay, but time will not.
6. The way to be safe is never to be secure.
First three: stability; last three: agility.
Commonsense can be taught, as shown by Special Operators’ training.

The course alternates between routine and novelty, cultivating the mental recognition: “There’s something here I don’t know.” Recruits arrive with zero commonsense due to classroom and computer conditioning.
Three-step method to tune anxiety for commonsense.

1. Tune your anxiety – allow fear to alert to true environmental volatility.
2. Rid the future of the past – process anxieties from prior events and convert them into SOPs.
3. Focus on Now + 1 – concentrate on the immediate actionable future to gauge unknown unknowns.
Step 1 – Tune your anxiety.

Anxiety is a tool, signaling when your brain encounters branching possible futures. Children and operators alike use anxiety to detect environmental volatility.
Step 2 – Rid the future of the past.

Past anxieties replay previous events. To avoid paralyzing worry, convert them into Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs), much like pilots updating procedures after incidents. Example: Buffett overcame stage fright by creating SOPs from prior experiences.
Step 3 – Focus on the immediate future (Now + 1).

Don’t look too far ahead; concentrate on the next action. This prevents overloading the mind with countless [what ifs]. Operators emphasize detecting and preparing for volatility one step ahead.
Anxiety must be calibrated.

Too little anxiety: oblivious to danger. Too much: paralysis. Properly tuned, anxiety directs attention to critical near-future risks while allowing confidence in stable patterns.
Application in investing – Past Anxiety: NONE

Warren Buffett used past anxiety correctly by converting prior negative experiences into SOPs. Focused on fundamentals, avoided overreacting to past market fears, enabled long-term gains.
Application in investing – Future Anxiety: NEAR

James Simons trained with the Institute for Defense Analysis to focus on near-future threats. Applied Now + 1 to market volatility, switching from a failing algorithm to adaptive human-guided trading, generating extraordinary returns.
Commonsense allows selective creativity.

Don’t innovate in stable situations; save imagination for volatile periods. This principle allowed stock pickers to profit while minimizing unnecessary risk.
Primal Intelligence framework.

Four interconnected story-based thinking skills:
1. Intuition – uncovers hidden rules and exceptions.
2. Imagination – develops flexible strategies.
3. Emotion – signals when past/future narratives are misaligned.
4. Commonsense – directs which strategies to follow or pivot from.
Primal Intelligence is biological, not algorithmic.

Cannot be replicated by AI. Requires sensing unknown unknowns and reacting to volatility. Most humans excel in some, but rarely all four areas.
Commonsense directs life planning.

Intuition sparks plans. Imagination shapes plans. Emotion sustains plans. Commonsense selects plans. These four powers create an integrated mental toolkit for thriving in uncertainty.
Stock pickers and operators use similar techniques.

Both process past jitters (Past Anxiety: NONE) and focus on immediate market conditions (Future Anxiety: NEAR) to maximize situational awareness and profits.
Buffett case study – converting anxiety into SOPs.

Young Buffett experienced severe stage fright. Using Dale Carnegie methods, he developed routines (SOPs) to handle public speaking. Applied the same to investing: Be greedy when the market is scared.
Simons case study – Now + 1 and adaptive strategy.

Simons’ hedge fund initially failed with a static algorithm. Applying near-future focus, he replaced it with Berlekamp’s adaptive model, detecting anomalies and exploiting short-term market inefficiencies.
Core commonsense principle.

Know when you don’t know. Detect environmental volatility, use past experience for stability, and reserve action for emerging opportunities.
