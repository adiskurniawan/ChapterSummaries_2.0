Chapter 1 — Financial Governance and Control
Purpose & objective
Establish a single, auditable organisational governance framework that protects assets, ensures funds are used for intended purposes, maintains fiduciary accountability to donors and stakeholders, manages financial risks and supports timely, transparent financial decision-making. The framework must enable compliance with donor terms and local law, enforce segregation of duties, maintain a clear audit trail, provide escalation routes for exceptions, and support continuous improvement. Governance is both policy and practice — controls must be embedded in day-to-day operations and evidenced.
Scope
Applies to all legal entities, country offices, sub-offices, projects, programmes, partners, suppliers and staff involved in financial activities. Covers budgeting, commitments, procurement, contracting, payments, payroll, treasury, accounting, partner funding, asset management, reporting, internal controls, monitoring and audit. Excludes activities explicitly covered under separate statutory or donor-mandated governance frameworks where those rules are stricter — in such cases donor/statutory rules prevail and any deviation must be documented and approved.
Key definitions & core recognition principles
• Governance: structures, policies and processes used to direct, manage and control the organisation’s financial affairs.
• Control environment: tone at the top, policies, organisational structures and human resource practices that underpin other controls.
• Segregation of duties (SoD): clear separation of initiation, approval, execution, recording and reconciliation tasks.
• Delegation of Authority (DoA): formal limits and authorities for spending, commitments and approvals.
• Materiality: thresholds for reporting, remediation and escalation based on financial and reputational impact.
Core principle: apply risk-based, proportionate controls that are documented and independently verifiable.
Core governance principles
1. Accountability: named individuals accept responsibility for actions and decisions; ultimate in-country accountability rests with the Country Director (CD).
2. Transparency: decisions, approvals and financial positions are documented and accessible to authorised reviewers.
3. Legality & compliance: all activities comply with donor agreements, laws and internal policies.
4. Economy, efficiency & effectiveness: buying and spending decisions must demonstrate value for money and alignment with objectives.
5. Proportionality: controls should match risk and scale of operations.
6. Continuous assurance: monitoring, internal audit and external audit are core components of governance.
Governance & responsibilities (RACI-style)
• Board / Trustees: approve policies, risk appetite, budgets and audited accounts; receive and act on assurance reports.
• Audit & Risk Committee: review control effectiveness, oversight of internal/external audit findings, approve audit plans and follow-up.
• Executive Director / CEO: enforce Board policy, ensure adequate resources for controls and act on escalations.
• Country Director (CD): ultimate local accountability for compliance, resource stewardship and escalation of material issues.
• Country Finance Director / Manager (CFD/CFM): responsible for day-to-day operation of financial controls, reporting, reconciliations, system integrity and staff competence.
• Budget holders / Programme Managers: prepare and manage budgets, authorise expenditure within DoA, justify variances and ensure programme legitimacy of expenses.
• Procurement / Contracts: run procurement processes per policy and maintain supplier due diligence records.
• HR: ensure personnel policies, payroll integrations and supervision of expense-related people controls.
• Treasury / Centre Finance: provide policy guidance, manage cross-border flows, central bank relationships, and consolidated reporting.
• Internal Audit / Assurance: independent reviews of control effectiveness, testing and reporting to senior governance bodies.
• All staff: comply with policies, report concerns via whistleblowing channels and maintain accurate records.
Delegation of Authority (DoA) — design & operation
• Formalisation: DoA must be documented, published, version-controlled and acknowledged by named signatories.
• Scope: approvals for commitments, contract signature, budget reallocation, payment authorisation, journal entries, write-offs, asset disposals, and HR remuneration actions.
• Limits & tiers: include monetary thresholds, role-based permissions and special approvals for related-party transactions and conflicts of interest.
• No self-approval: persons may not approve transactions where they or close family/associates have a direct benefit.
• Temporary & acting delegations: documented, time-limited and approved by a higher authority; recorded in an exceptions register.
• Enforcement: DoA must be enforced in financial systems; any breach triggers investigation and remediation.
Segregation of duties (SoD) & compensating controls
• Minimum requirement: separation between initiation, approval, execution, recording and reconciliation.
• Small-office guidance: where SoD cannot be fully implemented due to staffing, compensate with mandatory independent review by regional/centre, use of dual approvals, post-transaction audit sampling, or segregation via system roles and two-person controls for cash and payments.
• System-enforced SoD: use role-based access controls (RBAC) and logging; periodic access review to detect SoD erosion.
Policy & control hierarchy
• Global policies: set mandatory minimums; non-negotiable.
• Regional/country procedures: may raise standards or add local steps to reflect legal or operational realities; must not reduce global requirements.
• SOPs & checklists: operationalise policy into step-by-step procedures, templates and control checklists.
Risk management — financial risk lifecycle
• Identification: maintain a register of finance-specific risks (fraud, FX, liquidity, partner failure, sanctions exposure, payment fraud, system outages).
• Assessment: rate likelihood and impact; define residual risk after controls.
• Mitigation: assign owners and track actions with deadlines; include insurance, hedging, multi-bank relationships and reserve policies where appropriate.
• Monitoring & reporting: include risk indicators in management dashboards and escalate when tolerance thresholds are breached.
Fraud, corruption & whistleblowing
• Zero tolerance: enforce robust anti-fraud controls and sanctions.
• Preventive controls: SoD, supplier checks, supplier bank validation, segregation of payment duties, periodic PEP/sanctions screening and mandatory conflict-of-interest disclosures.
• Detection: data analytics, exception reporting, reconciliations and internal audit testing.
• Reporting channels: confidential whistleblowing mechanisms with protections against retaliation; multiple channels (local, regional, centre, anonymous hotline) and clear guidance on reporting timelines.
• Investigation: documented protocol for evidence preservation, investigation ownership, law enforcement engagement, and timely remedial action.
• Recovery & remediation: recover funds where possible, apply disciplinary action, update controls and report material incidents to donors/regulators as required.
Compliance obligations — laws, donors & sanctions
• Local law compliance: tax, labour, banking, registration and statutory filings must be met in each jurisdiction.
• Donor compliance: adhere to grant agreements, allowability rules, earmarking, and reporting cycles; seek written waivers for deviations.
• Sanctions/AML/CFT: run sanctions screening for counterparties and implement AML controls for cash and banking flows; escalate matches immediately.
• Record of compliance changes: maintain a legal & regulatory change log and assess impact on policies/SOPs.
Budget governance & financial planning
• Budget lifecycle: preparation, approval, monitoring, reforecasting and closure.
• Approval before spend: budget must be approved and available in system before commitment except in pre-defined emergencies with retrospective approval.
• Monitoring & variance analysis: monthly budget vs actual reviews with commentary on material variances and corrective actions; budget holder sign-off required.
• Reforecasting: periodic re-forecast on material programmes or when variance thresholds are breached.
Procurement, contracting & supplier management
• Procurement policy: thresholds, competition requirements, supplier evaluation, conflict-of-interest declarations and contract authorisation.
• Contract lifecycle: pre-approval checks (budget, legal, tax), sign-off per DoA, contract register, and performance monitoring.
• Supplier due diligence: anti-bribery, tax status, sanctions check, capacity assessment for partners and critical suppliers; enhanced due diligence for high-value or high-risk suppliers.
• Purchase commitments: commit only against approved budgets; record purchase orders or equivalent contractual instruments; monitor delivery and acceptance before payment.
Partner & sub-office oversight
• Due diligence: financial health, governance, compliance history and operational capacity assessments before awarding funds.
• Partner agreements: clear deliverables, reporting cadence, audit rights, use of funds clauses and sanctions for misuse.
• Monitoring: regular financial reports, site visits, spot-checks, submission of audit reports, and corrective action plans for weaknesses.
• Capacity building: provide technical assistance where partner weaknesses are identified and use staged payments when risk is higher.
Treasury & banking controls
• Bank relationships: maintain approved bank list, multi-bank approach to mitigate counterparty risk and defined signatory lists with specimen signatures.
• Bank reconciliations: regular, timely reconciliations with independent review; investigate outstanding items promptly.
• Cash management: maintain optimal cash levels, invest surplus consistent with policy, apply FX hedging policy where material exposures exist.
• Payments security: two-step approval for bank file creation and upload, dual signatories, multi-factor authentication for bank portals and positive-pay/validation services where available.
Financial reporting & management information
• Timeliness & accuracy: monthly management accounts, key dashboards and narrative commentary distributed to senior management and budget holders.
• Format & content: profit & loss / statement of financial activity, balance sheet, cashflow, committed expenditure, donor reporting pack and variance analysis.
• Management review: documented review meetings with minutes capturing actions, owners and deadlines.
Systems & IT access controls
• System governance: approved systems for finance, procurement and payroll with formal change control, backup and disaster recovery arrangements.
• Access control: RBAC, least privilege, periodic certification, immediate revocation on exit or role change, and mandatory unique user IDs (no shared logins).
• Logging & monitoring: transaction logs retained for forensic review, alerts for anomalous activities, and periodic review of high-risk user actions.
Record keeping & audit trail
• Documentation standards: all transactions supported by original documentation or acceptable electronic equivalents; indexable, searchable and cross-referenced to ledger entries.
• Retention: follow legal/donor retention requirements; typical minimum 6–10 years or longer where jurisdiction/donor requires; anonymised summaries where PII cannot be retained.
• Electronic storage: secure, version-controlled and backed-up repository with clear access controls.
Data protection & confidentiality
• PII handling: treat financial and payroll data as highly confidential; encrypt data in transit and at rest; limit access and use secure distribution channels for sensitive documents.
• Data processing agreements: in place with third-party processors; ensure cross-border transfers have legal basis and safeguards.
• Breach response: documented incident response and notification protocols.
Audit, assurance & external scrutiny
• Internal audit: independent programme of assurance covering key risk areas, with direct reporting to Audit Committee/Board; ensure timely remediation of findings.
• External audit: annual statutory audit and donor audits as required; provide adequate documentation and support for audit sampling.
• Donor audits & spot checks: cooperate fully, provide evidence and implement agreed actions arising from audits.
Monitoring, KPIs & management information
Suggested KPIs and dashboards to monitor governance effectiveness:
• % of reconciliations completed on time.
• Number of control exceptions per month and mean time to resolution.
• % of payments dual-signed/approved per DoA.
• Ageing of outstanding commitments and liabilities.
• Variance analysis: budget vs actual (material variances explained).
• Number of fraud incidents reported and resolved.
• Coverage and closure rate of internal audit findings.
• % of staff with up-to-date mandatory finance/compliance training.
Continuous improvement & change management
• Lessons learned: capture from audits, incidents and donor feedback; implement improvements via change projects.
• Policy review cycle: periodic review and update of policies (at least annually or when regulatory/donor changes occur).
• Change control: formal programme for policy/system changes with training, documentation and phased roll-outs.

Templates, repositories & naming conventions
Maintain standardised templates and a protected central repository for:
• DoA register and signatory lists.
• Budget templates and approvals.
• Procurement checklists and contracts.
• Supplier due diligence records.
• Reconciliations and management reports.
Naming convention example: <DocType>_<Country>_<YYYYMMDD>_<Reference> (e.g., PO_CountryX_20251201_PO12345.pdf).
Incident handling & escalation
• Immediate containment: stop further exposure, secure evidence and freeze payments where possible.
• Notification: inform CD, CFD, regional/centre finance and internal audit as per incident severity thresholds.
• Investigation & remediation: follow investigative protocol, take disciplinary/legal action where appropriate and update controls to prevent recurrence.
• Reporting: notify donors/regulators when required and maintain an incident register with status and closure evidence.
Audit expectations & audit pack
Prepare audit packs that provide clear traceability: approved budgets, supporting invoices, contracts, bank confirmations, reconciliations, DoA evidence, system access logs, minutes of management review and corrective action plans. Ensure packs are indexed and cross-referenced to ledger lines to facilitate efficient auditor sampling.
Training, capacity building & competence
• Mandatory training: finance policies, DoA, SoD, procurement and anti-fraud modules for relevant staff.
• Role-specific training: budget holders receive training on budget management and reporting; procurement staff trained on competitive sourcing and contract management.
• Continuous professional development: encourage certifications and peer learning.
Special cases & exceptions
• Emergency procurement / rapid response: pre-approved contingency procedures with strict retrospective reporting, justification and heightened oversight.
• Small or remote offices: documented compensating controls, regional oversight and periodic spot checks.
• Related-party transactions: enhanced review, independent valuation and Board-level approval where material.
Cross-references & related policies
Refer to the organisational Finance Policy, Procurement Policy, DoA Document, Payroll Policy, Treasury Policy, Fraud & Whistleblowing Policy, Data Protection Policy, Partner Management Guidance and Award/Grants Manuals. Where donor terms differ, donor requirements take precedence and deviations must be documented.
Quick reference — concise workflow
1. Budget approved and recorded in system.
2. Request/PO raised against budget with required approvals per DoA.
3. Goods/services received and invoice matched to PO/contract.
4. Payment authorised and executed with two-person controls where required.
5. Reconciliation: bank ↔ GL ↔ sub-ledgers; management review documented.
6. Archive documentation and update registers.
Common errors & mitigations
Common errors: unauthorised spend, weak SoD, outdated DoA, missing supplier due diligence, late reconciliations, failure to escalate breaches.
Mitigations: mandatory cut-offs, automated validation, periodic access reviews, supplier vetting, enforcement of reconciliations, regular training and internal audit sampling.
KPIs & management information (expanded)
• % of reconciliations completed on time.
• Average time to close internal audit findings.
• % of payments with complete supporting documents.
• Number of DoA breaches and corrective actions.
• % coverage of mandatory training.
• Value of exceptions identified in procurement spot checks. Monitor monthly and escalate adverse trends.
Governance maturity roadmap (example)
Level 1 – Ad hoc: basic policies exist but inconsistent application.
Level 2 – Defined: policies and SOPs in place; selective controls operating.
Level 3 – Managed: consistent controls, monthly monitoring and regular training.
Level 4 – Optimised: automated controls, integrated systems, continuous assurance and proactive risk management. Provide actions to move from current to target level.

Chapter 2 — General Accounting & Coding Structure
Purpose & scope (Overview)
Sets out the accounting and coding structure to ensure accurate financial management of country offices in line with SCI accounting policies and procedures. References the finance pages on OneNet and notes that additional guidance can be provided by regional finance teams and SCI centre finance. Emphasises that expenditures must be incurred and coded in line with programme requirements and donor regulations and must be traceable to award reporting cycles/end of awards. Budget holders must code budgets by both SCI chart of account and donor expense analysis, and must identify the complete budget code when authorising purchase requests, commitments, programme advances or payments.
2.1 Overview
Financial management of country offices is governed by SCI accounting policies and procedures available on OneNet; regional and centre finance teams provide additional guidance.
2.2 Management accounting structure (coding structure)
Expenditure must be accurately coded to enable award reporting. Transactions entered into the finance system require specific coded fields called attributes. There are three attribute types: account code, posting attributes (direct dimensions), and reporting attributes (indirect dimensions). Procedures to create attributes in Agresso are in the Data Maintenance Manual on OneNet.
2.2.1 Attributes (codes)
Defines attributes as coded fields required when entering transactions. Three types:
• Account code
• Posting attributes (direct dimensions)
• Reporting attributes (indirect dimensions)
Reference to Data Maintenance Manual for creation of new attributes.
2.2.2 Account codes and rules
The chart of accounts is standardised across SCI and available in Agresso/OneNet. Account codes fall into three groups:
• Balance sheet codes: 4-digit codes 1000–2999.
• Income codes: 4-digit codes 3000–3999.
• Expenditure codes: 4-digit codes 4000–9999.
Each account code is linked to an account rule which enforces which other codes/attributes must be completed when entering a transaction (example: posting bank payment with account code 1000 requires specific bank account code). The chart of accounts is the master listing (refer to Agresso report / OneNet).
2.2.3 Posting attributes (direct dimensions)
A posting attribute is a required code entered in addition to the account code (examples: cost centre, source of funds). Up to eight codes may be required (including account code). Standard attributes, digit conventions and requirement summary:
Attribute — Digits — Convention — Required
• Account — 4 — Harmonised SCI chart of accounts — All transactions.
• Cost Centre — 5 — CCC00 — All transactions.
• Project — 7 — CCC0000 — All income & expenditure transactions.
• Source of funds (SOF) — 8 — MMM00000 — All income & expenditure and some balance sheet transactions.
• Donor expenditure analysis (DEA) — 6 (or 5 for pre-Apr2013) — 000000 unique code allocated by Agresso — All expenditure transactions.
• Analysis — Varies — See below — Only required for certain account codes.
• Lclcoa — Varies — Legal requirement in country, if needed — Only if legal requirement in country (rare).
• Country — 3 — CCC — All transactions (auto-populates based on cost centre).
Notes on conventions: CCC = country ISO code; MMM = member ISO code; '0' = placeholder digits to be assigned. Each posting attribute identifies aspects of the transaction (origin, award/funding source, project). Combining codes or using them as free codes (except lclcoa if legally required) is prohibited unless approved by Director of IP Finance.
Cost centre
• A cost centre allocates costs and normally represents a physical location (main office or sub-office).
• 5-digit code: first 3 digits = country ISO; last 2 digits = office identifier (e.g., CCC00 = main office; CCC01 = sub-office).
• Example: 03100 Azerbaijan main office; 03101 Ganja sub-office.
Project
• 7-digit code identifying a specific activity/group of activities; required for all income & expenditure transactions.
• Examples: 0047112 Kandahar working children; 0500770 Flood emergency relief (Bangladesh).
• Definitions: project = an activity within a sub-thematic area in a specific context (lowest level of activity you wish to track). Themes = programmatic response areas (education, nutrition); sub-theme further breakdown (basic education, secondary, etc.). Example: 'inter-ethnic kindergartens in Kosovo'.
Source of funds (SOF)
• 8-digit code: first 3 digits identify member country providing funds, other five uniquely identify the award.
• Required for all income & expenditure and some balance sheet transactions.
• Example: 84044775 Creating Brighter Futures (source country: US).
• Note: AMS retains member legacy SOF but AMS and SCI may both assign codes — use SCI SOF code for coding transactions.
Donor expenditure analysis (DEA)
• Unique 6-digit code (or 5-digit for pre-April 2013 awards still live) to identify donor budget categories for reporting to donors and members.
• Required for all expenditure transactions.
• DEA is specific to each SOF and is loaded into Agresso via an Excel tool. Agresso rules link DEA to the correct SOF preventing posting to wrong SOF when correct DEA used.
• DEAs are based on donor report codes (DRCs) from fund summaries — giving reporting detail the country office must provide to donors.
Analysis (variable field)
• Analysis field varies depending on account code chosen. The field name changes (e.g., account 5030 Vehicles → analysis field named 'vehicle' and must enter vehicle code; account 4920 Relocation expenses → analysis field named 'staff' and must enter staff ID number).
• Below is a list of common analysis types with coding convention and examples:
Analysis attribute table (selected types)
• SubAward — Identifies partner award; used for partner payments/advances — 5 digits — Created in AMS. Example: Food for Kids.
• Staff — Identifies SCI staff via Staff ID — format CCC000000000 (first 3 digits country where staff paid; remaining digits assigned by HR system; must NOT be 8 digits to avoid conflict with suppliers). Created in HR system. Example: John Paul Brown.
• Customer — Non-donor debtor (e.g., tenants or SC member) — CCC00000 (first 3 digits represent country where Customer created) — Created in Agresso. Example: Paul John Brown / Save the Children UK.
• Supplier — Organisational creditor or third-party receiving payment — CCC00000 for domestic (first 3 digits = country where supplier created) or 998 for international suppliers; remaining digits assigned consecutively. Created in Agresso. Example: Hotel Everest.
• Vehicle — Fixed asset vehicle code — CCCV0000 (V denotes vehicle) — Example: Nissan 4x4 XTrail.
• Property — Owned/leased property code — CCCP0000 (P denotes property) — Example: SCI Centre Floor 1.
• Other asset — Asset that is neither vehicle nor property — CCCA0000 (A denotes asset) — Example: Dell computer model DCCY S/N: 3839V2J.
• Bank — Bank account used in bank transactions — CCC00 (first 3 digits = country where bank located; remaining 2 digits for account number) — Include bank name, account number and currency. Example: Nepal 90744158 Barclays NPR current.
• Petty cash — Cash holding/petty cash fund — CCC000 (first 5 digits represent cost centre where cash holding is based, followed by 1 digit) — Created in Agresso. Example: Nepal Biratnagar NPR.
• Country — Automatically populated based on cost centre.
Country attribute
• The country attribute auto-populates from the cost centre and shows where the transaction was generated — useful for intra-country/centre transactions.
Reference manuals
• For instructions on using attributes in Agresso transaction entry, refer to the Introduction to Agresso FMS and General Ledger Manual on OneNet — section 'Registering / Posting a standard journal to the General Ledger'.
2.2.4 Reporting attributes (indirect dimensions)
• Reporting attributes are used only for reporting and are linked to posting attributes. They are not manually completed when recording a transaction. Example: 'donor' is linked to SOF code so reports can show donor once the standard posting attributes are used.
• Reporting attributes examples by posting attribute:
– Account → Income/expense, local account (for regulatory purposes).
– Cost centre → Unit/Country, Department (region), Division (e.g., emergencies, international programming).
– Project → Theme (programmatic response), Subtheme, Lead project (links related projects).
– Source of funds → Donor (ECHO, USAID, DFID), Donor type (UN agency, etc.).
• Details of reporting attributes: see Introduction to Agresso FMS and General Ledger Manual (section Reporting attributes - Indirect Dimensions).
2.2.5 Process for setting up new codes
• New codes are required when operations change (new location needing cost centre, new staff needing staff number, new supplier, etc.).
• Follow the approval procedures before requesting new code set-up. Submit evidence to country finance director with completed 'FMS data maintenance form'.
• Distribution of requests: centrally set up codes = Director of Financial Governance or delegate; locally set up codes = country finance director/regional finance director/Director of Financial Governance or delegate (depending on item and location).
• Centrally set up codes include: account code, cost centre, strategic initiative, country, currency, tax code, bank, petty cash.
Approval requirements summary (attribute → approval process)
• Account: Request from country/regional finance director using FMS form; Director of Financial Governance must approve new account codes.
• Cost centre: Approval required from Director of IP Finance.
• SubAward: Approved via award management process (see Award Management procedures on OneNet).
• Programmatic project / Source of funds / Donor expenditure analysis: Budget approved via Award Management process (see Award Management procedures on OneNet).
• Non-thematic project: Non-humanitarian — business case to regional finance director explaining need; Humanitarian — request to Director of IP Finance.
• Lead project: If not impacting existing mapping: request to country director via FMS form; if it impacts mapping: request to Director of IP Finance via FMS form.
• Budget holder: Delegation of authority process.
• Strategic initiative: Authorised as part of annual planning; approved by Director of IP Finance.
• Staff: Set up via HR recruitment procedures — national staff set up at country level; international staff at centre; allocated when staff join and uploaded into Agresso as part of payroll.
• Supplier: Logistics responsible for selection; follow procurement guidelines for supplier selection and approval.
• Customer: Rare; set up at country level with prior approval of country finance director and supported by contract (e.g., sub-lessee).
• Vehicle / Asset / Property: Capital asset purchases pre-approved through sign-off of annual capital budget; property purchases require prior approval of SCI CFO (country offices should not buy property); rental contracts require prior approval from appropriate budget holder. Refer to fixed assets section for detail.
• Bank: Authorisation for bank account opening per scheme of delegation; see bank section for detailed procedure.
• Petty cash: Approval required from country director and country finance director; see cash section.
• Local chart of account: Authorisation from country finance director.
• Country: Approved by SCI Board via strategic/financial plan (humanitarian) or Director of IP Finance (humanitarian).
• Currency: Approved by Treasurer and Director of Financial Governance (FMS form to Treasury & Director with evidence e.g., approval of new country or official notification of new currency).
• Tax codes: If related to new country, follow new country approval process; if existing country, approval by regional finance director and Director of Financial Governance.
• User: HR procedures for recruitment/change of duties; FMS data maintenance form to be completed by line manager explaining need for system access.
Reference: Procedures to create new attributes in Agresso are in the Data Maintenance Manual on OneNet.
2.3 Recording transactions — Responsibilities
• Country Finance Director: ensures transactions are entered into Agresso daily and appropriate controls exist.
• Sub-office managers: ensure transactions are entered into GLACOS daily at sub-offices and that spreadsheets are reviewed before submission.
• Finance staff: check entries are complete, accurate, authorised, auditable before entry; ensure referencing is accurate and complete.
Definitions & explanations
• Transactions are recorded in Agresso or offline spreadsheets. Where Agresso is available it should be used. Refer to Introduction to Agresso FMS and General Ledger Manual (Registering/Posting standard journal to GL).
Offline spreadsheets
• HROS (Human Resources Offline Spreadsheet): record new staff details, changes, closures.
• GLACOS (General Ledger and Cash Offline Spreadsheet): record transactions affecting bank/cash accounts and non-bank/cash account corrections/reallocations.
• GLACOS is uploaded into Agresso as part of month-end processing resulting in posting to the general ledger.
• Data Validation (DV) spreadsheet: contains all MAS codes used in Agresso and ensures only valid MAS combinations are entered into GLACOS.
• For procedures on GLACOS/DV, refer to Offline Spreadsheet Completing and Posting Manuals on OneNet.
2.3.1 Regularity of recording transactions
• Transactions should be recorded in Agresso/offline spreadsheets regularly — normally daily — to keep accounting records up to date. It is unacceptable to wait until month-end to update Agresso for earlier-in-the-month activity.
2.3.2 Controls
To ensure completeness, accuracy, and authorisation:
• Register transactions first in Agresso and post only once authorised.
• Segregation of duties between registering and posting staff (refer to Section 1.2.1).
• Staff posting transactions must verify completeness, accuracy, authorisation, and auditable supporting documentation (see section 2.4.6 for required supporting documents).
• Offline spreadsheets must be reviewed and signed off by an appropriate person (e.g., sub-office manager) before submission to country office. Review checks must include:
o all transactions have supporting documentation; o transaction details are accurate (amount, date, description); o coding is accurate; o all transactions included in spreadsheet.
• Country finance team must review offline spreadsheets prior to import to identify obvious errors/omissions.
2.33 Control Accounts
• For all control accounts (except Programme Advances), the date of liquidation of the balance should match the date of issue to avoid exchange rate differences and ensure correct expenditure dates. Example: partner advance issued 10/04/14 and returned 10/05/14 should be liquidated with returned expenses matching date of issue (10/04/14).
• For Programme Advances see section 6.6.2.
2.34 Clear Audit Trail
• Auditors must be able to trace a transaction from Agresso/GLACOS to supporting documentation and vice versa. To enable this:
1. Every transaction entered into Agresso or GLACOS must have a voucher reference number: Agresso — voucher reference number = first item recorded in text field; GLACOS — in voucher number field.
2. Every Agresso entry's transaction number must be noted on supporting voucher.
3. Supporting documentation must be filed.
2.4 Payments — Key points
• Expenditure must not be incurred before a budget is authorised (either a funds summary with fully executed donor agreement or member pre-authorisation), except emergency code set up temporarily for an authorised emergency (International Programs Director can authorise up to $1m before member commitments).
• Staff must only incur expenses aligned with programme objectives, necessary and good value for money.
• Commitments/payments must be signed only when accompanied by a complete budget code, auditable documentation and budget holder authorisation per scheme of delegation.
• Commitments/payments for a single order must not be split to avoid DoA limits or procurement procedures.
• Authorisation must be on original invoices — not statements or photocopies — to reduce duplicate payments risk.
• SCI must not make payments on behalf of employees except business expenses.
• Payments in advance covering more than one year (e.g., prepaid rent) require prior authorisation from Director IP Finance.
Payments — Responsibilities
• Budget holder: authorise commitments/expenditure; ensure no spending commitment longer than appropriate; identify accruals & prepayments (at minimum quarterly).
• Finance: make payments; account correctly for expenditure, accruals, prepayments; check procurement procedures followed; file audit documentation; check donor regulation adherence; advise on policies; highlight deviations to management.

Chapter 3 — Sub-awards to partners
Purpose & objective
Sub-awards (also referred to as sub-grants, partner awards or partner agreements) are mechanisms by which SCI transfers resources — cash, commodities or in-kind assistance — to external partner organisations to deliver agreed activities that further SCI’s programme objectives. The purpose of this chapter is to set out mandatory controls and minimum standards for awarding, managing, monitoring and closing sub-awards so that: funds are used for authorised purposes; donor and statutory requirements are met; partner capacity is assessed and supported; programmatic and financial performance are evidenced and reconciled; financial risk (including fraud) is mitigated; and both SCI and donors have auditable documentation for all partner relationships. No transfer of resources may take place without a signed sub-award agreement and appropriate pre-award approvals.
Scope
This chapter applies to all SCI country offices, sub-offices and any SCI unit that manages awards that involve transfers of resources to third-party organisations. It covers: partner selection and due diligence, pre-award capacity assessment, the sub-award agreement and amendments, financial and programmatic monitoring, accounting and GL treatment, audit and assurance requirements, partner capacity-building, mitigation of financial and reputational risk, close-out procedures and records retention. It excludes standard vendor procurement for goods and services where suppliers are paid through Accounts Payable under procurement policy (unless the contract is structured as a sub-award).
Key definitions & core recognition principle
• Sub-award / sub-grant / partner award / sub-awardee: an agreement between SCI and an external organisation in which SCI transfers resources (cash or in-kind) for the partner to implement activities or provide services that advance SCI objectives. The partner remains an external legal entity and acts under an agreed scope of work and budget.
• Pre-award assessment / capacity assessment: financial systems and fiscal management review performed by Finance (with inputs from Programme, Legal or others) to assess partner ability to manage funds and comply with donor and SCI rules prior to award.
• Sub-award agreement: a signed document (contract) containing terms & conditions, budget, payment schedule, reporting requirements, audit obligations, liability clauses and termination/close-out provisions. No payments without a signed agreement.
• Sub-award amendment: an authorised, signed change to the sub-award agreement (scope, budget, duration, payment terms) agreed between SCI and the partner.
• Advance: cash paid upfront to a partner (recorded to Partners payments – advances 5200) that must be supported by a subsequent financial report and/or reconciliation.
• Core principle: ensure full documentary evidence, pre-award checks and risk-based monitoring are in place before funds transfer; align programmatic and financial reporting; retain audit trail to enable donor and statutory scrutiny.
Core principles / Key principles
1. No transfer of funds or in-kind assistance without a signed sub-award agreement.
2. Conduct an evidence-based pre-award financial and capacity assessment for every partner prior to award.
3. Use a risk-based approach for monitoring frequency and depth; higher value or higher-risk partners require more frequent and deeper monitoring.
4. Maintain segregation of duties — partner selection, technical oversight, financial approval and payment execution must involve distinct roles.
5. Ensure programmatic deliverables and financial reports reconcile and that programme staff sign off on programmatic acceptability before finance authorises final payments.
6. Keep full documentary support (signed agreement/amendments, capacity assessment, financial reports, bank reconciliations, monitoring visit reports, audit reports) in partner file.
7. Implement partner capacity strengthening where gaps are identified; monitor delivery of capacity-building plans.
Governance & responsibilities (RACI)
Country Director (CD): accountable for overall partnership strategy and oversight of sub-award procedures; approves and signs (where in DoA) partner monitoring visit plan; approves high-risk partner engagements and material amendments.
Country Finance Director (CFD): owner of financial controls for sub-awards; responsible for pre-award financial capacity assessments, ensuring payments only occur against signed agreements and approved reports, oversight of financial monitoring, implementation of partner capacity building plans, ensuring accurate accounting and reconciliation, and closing sub-awards only after all conditions met.
Programme Manager / Project Manager: responsible for partner selection inputs, programmatic due diligence, ensuring programmatic monitoring is carried out, certifying that programmatic deliverables have been met and that programmatic and financial reports are consistent.
Procurement / Legal: provide inputs to partner vetting (where relevant), review contract terms and ensure compliance with donor and organisational procurement rules.
Payments Officer / Treasury: executes payments only after receipt of payment authorisation and evidence of signed agreement; maintains bank advices and ensures maker-checker controls on payment execution.
Monitoring & Evaluation (M&E) / Partnership Manager: supports programmatic monitoring, contributes to partner capacity assessments and ensures learning and reporting requirements are met.
Partner / Sub-awardee: responsible for implementing activities, maintaining financial records, producing timely programmatic and financial reports, cooperating with monitoring and audit, and returning unused funds or interest as per agreement.
Systems & security / Reference to systems
• Use the approved contract template and sub-award agreement stored on OneNet.
• Financial transactions for partner payments and advances must be recorded in Agresso (or approved GL system) following the ‘Payments to Partners’ procedure (see Introduction to Agresso FMS and General Ledger Manual — section ‘Payments to Partners’ on OneNet).
• Maintain partner files in a secure, access-controlled repository (electronic or physical) with version control for agreements, amendments, capacity assessments, monitoring reports and audit documents.
• Ensure sensitive partner financial data is treated as confidential and protected according to data protection policies — encrypt files in transit and at rest, limit access to authorised staff only.
Partner selection & pre-award requirements
Before any sub-award is issued, the following must be completed and evidenced in the partner file:
1. Needs & fit assessment: programme team documents rationale for partnering, scope of work, expected deliverables, budget estimate and alignment with SCI objectives.
2. Partner due diligence: verify partner legal status, governance structure, registration, tax status, past performance, and reputation checks (incl. adverse media where appropriate).
3. Financial systems & fiscal management capacity assessment (pre-award assessment): finance conducts a documented review of partner accounting systems, internal controls, financial reporting capacity, bank arrangements, segregation of duties, procurement and asset management processes, historical audits and any prior donor-funded experience. Record findings, risk rating and recommended mitigation actions or capacity-strengthening measures.
4. Risk assessment & mitigation: use a standard risk matrix that evaluates award value, fiduciary risk, partner history, geography/security context and donor requirements. Establish monitoring intensity, reporting frequency and any special clauses (e.g., restrictive use of advances, escrow, retention).
5. Approval: award is approved per Delegation of Authority (DoA) and documented. Evidence of approval and assigned budget codes must be in the partner file.
Partner training and orientation to award regulations (3.1.1)
SCI must ensure partners understand contractual obligations, donor rules and reporting formats. Finance, in coordination with the country award management contact and programme staff, shall:
• Provide partner induction/orientation covering financial reporting templates, allowable costs, documentation requirements, bank and cash handling expectations, advance management, procurement rules, asset treatment and audit obligations.
• Issue clear contact points and escalation channels for financial queries.
• Provide written guidance/manuals (financial procedures for partners) and example report templates.
• Document attendance and materials provided; include sign-off by partner acknowledging understanding of rules.
Partner capacity building (3.1.2)
Where pre-award assessment identifies weaknesses, Finance and Programme must agree a capacity-building plan with the partner prior to or as a condition of award. Elements include:
• Targeted trainings on accounting, record keeping, bank reconciliation, cash management, procurement and donor compliance.
• On-the-job coaching during the first months of implementation and hand-holding with financial reporting.
• Deliverables and timelines for closing identified control gaps, with evidence to be provided to SCI.
• Link capacity-building to conditional release of funds (e.g., reduced advance until controls evidenced).
• Maintain a log of capacity-building activities and monitor progress with defined milestones.
Bank & payment considerations
• Partners should use banks that SCI considers reputable and where bank controls and traceability are adequate. If a partner’s bank is not acceptable, SCI should advise the partner to move funds to an acceptable bank or propose alternative payment mechanisms.
• Payment method (bank transfer, mobile money, cash) must be agreed in the sub-award. Cash is discouraged and only permitted under exceptional circumstances with strict controls documented in the agreement and supported by the monitoring plan.
• For bank transfers, ensure partner bank details are verified and changes to bank accounts require written, authorised confirmation and additional verification steps (e.g., independent confirmation call to known signatory).
Sub-award agreement & amendments
• All sub-awards require a signed sub-award agreement using the approved SCI template. The agreement must define scope, budget, payment schedule, reporting requirements, audit requirements, asset provisions, liability & indemnity clauses, termination and close-out procedures, and any donor-specific conditions.
• Payments cannot be made without the signed agreement.
• Any change to scope, budget, schedule or terms requires a signed sub-award amendment. Inform donors/central office as required by donor rules prior to implementing material amendments.
• Retain signed agreement and any amendments in the partner file; index and reference in GL journal narratives.
Payments — pre-payment checks (summary from key points)
Before authorising any payment to a partner, Finance must confirm and document that:
• There is an appropriately authorised sub-award agreement (stored on OneNet).
• Pre-award partner capacity assessments are complete and any required mitigation actions or capacity-building measures are recorded.
• Payment is authorised by a person with delegated authority under the scheme of delegation (DoA).
• Previous financial reports from the partner have been submitted and accepted; programme manager has confirmed that programmatic deliverables tie to the financial report.
• For advances: an updated cash plan / quarterly cash-flow projection has been received and reviewed.
• For final/retention payments: all deliverables, audit reports (if required) and reconciliations are completed and approved.

Monitoring — financial & programmatic (3.1.4)
Monitoring is an essential control. Financial and programmatic monitoring must be complementary and documented. Minimum monitoring activities include:
• Regular partner visits: at least quarterly as a minimum standard, with frequency determined by risk (award value, partner history, pre-award assessment findings, security context). • Partner financial monitoring visit plan: prepared by Finance and signed off by the Country Director. The plan must state frequency (quarterly/biannual), scope (cash, procurement, assets, records), sample sizes and escalation routes.
• Desk-top reviews / audits: Finance must review submitted financial reports, bank reconciliations, cash-on-hand statements and supporting documents. Findings must be documented and corrective actions requested where necessary.
• Analysis of cash balances & cash flows: partners receiving advances must submit a detailed project financial plan for the total project and an annual cash-flow projection by quarter. • Reimbursements reviews: finance must check that partner expenditures are allowable, auditable and in line with the budget; that programmatic and financial reports match; and that supporting evidence is adequate. Seek clarifications and corrective actions when necessary.

• Partner audits / external assurance: where donor requires audits, ensure audit is conducted and final report is on file; where audit is not required, perform periodic desk reviews and spot audits as determined by risk.
• Monitoring tools: use the three standard worksheets in Award Management guidance (refer to OneNet) as part of monitoring and document any issues requiring escalation to regional/central management.
Monitoring — detailed procedures & evidence

Chapter 5 — Fixed Assets (Capital Assets)
Purpose & objective

Fixed assets represent a significant investment, carry heightened compliance, audit and donor risk, and require robust lifecycle controls from acquisition through disposal. This chapter establishes a comprehensive framework for identifying, approving, recording, safeguarding, depreciating, monitoring and disposing of fixed assets and non-capital assets managed by Save the Children International (SCI).

The objectives of this policy are to ensure that:
• assets are correctly classified as capital or non-capital in line with SCI policy, accounting standards and donor requirements;
• capital assets are capitalised, depreciated and reported accurately in the financial system;
• donor-funded assets are managed strictly in accordance with donor and member regulations;
• asset registers provide a complete, accurate and auditable record of all assets owned or managed by SCI;
• assets are safeguarded against loss, misuse, theft or unauthorised disposal;
• asset acquisitions, transfers and disposals are properly authorised and documented; and
• financial statements, donor reports and audits are supported by a full, transparent audit trail.

Given SCI’s limited availability of unrestricted funds, the volume of capital assets at country office level is expected to be low. However, the control standards described in this chapter apply regardless of volume and must be followed consistently.
Scope

This chapter applies to:
• all tangible fixed assets (capital assets) owned by SCI;
• all non-capital assets recorded in asset registers for control and logistics purposes;
• assets purchased with unrestricted (general) funds;
• assets purchased with restricted (award/donor) funds;
• assets managed by SCI but legally owned by donors, members or third parties;
• asset acquisition, capitalisation, depreciation, revaluation, transfer and disposal; and
• all country offices, sub-offices and field locations.

This chapter does not cover:
• inventory or consumables;
• intangible assets other than computer software;
• assets purchased and owned directly by partners under sub-awards (unless temporarily held by SCI).
Key definitions & asset classification principles

Capital asset (fixed asset) — SCI defines a capital asset as tangible property or equipment that meets all of the following criteria:
• purchased using SCI unrestricted funds;
• has a total unit cost of USD 5,000 or more, inclusive of taxes, shipping, duties, installation and other costs necessary to bring the asset into use; and
• has an estimated useful life of more than one year.

Capital assets are:
• recorded on the balance sheet;
• capitalised at cost; and
• depreciated over their estimated useful economic life.

Assets purchased using award (restricted) funds are not capitalised, regardless of value, because legal ownership rests with the donor rather than SCI.
Non-capital assets

SCI recognises non-capital assets, which are items of strategic or operational importance that do not meet the full definition of a capital asset. Non-capital assets:
• are recorded in the asset register for accountability, logistics and safeguarding;
• are expensed in full in the year of acquisition;
• are not capitalised or depreciated; and
• remain subject to donor, member and asset management rules.

An item is classified as a non-capital asset if any of the following apply:
Value-based criteria
• unit cost between USD 1,000 and USD 5,000 (e.g., HF radios, generators, computer equipment);

Strategic importance criteria (even if value is below USD 5,000)
• expected useful life of more than one year and powered by electricity or fuel and has a serial number (e.g., laptops, desktops, VHF radios);
• incurs ongoing running costs that require monitoring and control (e.g., mobile phones, satellite phones);
• value exceeds USD 5,000 but purchased with restricted (donor) funds (e.g., donor-funded vehicles);
• defined as an asset by the donor, regardless of value (e.g., digital cameras funded by a donor).
Core recognition principle

• Capital assets: recognised on the balance sheet and depreciated over their useful life.
• Non-capital assets: expensed immediately but recorded and tracked in the asset register.
• Donor-funded assets: tracked for control and reporting but not capitalised.

5.1 Capital and non-capital assets

• licences;
• insurance;

Chapter 6 — Debtors, Creditors and Bad Debts
Purpose & scope
Purpose: ensure timely, accurate recognition, monitoring, collection and settlement of receivables and payables; consistent identification, provisioning and write-off of doubtful or irrecoverable amounts; preservation of donor/member compliance, transparency and full auditability.
Scope: applies to all receivable and payable activity across country offices, sub-offices and field missions, including donor income receivables, member and inter-office recharges, partner and sub-award recoverables and advances, tax and statutory receivables (VAT, GST, withholding), supplier and partner payables, accrued liabilities, employee advances, provisions and write-offs. Covers on-system processing in Agresso/GLACOS and any formally approved off-system arrangements. Applies to all staff involved in contracting, invoicing, collections, payments, accounting, reconciliation, reporting and approval.
Key definitions & classifications
• Debtor / Accounts Receivable (AR): amount due to SCI from an external party (donor, member, partner, customer, government).
• Creditor / Accounts Payable (AP): amount SCI owes to a supplier, partner, employee or statutory authority.
• Provision for doubtful debts: contra-asset representing the estimated portion of receivables expected to be uncollectible.
• Bad debt / write-off: receivable determined to be irrecoverable and removed from the ledger following approval.
• Ageing bands (typical): Current (0 days), 1–30, 31–60, 61–90, 90+ days (local policies may refine).
• Transaction currency: currency of invoice or entitlement.
• Functional/local currency: currency in which the office reports its accounts.
Entitlement & recognition rules
Recognise a receivable only when SCI has an unconditional right to consideration, supported by enforceable documentation (e.g., signed contract or award letter, delivery or performance certificate, donor notification, member acceptance). For staged or performance-based arrangements, recognise receivables only for completed and evidenced milestones. Partial recognition requires documented judgement, reference to contract terms and donor-specific rules. Recognition decisions and assumptions must be retained in the accounting file and be auditable.
End-to-end receivable lifecycle — process & responsibilities
Trigger / entitlement: Programme or Budget Holder with Contracts/Grants team confirms entitlement.
Invoice creation: Finance (invoicing) prepares invoice using standard template with full coding (SOF, cost centre, project, DEA), narrative and supporting documents.
Approval & dispatch: Invoice approved per delegated authority and dispatched; dispatch date recorded (Finance/Budget Holder).
Recording: Finance ledger team posts invoice in Agresso/GLACOS and attaches evidence.
Monitoring & collection: Credit control with Budget Holder follow up, negotiate and escalate as required.
Receipt & posting: Treasury/Cashier/Finance posts receipts, matches to invoices and reconciles bank.
Investigation & resolution: Budget Holder, Finance, Treasury and Legal investigate disputes or delays.
Provision & write-off: Finance prepares provisioning and write-off papers; Country Finance Director (CFD) and approving authority approve per Scheme of Delegation (DoA).
Invoice standard — minimum fields & supporting documents
Mandatory fields: sequential invoice number; invoice date; due date; currency; customer name and address; SCI coding (SOF, project, DEA, cost centre); clear description referencing delivery or performance; gross amount, tax/withholding and net amount; payment instructions and bank details; contact details for queries; contract or award reference.
Attachments: signed contract or award letter, delivery or performance certificates, timesheets, acceptance letters, partner or member acknowledgements and any donor-specific evidence.
Controls: enforce sequential numbering without gaps, authorised signatory approval, and mandatory attachment of scanned documents to the voucher.
Invoice numbering, control & retention
Maintain a monthly invoice log; invoice numbers must never be reused or altered. All issued invoices must be stored electronically and attached to the accounting entry. Retain invoices and supporting documents in line with SCI retention policy, donor requirements and local law (typically a minimum of 6–7 years, or longer where required).
Credit approval, onboarding & terms
Onboard new counterparties with identity verification, bank detail validation and classification (member, donor, partner, commercial). Obtain credit references where relevant for commercial partners. Record agreed payment terms, penalties and credit limits. Standard credit terms are typically 30 days net unless otherwise approved. Deviations require written approval by the Budget Holder and Finance, with higher thresholds approved per DoA. Maintain a credit limit register and consult Treasury or Centre for high-value or cross-member exposure.
Collections workflow, escalation & logging
Indicative timeline: T+0 issue and record invoice; T+7 friendly reminder; T+14 second reminder and phone call; T+30 formal demand and escalation to Budget Holder; T+45–60 negotiated payment plan documented in writing; T+60–90 senior management review; T+90+ escalate to Treasury/Legal, increase provision and consider write-off; >T+180 consider write-off per approval. All contact attempts must be logged with date, medium, contact person, outcome and next action.
Customer disputes & resolution
Disputes must be flagged in the sub-ledger with an assigned owner, target resolution date and documented root cause. Resolution options include credit notes, rebates, deferred payment plans or arbitration. Donor-funded invoices must not be adjusted without donor or member approval where required by award terms.
Ageing analysis, reporting & month-end checks
Produce a monthly aged debtor report by ageing band, SOF, project and customer. Minimum columns: customer; invoice number; invoice and due dates; currency; invoice amount; outstanding balance; age in days; ageing band; project/DEA; Budget Holder; dispute flag; provision rate and amount; notes. Include pivots by SOF, top 10 debtors, Days Sales Outstanding (DSO) and trend analysis. Reconcile AR control account to sub-ledger, ageing and bank receipts. Investigate and document reconciling items older than 30 days. Reports must be reviewed and signed off by the preparer and an independent reviewer. FX revaluation is performed per Chapter 14.
Provision policy, methodology & governance
Objective is timely and consistent recognition of expected credit losses. Methodology and assumptions must be documented and reviewed monthly. Acceptable approaches include: (A) specific identification for large or high-risk balances; (B) ageing-based rate matrix for the remaining portfolio; (C) Expected Credit Loss overlays for significant counterparties or country risk. Both qualitative factors (solvency, disputes, country risk) and quantitative data must be considered. Changes to rates or methodology require documented approval and periodic (quarterly or annual) review.
Illustrative ageing-rate matrix
Current: 0%; 1–30 days: 2%; 31–60 days: 5%; 61–90 days: 20%; 90+ days: 50%. Individual assessment thresholds (e.g., invoices above USD 10,000) must be defined in local policy. Final rates are set locally with CFD approval.
Provision calculation procedure & movement
Extract the ageing report; apply ageing rates; identify and calculate individually assessed provisions; total provision equals ageing-based provisions plus individually assessed amounts. Prepare a movement schedule showing opening balance, charges, releases, utilisation/write-offs and closing balance. Support calculations with assumptions and evidence, obtain approvals and post journals accordingly.
Journal examples — receivables, provisions & write-offs
• Recognise receivable: Dr Debtors / Cr Income.
• Receipt of cash: Dr Bank / Cr Debtors.
• Provision charge: Dr Bad Debt Expense / Cr Provision for Doubtful Debts.
• Write-off using provision: Dr Provision (and Dr Bad Debt Expense if insufficient) / Cr Debtors.
• Write-off without provision: Dr Bad Debt Expense / Cr Debtors.
FX impacts are posted separately per Chapter 14. Journals must reference supporting schedules, preparer, reviewer and attachments, and use consistent nominal codes and narratives.
Write-off policy, evidence & authorisation
Write-offs require evidence of exhaustive collection efforts, including contact logs, final demand letters, bank confirmations, legal opinion where relevant, Budget Holder statement and donor/member confirmation if required. Approval thresholds must align with the SCI Scheme of Delegation (e.g., Finance Manager ≤ USD 1,000; CFD USD 1,001–10,000; Country Director/Regional/Centre above). Signed approval memos must be attached to vouchers.
Credit notes & corrective adjustments
Credit notes must reference the original invoice, follow sequential numbering and be authorised by the Budget Holder and Finance. They must be posted promptly, communicated to the counterparty and reflected in donor reports where required. Maintain a credit note register and reconcile to original invoices.
Inter-member & inter-office recharges
Use standard recharge templates and obtain acceptance evidence (email, SDP or signature). Apply consistent timing (monthly or quarterly), coding standards and reversal codes to neutralise internal flows. Reconcile inter-member balances monthly and escalate unresolved differences. Acceptance evidence must be retained for donor audit.
Partner and sub-award accounting & recoverables
Record partner advances in partner-specific advance accounts. Recognise recoveries only when substantiated by approved partner financial and technical reports. Ensure sub-award coding maps correctly to donor DEA and fund summaries. Reconcile partner accounts regularly and retain full supporting documentation.
Foreign currency & FX treatment
Record receivables and payables in original transaction currency. Perform month-end translation and revaluation per Chapter 14, posting FX gains or losses to designated accounts. Minimise open FX exposure near period close and document material FX risks and Treasury mitigation actions.
Creditors / payables controls (procure-to-pay)
Standard flow: purchase requisition → purchase order (where required) → goods receipt note or service approval → supplier invoice → two- or three-way matching → payment. Validate supplier master data, perform duplicate invoice checks and segregate requisition, approval and payment roles. Reconcile AP control account to sub-ledger monthly and investigate stale balances.
Supplier statements & stale liabilities
Obtain supplier statements regularly and reconcile to the AP sub-ledger within 30 days. Investigate unmatched or aged items over 90 days and obtain supplier confirmations for audit. Stale liabilities require investigation and formal authorisation before release or reversal.
Tax, withholding & statutory receivables/payables
Record VAT, GST and withholding receivables with valid tax invoices and certificates. Reconcile to statutory returns, track refund claims and retain acknowledgements. Ensure withholding treatment complies with local law and donor rules. Maintain a complete tax file with calculations and correspondence.
Controls & segregation of duties
Segregate, where feasible: invoice issuance; receipt posting and bank reconciliation; ageing, reconciliation and provisioning; collection communications; and approval of credit notes and write-offs. In small offices, apply compensating controls such as independent reviews, manager sign-offs, surprise checks and periodic reconciliations by a separate person. Conduct monthly independent reviews and quarterly CFD reviews of ageing and provisioning.
Month-end & year-end checklist
Prepare and attach: aged debtor schedules; GL control reconciliations; bank reconciliations; provision calculations and movements; action plans for items over 90 days; credit note and adjustment logs; write-off approval memos; FX revaluation workings; donor impact statements and accrual reversals. Complete before soft close and finalise before hard close.
Donor reporting & award compliance
Ensure recognition, adjustments and write-offs comply with award terms and fund summaries. Obtain donor approvals where required and post donor-impacting adjustments before hard close. Disclose material arrears or disputes to members per rules and segregate donor-restricted balances.
KPIs, dashboards & management reporting
Key indicators include DSO, collection rate, provision coverage, percentage of AR over 90 days and concentration of top 10 debtors. Dashboards should show top aged balances, trends in receivables and provisions, DSO trends and overdue invoices by Budget Holder or project. Use KPIs to prioritise collection actions.
Responsibilities & role expectations
Budget Holders approve invoices, resolve programme disputes and support collections. Finance teams raise invoices, post receipts, reconcile ledgers, calculate provisions and prepare write-off papers. CFD reviews and signs off reconciliations and material provisions. Country Director or Regional Office approves material write-offs and policy exceptions. Treasury and Centre advise on FX, central postings and complex recoveries.
Good practice & preventive measures
Issue invoices promptly (e.g., within five working days of entitlement), maintain clear signed contracts and fund summaries, codify credit terms and limits, perform periodic member reconciliations, automate reminders and ageing reports, validate vendors and banks, and involve Legal or Treasury early for complex cases.
Common pitfalls & mitigations
Late invoicing mitigated by invoicing calendars; weak documentation mitigated by mandatory attachments; incorrect coding mitigated by validation rules and training; donor non-compliance mitigated by pre-billing checks; insufficient provisioning mitigated by monthly reviews; weak segregation mitigated by compensating controls and audit sampling.
Audit evidence & working papers
Auditors expect aged debtor schedules and movements, GL to sub-ledger reconciliations, provisioning workbooks and rationale, credit notes and write-off approvals, collection correspondence, supplier confirmations, partner substantiation and donor communications. All working papers must be indexed and attached to the month-end pack.
Templates & annexes
Maintain version-controlled templates: standard invoice, invoice log, collection emails, write-off memo, provision calculation workbook, AR ageing export, supplier reconciliation template, Agresso/GLACOS CSV upload mapping and month-end AR reconciliation pack. Store centrally with controlled access.
Sample skeletons & formulas
Include standard invoice skeleton, staged collection email texts, write-off approval memo outline, provision formulas by ageing band, movement reconciliation and KPI formulas for DSO and collection rate.
Journal posting & control standards
All journals must clearly state the reason, reference supporting schedules, identify preparer and reviewer, and include attachments. Use consistent nominal codes and narratives and separate FX impact lines. Maintain a sample journal log for reference.
Systems & automation recommendations
Configure Agresso/GLACOS to enforce mandatory invoice fields and attachments, generate ageing reports, automate reminders, validate supplier and bank data, capture collection logs and AR ownership, and provide dashboards for aged balances and KPIs.
Implementation & roll-out checklist
Adopt and publish the policy; configure systems and templates; publish credit limits and DoA; train finance and Budget Holders; establish a monthly AR review meeting; centralise approved templates; pilot for one quarter, refine and roll out fully.
Governance, review & continuous improvement
Hold quarterly governance reviews covering provisioning methodology, major recoveries, write-offs and exceptions. Conduct annual policy reviews to align with donor and legal changes. Perform root-cause analysis on recurring delinquencies and implement corrective actions.

Chapter 7 — Fundraising and Locally Received Funds
Purpose & objective
This chapter establishes a comprehensive, mandatory and auditable framework governing all fundraising activities and the receipt, safeguarding, banking, accounting, stewardship and reporting of locally received funds. Fundraising income represents a high reputational, compliance and fraud-risk area because it involves public trust, donor intent, regulatory scrutiny and third-party handling. The objectives are to:
1. Ensure all funds are raised ethically, transparently and lawfully.
2. Ensure funds are applied strictly in accordance with donor intent, member agreements, SCI policies and local law.
3. Maintain full traceability from solicitation through receipt, banking, accounting, transfer and final use.
4. Protect donor confidence, organisational reputation and brand integrity.
5. Prevent loss, misappropriation, fraud, money laundering, sanctions breaches or misuse.
6. Support accurate, timely reporting to donors, members, regulators, auditors and SCI centre.
Scope (comprehensive)
This chapter applies to all locally raised income handled by country offices, sub-offices, field offices, prospect members and any entity acting on behalf of SCI, regardless of size, channel, currency or form. It covers: individual donations; corporate donations and sponsorships; foundations; community fundraising; events (ticket sales, raffles, auctions); digital and online giving; payroll giving; peer-to-peer fundraising; membership drives; legacy or bequests where locally administered; third-party or agency collections; and gifts-in-kind generated through fundraising activities. It also covers all associated banking, treasury, cash handling, accounting, reconciliation, donor stewardship, data management, inter-office transfers, programme allocation, translation into SOFs, remittances to members or centre, and donor/member reporting obligations.
Key definitions & core concepts
• Locally received funds: all monetary donations or financial contributions raised or collected within the country office’s jurisdiction, regardless of the ultimate programme, member or destination.
• Restricted funds: donations subject to donor-imposed conditions on purpose, geography, beneficiary, timing or usage.
• Unrestricted funds: donations available for general organisational use in line with SCI policy and approved priorities.
• Prospect member: a locally constituted fundraising entity operating under approved transitional governance arrangements on behalf of SCI.
• Gifts-in-Kind (GiK): non-cash goods or services donated as part of a fundraising activity or campaign.
• Core principle: all fundraising income must be traceable, properly authorised, promptly banked, correctly recorded, transparently reported and used strictly in accordance with donor intent and SCI policy.
Core principles / key control principles
1. Ethical fundraising aligned with SCI mission, values and branding standards.
2. Prior approval for all fundraising activities and mechanisms.
3. Prompt banking and prohibition of off-book holding of funds.
4. Segregation of duties between fundraising, receipting, banking, recording and reconciliation.
5. Accurate and timely recording in Agresso/GLACOS with full documentation.
6. Strict tracking and compliance with donor restrictions.
7. Robust AML, sanctions and fraud-prevention controls.
8. Full audit trail and documentary evidence for every transaction.
Governance & responsibilities (RACI)
• Country Director (CD): overall accountability for fundraising compliance, reputational risk, acceptance/refusal of high-risk donations, approval of major campaigns and escalation to centre.
• Country Finance Director (CFD): ownership of financial controls, banking arrangements, recording, reconciliations, fund tracking and reporting of locally raised income.
• Fundraising / Awards Lead: campaign planning, donor engagement, compliance with branding and fundraising protocols, submission of approvals and donor restriction details.
• Finance Officer: receipting controls, system recording, bank reconciliations, donation registers and month-end close.
• Independent Reviewer: review of reconciliations, exception logs and compliance with SOPs.
• Prospect Member Oversight (Centre/Region): governance, assurance, reconciliations and audits for prospect-member fundraising.
Authorisation & pre-approval requirements (detailed)
No fundraising campaign, event, solicitation or third-party arrangement may proceed without documented prior approval. Required approvals include: campaign concept note; fundraising method; branding and communications plan; budget and cost-to-raise analysis; banking and payment channels; cash-handling arrangements; third-party involvement; risk assessment and mitigation plan. Approvals must be signed by the CD and relevant functional leads. New fundraising mechanisms, high-value campaigns, use of third parties, cross-border fundraising or deviations from SCI standards require regional or centre approval with documented waivers where applicable.
Campaign planning & risk assessment
Each campaign must have a documented plan covering objectives, timeline, target audience, fundraising channels, expected income, donor restrictions, acknowledgement methods, banking pathways, staffing, segregation of duties, donor data handling, and reporting obligations. A risk register must explicitly assess fraud risk, cash exposure, AML risk, reputational risk, regulatory compliance, third-party dependency, IT/system risks and contingency plans. Event-based fundraising must additionally document ticket controls, float limits, refund policies, physical security, insurance coverage and daily close procedures.
Banking & cash management (detailed)
All locally raised funds must be deposited intact and promptly into designated SCI bank accounts. Cash must not be netted against expenses. Deposits must occur same day or within approved SLAs. Cash holding is permitted only within approved petty-cash limits and secured in approved safes with restricted access. Bank mandates must specify authorised signatories and dual-signatory requirements. All fundraising-related bank accounts must be registered in Agresso/GLACOS and the treasury master register. Periodic reviews of bank accounts must confirm continued necessity and compliance.
Receipting & sequential control
Every donation must be acknowledged with a pre-numbered receipt (paper or electronic) carrying a unique identifier. Receipt control logs must track issuance, usage, voids and cancellations. Receipts must include donor details (or anonymous flag), amount, currency, date, donation type, campaign/event code, restriction status, collector ID and issuer. Receipt books and electronic receipt systems must be issued under control, reviewed periodically and reconciled to deposits and GL postings.
Immediate recording & coding in Agresso/GLACOS
Donations must be recorded promptly using correct GL accounts, SOFs, cost centres, projects/DEAs and donor or campaign codes. Restricted status must be flagged at entry. Each entry must reference supporting documentation: receipt copy, deposit slip, gateway settlement, event reconciliation or third-party report. Separate GL accounts must be used for gross income, gateway fees, refunds and fundraising costs to ensure transparency.
Cash handling controls (operations – detailed)
Mandatory controls include: two-person cash counts; dual custody during transfers; independent verification; restricted safe access; surprise cash counts; segregation between collection, banking and recording; documented float management procedures; approved float limits; reconciliation of floats after events; and formal investigation of discrepancies.
Third-party collectors & event organisers
Third-party involvement requires written agreements defining scope, timelines, reporting, receipting, branding, liabilities, audit rights and termination clauses. Third parties must provide detailed reconciliations, deposit evidence and receipt sequences within defined SLAs. SCI retains ownership of funds at all times. Non-compliance must trigger escalation and potential termination.
Gifts-in-Kind (GiK) from fundraising

Donor restrictions & fund allocation (detailed)
Restrictions must be captured at receipt and tracked through dedicated fund or campaign codes. Restricted balances must be reconciled regularly to qualifying expenditure. Country offices must maintain acceptance criteria covering legality, ethics, reputational risk, sanctions, conflicts of interest and alignment with SCI mission. Donor data, privacy & data protection

Recognition & timing of income (accounting principle)
Unconditional donations are recognised on receipt when entitlement is established. Pledges are recognised only when collectability is probable and measurable. Restricted income follows donor conditions. GiK recognition follows Chapter 8.
Refunds, cancellations & donor complaints
A formal refunds policy must define circumstances, approval thresholds and timelines. Refunds require documented justification and approval. A donor complaints log must be maintained with response SLAs, escalation routes and resolution tracking.
Event revenue control & reconciliation
Event fundraising requires reconciliation of ticket sales, sponsorships, cash collections, deposits and costs. Daily closes and post-event reconciliations are mandatory. Variances must be investigated and approved.
Online payments & gateways
Only approved gateways may be used. Daily or periodic settlement reconciliations are required. Gateway fees must be recorded separately. Chargebacks and disputes must be monitored and resolved promptly.
Internal transfers, programme allocation & inter-office flows
Transfers require documented approval and confirmation of donor restrictions. Inter-office control accounts must be reconciled monthly and acknowledged by recipients.
Recording, tagging & reporting (GL best practice)
Dedicated GL codes must be maintained for income, events, restricted funds, GiK, fees and refunds. Transactions must be tagged for campaign, donor type and project. Donation registers must be exportable for audit and reporting.
Reconciliation & month-end controls
Monthly reconciliations must match receipt logs to bank deposits and GL postings. Gateway settlements must reconcile. Variances must be documented and resolved. Fundraising must form part of the formal month-end close checklist.
KPIs & management information
Monitor: total funds raised by source; % banked within SLA; reconciliation timeliness; acknowledgement turnaround; cost-to-raise; donor retention; proportion of restricted funds; refunds/chargebacks; top-donor concentration. Review trends and escalate adverse movements.
Segregation of duties & compensating controls
Fundraising staff must not bank, post or reconcile income. Finance performs independent reconciliations. Where segregation is limited, compensating controls (two-person handling, senior review, surprise counts) are mandatory.
Fraud prevention, AML & sanctions
Apply risk-based AML checks to large or unusual donations. Screen donors against sanctions lists where required. Train staff on red flags. Escalate suspected fraud immediately per SCI procedures.
Audit & assurance expectations
Auditors will expect donation registers, receipt logs, bank evidence, event reconciliations, third-party agreements, refund documentation, prospect-member reports and reconciliations. Centre or regional spot checks may occur.
Documentation, retention & filing
Retain fundraising documentation per policy and law (typically 6–10 years). Documents must be scanned, indexed, attached to ledger entries and securely backed up with controlled access.
Donor stewardship & reporting
Timely acknowledgements are mandatory. Restricted donors must receive reports per agreements. Communication preferences must be respected.
Legal & reputational risk management
Screen donors and partners for sanctions and reputational risk. Ensure communications accurately reflect fund use. Misrepresentation constitutes a serious breach.
Systems, automation & continuous improvement
Implement system validations for receipt references, attachments and donor restrictions. Use dashboards and CRM integration to reduce manual error. Periodically review controls and update SOPs.
Training & capacity building
Provide regular training for fundraising, finance and programme staff. Maintain local SOPs, templates and refresher sessions after changes.
Common errors & mitigations
Errors include delayed banking, weak receipting, miscoding, poor restriction tracking and inadequate third-party oversight. Mitigations include enforced SLAs, mandatory documentation, GL validation and monthly reviews.
Escalation, exceptions & approvals
Define escalation routes for unusual donations, late deposits, disputes or suspected fraud. Approval thresholds must align with SCI DoA. Maintain an exceptions log.
Implementation checklist (immediate & medium-term)
Immediate: publish SOPs; confirm bank mandates; configure systems; train staff; start monthly reconciliations. Medium-term: automate gateway uploads; integrate CRM; conduct assurance review; refine KPIs.
Annexes & templates

Final compliance reminder
All fundraising activity must align with SCI central policy, local law and donor requirements. Deviations require formal approval. Every transaction and decision must be fully documented, auditable and defensible.

Chapter 8 — Gifts in Kind (GiK)
Purpose & objective
Define policy, controls and practical procedures so Gifts-in-Kind (GiK) are: correctly valued; recorded only when Save the Children International (SCI) obtains control for distribution; subject to stock control and secure custody; matched to donor/member reporting and award terms; auditable; and managed to reduce misstatement, fraud, loss and programme delivery risk. Ensure transparency of donor restrictions and that accounting, logistics and programme records reconcile by donor, award and SOF/DEA. Establish roles, thresholds and escalation routes for exceptions, write-offs and donor disputes.
Scope
All GiK received, stored, transferred and distributed by country offices, sub-offices and partners acting on SCI’s behalf — including member, donor, corporate, government, fundraising and in-kind commercial contributions. Covers acceptance, valuation, documentation, warehousing, insurance, customs/clearance, inter-office transfers, partner distributions, programme delivery, reporting, monitoring, impairment, disposal and sale of GiK. Applies to all staff, consultants, implementing partners and contractors involved in GiK handling. Excludes cash donations and vouchers unless specifically managed as GiK under award terms.
Key definitions & core recognition principle
• GiK: a non-cash donation of tangible goods or services provided by a donor to SCI (directly or via partners).
• Receipt (physical receipt/acceptance): the moment goods/services come into SCI custody or control for the purpose of storage/processing — no GL revenue/expense posting at receipt (except documented, approved exceptions).
• Distribution: the controlled handover to beneficiaries or implementing partners which is the standard recognition event for matched revenue and expense.
• Control transfer criteria (exception): when legal title and control, together with the risks/benefits, permanently transfer to SCI or when award/donor rules require immediate recognition (documented approval required).
Core principle: do not recognise GiK as revenue/expense at receipt. Recognise matched revenue & expense only on distribution (or on documented exceptions where control and recognition criteria are met and authorised by CFD/CD).
Responsibilities & RACI (detailed)
Country Director (CD): final approver for major policy deviations, acceptance of high-value/unusual GiK, final approver of write-offs above DoA thresholds and escalation point for donor disputes.
Country Finance Director (CFD): maintain GiK schedule, approve valuation policies locally, reconcile logistics schedule to GL, review and authorise donor-currency postings, oversee donor reporting and ensure donor restrictions are applied correctly.
GiK Coordinator / GiK Focal Point (Logistics lead): manage GiK schedule, ensure packing/waybill/valuation evidence collected, co-ordinate stock counts, prepare distribution packs, maintain cold-chain/expiry records, maintain attachments and support audits.
Logistics / Warehouse Manager: physical receipt, inspection, quarantine handling, storage security, inventory controls, batch/lot/expiry tracking, issue notes and delivery documentation.
Programme / Budget Holder: define intended use, beneficiary lists, authorise distributions, confirm valuation basis and SOF/DEA, accept post-distribution reports.
Partner / Implementer: comply with partner agreement for storage/distribution; provide signed distribution confirmations, beneficiary lists and monitoring evidence; accept responsibility for partner-held GiK per contract.
Payments / Procurement: where procurement/clearance actions required (e.g., local handling costs), ensure correct contracting and VAT/duty treatment.
Regional Finance / HQ / Internal Audit: perform oversight, consolidation, spot checks, policy updates and audit assurance; review sample reconciliations and disposals.
Acceptance & refusal criteria
Accept only if safe, lawful, usable for purpose, properly documented and aligned to programme needs and donor terms. Perform donor due diligence (reputational, sanctions/PEP screening, legal restrictions). Decline, return, quarantine or divert unsuitable donations. For hazardous or regulated items require pre-approval and permits. Escalate high-value, high-risk or conditional gifts to CFD and CD with full documentation and proposed handling plan. Record acceptance decision and rationale in GiK schedule.
Donor & partner due diligence
Screen donors/third parties for sanctions, AML/PEP risk, provenance and reputation. For high-value or frequent donors perform formal due diligence checks and obtain signed donor declarations on origin and valuation. Evaluate partners’ capacity to securely store and accurately distribute GiK; include GiK-specific clauses in partner agreements (stock control, reporting cadence, audit access, indemnities). Maintain due diligence evidence in file.
Pre-receipt planning
1. Confirm donor intent, packaging/palletisation, estimated quantities, declared valuation and donor currency.
2. Agree responsibilities for clearance, transport, warehousing, insurance and handling costs; secure budget lines for handling/certification/duty.
3. Identify storage requirements (temperature, security, segregation), ensure warehouse capacity and H&S arrangements.
4. Update GiK planning schedule with expected arrival, donor, award, item codes, and assigned warehouse/location.
5. If donor provides valuation, obtain valuation certificate/invoice and packing list prior to dispatch where possible.
Receipt procedures & immediate controls
On physical arrival: logistics inspects goods for quantity, condition and packaging; records waybill/B/L, assigns inbound reference and warehouse location; captures batch/lot/expiry and any damage. Update GiK schedule status = Received — Not for GL. Create inbound receiving document (warehouse receipt) with signatures of carrier and warehouse staff. Segregate quarantined/damaged goods. Notify finance and GiK Coordinator with copy of receiving documentation. Store securely and apply cold-chain or hazardous handling as required. Do not post GL at receipt. Secure sign-in/out logs and chain-of-custody records.
Valuation guidance — policy & methods
Principle: value at credible donor valuation or, if not available, local replacement/fair value at the point of distribution. Document method, date, source and approver.
Preferred valuation sources (ranked): 1) donor valuation certificate or commercial invoice; 2) supplier invoice for identical goods; 3) three independent local market quotations (same geography and timeframe); 4) nationally published commodity/pricing indices (where reliable); 5) replacement cost (local procurement).
Unit valuation: record unit price × qty and show any discounts/obvious defects; include freight, insurance and handling costs incurred up to distribution only where donor valuation excludes them (treatment of incidental costs must be consistent and disclosed).
Pharmaceuticals & medical supplies: value by batch with expiry-adjusted valuation (discounts for short shelf life), include pharmacovigilance liabilities if material. Maintain separate valuation methodology and require qualified pharmacist sign-off for unit values.
Damaged or partial-use items: evidence-based discounted valuation with documented percent reduction and approver signature.
Services: value at market rates × time with supporting contractor invoices or local market rates and service certificates.
Independent valuation: require independent valuation where value > local threshold (e.g., per DoA) or where market quotes are unreliable; thresholds set locally with CFD/HQ guidance.
Documentary evidence: attach valuation certificate, quotations, calculation sheet (unit × qty), approver sign-off and donor communication.
Currency & translation treatment
Maintain donor currency in GiK schedule and post distribution journals in donor currency. Agresso/FMS or local ERP translates to functional/reporting currency at the system exchange rate on posting (distribution) date. Capture the exchange rate, source and translation worksheet in the posting pack. Disclose translation gains/losses if material and map FX postings to designated GLs. Where donor requires reporting in multiple currencies, maintain reconciled donor currency and local currency views.
Stock control, systems & warehousing
Maintain central GiK stock register or module with fields: GiK ref, donor, award ID, donor valuation, receipt date, warehouse location, item code/description, batch/lot/expiry, qty received, qty distributed, qty on hand, unit valuation (donor & functional), total donor value, attachments (waybill, valuation, posting pack), movement history and custody log. Use FIFO for perishables; LIFO only with documented justification and approval. Implement barcode or RFID scanning for high-volume operations. Segregate GiK stock by donor/award where donor restrictions apply. Implement inventory aging reports and exception alerts (expiry < X days). Integrate warehouse management with finance where possible; log all stock adjustments with authoriser and evidence.
Insurance, safety, permits & customs
Arrange transit and storage insurance proportionate to risk/value. For hazardous or cold-chain items maintain validated temperature logs and calibration records. Capture customs clearance documentation, duty invoices and exemption certificates; record duties & clearance as operating expenses (do not net against GiK valuation unless donor specifies). Seek duty exemptions or waivers where available and maintain correspondence. Store permits, licences and H&S documentation in GiK file.
Distribution controls & authorisation
Distributions must be authorised by the programme/budget holder. Required distribution pack: authorised partner distribution form or internal issue note; beneficiary list with signatures/IDs where feasible; waybill/delivery note; valuation support; programme authorisation; partner agreement/terms; recommended donor-currency journal lines (Dr expense / Cr income); custody/chain-of-custody evidence; monitoring checklist and photos. Finance posts donor-currency journal only after pack completeness checked and budget holder approval obtained. For distributions via partners, obtain signed partner confirmation including beneficiary sample verification and attach monitoring evidence. Ensure SOF/DEA mapping is provided.
Accounting treatment & journal workflow
Primary rule: match GiK income and corresponding expense on the distribution date in donor currency.
Standard donor-currency posting (on distribution):
• Dr Expense — relevant programme cost code (e.g., 5050 Food Aid).
• Cr Income — corresponding GiK income code (e.g., 3300 GiK — Food Aid).
Include GiK schedule ID, donor name, waybill number, distribution date, SOF/DEA, and valuation reference in the journal narrative. Attach distribution pack, valuation sheet and translation worksheet. System will translate to functional currency at posting date; record FX effect and map to FX GLs where required. Ensure postings are traceable to physical distribution evidence.
Capital items & fixed assets donated as GiK
Capital GiK (vehicles, generators, furniture, ICT equipment) that meet capitalization thresholds must be recognised as donated non-monetary assets on the date control transfers for productive use (this may be distribution or earlier if control is transferred and donor terms require). Accounting entries for capital GiK on recognition: Dr Fixed Asset (at fair/valuation) / Cr Income — GiK (capital). Capitalise at donor valuation or fair market value; record depreciation policy, useful life and residual value; tag physical assets and assign custodian. For assets transferred to partners, treat per local rules: capital recognition only when SCI assumes control/use or when award requires. Document asset maintenance plans and insurance.
Numeric examples
1) Consumable distribution: donor supplies 1,000 food kits valued EUR 10 each = EUR 10,000. On distribution date: Dr 5050 Food Aid EUR 10,000 / Cr 3300 Food Aid EUR 10,000. System converts to functional currency at posting date; disclose translation impact if material.
2) Capital gift: donor donates 1 vehicle valued USD 15,000 for country office use. On acceptance into use (control transfer): Dr Fixed Asset — Vehicles USD 15,000 / Cr 3305 GiK Capital Income USD 15,000; set depreciation per policy.

4. Budget holder confirms valuation and obtains CFD sign-off for donor-currency posting (documented).
5. Prepare posting pack (waybills, valuation, approvals, recommended donor-currency journals, SOF/DEA and monitoring evidence).
6. Finance prepares donor-currency journal referencing GiK schedule ID; checks attachments and translation worksheet.
7. Budget holder authorises journal (signature or electronic approval).
8. Finance posts in Agresso/ERP; checks for system translation and errors; record posting reference in GiK schedule.

10. Conduct post-distribution verification (sample beneficiary confirmation, photos) and record results.
Quarterly stock & reconciliation procedures
Frequency: minimum quarterly plus spot checks and risk-based cycle counts (monthly for high-value/pharmaceuticals).
Steps: logistics performs physical count → GiK Coordinator compiles count worksheet → finance reconciles GiK schedule, logistics stock report and GL by donor/award/SOF → any variances investigated and evidenced → reconciliation signed off by Logistics, GiK Coordinator, Finance and CD → unresolved material variances escalated to Regional Finance. Reconciliation columns: item, batch, opening qty, receipts, distributions, adjustments, closing qty, unit valuation (donor currency), closing value, GL balance, variance & explanation, supporting doc IDs. Maintain ageing of receipts and distributions and expiry alerts.
Losses, shortages, write-offs & incident handling
Losses before distribution (damage, theft, spoilage) reduce physical stock and require incident documentation and authorisation per thresholds. Procedure: immediate incident report (photos, witness statements, CCTV where available), isolate evidence, calculate donor-currency quantum, determine cause, obtain approvals per DoA for write-off, notify donor and insurer where required, and record accounting adjustment only after approval. For post-distribution losses (partner misappropriation), conduct investigation, consider accounting reversals or recovery actions, update partner controls and pursue legal/insurance claims. Maintain loss register and trend analysis. Do not record accounting revenue/expense for lost stock prior to authorised distribution accounting except where donor/award rules require different treatment and documented approval exists.
Controls to minimise losses & fraud
Secure warehouse access controls, CCTV, temperature monitoring, tamper-evident seals, segregation of duties (receipt, stock update, distribution authorisation, GL posting), surprise counts, rotation of custodians, dual custody for high-value items, authorised sign-off for all movements, independent reconciliation and access logs. Implement beneficiary verification sampling and partner spot checks. Use analytics to detect anomalous distribution patterns (e.g., repeated round quantities, rapid depletion). Maintain whistleblower channels and clear escalation protocols.
Partner distributions & monitoring
Include GiK-specific clauses in partner contracts: stock custody, segregation, reporting cadence, retention of beneficiary receipts, right to audit, insurance and indemnities. Require partners to maintain separate ledgers for donor GiK and provide monthly movement reports and signed distribution confirmations. Implement risk-based monitoring (on-site visits, sample beneficiary verification, reconciliation to GL) and hold back a retention percentage or require financial guarantees for high-value flows where appropriate.
Disposal, sale, exchange & substitution of GiK
Where GiK cannot be used as intended (damaged, obsolete, unsuitable), assess options (repair, re-allocate, sell, donate to alternate eligible beneficiary, return to donor). Sales/proceeds from disposal are treated per donor terms: proceeds may be recorded as restricted income (donor-specific) or returned to donor; obtain donor approval where required. Document disposal decision, valuation of proceeds, approvals per DoA and accounting treatment. For substitutions where GiK replaces planned cash purchases, disclose avoided cash cost per donor rules and ensure donor consent for substitution where required. Maintain detailed disposal file and reconciliation of proceeds.
Tax, customs, duty & procurement integration
Record duties, VAT and clearance costs as separate operating expenses; secure duty exemption documentation where available. Ensure procurement integration for handling contracts, warehousing, transport and customs brokers. When GiK displaces planned purchases, consider donor restrictions on cost savings and record avoided cost treatment per donor policy. Ensure correct VAT treatment and seek tax/legal advice for non-routine cases (e.g., cross-border donations of capital goods).
Systems, templates & KPIs
Maintain GiK schedule in a controlled spreadsheet or integrated ERP/WMS module linked to Agresso/GLACOS. Required templates: GiK schedule, receipt/warehouse record, distribution authorisation, partner distribution agreement, valuation certificate, stock count checklist, reconciliation pack, loss incident report, cold-chain log, disposal authorisation. Suggested KPIs: total GiK received by donor/type; % GiK distributed within SLA; stock days on hand; number/value of losses; % reconciled monthly; time from receipt to distribution; % of item batches expiring within X days; top donors by value; partner distribution accuracy rate. Monitor KPIs monthly and escalate adverse trends.
Common errors, control mitigations & automated checks
Common mistakes: posting at receipt; posting in local currency instead of donor currency; missing valuation support; incorrect SOF/DEA mapping; not updating GiK schedule; lack of batch/expiry tracking; filing gaps; partner-led misreporting. Mitigations: enforce recognise-on-distribution rule via system controls, mandatory attachment of valuation evidence, GL validation rules preventing donor-currency bypass, mandatory reconciliations, expiry alerts, access controls, role separation and targeted training. Implement automation where feasible: electronic receiving, barcode scanning, WMS-ERP integration and automatic generation of distribution packs.
Audit expectations & audit pack
Auditors will expect chain-of-custody evidence, valuation support, documentation that journals are only posted on distribution (or approved exception), reconciliations between logistics and finance, approval trails for write-offs, sample distribution confirmations and partner audit rights. Audit pack checklist: donor packing list/valuation, waybill/B/L, customs clearance, warehouse receipt, GiK schedule line, distribution authorisation, beneficiary/partner signed receipt, valuation support, GL distribution journal with narrative & translation worksheet, stock reconciliation, loss/variance report, disposal documentation, approval memos and monitoring evidence. Define escalation routes for acceptance refusals, high-value donations, losses, write-offs and donor disputes. Approval thresholds must align to SCI DoA for write-offs and disposal authorisations; document delegated authorities for independent valuation and partner approvals. For reputational or regulatory risk refer to Legal/Compliance immediately.

Chapter 9 — Staff Payroll and Benefits
Purpose & objective
Payroll is typically the largest operating expense and a high-risk area for compliance, fraud and donor scrutiny. Country offices must operate a controlled, auditable payroll process that ensures: timely and accurate payment; correct period recognition (accrual); appropriate allocation to funding sources; lawful tax and statutory compliance; protection of staff personal data; and full documentary support for audit and donor reporting. Bank transfer is the preferred payment method; cash/cheque limited to exceptional cases with strict controls. Ensure processes protect staff rights, meet statutory and donor requirements, secure payroll data, correctly allocate labour costs and minimise fraud and compliance risk.
Scope
All payroll activity for employees and worker-types managed via payroll (local, national, international, expatriate), including allowances, benefits in kind, advances/loans, statutory remittances, payroll-related GL postings, outsourced payroll providers and country-level payroll interactions with centre/treasury. Excludes supplier/vendor payments (contractors paid via AP unless treated as payroll by contract).
Key definitions & core recognition principle
• Payroll run / Payroll period: the defined payroll cycle (monthly/biweekly) and the set of transactions prepared for payment for that period.
• Payroll control sheet / pack: the master reconciliation document containing gross, deductions, net, employer costs, apportionment and supporting evidence for a payroll run.
• Accrual recognition principle: payroll expense is recognised in the accounting period services were rendered, irrespective of payment date (accrual basis).
• Master data: secure authoritative employee records (contracts, tax IDs, bank details) maintained in HROS or equivalent.
• Payslip: formal payslip issued to employee showing gross, deductions and net pay and statutory information.
• Statutory remittances: employer/employee tax and social security contributions due to tax authorities and social agencies.
Core principle: recognise payroll costs on an accrual basis; maintain segregation of duties and an auditable payroll trail that supports donor and statutory reporting.
Core principles / Key principles
1. Record payroll in the accounting period in which employment services were rendered (accrual basis).
2. Segregation of duties: preparation, review/authorisation, and payment execution must be distinct roles (maker-checker).
3. Secure, authorised master data (HROS) with auditable change history.
4. Retain full documentary support (contracts, payslips, timesheets, remittances, approvals).
5. Dual signatory/independent approval for payroll transfers and exceptions.
6. Timely reconciliations: payroll control sheet ↔ bank ↔ GL.
Governance & responsibilities (RACI)
• Country Director (CD): oversight of legal/tax exposure; sign-off on material payroll adjustments; approval of policy deviations and advance authorisations per thresholds.
• Country Finance Director (CFD): owns payroll controls, month-end process, reconciliations, GL postings and apportionment sign-off; review advance recovery and payroll-related GL accounts.
• HR: maintain master data (contracts, joiner/leaver, grade, salary, bank details); submit changes by cut-off; provide timesheets/leave records.
• Payroll Preparer: build payroll run, calculate gross-to-net, prepare control sheet and upload files.
• Independent Reviewer / Authoriser: validates control sheet, performs sample checks and must not be the preparer.
• Bank/Payments Officer: prepares and executes transfers, obtains bank confirmations; enforces dual sign-off per DoA.
• Budget Holders: approve apportionment and verify project coding.
• Treasury/Centre: manage central/international flows and inter-company clearing where applicable.
Systems & security (HROS, GLACOS, Agresso or equivalent)
Use HROS for secure master data with audit trail. Use GLACOS or approved upload templates for batch GL posting to Agresso (keep upload preview and reconciliation files). Restrict payroll file access; enforce encryption, password/sheet protection, version control and backups; maintain secure archive of payroll packs and bank confirmations. Maintain access logs and review periodically. Implement role-based access (view only / amend / approve) with periodic access certification. Where possible, implement read-only dashboards for budget-holders to view apportioned costs without exposing PII. Maintain vendor contracts and security attestations for any hosted payroll systems.
Payroll calendar & cut-offs
Establish and publish a fixed monthly payroll calendar with clear cut-offs for HR changes, timesheets, allowances, advances and claims (example: HR cut-off 5th; payroll finalisation by 18th; posting by 25th). Incorporate bank processing lead times and communicate calendar to HR, finance and programme teams. Include contingency run dates for failed or amended runs and document cut-off exceptions procedure with required approvals.
Required inputs & supporting documentation
• Employee master record: full name; national ID/tax ID; contract type & dates; grade; salary; allowances; bank details; home/host country; payroll frequency; tax residency; social security numbers.
• Employment contract and amendments; signed approvals for salary changes, advances, overtime, bonuses, apportionments.
• Authorised payroll control sheet (gross, statutory deductions, employer costs, net pay, project apportionment).
• Timesheets/effort reports (if required by donor); payslips issued and filed; bank payment advices; statutory remittance proofs; upload/reconciliation files.
• Supporting approvals for any manual/exception payments (emergency payment forms signed by CD/CFD).
Payroll calculation components (detailed)
Earnings: basic salary; locality/hardship/housing allowances; post adjustment; premiums; overtime; shift allowances; standby/on-call; gratuities; bonuses (if contractual); leave encashment; termination/severance.
Employee deductions: PAYE/withholding tax; employee social security/pension; union fees; loan/advance recovery; garnishments.
Employer costs: employer social security; pension contributions; employer taxes; insurances (medical/WorkComp); fringe benefit tax where applicable.
Benefits in kind: company-provided housing, vehicle, school fees — assess taxable value per local law and reflect on payslip/GL. Document valuation methodology for BIKs and apply consistent tax treatment.
Accounting treatment & sample journals (including GL guidance)
Recognise gross payroll expense in the month services were rendered; accrue employer liabilities and taxes at month-end; record net pay on payment date; clear statutory liabilities when remitted. Include payroll run ID and period in narration.
Typical journals (examples):
1. Gross payroll accrual (month-end): Dr Payroll expense (by cost centres/projects) — Cr 2100 Salary recharged / Accrued payroll liability.
2. Employee deductions & statutory accruals: Dr 2100 Salary recharged (contra) — Cr 2130 Accrued payroll taxes; Cr Accrued social security/pension liabilities.
3. Net pay payment (on payment date): Dr 2100 Salary recharged / Cr Bank (or reverse accrued liability).
4. Statutory remittances (when paid): Dr 2130 Accrued payroll taxes / Cr Bank.
5. Salary apportionment (allocation): Dr Project expense codes / Cr 2100 Salary recharged.
6. Advances issued & recovery: Dr 1140 Advances to staff / Cr Bank; recovery via payroll: Dr Bank (or payroll clearing) / Cr 1140 Advances to staff (and net pay adjusted via payroll entries).
Journal narrative guidance: always include PayrollRun ID, period (YYYYMM), country, total gross and currency; include employee range or reference and bank batch ID for audit traceability.
Payroll cycle — operational steps (pre-run, run, post-run)
1. HR confirms joiners/leavers/contract changes by cut-off.
2. Pre-run: preparer calculates gross-to-net, statutory deductions, employer costs, advances/loans, overtime and allowances; prepare draft payroll control sheet.
3. Sample checks: arithmetic, variance analysis vs prior month, sample tax & social checks (e.g., 10% sample or high-value staff).
4. Independent review & authorisation: reviewer signs control sheet confirming checks and authorisation to pay.
5. Bank payment preparation: payments officer prepares transfer file; require two authorised signatories per DoA and maker-checker segregation.
6. GL posting/upload: upload via GLACOS/Agresso or post journals; keep upload previews and reconciliation evidence.
7. Post-run: distribute payslips securely; prepare statutory remittances; reconcile control sheet ↔ bank ↔ GL; file payroll pack. Document exception logs for any manual adjustments and approvals.
Apportionment & effort reporting
Prepare apportionment schedules showing allocation basis (FTE, % time, fixed split, project-coded timesheets). Obtain budget-holder sign-off. Post apportionment journal or upload via GLACOS to distribute salary expense across SOF/DEAs. Where donors require effort reporting, retain signed timesheets and reconcile to apportionment schedules; retain allocation methodology and calculations for audit. Maintain change logs where apportionments are revised and retain evidence of approvals.
International / outsourced payroll processing
For centrally processed or outsourced payrolls, post local clearing/inter-company entries and reconcile balances monthly. Maintain SLA, reconciliation evidence, provider control reports (SOC/SOC2 if available) and confirm data protection compliance. Country office remains ultimately responsible for statutory compliance and must reconcile with centre-led ledgers. Maintain a register of outsourced providers, their contact details, and evidence of background checks and contract terms (SLA, confidentiality, backup, liability).
Overtime, bonuses, termination pay & leave accruals
Overtime requires pre-authorisation and time records. Bonuses paid only if contractual/approved; record in period earned if policy requires. Termination/severance requires HR/legal and CD approval; accrue when liability exists. Accrue unused leave at period end (Dr Leave expense / Cr Accrued leave liability); document formula and sample calculations in payroll pack. Maintain sample calculations for leave accrual methodology and apply consistently across staff types. Document exit checklists including final pay calculation, recovery of advances, return of assets and clearance certificates.
Statutory tax, social security & employer compliance
Maintain register of local statutory obligations (PAYE, social security, pension, employer taxes). Apply current rates and update systems when rates change. File and remit on time and retain proof. For expatriates, confirm tax residency and tax equalisation rules; obtain professional advice for complex cross-border cases. Maintain a statutory compliance calendar, reminder controls and escalation for missed filings. Retain correspondence with tax authorities and advice memos for complex cases.
Advances, loans & recovery
Issue advances only with authorised request and repayment schedule; record to Advances ledger (e.g., 1140) on issuance. Require signed advance request with repayment schedule; recover via payroll as agreed. Reconcile advances monthly and escalate unrecovered balances prior to staff exit. For non-recoverable balances, follow DoA for write-off and document recovery efforts. Record interest or administrational charges where policy permits and document approval.
Payroll bank controls & payment security
Enforce maker-checker segregation for payment file creation and approval. Require two authorised signatories per DoA for payroll transfers. Retain bank confirmations/SWIFT advices; reconcile immediately. Investigate cancelled/returned payments and adjust GL promptly. Maintain change controls and multi-factor authentication for bank portals; store bank signatory lists with specimen signatures and update bank on authorised signatory changes promptly. For cross-border payments, confirm beneficiary bank details and use two-factor validation for new payee additions. Implement bank-reconciliation KPIs and SLAs for resolution.
Data protection & confidentiality
Treat payroll data as highly confidential personal data/PII. Restrict access to authorised personnel; encrypt files in transit and at rest; protect spreadsheets (sheet protection, read-only storage); distribute payslips only via secure channels. Maintain access logs and review periodically. Ensure compliance with local data protection law and corporate privacy policy; use data processing agreements for third-party processors; anonymise reports where possible; restrict export of PII cross-border unless covered by legal basis and safeguards. Dispose of obsolete payroll files per retention policy and ensure secure deletion.

Chapter 10 — Bank Accounts and Banking Controls
Purpose & objective / Overview
Establish robust controls and procedures for opening, operating, monitoring and closing bank accounts; secure and control cheque/electronic payments; ensure timely and accurate bank reconciliations; prevent and detect fraud, theft and unauthorised transactions; maintain an auditable bank file and clear lines of responsibility; ensure compliance with treasury policy, donor rules and local law (including AML/KYC and sanctions). Reconciling items must be investigated promptly; unresolved items older than one month require documented explanation and authorised posting to suspense only.
Scope
All bank accounts (central, country, sub-office, project, collection, foreign currency), treasury arrangements (sweep/cash pooling), payment channels (cheque, bank transfer, electronic banking, payment gateway), merchant/collection accounts, petty cash banking interactions, and bank-related instruments (guarantees, letters of credit). Applies to staff, partners and third-party collectors acting on organisation’s behalf.
Core / Key principles & control objectives
1. All accounts authorised and recorded in Agresso/GLACOS (or local finance system).
2. Segregation of duties for initiation, authorisation, execution and reconciliation; preparer must not be signatory.
3. Dual signatory/independent approval for material payments per bank mandate and DoA.
4. Monthly bank reconciliations with timely investigation of variances; items >30 days require documented action/owner and escalation.
5. Maintain comprehensive bank file and audit trail per account.
6. Proactive treasury engagement for multi-currency, large balances and liquidity management.
Governance & responsibilities / RACI
Country Director (CD): final authoriser for opening/closing primary accounts; reviewer of reconciliation exceptions; authoriser for material suspense postings.
Country Finance Director (CFD): implement bank controls, ensure reconciliations, maintain bank files, investigate reconciling items, manage signatory changes, liaise with treasury/centre.
Treasury/Centre: advise on bank selection, foreign exchange, cash pooling; approve treasury-preferred banks where applicable.
Finance / Cashier / Payments Officer: prepare payment instructions, post journals, obtain statements, process payment vouchers and files.
Reconciler: prepares monthly reconciliation (must not be signatory).
Reconciliation reviewer / Independent reviewer: CFD or authorised delegate signs off.
IT / Security: manage electronic banking access and security tools.
Internal Audit / Regional Finance: periodic review and spot checks.
Bank account opening — step-by-step
1. Assess need: purpose, currency, donor rules, cash-flow profile, location, merchant/collection requirements.
2. Shortlist banks and perform due diligence: financial strength, AML/KYC, fees, services, correspondent capability, branch reliability.
3. Seek treasury/centre approval where required; document rationale if using non-preferred bank.
4. Obtain internal authorisation per DoA (CD + treasurer/Regional Director if required).
5. Complete account opening request with business justification, signatory list, specimen signatures and KYC docs; apply to bank.
6. Obtain account contract, mandate, fee schedule and specimen signatures.
7. Enter account in Agresso/GLACOS (account number, currency, bank, branch, GL mapping) and create bank file; notify treasury if required.
Bank account closing — step-by-step
1. Request closure with internal authorisation per DoA.
2. Ensure cheques cleared, transfers settled and payroll/top-ups handled.
3. Transfer final balance to authorised account; obtain bank confirmation of transfer and zero closing balance.
4. Obtain final bank statement showing zero balance and formal closure confirmation.
5. Update Agresso/GLACOS to set account inactive and archive bank file per retention policy; retain closure documentation.
Bank file contents & naming convention / Bank account naming & recordkeeping
Minimum contents: opening authorisation, bank mandate & signatory list, specimen signatures, bank agreement/contract, fee schedule, KYC/due diligence notes, monthly bank statements, payment vouchers and supporting docs, bank correspondence, bank charges reconciliations, guarantees/LCs, closure confirmation (if closed).
Maintain master bank register with account number, branch, SWIFT/BIC, currency, opening date, authorised signatories, mandate ref, intended use, limits and last mandate review date.
Naming convention: BankFile__.pdf (example).
Authorised signatories & mandate changes / Bank signatory lists
Document signatory matrix linked to DoA; require two authorised signatories for payments above thresholds. Maintain up-to-date signed signatory list and specimen signatures in bank file. Any change requires formal internal authorisation, bank notification and updated mandate; ensure reconciliation reviewer is not a signatory. Keep log of signatory training and token/smartcard assignments; rotate signatories periodically and remove access promptly on leavers.
Bank selection, due diligence & preferred banks
Prefer treasury/centre-approved banks. If local alternatives used, document rationale and perform enhanced due diligence: bank ratings, correspondent access, AML history, sanctions checks, fees, service levels. Reassess relationships periodically and notify treasury/centre.
Cheque controls (in-depth)
Pre-printed cheque stock securely stored and logged; issued only by authorised personnel. Two authorised signatories where policy dictates; never sign blank cheques. Use crossed cheques payable to named payee; avoid “cash”. Retain cancelled/spoiled cheques with annotations; black out signatures if retained. For lost/stolen cheques: stop payment immediately, document incident and reissue only after bank stop confirmation and internal approval. Cheque distribution must be recorded; prefer secure courier or direct deposit. Pre-signed cheques allowed only in exceptional documented cases with immediate second signature and independent review.
Bank transfer & payment instruction controls / Payment instruction controls
Use pre-printed/pre-numbered bank instruction forms where manual; control stock. Payment instruction must include authorised voucher, approver’s approval and supporting docs (invoice/PO/payroll sheet). Two-person rule: preparer (non-signatory) creates instruction; authoriser (signatory) reviews and signs. Reconcile payment batch totals to supporting journal and retain batch audit trail. For cash top-ups follow agreed bank arrangements (nominated staff, ID checks).
Electronic banking & e-payments (security & controls)
Obtain treasury approval for e-banking setup and define limits. Enforce strict access controls (unique IDs, role-based access, MFA/2FA), maker-checker segregation (preparer/approver/administrator), token security and immediate deactivation on leavers. Maintain user access logs and periodic access reviews. Configure dual authorisation for high-value payments; use secure devices, VPNs and encrypted channels. Implement positive-pay/payee validation where available. Document emergency/delegated access procedures and disaster recovery.
Payment file lifecycle
Maker creates payment file with voucher refs; checker/authoriser reviews file vs vouchers and approves. Upload to bank via secure channel and retain copy of upload and bank acknowledgement. Reconcile bank confirmation to batch and GL; retain digital evidence of approvals.
Electronic banking incident & disaster recovery
Document fallback procedures for outages (manual transfer authority, telephone authorisation), maintain backup signatories and emergency payment procedures with clear limits, and regularly test business continuity for critical banking functions.
Bank statements & retrieval / Bank statement management
Obtain statements at least monthly; prefer daily access via secure portal or SWIFT. Ensure statements are date-stamped and complete. Attach statements to reconciliations and file in bank file. Retrieval methods must be auditable (download logs); retain electronic copies with evidence of retrieval.
Bank reconciliation process (detailed)
1. Obtain month-end statement and extract finance system bank listing to same date.
2. Tick-match transactions between bank statement and GL (deposits, withdrawals, transfers).
3. Identify outstanding deposits in transit, outstanding cheques, bank charges, interest, bank errors and timing differences.
4. Investigate and document each reconciling item with evidence (deposit slips, payment advices, SWIFT confirmations).
5. Post authorised GL adjustments (charges, interest, corrections) attaching statement copy.
6. Prepare reconciliation schedule listing reconciling items and ageing; sign-off by preparer and independent reviewer (not signatory).
7. File reconciliation and supporting docs in bank file.
Handling reconciling items & aged items
Investigate reconciling items immediately. Items >30 days require documented reasons and active follow-up; escalate to CFD and CD if unresolved. Only with CD authorisation may unresolved items be posted to an authorised suspense account; continue investigation until cleared. Stale outstanding cheques follow bank processes to cancel/stop and reissue or write-off per policy; document decisions and approvals.
Suspense accounts & posting policy
Use suspense accounts only temporarily for items under investigation. Posting to suspense requires documented authorisation (CD/CFD) and description of actions. Reconcile suspense monthly and clear items promptly; escalate large or long-outstanding balances to Regional Finance/Treasury.
Cash-in-transit (CIT) & interbank transfers controls
Record CIT accounts for funds moving between banks and reconcile CIT clearing accounts monthly. Capture transfer confirmations (SWIFT, advices) and attach to reconciliations. For armoured courier/carrier pick-ups, retain proof of collection/delivery receipts and reconcile to bank deposit.
Foreign currency accounts & FX controls
Maintain separate accounts where required; record currency and GL mapping. Track bank balances and FX exposure; coordinate with treasury for hedging or conversions. Reconcile realised/unrealised FX items and post FX gains/losses to designated GL (e.g., GL 7435) using documented exchange rates. For donor-restricted foreign currency accounts, reconcile donor currency balances and include in donor reports. Minimise holding foreign currency cash at period-end.
Bank charges, interest & fee monitoring
Record bank interest and charges monthly with supporting bank advice; attach to journals. Reconcile bank fees vs fee schedule and query discrepancies. Accrue expected fees at month-end if not yet charged. Code charges to correct GL (program vs admin) per policy and donor rules.
Overdrafts, limits & approvals
Planned overdrafts require prior authorisation per delegation and treasury approval. Monitor account limits daily and report breaches to CFD/treasury. Maintain facility documentation for overdraft/credit facilities and ensure compliance with terms.
Bank security & fraud prevention
Monitor account activity and use alerts for high-value transactions. Enforce dual controls, beneficiary verification, positive-pay and maker-checker. Conduct periodic bank confirmations, surprise checks and reconciliations. Investigate unusual transactions immediately and report suspected fraud to CD, Regional Finance, internal audit and bank; freeze accounts if needed. Preserve evidence and cooperate with investigations.
Electronic payments, SWIFT & remittance controls
Retain SWIFT/payment advices and attach to payment vouchers. Match SWIFT confirmations to statement entries and GL postings. For urgent/high-value payments follow escalation and second-level verification procedures.
Bank guarantees, letters of credit, escrow & donor-restricted accounts
Obtain facilities only with Treasury/centre approval. Maintain register of guarantees and expiry dates; track covenants and report breaches. For donor-restricted accounts, agree naming and usage restrictions with donor; monitor and report balances per award terms. Keep documentation in bank file.
Merchant accounts & payment gateways
Reconcile merchant receipts daily to bank deposits and gateway reports; record merchant fees separately. Ensure PCI-DSS or local card security compliance; minimize storing cardholder data. Manage chargebacks and disputes promptly and maintain logs.
Anti-money laundering (AML), sanctions & KYC
Enforce KYC on banks and on large/unusual depositors when relevant. Screen counterparties for sanctions and PEP status where relevant. Report suspicious transactions internally and to authorities per local law; maintain SAR logging and escalation per compliance policy.
Vendor bank detail change controls
Require written change request and supplier-signed form; independently verify via callback to previously recorded contact and maintain change log. Implement dual control on bank detail updates (requestor + independent verifier) and attach verification evidence to payment voucher.
Imprest & petty cash accounts
Imprest floats have fixed limits; replenishment on submission of vouchers. Petty cash custodian maintains cash book and receipts; custodian must not reconcile bank account. Surprise cash counts and periodic independent checks required.
Payroll & supplier payment special controls
Payroll files reviewed vs payroll control sheet; bank payments executed against authorised payroll file with ID checks for cash payroll. Supplier bank detail changes require enhanced verification (paper form + call-back). Recurring supplier payments via standing instructions only with formal contract and regular review.
Inter-office & inter-company transfers
Pre-approve transfers and use standard memos/supporting docs. Post CIT and clearing entries; reconcile inter-company accounts monthly. Ensure correct SOF/project mapping on transfers and document purpose.
System mapping & GL posting notes / Examples of journal entries
Map each bank account to the correct GL cash account and cost centre/project codes. When posting bank-related journals (charges, interest, transfers), attach bank statement copy and payment voucher ID. Examples: Bank deposit (Dr Bank / Cr Revenue or Debtors); Bank charge (Dr Bank charges / Cr Bank); Interest income (Dr Bank / Cr Interest); Suspense posting (temporary) only with documented reason and approval.
Bank signatory rotation & periodic review
Review signatory list at least annually; rotate signatories periodically per policy. Remove access promptly on role changes/leavers and update mandates with bank; keep evidence of date of change and authorising documentation.
Audit confirmations, external audit support & audit expectations

Expect auditors to test signature compliance, reconcile GL to bank, review stale items and test segregation of duties. Respond to audit queries promptly and retain evidence of corrective actions.
