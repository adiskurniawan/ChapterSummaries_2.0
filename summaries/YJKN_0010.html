Learning outcomes — success definitions & funder-aligned indicators (inputs → impact)
Primary learning outcomes (what learners will be able to do):
1) Define success for a research project at each stage: Input, Activity, Output, Outcome, Impact.
2) Design 3–5 measurable indicators for each stage aligned to funder expectations (UKRI/NIHR/Wellcome/REF styles).
3) Produce a short monitoring plan (indicator, data source, frequency, owner) that satisfies common UK funder requirements (data management, open access, EDI, value-for-money).
4) Critically appraise whether a proposed indicator is feasible, proportional and robust (avoids perverse incentives).
Stage definitions (one-line each)
Input: resources mobilised to enable work (funds, staff, equipment, data access, approvals).
Activity: the work you do (fieldwork, lab experiments, analysis, workshops, training).
Output: direct, tangible products of activities (papers, datasets, protocols, policy briefs, software).
Outcome: short–medium term changes in use, behaviour, policy, capability (adoption by services, uptake in guidelines, trained staff applying skills).
Impact: longer-term, sustained change in society, economy, health, environment or systems attributable (reduced disease burden, changed policy, economic benefit).
Success indicators — illustrative examples by stage (measurable & funder-friendly)
Inputs (examples): Budget variance ≤10%; named staff in post by project month 1; secure data access agreement signed before analysis.
Activities (examples): # participants recruited per month; % protocol adherence across sites; number of co-design workshops delivered.
Outputs (examples): peer-reviewed articles (OA) published; dataset with DOI deposited; policy brief disseminated to X stakeholders.
Outcomes (examples): # clinical sites adopting protocol within 12 months; % increase in practitioner knowledge (pre/post); citation of report in a governmental review.
Impact (examples): change in service metrics (e.g., 12% reduction in waiting time within 36 months), documented cost-savings to health system, contribution to a statutory guideline.
Indicator design rules (simple checklist)
- Specific: clearly states what is measured.
- Measurable: has a numeric or binary metric.
- Achievable: realistic given resources & timeline.
- Relevant: links to project theory of change and funder priorities.
- Timebound: defined measurement period.
- Proportionality: avoid heavy reporting burden on small partners.
How UK funders typically frame success (what to reflect in indicators)
- Pathways to impact / planning for impact: include who will use outputs and how (policy makers, clinicians, industry). (Include a short "pathway" narrative with each outcome indicator.)
- Open research & data management: outputs must plan for open access and data stewardship (DOI, repository, DMP).
- Research integrity & ethics: demonstrable approvals, safeguarding & ethical compliance.
- Value-for-money & feasibility: realistic budgets, staged spending, milestones linked to tranches.
- Equality, diversity & inclusion (EDI): EDI actions and monitoring where relevant (e.g., representative recruitment targets, authorship fairness).
Typical evidence types funders expect (mapped to stage)
- Inputs: signed contracts, CVs, budget spreadsheets, DSA/agreements.
- Activities: attendance lists, lab logs, protocols, training records.
- Outputs: DOIs, publication links, software repo links, downloadable reports.
- Outcomes: uptake letters, policy citations, monitoring dashboards, pre/post evaluation data.
- Impact: independent evaluations, routine administrative data showing system change, economic appraisal.

Selecting indicators for UK funder expectations — practical rules
1) Start with funder guidance: map funder priorities (impact, open access, ethics) to your outcomes.
2) Choose 1–2 primary indicators per stage and 1–2 risk/process indicators.
3) Use qualitative evidence for outcomes/impact as well as quantitative.
4) Pre-register data sources and ownership to avoid surprise audits.
Common KPIs funders ask for (short list to include where relevant)
- Number of outputs (OA papers / datasets with DOIs).
- Engagement events with named policy/practice stakeholders.
- Documented adopters (organisations/sites) of an intervention within X months.
- Demonstrable capacity built (e.g., number trained + evidence of application).
- Data management compliance (DMP approved, data deposited).
- Ethical approvals in place and safeguarding training completed.
- Value-for-money indicators: spend vs planned milestone, % budget on local partners (for equitable partnerships).
Measurement methods & frequency — pragmatic guidance
- Activity monitoring: weekly or monthly trackers for recruitment, lab runs, workshop attendance.
- Output verification: repository checks at publication; automated DOI capture.
- Outcome assessment: short surveys, stakeholder interviews at 6–12 months post-output; routine data linkage where possible.
- Impact assessment: commissioning an external evaluation between 24–48 months where feasible, and using administrative datasets for long-term metrics.
How to write indicators that survive review (language examples)
- Weak: "Improve practice."
- Strong: "Increase the proportion of participating clinics implementing protocol X from 0% to 40% within 12 months (measured by monthly implementation checklist)."
- Weak: "Engage policymakers."
- Strong: "Hold 3 bilateral briefings with named decision-makers in Department Y and obtain written feedback or agreed next steps."
Risk & process indicators to include (funders expect governance flags)
- Ethics approval obtained by [date].
- Data-sharing agreement signed before any data extraction.
- % of local partner invoices paid within agreed terms.
- % milestone deliverables on time.
Equity & EDI indicators (sample, funder-relevant)
- Proportion of project budget allocated to local partners (%) and admin support lines.
- Gender/age/ethnic representation targets in study recruitment or advisory boards where relevant.
- Number of junior researchers from partner institutions with named authorship roles or training.
Mapping indicators to typical funder reporting cycles (practical)
- Quarterly reports: activity & process KPIs, finance snapshot, immediate risks.
- Mid-term report (6–12 months): outputs produced, preliminary outcomes, corrective actions.
- Final report: outputs, outcomes achieved, evidence of impact pathways, lessons learned.
- Longer-term impact reporting (where required): follow-up evidence (REF/NIHR/UKRI impact statements or equivalent).
Worked example (short: applied to a health intervention trial)
- Inputs: staff recruited (3/3 by month 1). KPI: staff in post = 3 (target met).
- Activities: 12 site training workshops delivered (KPI: 12 workshops; attendance lists).
- Outputs: protocol paper published OA (KPI: DOI by month 10); dataset deposited (KPI: DOI).
- Outcomes: 6 clinics adopt intervention within 12 months (KPI: documented adoption letters).
- Impact: reduction in readmission rate by X% at 36 months (KPI: routine data comparison adjusted for confounders).
Evidence quality & triangulation — funder expectations
- Combine routine administrative data, independent evaluation, stakeholder testimony and documented outputs to make a persuasive evidence bundle.
- Avoid single-point claims (e.g., one testimonial) — triangulate with data or documentary evidence.
Short plan to operationalise these learning outcomes in a training session
1) 20-min micro-lecture: stage definitions + funder priorities. 2) 30-min exercise: small groups draft 2 indicators for a provided project brief. 3) 20-min peer critique: check feasibility/proportionality. 4) 20-min plenary: map indicators to reporting cycle. 5) Provide a downloadable monitoring-plan template.
Quick checks before you finalise indicators for a funder application
- Are indicators linked to the project's theory of change? (Yes/No)
- Are data sources realistic and owned? (Yes/No)
- Is reporting burden reasonable for partners? (Yes/No)
- Do indicators reflect funder priorities (impact, open access, EDI)? (Yes/No)
- Are owners and timelines assigned? (Yes/No)
Short reading & resources to consult (starter list)
- UKRI evaluation and impact guidance for planning and evaluation.
- NIHR materials on assessing impact plans and responsible metrics.
- Wellcome guidance on research assessment, open research and research integrity.
- REF guidance on engagement and impact (where outputs underpin institutional assessments).
One-line aide-memo for learners:
"Define success at each stage, pick a small number of measurable indicators tied to the pathway-to-impact, make evidence sources explicit, and protect partner capacity by keeping monitoring proportionate."

Logic model — Pharmacological assessment project (idea → impact)
Context & one-line stage definitions
Input: resources mobilised to enable work (funds, staff, equipment, data access, approvals).
Activity: the work you do (fieldwork, lab experiments, analysis, workshops, training).
Output: direct, tangible products of activities (papers, datasets, protocols, policy briefs, software).
Outcome: short–medium term changes in use, behaviour, policy, capability (adoption by services, uptake in guidelines, trained staff applying skills).
Impact: longer-term, sustained change in society, economy, health, environment or systems attributable (reduced disease burden, changed policy, economic benefit).
Project summary (one line): Assess the safety, effectiveness and real-world use of a pharmacological intervention and produce evidence and tools for safer, more effective prescribing and policy adoption.
Inputs (examples tailored to pharmacology)
- Funding (phases: pilot, pivotal, dissemination).
- Human resources: clinical pharmacologists, pharmacovigilance specialists, clinicians, biostatisticians, data engineers, trial coordinators, regulatory/legal advisor, community liaison.
- Infrastructure: clinical sites, labs (bioanalysis/PK), cold chain, secure data environment, EHR access agreements.
- Approvals & governance: ethics committee, national drug regulator (if needed), data-sharing agreements, indemnity.
- Materials: investigational product (if interventional), comparator drugs, assay kits, consumables.
- Partnerships: hospitals, primary-care networks, public health agencies, patient groups.
Key activities (typical workstreams)
- Protocol & SOP development (clinical, lab, data).
- Regulatory & ethics submissions and approvals.
- Site selection & training (GCP, safety reporting).
- Data access setup (EHR extract, linkage, pseudonymisation).
- Laboratory assays (PK/PD, biomarker validation).
- Clinical data collection or trial conduct (observational cohorts, RCT or pragmatic trials).
- Pharmacovigilance & safety monitoring (AE reporting, DSMB reviews).
- Statistical analysis (pre-specified analysis plan, population definitions).
- Stakeholder engagement & co-design (clinicians, guideline bodies, patients).
- Dissemination & implementation planning (policy briefs, training).
Immediate outputs (tangible deliverables)
- Study protocol, SOPs, data management plan.
- Cleaned, documented dataset(s) and code (repo + DOI).
- PK/PD assay validation reports and lab SOPs.
- Safety reports and DSMB minutes.
- Statistical analysis report and preprint / peer-reviewed article(s).
- Policy brief, clinical guidance draft, and clinician training materials.
- Data-sharing package and regulatory submission dossiers (if required).
Short–medium outcomes (use & adoption)
- Clinician uptake: guideline or formulary adoption in pilot regions.
- Practice change: measurable change in prescribing patterns or dosing.
- Improved clinical pathways: reduced inappropriate use or better monitoring.
- Capability: trained local staff performing PK assays or safety monitoring.
- Policy traction: regulator or payer engages with evidence for label/coverage change.
Impact (longer-term, measurable change)
- Reduced adverse drug reactions and hospital admissions attributable to medication.
- Improved patient outcomes (disease-specific metrics).
- Cost-savings for health services via optimized prescribing.
- Sustained integration of monitoring in routine care and updated national guidance.
Indicators & example metrics (mapped to model levels)
Inputs: funding disbursed vs plan (%), staff FTE onboarded, number of site agreements signed.
Activities: % sites activated, % protocol adherence, number of samples processed/month.
Outputs: datasets published (count), code repo with DOI (yes/no), number of peer-reviewed articles, number of policy brief downloads.
Short outcomes: % clinicians adopting guidance in pilot region, change in prescribing rate (%) within 6 months, number trained applying new protocol.
Impact: change in ADE-related hospitalisations per 1000 patients over 24 months; QALYs or DALYs averted if applicable; budget impact for health service.
Assumptions (what must hold true)
- Data access agreements and high-quality data are available.
- Regulatory and ethics approvals are granted without major changes to protocol.
- Clinical sites have capacity and staff willingness to collect required data.
- Stakeholders (clinicians, policymakers) will engage with co-design and implementation.
Risks & mitigations (practical examples)
Risk: delayed regulatory/ethics approvals → Mitigation: parallel submission prep, conservative timelines, early regulator engagement.
Risk: data linkage impossible or poor quality → Mitigation: plan alternate data sources, sample-based validation, stepwise analysis plan.
Risk: safety signal requiring pause → Mitigation: DSMB charter, pre-defined stopping rules, rapid communication plan and contingency budget for additional investigations.
Risk: low recruitment/participation → Mitigation: broaden inclusion sites, community engagement, small incentives, adaptive design.
Decision points & go/no-go criteria (operational triggers)
- Go to pilot: protocol finalised, ethics/regulatory positive opinion, at least X sites activated, data pipeline tested end-to-end.
- Interim safety review: pre-specified thresholds for SAE rates; if exceeded, pause and investigate.
- Analysis decision: pre-specified effect size and confidence interval thresholds for moving from pilot to scale; if not met, trigger redesign or targeted implementation.
Evaluation & attribution strategy
- Use mixed methods: quantitative (pre/post prescribing, interrupted time series, cohort comparisons) + qualitative (clinician interviews, implementation case studies).
- For causal claims, use design-appropriate approaches (RCT or quasi-experimental methods) and document contribution pathways when attribution is partial.
- Pre-register analysis plan and publish protocols to reduce bias.
Roles & responsibilities (recommended assignments)
- PI / Clinical Lead: strategy, regulator liaison, ultimate decisions.
- Project Manager: schedule, budget, coordination, records.
- Data Lead: data pipeline, linkage, environment.
- Lab Lead: assay validation, sample logistics.
- Safety Officer / DSMB Secretary: AE reporting, safety monitoring.
- Implementation Lead: stakeholder engagement, training, guideline drafting.
Timeline example (high-level phases)
- Months 0–3: set-up — protocol, approvals, site agreements, data environment.
- Months 3–9: pilot data collection, assay validation, interim safety checks.
- Months 9–15: analysis, stakeholder co-design of guidance.
- Months 15–24: dissemination, implementation pilot, evaluation of uptake.
- Months 24+: scale-up & impact monitoring. (Adapt to project scale and regulatory timelines.)
Budget priorities (must-lines for pharmacological work)
- Regulatory & ethics costs; secure data environment fees; lab assays & consumables; personnel (site coordinators, lab technicians, statisticians); site reimbursements and local admin; travel/meetings for stakeholder co-design; contingency for safety investigations.
Implementation supports (to turn outputs into outcomes)
- Clinician training modules and quick reference dosing cards.
- Decision support integrated into EHR (order sets, alerts).
- Policy briefings with economic modelling for payers.
- Local pilot champions and mentorship for sites adopting new protocols.
Monitoring dashboard suggestions (minimum fields)
- Project status (Red/Amber/Green), sites activated, samples processed, AEs reported (by severity), actions overdue, budget burn vs plan, next decision milestone. Update cadence weekly for ops, monthly for leadership.
Quick checklist for proposal text (copy-ready bullets)
- Objective: measure safety and real-world effectiveness of [drug/intervention] and produce implementable guidance.
- Primary indicators: SAE rate per 1000 patient-months; change in prescribing appropriateness (%) at 12 months.
- Key governance: DSMB, ethics approvals, data sharing MOU.
- Dissemination: peer-reviewed paper, policy brief, EHR decision support package, clinician workshops.
Common pitfalls specific to pharmacological projects
- Underestimating regulatory timelines for drug-related work.
- Poorly specified safety monitoring leading to slow responses.
- Data heterogeneity across sites undermining pooled analysis—plan harmonisation up-front.
- Overlooking supply/logistics for investigational product or comparator.
One-line summary (project success criterion): Demonstrable, clinician-adopted, evidence-based change in prescribing or monitoring—supported by robust safety data and sustainable implementation pathways—leading to measurable patient benefit.

Outcomes & Impacts — pharmacological assessment project (focused operational guide)
Purpose & scope
Focus this section of the logic model on the measurable short, medium and long outcomes and the pathways to impact for a pharmacological assessment project. Provide indicators, evaluation designs, attribution approaches, equity lenses, risk mitigation and operational triggers that convert outputs (datasets, reports, guidance) into sustained improvements in prescribing, safety monitoring and patient outcomes.
Outcome definitions (one-line each)
Short outcomes (0–12 months): immediate changes in knowledge, availability of tools and early adoption signals (e.g., training completed; decision-support installed).
Medium outcomes (6–24 months): measurable changes in practice, policy or capability (e.g., prescribing patterns change, regulator engages, piloted guidelines adopted locally).
Long outcomes / impact (24+ months): sustained population-level or system-level changes (e.g., reduced ADE hospitalisations, cost-savings, guideline incorporation nationally).
Outcome pathway summary (chain of change)
Outputs (validated dataset, assay, guidance, training) → Implementation supports (EHR integration, training, champions) → Short outcomes (awareness, tools available, initial adoption) → Medium outcomes (practice change, policy adoption, monitored uptake) → Impact (health outcomes, economic benefits, system resilience). Each link needs indicators, owners, and evidence.
Key short outcomes — what to measure and why
1) Tool availability: Decision-support tool deployed in X pilot clinics — Indicator: deployment count (target X).
2) Training delivered: #clinicians trained and % passing competency check — Indicator: trainee count & competency pass rate.
3) Access to evidence: policy brief downloads, repo DOI hits — Indicator: downloads / repo clones.
4) Safety surveillance active: pharmacovigilance reporting channel live — Indicator: reports submitted per month.
Why: short outcomes signal readiness and capacity to act on outputs.
Key medium outcomes — what to measure and why
1) Prescribing change: % reduction in inappropriate prescriptions or adjusted dosing rate — Indicator: prescribing rate change vs baseline.
2) Guideline uptake: number of institutions adopting new protocol or formulary change — Indicator: adoption count & coverage.
3) Improved monitoring: % patients receiving recommended monitoring (labs, PK sampling) — Indicator: monitoring compliance.
4) Regulatory action/stance: regulator issues guidance or label change — Indicator: engagement milestone or formal communication.
Why: medium outcomes show behaviour and policy change required for impact.
Impact outcomes — measurable long-term change
1) Clinical outcomes: reduction in ADE-related admissions per 1,000 patients over 24 months.
2) Health gain: QALYs gained or DALYs averted (if estimable).
3) Economic outcomes: net cost-savings to payer (drug + hospital costs) or cost per QALY.
4) System resilience: routine pharmacovigilance sustained and integrated into national surveillance.
Why: impact demonstrates value and justifies scale and policy changes.
Indicator design — principles
- SMART: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound.
- Feasible: data source exists or is collectable without excessive burden.
- Balanced: include process, output, outcome and impact indicators.
- Equity-disaggregated: by age, sex, geography, socio-economic status to detect unequal effects.
Example indicator matrix (short → impact)
- Short: Clinician training count → Source: training registers.
- Short: Decision-support installed (n clinics) → Source: IT logs.
- Medium: % appropriate prescriptions (6 months) → Source: EHR extracts.
- Medium: Guideline adoption (policy memo) → Source: institutional records.
- Impact: ADE admission rate per 1,000 (24 months) → Source: hospital admin data.
Data sources & quality notes
- EHR / prescribing databases: primary source for prescribing, monitoring and outcomes. Validate coding and denominators.
- Hospital administrative data: ADE admissions, length-of-stay. Check ICD/diagnosis mapping.
- Pharmacovigilance reports: SAE counts; may under-report — use triangulation.
- Training databases & surveys: measure self-reported behaviour change but triangulate with prescribing data.
- Economic data: unit costs, drug prices, hospital costing templates required for budget impact modelling.
Measurement methods & evaluation designs
- Randomised designs (gold standard): cluster RCT if interventions can be randomised (e.g., messaging, decision-support). Outcomes: prescribing rates, ADEs. Use intention-to-treat analysis.
- Quasi-experimental: stepped-wedge, interrupted time series (ITS) for phased rollout; appropriate when randomisation infeasible.
- Observational: cohort or case-control with careful confounder control (propensity scores).
- Mixed-methods: qualitative process evaluation to explain adoption, barriers and fidelity.
- Economic evaluation: trial-based cost-effectiveness or modelled cost-utility (QALY).
Attribution & contribution strategies
- For outputs → short outcomes: direct linkage plausible; use usage logs and immediate pre/post comparisons.
- For medium outcomes: use ITS or controlled before-after designs where possible; document alternative explanations.
- For impact: build a contribution narrative supported by quantitative estimates, sensitivity analysis, and stakeholder testimony; include counterfactuals where feasible.
- Always pre-register analysis plan and assumptions.
Equity & distributional outcomes
- Measure differential uptake and impact by geography, income, gender, ethnicity/indigeneity, and facility type.
- Indicators: adoption rates per subgroup, change in prescribing inequities, access to monitoring.
- Include targeted mitigation (subsidies, training, connectivity) when early signals show unequal uptake.
Unintended consequences to monitor
- Risk of switching harms (replacing one drug with another causing other ADEs).
- Alert fatigue from decision-support leading to override behaviour.
- Inequitable access (richer hospitals adopt faster).
- Perverse prescribing behaviour to meet metrics.
Mitigation: monitor for signal patterns, iterative design, clinician feedback loops, adjust thresholds.
Fidelity & process outcomes
- Fidelity metrics: % adherence to protocol, % sites implementing SOPs fully.
- Dose-delivered vs dose-received for training.
- Contextual process measures: staffing levels, supply stability, IT uptime.
Why: poor fidelity explains lack of outcome even when outputs exist.
Economic & budget impact outcomes
- Short-term costs incurred: implementation, training, IT integration.
- Medium-term budget impact: drug cost changes, monitoring costs, potential savings from avoided ADEs.
- Impact-level: net budget impact and cost-effectiveness (cost per QALY).
Metrics: incremental cost-effectiveness ratio (ICER), net monetary benefit, payback period.
Policy & governance outcomes
- Evidence uptake by guideline committees: number of meetings engaging evidence, official citations in guidance.
- Procurement/formulary decisions indicating coverage.
- Embedding monitoring responsibilities into institutional SOPs.
Indicators: date of guideline update, wording changes referencing project outputs, budget allocation lines created.
Capacity-building outcomes
- Number of staff certified in PK/PD assays or safety monitoring.
- Local lab capability sustained post-project (assay runs/month).
- Knowledge transfer measured via co-authored outputs or new local protocols.
Behavior change & clinician adoption outcomes
- Adoption curve: early adopters → majority → laggards tracked via monthly prescribing data.
- Clinician behaviour indicators: proportion of clinicians following new dosing, percentage using decision-support.
- Sustainment: adherence measured at 6, 12, 24 months post-intervention.
Patient-level outcomes & experience
- Clinical endpoints: symptom control metrics, adverse event rates, readmissions.
- Patient-reported outcomes: satisfaction, adherence, side-effect burden.
- Equity lens: differential patient outcomes by subgroup.
Scaling & sustainability outcomes
- Demonstrated feasibility of handover to local systems (technical, financial).
- Funding or budget lines secured for ongoing monitoring.
- Institutional champions and maintenance plans in place.
Indicators: handover plan signed, budget lines allocated, helpdesk/support arrangement active.
Decision triggers & operational thresholds (examples)
- Continue to scale: pilot effect size ≥ pre-specified threshold AND safety signal absent.
- Pause: SAE rate exceeds threshold or data integrity fails.
- Redesign: adoption below threshold and fidelity < 60% with identifiable causes.
- Stop: irreconcilable ethical/regulatory barrier or sustained net harm.
Reporting cadence & audiences
- Operational dashboard: weekly ops for sites (SAEs, recruitment, actions).
- Leadership report: monthly RAG summary + key risks.
- Policy briefs: staged at analysis completion and pre-scale.
- Public outputs: peer-reviewed publication and open datasets on archive (with DOI).
Evaluation plan — minimum components
- Pre-specified primary and secondary outcome measures with analysis methods.
- Data management plan and provenance for each indicator.
- Power/sample calculations for main outcomes (if inferential).
- Mixed-methods process evaluation to explain outcomes.
- Economic evaluation plan.
Sample evaluation timeline (focus on outcomes)
- Baseline data extraction: month -1 to 0.
- Short outcome measurement: months 3, 6, 12 (training uptake, tool usage).
- Medium outcome measurement: 6–18 months (prescribing changes, guideline adoption).
- Impact measurement: 24+ months (ADE admissions, cost-savings).
Causal inference checklist (to support attribution)
- Clear pre/post timelines and intervention start date.
- Control or comparison sites where feasible.
- Adjustment for secular trends and seasonality (ITS).
- Sensitivity analyses (different model specifications).
- Triangulation with qualitative evidence.
Stakeholder & communication outcomes
- Stakeholder engagement: number & diversity of stakeholders consulted, signed MOUs.
- Communication efficacy: policy brief distribution metrics and targeted meetings completed.
- Media & public engagement metrics where relevant (reach, sentiment).
Ethics & safety outcome reporting
- Timeliness of SAE reporting (median reporting lag).
- Number of safety-related protocol amendments and reasons.
- Evidence of enacted mitigations from DSMB recommendations.
Equity & harm-minimisation outcomes
- Monitoring for differential harms by subgroup and rapid mitigation plans (e.g., targeted outreach, subsidy for monitoring costs).
- Outcome: reduction in inequity gap in prescribing or ADEs over time.
Data governance & privacy outcomes
- Number of successful secure analyses completed in approved environment.
- Breach incidence (target zero) and response time.
- Data sharing agreements executed and tracked.
Practical KPIs (dashboard-ready)
- % clinicians trained (target X%).
- % sites with decision-support active (target X%).
- % appropriate prescribing change (target % reduction).
- SAE rate per 1,000 (target threshold).
- Time to policy engagement (days from analysis to meeting).
Templates for outcome targets (copy-ready)
- "By month 12, increase appropriate prescribing rate in pilot hospitals by 20% (baseline X% → target Y%)."
- "By month 18, at least 3 regional guideline committees will have received and considered the evidence; one will formally adopt wording into local guidance."
Risk-adjusted interpretation of outcomes
- Always contextualise outcome changes by fidelity, supply constraints and external shocks (e.g., drug shortages).
- Use adjusted models and report both crude and adjusted estimates.
Rapid feedback & iterative improvement loop
- Use monthly outcome reviews to identify low-adoption sites and deploy targeted remedial actions (training, IT fixes).
- Document change cycles and measure post-intervention effects to show improvement over time.
Cost-effectiveness & budget-impact outputs to support impact claims
- Present ICERs, net budget impact and scenario analyses (best, base, worst).
- Use local costing data and sensitivity analysis for generalisability.
Policy translation & impact evidence package
- Combine: executive summary, short evidence brief, economic model, implementation guide, slide deck, and local pilot case study.
- Include clear ask for policymakers (e.g., "adopt guideline X in these settings and allocate Y funding").
Sustainability indicators (handover success)
- % of pilot sites with allocated maintenance budget 12 months post-project.
- % of trained staff still performing tasks at 12 months.
- Local ownership: number of local-led adaptations and publications.
Final checklist — outcomes to demonstrate project success
- Short outcomes: tools deployed, staff trained, surveillance active.
- Medium outcomes: measurable prescribing change, guideline adoption, regulator engagement.
- Impact: reduced ADE admissions, improved patient outcomes, cost-savings.
- All layers: include equity metrics, fidelity assessment, and documented causal narrative.

Why a contract is needed — improved operational guide (organized by your outline)
Purpose (short): A contract turns mutual expectations into enforceable, auditable commitments so teams can act with clarity, protect assets, manage risk, and sustain collaboration. Contracts are governance tools for decisions, money, data, IP and reputation.
Core reasons mapped to your outline
Clarity & responsibilities: precisely states who does what, deliverable definitions, acceptance criteria, owners and deadlines so operational ambiguity is eliminated.
Protect intellectual property: defines foreground/background IP, ownership, licensing, commercialisation rights, moral rights and review/embargo windows for manuscripts.
Ensure legal & compliance: sets governing law, authorised signatories, sanctions/PEP checks, export controls, ethics/IRB obligations and compliance reporting.
Manage risk & liability: allocates financial and operational risk, liability caps, indemnities, insurance requirements, and termination triggers.
Control data usage: specifies data access, permitted uses, secure environments, retention/deletion, audit logs, breach notification and cross-border rules.
Enable collaboration: specifies decision-making bodies, authorship rules, dispute resolution, steering/management structures, and resource-sharing rules.
Sustainability & stewardship: requires long-term stewardship (data, sample + code), archiving, maintenance responsibilities, and handover plans.
Protect institutional interests: confirms institutional sign-off, fund-flow routing, procurement rules, tax compliance and audit rights.
Anatomy of a good research/partnership contract (layered view)
Framework layer (governance & metadata): parties, authorised signatories, term, governing law, definitions, annex list, insurance summary, and high-level decision/governance map (steering group, PM, WP leads).
Scope & responsibilities (Statement of Work / SOW): clear deliverables, acceptance tests, milestones, outputs, owner per deliverable, timeline and budget lines.
Provisions (commercial & protective clauses): payment schedule, invoicing, audit rights, change control, termination, confidentiality, sanctions/compliance, force majeure, indemnities and insurance.
Publication & dissemination: authorship criteria, review windows, embargo periods, acknowledgement text, communications protocol and approval process for public statements.
Data & materials management: DSA/Annex: data types, access environment, permitted uses, anonymisation/pseudonymisation, retention, destruction, transfer, archiving repository, sample handling (if biological), and breach processes.
Risk management & controls: risk register references, remedial actions, milestone-linked holdbacks/escrow, performance remedies, and conditional payment triggers.
Concrete contract clauses & short explanations
Scope / acceptance: "Deliverable D accepted when [test], acceptance notice within 10 business days or deemed accepted."
Payment & control: "Payments: 30% on start, 40% on delivery, 30% on acceptance. Payments conditional on receipt of evidence and cleared audit."
IP & publications: "Foreground IP jointly owned / licensed as follows. Publication requires 30 days' review for confidential data and comments must be reasonable."
Data security: "Data processed in approved secure environment; access limited to named staff; exports require written permission; breach notified within 72 hours."
Liability & indemnity: "Liability capped at [amount], excluding wilful misconduct. Each party indemnifies for negligent acts and third-party claims arising from its breach."
Termination & suspension: "Material breach with 30-day cure; funder/PI may suspend payments pending remediation."
Contract types — when to use each (short guidance)
Funding agreement (funder → recipient): use for grants; prescriptive reporting, audit, allowable costs, branding, and termination for funder non-compliance.
Collaboration agreement / consortium agreement: use when multiple partners co-design and co-own outputs; emphasises governance, IP sharing, authorship rules, decision procedures and dispute resolution.
Subaward / subcontract: use when passing funds or services through a prime; ensure flow-down of data, audit, IP and termination clauses; prime remains ultimately accountable to funder.
How contracts achieve your eight objectives (practical checks)

Intellectual property (IP) in research — operational guidance
Definition (short): IP = legally or commercially protectable outputs from research (patents, copyright, database rights, designs, trade secrets, know-how, materials, trademarks). In research contexts IP management balances openness (scholarship) with protection (commercialisation, compliance, stewardship).
Why IP matters for research projects:
- Protects downstream commercial value and incentivises translation.
- Clarifies ownership, preventing disputes that can delay publication or funding.
- Satisfies funder and institutional compliance (reporting, benefit-sharing).
- Enables controlled sharing (licenses, MTAs) that protect participants and data subjects.
Common IP types you will meet:
- Patents (inventions): new, inventive, industrially applicable technical inventions.
- Copyright: text, figures, software source code, slide decks, reports.
- Database rights / sui generis: curated datasets and structured collections.
- Designs & models: industrial designs, CAD models.
- Trade secrets / know-how: non-public methods, protocols, algorithms.
- Materials & biological samples: require MTAs.
- Trademarks & brand: project names, logos.
Background vs foreground IP (crucial distinction):
- Background IP: pre-existing IP contributed into the project (licence needed to use).
- Foreground IP: new IP created during the project (ownership must be assigned, licensed, or shared).
Practical: always capture a background inventory at project start and require partners to declare restrictions.
Who usually owns IP? (typical rules & variations)
- Employment / institutional policies: most universities assign invention ownership to the institution with revenue-share to inventors.
- Contract/funder rules: funder agreements may require specific ownership/licensing outcomes (open access, retained rights).
- Collaborations: joint ownership is possible but complex—prefer licensed arrangements or clear assignment rules.
- Small local partners / freelancers: confirm contractual assignment and signatory authority.
Joint ownership pitfalls (practical warnings):
- Joint ownership without clear licence rules leads to blocking rights and litigation risk.
- Avoid unspecified joint ownership; prefer: (a) one owner + cross-licence terms, or (b) defined field/territory/time licences.
Publication vs patent tension (essential operating rule):
- Public disclosure (conference, preprint, thesis) can destroy novelty for patents.
- Rule: disclose inventions to Technology Transfer Office (TTO) / IP lead BEFORE public disclosure. Use provisional filings to protect patentability while allowing time for publication planning.
Data, software & code (practical rules):
- Treat software as copyright + potential patent if it embodies a technical invention.
- Choose licences deliberately: permissive (MIT/BSD) vs copyleft (GPL) vs custom research licences.
- For datasets use clear data licences (e.g., CC0/CC BY/ODC-BY) and note funder open-access requirements.
- Maintain reproducible code, version control and clear contributor records for authorship/IP clarity.
Materials & biological samples (controls):
- Use Material Transfer Agreements (MTAs) to control use, restrictions, benefit sharing and IP arising from downstream use.
- Capture chain-of-custody, consent terms and local regulations for genetic resources (CBD / Nagoya principles may apply).
Confidentiality & NDAs (when to use):
- Use NDAs only where necessary (commercial discussions, early tech disclosure).
- NDAs should be time-bounded, specify permitted disclosures and permit disclosure to institutional TTO under safe terms. Avoid NDAs that prevent publication by academics without clear exceptions.
Licensing models (how you can share IP):
- Non-exclusive licence: typical for research reuse, commercialization open to multiple licensees.
- Exclusive licence: one licensee for a field/territory — better monetisation but restricts research use.
- Field/territory/time-limited licences: balance revenue and academic freedom.
- Open licences (code/data): enable reuse and reproducibility; ensure contributor consent and no donor restrictions.
Revenue & benefit sharing (practical patterns):
- Institutional revenue share: standard university formula (inventor share + central reinvestment).
- Collaborative projects: pre-agree revenue splits, reinvestment into project/community and capacity building.
- For community or indigenous contributions plan benefit-sharing and prior informed consent.
IP management workflow (operational steps)
1) Identify: invention disclosure, code/data/material declarations during project lifecycle.
2) Record: log in IP register with date, contributors, context.
3) Assess: TTO/legal performs novelty, freedom-to-operate, and market/impact scan.
4) Protect: patent filing, copyright registration, trade-secret controls, MTAs or NDAs as needed.
5) Manage: assign steward (TTO), negotiate licences, subaward flow-downs.
6) Commercialise / steward: license, spin-out or place in repository with stewardship plan.
7) Close & archive: final reports, repository deposits, licensing records.

- Disclosure & patent filing clause: mandatory invention disclosure to TTO; publication hold duration for patent filing (e.g., 30 days).
- Licence grant clause: background licence to partners for project purposes; post-project licensing terms.
- Moral rights & authorship: recognition and review periods for manuscripts.

- Before signing collaboration, licence, MTA, DSA, subcontract or commercial deal.
- Prior to public disclosure or conference presentations.
- Before accepting third-party code/material into your pipeline.
- When a commercial partner or investor requests IP certainty.
High-level outcomes of good DD:
- Clear inventory of background IP and encumbrances.
- Freedom-to-operate risk assessment + mitigation plan.
- Confirmed authorised signatory for transfers/assignments.
- Contractual routes (licences, MTAs, assignment wording) ready to flow-down.
- Decision: Proceed / Proceed-with-conditions / Defer / Decline.
Scope — what to check (domains):
1) Background IP inventory & licence terms.
2) Foreground IP assignment policy (institutional).
3) Publication/patent conflict & novelty risk.
4) Third-party code/data licences (OSS compliance).
5) MTAs, DSAs and consent terms for samples/data.
6) Export control, sanctions & regulatory restrictions.
7) Existing agreements, encumbrances, liens or exclusive licences.
8) Staff/student/consultant agreements and inventor declarations.

- Background IP list (short inventory) + proof of ownership.

- Identify viral licences (GPL, AGPL) and their implications.
- Confirm contributor agreements or CLA for external code.

Data & materials checks:
- Confirm consent covers intended reuse/commercialisation.
- Check Nagoya/CBD obligations for genetic resources.

- Verify institutional policy re: employee inventions.
- Check student thesis/publication timelines and embargo options.
- For contractors: confirm signed IP assignment or work-for-hire clauses.
- Confirm transfer/assignment authority for signatories.
Third-party encumbrances & existing licences:
- Search for exclusive licences on core tech; request licence abstracts.

Publication & patentability checkpoint:
- Require invention disclosure before any public talk/manuscript.
- If invention likely patentable, provisional filing or publication hold (typical 30 days) before public release.
Export control, sanctions & regulatory checks:
- Identify whether tech/data falls under dual-use, encryption, biological agents or restricted chemicals.
- Run sanctions/PEP checks on signatories.
- Flag cross-border transfer restrictions early.

- Score domains 1 (low) / 2 (medium) / 3 (high).
- Weight data/third-party licences higher where project depends on them.
- Aggregate to decision bands: <=1.4 accept; 1.5–2.0 accept-with-conditions; >2.0 escalate/defer/decline.

Nagoya Protocol — concise operational briefing
What it is (short): An international agreement under the Convention on Biological Diversity that creates a framework for access to genetic resources and fair & equitable sharing of benefits arising from their use (ABS). It establishes obligations for users and providers and creates the ABS Clearing-House for transparency. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}
Scope — what it covers (practical):
• Genetic resources and associated traditional knowledge when used for research, development or commercialisation.
• Does not apply to human genetic resources.
• National implementing measures determine domestic scope, definitions and permit processes — so applicability depends on the provider country’s law. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}
Core legal requirements (plain language):
1) Prior Informed Consent (PIC) — obtain permission from the provider (country or authorised body) before access where national law requires it.
2) Mutually Agreed Terms (MAT) — negotiate and record benefit-sharing terms (monetary or non-monetary) in writing (contract).
3) Compliance / check & report: users must be able to show due diligence and evidence that access complied with PIC & MAT (ABS Clearing-House and national checkpoints). :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}
ABS Clearing-House (what to use it for):
• Primary international registry for party measures, national contact points, checklists, and certificates of compliance.
• Use it to identify legal requirements and national focal points before any fieldwork or acquisition. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}
Digital Sequence Information (DSI) — current practical note:
• The Protocol’s material scope applies to physical genetic resources; DSI (digitised sequence data) has been debated at CBD COP processes and may be addressed by separate/parallel measures — check current negotiations and funder/national policy before relying on an exemption. :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}
Why it matters for researchers (concrete impacts):
- Legal obligation to comply or face sanctions, withheld funding or reputational harm.
- Affects sample collection, export/import permits, data sharing, publication timing and collaborations.
- Non-compliance can block downstream commercialisation or journal acceptance if data provenance is required. :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}
Practical due-diligence checklist for researchers (before fieldwork or acquiring materials):
1) Check ABS Clearing-House for provider country measures and national focal point. :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}
2) Confirm whether the resource is within Nagoya scope (species, use, and country rule) and whether DSI rules affect your plan. :contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7}
3) Obtain PIC or confirm that access is permitted without PIC under national law.
4) Negotiate MAT (written contract) specifying permitted uses, benefit-sharing, IP, authorship, capacity building, and commercial terms.
5) Secure export/import permits and any institutional approvals (ethics, biosafety).
6) Record everything: permit copies, PIC/MAT, correspondence; log in project’s ABS register.

- Practical clauses: sample disposal, data retention, dispute resolution, and contact points. :contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9}
