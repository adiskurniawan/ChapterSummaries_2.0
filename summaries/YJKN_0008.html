Communication workshop — engagement guidance
Purpose:
Goal: Strengthen communication skills that reduce errors, speed approvals and improve stakeholder trust.
Outcomes: Participants leave with (a) 3 communication commitments, (b) one revised template (email or status line), (c) a plan to reduce rework from unclear requests.
Success criteria: 90% of emails regarding payment or exceptions include required metadata (EventCode, LineID, Action, Due).
Pre-work (logistics & materials):
1) Confirm attendees and their communication pain points.
2) Collect 3 anonymised examples of unclear emails or chat threads.
3) Prepare: agenda, projector, printed email templates, chat etiquette one-pager.
4) Print: standard subject line cheat-sheet, status-line sticky notes.
5) Tech check: ensure participants can open shared tracking sheet.
Opening script (concise + variants):
Core: "Today we focus on clear, evidence-first communication that reduces rework and audit risk. We will practice writing and speaking precisely, and leave with templates you use tomorrow."
Short variant: State outcomes, set norms (subject lines + one-line status updates).
Agenda (timed segments):
1) Framing: why communication matters for controls — 10 min.
2) Principles & templates — 20 min.
3) Writing practice — 20 min.
4) Voice & in-person clarity — 15 min.
5) Casework (3 x 10 min) — 30 min.
6) Commitments & close — 10 min.
Framing: communication priorities (subheads):
Accuracy: avoid ambiguous amounts, dates, references.
Traceability: include LineID, EventCode, file path or index.
Brevity with evidence: one-line summary + attachments/links.
Core communication principles (each with subpoints):
1) Subject-first clarity: include EventCode, short issue, owner and due in subject.
2) One-line summary: first sentence states Issue, Impact and Requested Action.
3) Evidence-link habit: always include a path or index to supporting doc.
4) Option-based escalation: present recommended fix plus 1 alternate.
5) Close-the-loop updates: one-line completion message with link to evidence.
Email templates & structure (copy-ready):
Template A — Escalation email:
Subject: [Escalation] [EventCode] — [Short issue] — Action by [date].
Body:
1) One-line summary (Issue, Impact, Ask).
2) Evidence link(s) (Archive path / AuditIndex).
3) Options (2) + recommended option (brief rationale).
4) Owner & due (Name — YYYY-MM-DD).
5) Next step if no reply (e.g., escalate to CFD after X hours/days).

Template B — Payment request:
Subject: [Payment] [EventCode] [Supplier] [Amount] — Ready to pay.
Body:
- One-line approval request.
- ControlSheet LineID: [CTL-xxxxx].
- BatchID: [BBYYYYMMDD_NNN].
- EvidencePath / AuditIndex: [path or IndexNo].
- Special notes (VAT, withholding, urgency).
Chat & IM etiquette (fast rules):
- Use chat for non-record decisions and rapid clarifications only.
- Summarise any decision made in chat into the tracker or via email within 24h.
- For approvals, require a short written confirmation: "Approved — [Name] [Date]".
Document naming & links (enforcement):
- Filename token: {EventCode}{DocType}{YYYYMMDD}{Supplier/Participant}{INVxxx}.pdf.
- Always paste archive path or AuditIndex number in the email.
- If file not yet archived, state "pending archive — expected by [date]".
Practical behaviours to model (examples + micro-skills):
- Start messages with the required metadata line: [EventCode] | [LineID] | [Owner] | [Due].
- When asking for info, specify format (e.g., "Please attach receipts as PDF, one per file, file name token required").
- Use bullet points for multiple asks and number them to make it easy to respond.
Facilitation techniques (to practice communication):
Write-then-share: 4-minute quiet writing of an email then peer review.
Cold-edit: edit a poorly written email into the template in 3 minutes.
Read-back: when receiving an instruction, repeat back the action, owner and due.
Case prompts (ready-to-run with expected actions & debrief):
Case 1 — Confusing payment request email:
- Prompt: craft a reply that clarifies missing LineID and evidence path and sets a 2-hour hold.
- Expected: short reply requesting LineID, proposed temporary hold and next step.
Case 2 — Chat approval with no written confirmation:
- Prompt: convert chat decision into formal confirmation for audit.
- Expected: email with quoted chat and explicit "Approved — [Name][Date]".
Case 3 — Vendor disputes amount in bank descriptor:
- Prompt: compose vendor update, evidence request and proposed reconciliation step.
Short scripts (precise language templates):
Clarify ask: "Please confirm LineID and attach receipts at this path: [path]. Without these we cannot process payment — hold until [time/date]."
Request for approval: "Approval requested: [one-line issue]. Evidence: [path]. Recommend option A (reason). Please respond 'Approved — [Name]' by [time]."
Acknowledge receipt of evidence: "Received — Evidence attached at [path]. Processing now. Owner: [name]."
Writing practice (exercises + timings):
Exercise A — Email rewrite: 6 min (write) + 4 min (peer review).
Exercise B — One-line status updates: 4 min to convert long status into 1-line canonical form.
Exercise C — Attachment verification drill: 6 min to verify three sample emails contain required metadata.
Voice & face-to-face communication (short rules):
- Open with context: "This is about EventCode X, LineID Y."
- State the ask and deadline in first 15 seconds.
- End with "Do you accept? (Yes/No) by [time]" and capture response in writing.
Handling unclear responses (process):
1) Ask one clarifying question only.
2) Propose a default action and ask for objection within a short SLA.
3) If no response, escalate per the escalation flow.
4) Record decision and rationale.
Closing: commitments & communication governance:
- Each attendee proposes one communication habit they will adopt (e.g., subject-line format, evidence habit).
- Capture habits in shared sheet: columns Owner | Habit | StartDate | CheckDate.
- Set a 2-week review for improvement and metrics.
Post-workshop deliverables (what to send, template):
1) One-page summary with chosen templates and agreed habits.
2) Revised email/chat templates as DOCX/PDF.
3) Shared tracking sheet link and date for 2-week check.
Follow-up workflow (to ensure adoption):
1) Within 24 hours: send templates and summary.
2) Owners update examples of their new emails in the tracker.
3) At 2-week check: sample 10 communications and report compliance rate.
4) Escalate persistent non-compliance items to CFD.
Quick templates (copy-ready):
One-line status: [EventCode] | [LineID] | [Status] | [Owner] | [Due] — [evidence link].
Approval reply: "Approved — [Name] [Date] — Evidence: [path]".
Follow-up reminder: "Reminder: [one-line ask]. Current status: [status]. Action required by [date]."
Role definitions for communication governance:
Communication Lead (workshop champion): maintains templates and enforces standards.
Accounting Manager: approves template changes and policy exceptions.
Payments Officer: ensures outgoing payment emails include required metadata.
DonorComplianceOwner: approves donor-facing language.
IT/Data Owner: ensures links and archives are accessible.
Risk matrix (top communication failures & mitigations):
Risk: Missing LineID in approval → payment ambiguity.
Mitigation: Block payment until LineID provided; send standard hold message.
Risk: Chat decision not recorded → audit gap.
Mitigation: Mandatory written confirmation within 24h appended to ControlSheet.
Risk: Incorrect evidence link → delays.
Mitigation: Verify link before sending, include ArchiveIndex.
Metrics & KPIs to monitor after workshop:
- % emails with required metadata.
- % chat decisions converted to written confirmation within 24h.
- Average number of clarification rounds per payment request.
- % commitments completed by due date.
Materials & checklists (handout-ready):
- Subject-line cheat-sheet.
- One-line status sticky note template.
- Evidence-link checklist.
- Chat-to-email conversion mini-guide.
Assessment & evaluation (short tools):
- End-of-session micro-survey (3 Qs: clarity, usefulness, one change).
- Two-week sample audit of 10 communications to measure compliance.
- Quarterly review of clarification rounds metric.
Training reinforcement plan (post-workshop cadence):
- Day 1: distribute templates.
- Week 1: peer-review of real emails in team meeting.
- Week 2: compliance check and short refresher.
- Month 3: integrate communication KPIs into performance conversations.
Annex A — sample emails to use in exercises:
Sample 1 — Payment request (poor): show bad example.
Sample 1 — Payment request (improved): show corrected template with LineID, evidence path, batch info.
Sample 2 — Chat decision (raw) vs converted email (formal).
Annex B — escalation language for donor & auditors:
- "For audit purposes, please confirm in writing: [one-line]. If unavailable, please provide remediation plan by [date]."
- "Donor query received: [one-line summary]. Proposed response: [draft]. Approve by [time]."
Annex C — technical link & archive rules for leaders:
- Always paste full archive path and IndexNo.
- If using cloud links, set access to the donor/auditor before sending and note access status in the email.
One-line aide-memo (printable):
Subject-first → One-line summary → Evidence link → Option + recommended action → Written confirmation.

Audience — operational guidance
Purpose: Clarify who must be reached, why they matter to the activity, and how to tailor message, channel and ask so the audience acts reliably.
Goal: Ensure each communication has a single primary audience, clear secondary audiences, and a defined expected response or behaviour for each.
Outcomes:
- Named primary audience and 1–2 secondary audiences for the task.
- Tailored one-line message for each audience.
- Channel & timing assigned.
- Acceptance criteria for the response.
Success criteria: > 90% of primary-audience recipients acknowledge or act within SLA;
primary-audience messages require ≤1 clarification round on average.
Pre-work (logistics & materials):
1) Produce a stakeholder map (see template below).
2) Collect contact-preferences and access constraints.
3) Draft one-line messages for up to 3 audience segments.
4) Prepare evidence links that each audience needs to decide.
Stakeholder mapping template (copy-ready):
Name | Role | Interest (High/Med/Low) | Influence (High/Med/Low) | Preferred channel | Decision required | SLA
Primary audience rules:
- Single owner per decision message.
- Message must specify action, due date, and consequence of no response.
- Use the audience's preferred channel for initial contact; confirm by written backup if decision impacts controls.
Secondary audience rules:
- Place on FYI only if they need context; limit CCs to reduce noise.
- If secondary may block the action, include them as reviewers with explicit review SLA.
Audience segmentation (practical buckets):
- Decision-makers (approve/reject).
- Implementers (do the work).
- Validators/auditors (need evidence).
- Inform/partners (need awareness).
Message tailoring (by audience):
- Decision-makers: 1-line summary + impact + recommended option + evidence link + ask (Approve/Reject) + due.
- Implementers: step-by-step tasks, owner, due, templates/links.
- Validators: evidence index, control references, acceptance criteria.
- Inform: one-line status and next visible milestone.
Channel & tone guidance:
- High-influence/low-availability: use direct call + follow-up email with one-line summary.
- Validators/auditors: formal email with archive path and IndexNo.
- Implementers: task tracker entry + short chat for clarifications.
- Tone: executive/decision = concise; operational = prescriptive; audit = documentary.
Timing & cadence:
- Set response SLAs per audience and include them in the subject.
- For decisions: initial + 48h reminder + escalation if no response.
- For implementers: weekly status cadence unless urgent.
Engagement tactics (practical):
- Pre-brief critical decision-makers with a 2-minute call before sending a formal approval request.
- Use templates to reduce cognitive load (subject + one-line + evidence link).
- For complex asks, include 1 recommended option and 1 alternate.
Risks & mitigations:
- Risk: wrong audience = delay.
- Mitigation: stakeholder map and confirm primary owner before sending.
- Risk: oversharing to low-relevance audiences = noise.
- Mitigation: apply "need-to-know" test; limit CCs.
Measurement & KPIs:
- % of messages acknowledged within SLA by audience type.
- Avg clarification rounds per message.
- % of actions completed by due date by implementers.
Templates (copy-ready):
Subject (decision): [Decision] [EventCode] — [Short issue] — Response by [YYYY-MM-DD].
Body (decision): 1) One-line: Issue, Impact, Ask. 2) Evidence: [path/Index]. 3) Options (A/B) + recommended + brief rationale. 4) Owner & due. 5) If no reply: escalate to [Name] after [X days].
Quick scripts (audience-specific):
- Pre-brief DM: "Two-minute brief on [EventCode] — recommend Option A due [date]. I’ll send formal request now."
- Implementer task: "Task: [action]. Owner: [name]. Deliver by: [date]. Evidence: [path]."
Case prompts (training):
Case A — Missing approver: Identify primary approver from stakeholder map, prepare decision email, and script a 2-minute pre-brief.
Case B — Auditor needs evidence: Draft formal email with archive IndexNo and acceptance criteria.
Facilitation techniques (to practice audience work):
- Role-mapping: 6 min to assign decision/implementer/validator roles for a sample task.
- Message-edit: 4 min to convert a long explanation into a one-line decision request.
Post-engagement deliverables:
- Finalised stakeholder map saved at [path].
- Sent decision email archived with IndexNo.
- Updated tracker entry showing owner, due, and evidence link.
Follow-up workflow (ensure action):
1) Confirmation receipt from primary audience.
2) Update tracker within 24h.
3) Reminder per SLA.
4) Escalate if no response after defined SLA.
Annex — sample audience map (example):
Alice | Finance Director | Interest: High | Influence: High | Email/Call | Approve budget v1 | SLA: 48h

Purpose — operational guidance
Purpose: Clearly state the specific ask, show you understand the recipient's constraints/circumstances, and explain why fulfilling the ask benefits them and the programme (or what the cost of non-action is).
Goal: Ensure every message contains three elements: (1) a clear ask, (2) demonstrated empathy for constraints, (3) a concise reason (benefit or consequence) that motivates action.
Outcomes:
- Every request begins with a single-line ask.
- Each request includes one sentence recognising constraints (time, access, authority, data).
- Each request includes a one-line rationale: benefit to the recipient or consequence of inaction.
Success criteria: >90% of requests receive the required decision or acknowledgement within SLA;
recipient feedback confirms they felt the ask was understandable and feasible.
Pre-work (logistics & materials):
1) Verify recipient role, decision authority and typical SLAs.
2) Gather constraints: calendar availability, systems access, required approvals, data dependencies.
3) Prepare evidence links and minimal options (recommended + 1 alternate).
4) Choose channel that respects recipient constraints (e.g., short email vs call).
Core rules (must-haves for any purpose statement):
- Ask-first: open with the explicit ask (action + owner + due).
- Constraint-acknowledgement: follow with one line showing you understand their limits (e.g., "I know you only review budget items on Thursdays").
- Motivation: state why they should act (benefit to them/programme or audit/compliance consequence).
- Keep it 3–4 sentences total for decisions.
Message structure (copy-ready):
1) One-line ask: Action — Owner — Due.
2) Constraint line: "I understand [constraint]."
3) Rationale: "Completing this delivers/avoids [benefit/consequence]."
4) Evidence & options: short link + Recommended (option A) + Alternate (option B).
5) Close: "Please respond 'Approve/Reject' by [date]."
Templates (copy-ready):
Decision request:
Subject: [Decision] [EventCode] — [Short issue] — Reply by [YYYY-MM-DD].
Body: 1) Ask: "Approve budget v2 (Owner: [name]) by [date]?" 2) Constraints: "I know you review budgets weekly and prefer summaries." 3) Rationale: "Approval allows payment to proceed and avoids a two-week vendor delay." 4) Evidence: [link]. 5) Options: A) Approve v2 (recommended); B) Request revision (specify).
Tone & framing guidance:
- Use recipient-centric language: "so you can..." or "to avoid..." rather than programme-first phrasing.
- Be explicit about trade-offs when time or resources are constrained.
- Avoid vague asks; prefer binary asks (Approve/Reject) where possible.
Timing & SLAs:
- Set realistic SLAs that reflect recipient constraints.
- If the recipient has limited availability, offer a short pre-brief call and then send the formal request.
Engagement tactics (practical):
- Pre-brief high-impact recipients with a 60–90s call that references the forthcoming one-line request.
- When constraints are severe, propose a default action and ask for objection within a short SLA.
Risks & mitigations:
- Risk: ask is unclear → delay.
- Mitigation: mandatory one-line ask at top + checkbox of required evidence.
- Risk: request ignores constraints → refusal.
- Mitigation: include constraint line and propose low-effort option.
KPIs to monitor:
- % requests answered within SLA.
- % requests requiring >1 clarification round.
- Recipient satisfaction score (micro-survey).
Quick scripts (precise language):
- Ask-first: "Request: Approve payment batch BB20260205 by 2026-02-07."
- Constraint: "I know you only have 30 minutes on Fridays for approvals."
- Rationale: "Approval avoids vendor service interruption and reduces audit exceptions."
- Close: "Please reply 'Approve' or 'Reject' by 2026-02-07."
Case prompts (practice):
Case 1 — Busy approver: Draft a 3-line decision email that respects a 30-min Friday window and offers a fallback approver.
Case 2 — Limited systems access: Request evidence upload with an alternative (send to [Name] to upload) and state consequence if not uploaded.
Post-request workflow:
1) Record request in tracker with evidence link and SLA.
2) Send 48h reminder if SLA missed, referencing original constraint & rationale.
3) Escalate per escalation path if still no response.
Annex — checklist before sending any request:
- One-line ask present.
- Constraint line present and accurate.
- Clear benefit or consequence stated.
- Evidence link and recommended option included.
- SLA and escalation stated.

Outcome — operational guidance
Purpose: Define the expected result of the communication and the concrete consequences (positive and negative) that follow from action or inaction.
Goal: Every message must state (a) the desired outcome, (b) the direct consequences if the outcome occurs, and (c) the direct consequences if it does not.
Outcomes (deliverables):
- A one-line outcome statement (what will change if approved).
- Clear list of consequences: Immediate operational, financial, compliance, and reputational impacts for action vs inaction.
- Acceptance criteria that determine whether the outcome is achieved.
Success criteria: >90% of decisions include a measurable outcome and explicit consequence statement;
outcomes are verifiable against acceptance criteria within the stated timeframe.
Pre-work (logistics & materials):
1) Identify expected measurable change (e.g., payment processed, contract signed, report filed).
2) Quantify direct impacts (cost, delay, service interruption, audit exposure).
3) Prepare evidence to support consequence claims (invoices, SLA, audit rules).
4) Draft the one-line outcome and the two consequence blocks (action/inaction).
Core rules (must-haves for any outcome statement):
- Outcome-first: open with a single sentence that describes the result if the request is granted.
- Dual-consequences: include one short consequence for action and one for inaction (operational, financial, compliance, reputational as relevant).
- Quantify where possible (dates, amounts, % impact).
- Include acceptance criteria (how you will measure success) and timeframe.
Message structure (copy-ready):
1) Outcome: "If approved, [action] will [result] by [date]."
2) Consequence (action): "This will [positive impact—quantified]."
3) Consequence (inaction): "If not approved, [negative impact—quantified], leading to [escalation/penalty/delay]."
4) Acceptance criteria: "Success = [metric] achieved by [date]."
Templates (copy-ready):
Subject: [Decision] [EventCode] — Outcome & consequences — Reply by [YYYY-MM-DD].
Body: 1) Outcome: "Approval enables payment of $X to [vendor] on [date], preventing service interruption." 2) Action consequence: "Prevents vendor suspension and avoids $Y penalty." 3) Inaction consequence: "Delay will cause vendor hold, interrupt services from [date], and risk $Z in penalties/audit exceptions." 4) Acceptance: "Payment posted and reconciliation cleared in ControlSheet by [date]."
Tone & framing guidance:
- Be factual and proportional; avoid alarmist language.
- State only consequences you can verify or measure.
- Prefer short, quantifiable consequence lines over long narratives.
Decision levers tied to outcome:
- Time-bound approvals: highlight the cut-off date and the binary consequence.
- Conditional approvals: state conditions and the conditional outcome.
- Default paths: specify the default action if no response and its consequences.
Risks & mitigations:
- Risk: overstating consequences reduces credibility.
- Mitigation: attach evidence and quantify impacts.
- Risk: leaving consequences vague → inaction.
- Mitigation: use explicit dates, amounts and required next steps.
KPI & measurement:
- % decisions with quantified action/inaction consequences.
- % outcomes meeting acceptance criteria within timeframe.
- Number of escalations caused by inaction.
Quick scripts (precise language):
- Outcome-first: "If approved, payment $X will be made on YYYY-MM-DD and services remain uninterrupted."
- Action consequence: "Prevents vendor suspension and avoids a $Y penalty."
- Inaction consequence: "If not approved by YYYY-MM-DD, vendor will suspend service and we risk a $Z penalty and delivery delay of N days."
Case prompts (practice):
Case A — Payment request: Draft outcome + action/inaction consequences with exact amounts and dates.
Case B — Policy change: State operational change if accepted and compliance risk if not.
Case C — Resource reallocation: State delivery improvement if approved and project delay metrics if not.
Post-decision workflow:
1) Record outcome and acceptance criteria in the tracker.
2) Monitor and evidence the consequence (action) within the timeframe.
3) If inaction occurred, log reason, impact, and follow escalation path.
Annex — consequence checklist (before sending):
- Is the outcome statement clear and measurable? (Yes/No)
- Are both action and inaction consequences stated and quantified where possible? (Yes/No)
- Is evidence attached to support consequence claims? (Yes/No)
- Are acceptance criteria and timeframe specified? (Yes/No)
- Is the language proportional and verifiable? (Yes/No)

Leverage — operational guidance
Purpose: Generate proportional influence that increases the likelihood of the desired action without over-claiming, pressuring, or risking reputation.
Goal: Use credible assets (reputation, channel choice, evidence, clarity) and small behavioural nudges to make the decision or action obvious and low-effort for the recipient.
Outcomes:
- One prioritized leverage lever selected per ask (e.g., reputation + evidence).
- Message and channel chosen to maximise that lever.
- Expected response path and fallback identified.
Success criteria: >80% of high-priority requests secure the intended response without escalation;
reputational feedback (stakeholder survey) shows no increase in perceived pressure or overstatement.
Pre-work (logistics & materials):
1) Verify reputation signals you can legitimately use (titles, prior approvals, audit references).
2) Gather concise evidence (one-page summary, index numbers, receipts, short extracts).
3) Decide delivery mechanism based on impact vs traceability.
4) Prepare one-line message aligned to audience, purpose, outcome.
Core rules (must-follow):
- Do not exaggerate: use only verifiable facts and quantifiable impacts.
- Choose one dominant lever (reputation, evidence, channel, or clarity) and support with one secondary lever.
- Preserve credibility: if you cite a prior promise or approval, attach the source.
- Keep asks low-friction: recommended option + single alternate + clear next step.
Leverage tactics (practical):
Reputation: cite prior related approvals, role authority, or external endorsements succinctly (e.g., "As previously approved by [Name][IndexNo]"). Use sparingly — only when directly relevant.
Delivery mechanism: use face-to-face or call for hard, high-ambiguity decisions; use email for records, auditability, and low-complexity approvals.
Evidence: attach a 1-page evidence brief and include archive IndexNo in subject/body; highlight the single critical fact.
Clarity: ensure the message explicitly lists audience, purpose, and desired outcome in the first line.
Timing & sequencing: send high-leverage emails immediately after a short pre-brief call; time requests to match decision-makers' availability.
Defaults & narrow choices: offer a recommended default and one simple alternative to reduce decision fatigue.
Peer pressure (social proof): when appropriate, note others' agreement or precedent (e.g., "Finance approved identical request on 2026-01-10 — IndexNo"). Use only factual statements.
Delivery mechanism decision matrix (copy-ready):
- High impact, high ambiguity: Face-to-face/call + follow-up email with summary.
- High impact, low ambiguity: Email with one-line ask + evidence + 48h SLA; follow-up call if no reply.
- Low impact, low ambiguity: Task-tracker entry + chat ping.
- Audit/validator needs: Formal email with archive path & IndexNo regardless of impact.
Message framing (copy-ready):
1) One-line: Ask — Owner — Due.
2) Leverage line: "Reference: [prior approval/name/index] / Evidence: [IndexNo]" (only if true).
3) Constraint & benefit: "I know you [constraint]. Approving prevents [consequence] / enables [benefit]."
4) Options: A (recommended) / B (alternate).
5) Close: "Please respond 'Approve' or 'Reject' by [date]."
Templates (copy-ready):
Subject: [Decision] [EventCode] — [short issue] — Reply by [YYYY-MM-DD] — Index:[XXXX].
Body (email): 1) Ask: "Approve payment batch BB2026-02? (Owner: [name]) by 2026-02-07?" 2) Leverage: "Prior: Finance approved similar batch on 2026-01-10 (Index: CTL-20260110_45)." 3) Evidence: "[1-page summary attached — key fact: vendor will suspend service if unpaid]." 4) Options: A) Approve (recommended) — immediate payment; B) Delay — specify consequence. 5) Close: SLA and escalation.
Pre-brief script (60s): "Quick brief: recommend Option A to avoid vendor disruption. I’ll send a one-line email with IndexNo; can you respond by [date]?"
Risks & mitigations:
- Risk: over-claiming damages trust.
- Mitigation: verify every reputational claim and evidence; avoid words like "urgent" without documented impact.
- Risk: wrong channel reduces leverage.
- Mitigation: use the decision matrix and pre-brief for critical asks.
Measurement & KPIs:
- % requests using at least one verified leverage element (reputation/evidence/channel/clarity).
- Response rate within SLA for requests using explicit leverage vs not.
- Number of credibility complaints or corrections required.
Case prompts (practice):
Case 1 — Use reputation appropriately: Draft an email requesting budget approval citing a prior board note (include IndexNo) and attach one-page evidence.
Case 2 — Channel choice: Decide whether to call or email for a supplier hold notice; prepare pre-brief script and follow-up email.
Case 3 — Evidence-first leverage: Convert a multi-page report into a one-page evidence brief that highlights the single number that should move the decision.
Post-engagement workflow:
1) Archive the evidence and any cited approvals with IndexNo.
2) Record which leverage elements were used and outcome.
3) If outcome fails, capture reason and adjust leverage strategy.
Annex — quick leverage checklist (before sending):
- Is every reputational claim verifiable? (Yes/No)
- Is channel matched to impact? (Email/Call/Face-to-face/Chat)
- Is a one-page evidence brief attached with IndexNo? (Yes/No)
- Does the message open with audience, purpose, outcome? (Yes/No)
- Is the ask low-friction (recommended + 1 alternate)? (Yes/No)
- Did you avoid exaggeration or pressure language? (Yes/No)

Post-its — quick leverage ideas (with rationale)
Pre-brief key approver by phone, then send one-line decision email.
Rationale: primes the approver, reducing surprise and increasing likelihood of a fast, positive email response.
Attach a one-page evidence brief with a single highlighted fact.
Rationale: lowers cognitive load and draws attention to the decisive data point.
Open email with: Ask — Owner — Due.
Rationale: front-loads the action so recipients immediately know what is required.
Cite prior approval or precedent (IndexNo) — only if verifiable.
Rationale: leverages existing decisions to normalize and justify the request.
Offer a recommended default + one simple alternate.
Rationale: narrows choices, reduces decision fatigue and speeds approvals.
Use subject token: [Decision] [EventCode] — Reply by YYYY-MM-DD.
Rationale: makes purpose and deadline visible in inbox triage.
Match channel to impact: call for ambiguity, email for audit trail.
Rationale: optimises speed vs. traceability depending on decision needs.
Name the single owner; state consequence of no reply.
Rationale: removes ownership ambiguity and creates clear escalation logic.
Include archive path / IndexNo for every evidence item.
Rationale: enables quick verification and reduces follow-up requests for documents.
Time the request to recipient availability (their calendar preference).
Rationale: increases chance of timely review by respecting their working patterns.
Propose low-effort next step (e.g., “I’ll implement on approval”).
Rationale: reduces perceived work for the approver and clarifies post-approval actions.
Limit asks to one decision per message.
Rationale: prevents cognitive overload and speeds single-item approvals.
