What makes a strong research proposal — practical guidance
Internal coherence
Narrative: Internal coherence is the single most persuasive signal reviewers use to decide whether a proposal is credible. It requires a visible, verifiable "line of sight" from assumptions to questions to objectives to methods to data to analysis to outputs to budget to timeline. Practically, produce three compact artifacts that live early in the proposal: (A) a one page logic map with boxes and arrows that shows each objective, the method that produces evidence for it, the primary deliverable and the responsible person; (B) a one paragraph core assumptions list with three to six assumptions and a one line test for each; (C) a one paragraph contingency plan stating the single alternative if a critical dependency fails.
Examples and templates: Logic map entry: RQ1 → Obj A → cluster RCT (N=60 schools) → outcome: attendance; analysis: ITT with cluster robust SEs; deliverable: policy brief (M16); owner: PI.
Core assumptions sample: A1: local partner provides participant lists by month 1 (test: receive MOU in month 1). A2: baseline response rate at least 70 percent (test: pilot consent rates).
Contingency example: If Ministry approvals delayed more than 3 months, switch to publicly available administrative dataset and add a 6 month extension for re analysis; preserve qualitative fieldwork where permitted.
Practical checklist to show coherence: 1) Each objective is numbered and appears in methods timeline and budget.
2) Each deliverable has a month and named owner.
3) Risk items list the dependency trigger and mitigation.
4) Cross reference table links every budget line to an objective and milestone. Include a micro table in annex that reviewers can scan: Objective → Budget lines → Milestone date → KPI.
Field of inquiry (grant writing)
Narrative: Disciplines and funders use different vocabulary and priorities. State exactly where the project sits and what you will not do. Useful components: a one line positioning sentence three sentinel citations (seminal plus recent review plus topical method) and a two line statement of scope boundaries.
Examples: Positioning sentence: This project sits at the intersection of environmental economics and adaptation policy and focuses on community level flood resilience incentives in peri urban settlements.
Scope boundary example: This study will not evaluate macro level fiscal policy or long term climate modelling; it focuses on household level behaviour and short term institutional responses in months 0 to 24.
Practical outputs to include: a funder fit sentence that quotes the call priority and maps one deliverable to that priority. Provide one to two short comparative lines that distinguish your proposal from adjacent literatures by saying what you do and what you do not.
Clear focused title and short abstract
Narrative: The title should be compact descriptive and where appropriate include the intervention population and context. The abstract must be structured and scan friendly: use a four line micro structure with Problem Question Method Contribution and Feasibility. Provide a two to three line feasibility sentence at the end.
Example title and abstract: Title: Transport Subsidies and School Attendance: A Cluster RCT in Rural Province X.
Abstract micro paragraphs: Problem: Low secondary school attendance in rural Province X reduces learning and labor market prospects.
Question: Does subsidised transport increase monthly attendance and learning outcomes among girls?
Method: Cluster RCT in 60 schools stratified by baseline attendance with baseline and 12 month follow up; ITT analysis with heterogeneity checks.
Contribution: Provides causal evidence on an operationally scalable policy; deliverables include two policy briefs one open dataset and two journal submissions.
Feasibility: MOU with Ministry confirmed; pilot shows 72 percent consent; field team in place.
Practical tip: write the abstract last and test it by showing to someone who must decide in 30 seconds.
Identify the gap and its significance
Narrative: A strong gap statement is precise evidence based and quantified where possible. Use a short gap box in the proposal with four parts: what is known the missing piece why it matters and a quantitative anchor if available.
Examples: Gap box example: Known: Cash transfers raise school attendance by 1 to 5 percentage points in middle income contexts (Smith et al. 2018).
Missing: No causal evidence on gender differentiated effects in remote conflict affected districts.
Importance: Policymakers risk inefficient targeting; closing this gap informs equitable program design and budget allocation.
Scale: 200 000 children in target region potential annual welfare gain per child estimated at $X.
Practical tip: use a three sentence template: Sentence 1 state consensus Sentence 2 identify precise absence Sentence 3 state consequence.
Show how your research addresses the gap
Narrative: Convert the gap into a set of testable contributions. Provide an outputs table linking each major output to the gap components it addresses and the decision or theory it influences. Include a short mechanism map where applicable.
Examples: Outputs table row: Output: Causal estimate of A on B by month 12 → fills gap: lack of causal evidence in context → policy use: informs whether subsidy should target girls; metric: estimated effect size with 95 percent CI.
Mechanism map example: Financial barrier leads to reduced attendance; transport subsidy reduces marginal cost leading to increased attendance; test by comparing subsidised versus control schools and measuring travel time data plus survey responses on constraints.
Practical paragraph: Insert a what success looks like paragraph that lists specific observable outcomes that constitute success for the project such as a statistically detectable improvement in learning by 12 months dataset published with full codebook and replication scripts.
Sharp research question(s) and objectives
Narrative: Keep questions crisp and objectives operational. For each research question provide a precise phrasing primary and secondary outcomes analytic strategy deliverable and date and responsible person. Limit primary questions to three and list exploratory analyses separately.
Example template entry: RQ1: What is the impact of subsidised transport on monthly school attendance among 10 to 14 year olds in district Y?
Outcome: monthly attendance rate.
Analysis: DiD with school fixed effects.
Deliverable: working paper and policy brief by month 18.
Owner: Dr A.
Practical tip: number questions and objectives and show the owner and timeline for each.
Describe your methods
Narrative: Present the methods as a compact plan plus annexed technical details. Structure the methods section to answer why this method for each objective. Include subsections for Design Sample Instruments Field procedures Analysis Sensitivity checks and Software and reproducibility.
Example components: Design: cluster RCT randomisation at school level stratified by baseline attendance and district.
Sampling: stratified random sampling target N per stratum expected design effect and non response assumptions.
Instruments: validated learning assessment items sample items household consumption module GPS enabled travel logs.
Field procedures: enumerator training five days pilot instrumentation daily QA by supervisors double entry checks for ten percent of cases.
Analysis: pre specified primary outcome main estimator clustering strategy covariate adjustments heterogeneity analyses and approach to missing data including multiple imputation rules.
Practical tip: include a one page flowchart of data collection and a short annex with key instrument items.
Persuasive literature review
Narrative: Keep the review selective and purposeful. Map three strands theory empirical methods identify the precise gap and end with the gap line. Structure: map paragraph three to four lines short subsections per strand critique and limitation paragraph and one sentence gap statement.
Examples: Use signals: seminal theoretical claim two recent empirical contradictions methodological issues that your design addresses.
Practical tip: include no more than eight to twelve citations in the main body and put extended literature in an annotated bibliography annex for interested reviewers.
Methodology tightly aligned to the question
Narrative: Demonstrate traceability by providing a short mapping table where every research question row has method primary data required key assumptions robustness tests and deliverable.
Example mapping row: RQ2: Mechanism testing → Method: lab in the field experiment plus in depth interviews → Data: lab results plus behavioural logs → Key assumptions: lab games capture actual incentives → Mitigation: triangulate with real world behaviour logs → Robustness: alternative specifications → Deliverable: mechanism appendix.
Practical tip: include an assumption tests mini protocol in the annex with the test and threshold for action such as if baseline imbalance p less than 0.05 present adjusted and unadjusted estimates and consult the Steering Committee.
Feasibility and workplan
Narrative: Provide a realistic phased timeline with months owners milestones and acceptance criteria. Identify the critical path and single point failures and set contingency rules. Phases example include: Setup and approvals months 1 to 3 Pilot months 4 to 5 Main data collection months 6 to 11 Analysis months 12 to 18 Dissemination months 19 to 24.
Acceptance criteria examples: Setup: MOU and IRB received otherwise escalate.
Pilot: pilot retention at least 85 percent proceed otherwise revise instruments.
Main data collection: achieve 90 percent target sample by month 11.
Practical tip: attach a one page Gantt in the annex and a narrative explaining how delays will be managed such as staggered rollouts and backup data sources.
Preliminary data and pilot evidence if available
Narrative: Present pilot methods recruitment core metrics and how they altered the main design. Include a table of pilot findings such as consent rates variance estimates instrument reliability and response times and link each pilot result to a design change.
Example pilot output table: Pilot metric — Value — Design consequence
Consent rate — 72 percent — Keep planned sample size and increase community engagement where less than 60 percent.
Practical tip: include a short table summarising pilot outcomes and how they changed design choices.
Ethics permissions and data management
Narrative: Provide a data and ethics roadmap listing approvals IRB ministry facility consent procedures risk mitigation anonymisation pipeline storage and data sharing. Include consent language examples and a short data management plan DMP with file structure access rules and retention schedule.
Example checklist: IRB submission: month 1
Ministry data sharing agreement: months 2 to 4
Community consent meetings: month 3
Secure storage: encrypted server de identification rules remove direct identifiers perturb small cells public dataset embargo six months after primary publication.
Practical tip: include a one page DMP in the appendix and templates of consent wording for adults and minors.
Clear budget and justification overview
Narrative: The budget must be transparent objective linked and auditable. Provide an executive budget summary plus short narrative that justifies major categories.
Example summary: Personnel 45 percent Fieldwork 30 percent Equipment and software 5 percent Dissemination 5 percent Overheads 15 percent.
Practical tip: always show unit calculations for personnel and field costs. For each major line provide one to three sentences explaining necessity and link to deliverables. Include a sentence tying total cost to value for money such as cost per household surveyed and cost per major deliverable.

Examples include: personnel with name role percent FTE months salary band and employer on costs travel itemised trips purpose per diem and fieldwork enumerator rates training and incentives.
Practical tip: attach a detailed budget spreadsheet with formulas and a short line by line justification.
Budget itemisation and unitisation
Narrative: Provide reproducible formula for each line so reviewers and auditors can verify calculations.
Examples of unit formulas: Personnel cost equals monthly salary including benefits multiplied by months multiplied by percent FTE. Travel equals number trips multiplied by travellers per trip multiplied by sum of airfare per diem local transport. Provide vendor quotes in the annex for local items and indicate procurement timelines.
Practical tip: round numbers sensibly and ensure the detailed sheet totals match the one page summary.

Practical tip: keep justifications concise to maintain readability.

Contingency and sensitivity
Narrative: Include a contingency line recommended five to ten percent and provide a two scenario sensitivity analysis showing how deliverables change if the budget is reduced. Present each scenario as a table reduction amount actions and impact on outputs and timeline.
Example: a ten percent cut delays dissemination by six months and reduces sample size by ten percent while analysis remains possible. Document decision rules and who authorises contingency use such as the Steering Committee.
In kind and co funding
Narrative: Distinguish confirmed in kind versus pledged contributions. For each partner contribution provide a short statement and attach MOUs where possible.
Examples include: partner university providing office space and data entry facilities valued at $5 000 per year confirmed by letter community partner pledges venue access pending letter. For in kind valuation use conservative market equivalent calculations and show how in kind reduces requested cash if applicable.
Practical tip: attach MOUs or letters of support for confirmed contributions.

Collaborator vs Partner:
Short definitions
Collaborator: an individual or team contributing expertise or effort—usually informal, task-focused, and primarily engaged on technical or research activities.
Partner: an organisation (or formal group) with a broader, often contractual relationship that may include cash/in-kind funding, shared risks, governance roles, or legal obligations.

Key differences (practical accounting / management lens)

Contractual status — Collaborator: often informal (email, LOI).
Partner: formal (MOU, subcontract, grant agreement).

Financial flows — Collaborator: typically expense reimbursed or supported via PI budget lines.
Partner: may receive subcontracts, direct funding, or provide in-kind contributions that change cash flows and budget structure.

Decision & governance — Collaborator: contributes technically; little/no budget authority.
Partner: may have decision rights, reporting obligations, or seat on steering bodies.

Risk & liability — Collaborator: limited institutional liability.
Partner: institutional liability, compliance and audit exposure.

Duration & commitment — Collaborator: short-term, task-based.
Partner: medium/long-term strategic commitment with deliverables and milestones.

Documentation required — Collaborator: CVs, short TORs.
Partner: TORs, MOUs, subaward agreements, budget annex, insurance/compliance checks.

Accounting treatment — Collaborator costs usually booked to personnel/consultant lines; partner payments may be subcontracts or cost-share (in-kind/cash) with separate reporting.

Procurement & audit — Partner relationships trigger procurement rules, vendor due diligence and audit evidence; collaborators rarely do.

Visibility & mapping — Collaborator effort maps to objectives/deliverables; partner lines must be explicitly mapped in Objective→Budget mapping and MOUs.

Quick checklist for an accounting manager

Confirm status: collaborator or partner? (Email → informal; MOU/contract → partner.)

If partner: obtain MOU/subcontract, budget annex, and signatory authority; record in Partner Annex.

Map all partner budget lines to objectives and cashflow; mark confirmed vs pledged.

For collaborators: ensure TOR, day-rates, approvals and correct cost coding.

Require vendor quotes or salary band evidence for reasonableness; attach to line items.

Determine accounting treatment (consultant vs subcontract vs in-kind) and record in workbook.

Verify compliance needs (tax, insurance, indirects) and include in overhead/absorption calculations.

Log approvals and communications in the change log and Annex for audit readiness.

One practical rule of thumb
If money, legal obligations, or institutional reporting flows to the other party → treat them as a partner (formalise). If they only provide time/expertise with limited institutional commitments → treat them as a collaborator (document scope and cost).

What can be cut — prioritized options, impacts and approvals
1) Discretionary dissemination (low-priority events):
Cut: reduce number of workshops, printed brochures, or paid media buys; favour single high-impact policy brief + virtual webinar.
Impact: small loss of reach; preserves core outputs.
Approver: PI / Communications lead.
2) Non-essential travel & per-diems:
Cut: cancel non-critical conference travel; replace in-person meetings with virtual attendance; reduce per-diem rates to institutional norms.
Impact: marginal effect on quality; saves substantial travel and accommodation costs.
Approver: PI / Finance.
3) Postpone non-critical equipment purchases:
Cut: delay buying non-essential kit; rent or borrow instead for near-term needs.
Impact: delays in local capacity but delivery of core activities preserved.
Approver: Finance / PM.
4) Reduce consultant scope or delay hires (non-core):
Cut: reduce consultant days, scope of external reports, or postpone non-essential hires.
Impact: slower delivery on secondary analyses or optional outputs.
Approver: PI / Technical lead / Finance.
5) Scale back training (non-essential modules):
Cut: shorten training length, reduce participant numbers, or deliver remote modules.
Impact: smaller capacity-building footprint; retains core training objectives.
Approver: PI / Capacity lead.
6) Lower dissemination production costs:
Cut: use lower-cost design/layout (in-house), reduce print runs, limit translations to priority languages.
Impact: slightly lower polish but preserves distribution.
Approver: Communications lead.
7) Reduce event hospitality and non-essential allowances:
Cut: simplify catering, remove non-essential social events at workshops.
Impact: minimal programmatic impact; saves logistics budget.
Approver: PM / Finance.
8) Trim field non-essentials (stagger, not cut core):
Cut: reduce number of monitoring visits that are not critical, limit discretionary subsamples; keep core sample intact.
Impact: slight reduction in QA or secondary analyses; avoid cutting primary sample.
Approver: Technical lead / PI.
9) Postpone external validation or expensive MEL activities:
Cut: delay external audit/validation to post-award period or replace with internal QA checks temporarily.
Impact: potential perceived drop in independent verification; plan for later external check.
Approver: M&E lead / Steering Committee.
10) Reduce subcontract scope or re-negotiate rates:
Cut: negotiate lower fees, reduce deliverables or shift to fixed-price smaller packages.
Impact: depends on partner capacity; manage risk of quality drop.
Approver: Procurement & Finance.
11) Use confirmed in-kind more aggressively:
Cut (cash reduction): convert confirmed partner-provided services/facilities into budget offsets where appropriate.
Impact: reduces cash request without harming delivery if in-kind is solid.
Approver: PI / Partner contact / Finance.
12) Freeze discretionary hires or lower FTEs for non-core roles:
Cut: delay recruitment or reduce %FTE for roles not essential to primary objectives.
Impact: increased workload on core staff; avoid where it threatens delivery.
Approver: PI / HR / Finance.
What not to cut (preserve these):
- Core personnel directly delivering primary outcomes.
- Primary sample size / core fieldwork needed for statistical power.
- Ethics, IRB and legally required compliance items.
- Critical procurement with long lead times if required for main activities.
Quick 10% cut plan (example sequence):
1) Reduce dissemination events + printed materials (save ~2–3%).
2) Trim travel and per-diems (save ~2–3%).
3) Delay one non-essential equipment purchase or reduce consultant days (save remaining ~4–6%).
Principle: start with low-impact, high-savings items; avoid touching core personnel or sample.
Quick 20% cut plan (example sequence):
1) Apply 10% plan.
2) Further reduce training scale and postpone external MEL; re-scope consultant contracts; increase use of in-kind (additional ~10%).
Trade-off: expect slower deliverables, reduced capacity-building reach; document changes and notify funder if required.
Decision rules & process:
1) Map each proposed cut to Objective→Budget mapping and show lost outputs or delays.
2) Prioritise cuts that: a) do not reduce primary outcome data, b) have clear low programmatic impact, c) are reversible or delayable.
3) Require named approver depending on savings threshold (e.g., PI <5%, Finance 5–10%, Steering Committee >10%).
Communication & audit steps after cuts:
- Record every change in the change log with justification and approver.
- Update cashflow and contingency lines.
- Inform partners and update MOUs if their deliverables/fees change.
Final rule of thumb: cut where impact on primary objectives is minimal and where costs are easily reversible or replaceable; always document rationale and approvals.
If proposal is 10% over call limit and partner also +10% — Recommended actions
Situation summary: Proposal total = call limit +10%. Waiting partner confirms they are +10% too (i.e., partner costs push project further over). Decision required: reduce cost, reallocate, secure extra funding, or request a funder exception.
Immediate action — reconcile authoritative numbers:
Export the authoritative workbook, confirm totals, identify which lines create the +10% (personnel, fieldwork, partner, overheads). Create two simple scenarios in-sheet: (A) baseline overrun; (B) target = call limit (show cuts required).
Immediate action — build a short options pack:
Produce 2–3 clear options for decision-makers (e.g., Option 1 = reduce scope to fit limit; Option 2 = ask funder for +10% or seek co-funding; Option 3 = partner covers part via in-kind or revised scope). For each option show net budget, lost outputs, and cashflow effect.
Negotiate with partner (first contact):
Ask partner to: (a) re-price or reduce their scope/days; (b) convert part of their cash ask to confirmed in-kind; or (c) confirm whether they can absorb the overrun. Insist on emailed response or updated quote/MOU.
Priority internal cuts (low-impact first):
1) Reduce discretionary dissemination/events/print runs; 2) Cut non-essential travel & per-diems; 3) Delay non-critical equipment (rent if needed); 4) Reduce consultant days for non-core tasks; 5) Trim training scale (remote modules). Map each cut to savings and objective impact.
Contingency & overhead play:
Check contingency (if present) and institutional absorption of overheads. If contingency exists, document permitted use and approvals; consider institutional absorption of a small overhead gap if policy allows.
Rescope deliverables if needed:
Convert optional deliverables into stretch goals (clearly label deliverables as essential vs optional) so funder sees what will be delivered within limit.
Funder options — prepare a formal request (if necessary):
If cuts unacceptable, prepare a concise request to funder: explain cause of overrun, present 2 options (reduced scope vs +X% top-up), include objective→budget mapping and sensitivity, and attach partner response. Emphasise value-for-money and mitigation plan.
Co-funding & bridge finance:
Quickly identify institutional bridge or alternative co-funders for the gap; ask partner whether they can co-fund or introduce funders. Record commitments in writing.
Decision matrix & approvals:
Set who may approve what: PI for <5% reallocation; Finance Director for 5–10%; Steering Committee for >10% changes. Require written sign-off for any reallocation and update change log.
Cashflow & procurement checks:
Recalculate cashflow under the chosen option to ensure peak months still funded; check procurement lead times to avoid cutting items with long lead-time that undermine delivery.
Communications plan:
Prepare short, factual messages: (A) to partner — request revised quote or in-kind confirmation; (B) to funder — if seeking exception; (C) internal — proposed cuts and owners. Attach updated budget scenarios.
If partner cannot reduce and funder refuses top-up:
Implement the prioritized internal cuts, preserve primary sample and core personnel, and document residual risks and mitigation. Notify funder of scope changes as required.
Documentation & audit trail:
Record every negotiation, revised quote, MOU, approval and change in the Annex and change log so auditors and funder reviewers can trace decisions.
Final recommendation (practical):
1) Attempt partner renegotiation (aim convert cash → in-kind).
2) Simultaneously prepare a credible reduced-scope budget that meets the call limit (show exactly what is lost).
3) If essential, prepare a short funder request with clear options.
Combine small internal cuts + partner adjustments first; use funder request only if cuts materially damage primary objectives.
