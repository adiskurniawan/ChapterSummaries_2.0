Accounting Questions
Q1 – Funds receipt from Global office
Scenario: On 15 May 2026, FHF Indonesia received funds transfer from Global office (Head quarter) to the bank account of the office in Maldiri bank. Value: IDR 1.5 billion.
Accounting treatment:
(1) P&L or BS? Balance Sheet account, not P&L.
(2) When recognised? When cash is received in the bank account on 15 May 2026.
(3) Why / what? This is an internal funding transfer from head office to country office, so it should normally be recorded as intercompany funding / due to or due from head office, not as income. Typical GLs: Bank, Due to/from Global office, Intercompany clearing, and possibly branch equity or net assets depending on the legal/entity structure.
Q2 – FHF Indonesia paid on behalf of Global office
Scenario: In May 2026, FHF Indonesia received and paid on behalf of Global medical team an invoice of room charge and meeting package for a workshop funded by Global, not charged to country budget.
Accounting treatment:
(i) Invoice/cost treatment: Balance Sheet first, using intercompany receivable / payable or clearing account, not country P&L if FHF Indonesia is only acting as paying agent for Global.
(ii) General accounts involved: Bank, Accounts payable if unpaid at receipt, Due from Global office / Intercompany receivable, and Intercompany clearing. If the entity is later reimbursed, use a receivable from Global until settlement is received.
(iii) Recognition timing: Record when the invoice is received and when payment is made; clear against Global office when reimbursement or settlement is received.
Why / what? Because the cost is funded by Global and not charged to the country budget, FHF Indonesia should not treat it as a local program expense if it is only paying on behalf of another office. The main objective is to keep the local books clean and trace the amount to the correct funder.
Q3 – Staff advance and acquittal
Scenario: One staff requested and received advance of IDR 80 million for an outreach activity (cataract surgery) in Lombok. Two weeks later, staff acquitted the advance.
Accounting treatment:
(1) P&L or BS? The advance issued is a Balance Sheet item; actual spending becomes P&L only when the advance is acquitted and supported.
(2) When recognised? Advance is recognised when cash is given to staff; expense is recognised when supporting documents are submitted and approved in the acquittal.
(3) Why / what? The advance is a receivable from staff until it is justified by actual spending. The acquittal clears the advance and converts justified costs into project expense.

i) Total spending: IDR 85 million
- Recognise expense of IDR 85 million in P&L.
- Clear staff advance of IDR 80 million in BS.
- Record the extra IDR 5 million as amount payable to staff / reimbursement payable, then pay it out.

ii) Total spending: IDR 77 million
- Recognise expense of IDR 77 million in P&L.
- Clear staff advance of IDR 80 million in BS.
- Record IDR 3 million as cash returned from staff / advance refund and reduce the staff advance balance to zero.
Q4 – Partner advance and acquittal
Scenario: In April 2026, FHF Indonesia transfers IDR 500 million to YKB (implementing partner) as advance to implement activity plan of Q2-2026, project code ID23-02. By 30 June 2026: (i) partner has not yet submitted a financial report; (ii) next month (1st week of July), partner reports IDR 400,000,000 eligible spend; (iii) remaining balance will be deducted from next advance transfer.
Accounting treatment:
(i) When advance transfer was made: Balance Sheet account, recorded as partner advance / prepayment / advance to implementing partner.
(ii) When report was received: Recognise the eligible spend of IDR 400 million in P&L and clear the related amount from partner advance. The expense is supported by the financial report and acquittal documentation.
(iii) Remaining advance not spent yet: Keep the unspent IDR 100 million as outstanding partner advance in Balance Sheet until it is either spent and acquitted or deducted from the next transfer.
Why / what? Partner advance is not an expense until eligible expenditure is reported and approved. The unspent balance remains an asset / receivable from partner.
Q5 – Supplier invoices with withholding tax
Scenario: In May, FHF office received and paid invoice for translation fee IDR 10,000,000. VAT 11%. Income tax 2%.
Accounting treatment:
(i) Withholding tax amount and treatment: Withholding tax = IDR 200,000 (2% of IDR 10,000,000, assuming tax is calculated on the taxable service value before VAT). Record withholding tax payable in BS until remitted to the tax authority.
(ii) Net-to-pay amount: IDR 10,900,000 = 10,000,000 + 1,100,000 VAT - 200,000 withholding tax. Record cash/AP at net payable amount.
(iii) VAT treatment: VAT of IDR 1,100,000 is recorded separately. If creditable, record as input VAT asset / receivable; if not creditable under the organisation’s rules, charge it to expense or project cost.
Why / what? The translation fee is the expense base, VAT is separate, and withholding tax is not an expense of the supplier invoice but a tax liability of the payer until remitted.
Q6 – Month-end treatment: supplier invoice not received vs services completed
Scenario: As at 31 May 2026, a consultancy service for activity code ID2501112 has been fully delivered. Contract value: IDR 200,000,000. Supplier has not yet issued an invoice. Program team informs that project activity ID2501112 is reported completed in May 2026.
Accounting treatment:
(i) Expenditures recognised or not? Yes, the expenditure should be recognised in May because the service has been fully delivered and the cost relates to May activity.
(ii) Where recorded? Debit consultancy expense or project expense in P&L and credit accrued expense / accrued liability in BS at month-end.
(iii) Treatment when invoice received in June: Reverse or clear the accrual against the supplier invoice, then recognise accounts payable or settle payment as normal.
Why / what? Expenses follow the period in which the service was received, not the invoice date. Month-end accruals ensure the P&L reflects the correct period.
Q7 – Restricted donor funds vs expenses
Scenario: FHF Indonesia receives USD 50,000 restricted funding for project ID26-01 School Eye Health. Funds received 10 January. Funding is closed by June 2026. By 31 May, USD 40,000 has been spent.
Accounting treatment:
(i) Income immediately or liability? Initially liability / deferred income, because the funds are restricted and should be recognised as income only when conditions are met.
(ii) How to recognise USD 40,000 spend? Recognise USD 40,000 of eligible project expense in P&L, and release the same amount from deferred income / restricted fund liability into income or grant revenue according to the organisation’s policy.
(iii) Treatment of remaining balance as of 31 May: The remaining USD 10,000 stays on the Balance Sheet as deferred income / restricted funding liability until it is spent on eligible costs or returned / reallocated under the donor terms.
Why / what? Restricted funds are not free income on receipt; recognition follows donor conditions and eligible spend.
Q8 – Asset vs expense (borderline case)
Scenario: FHF purchases 10 eye-testing devices. Unit cost IDR 10,000,000 each. Useful life 2 years. Used for project activities.
Accounting treatment:
(i) Expense or fixed asset? Usually fixed asset if the organisation’s capitalisation policy is based on useful life, control, and threshold, because each device has a useful life of more than one year and supports future service delivery.
(ii) Key factors for identification: Control by the organisation, future economic benefit or service potential, useful life beyond 12 months, capitalisation threshold, and whether the devices are separately identifiable and reusable. If the policy threshold is high and the devices fall below it, they may be expensed as small equipment depending on policy.
(iii) If donor allows expensing but internal policy requires capitalisation: Follow internal accounting policy and the applicable accounting standard for the books, because accounting treatment must be consistent. Donor reporting can still show the cost as expensed for grant purposes if the donor requires that presentation, but the general ledger should follow the organisation’s capitalisation policy.
Why / what? The accounting policy determines whether the item is capitalised or expensed. Useful life and control are the main indicators, not only the funding source.
Q9 – Month-end accrued expense for salary not yet paid
Scenario: At 31 May 2026, staff have worked the full month, but salary will be paid on 5 June 2026.
Accounting treatment:
(1) P&L or BS? P&L expense in May, with accrued salary payable in BS.
(2) When recognised? At the end of May, because the service has already been received in May.
(3) Why / what? Payroll expense belongs to the month in which the work was performed, not the month when cash is paid. The unpaid amount is a liability until settlement in June.
Q10 – Prepayment for annual insurance
Scenario: FHF pays IDR 24 million on 1 May 2026 for a 12-month insurance policy covering May 2026 to April 2027.
Accounting treatment:
(1) P&L or BS? Balance Sheet initially as prepayment / prepaid expense.
(2) When recognised? Expense is recognised monthly over the coverage period, not all at once on payment date.
(3) Why / what? The payment gives future benefit over 12 months, so the cost should be matched to the period of coverage. As each month passes, one-twelfth is released to P&L.
Q11 – Donation received with no restriction
Scenario: FHF receives unrestricted donation funds from a private supporter for general use.
Accounting treatment:
(1) P&L or BS? Usually income when the organisation has control and no further condition remains outstanding; if the receipt is subject to a future obligation, it may start as liability first.
(2) When recognised? At receipt if all recognition criteria are satisfied and there is no donor restriction or refund condition.
(3) Why / what? Unrestricted funding is generally recognised as income because the organisation can use it for general operations. If there is a performance condition, recognition should be deferred until that condition is met.
Q12 – Purchase of office supplies
Scenario: FHF buys stationery and small office consumables for immediate use.
Accounting treatment:
(1) P&L or BS? Usually P&L expense when purchased and consumed, unless policy says otherwise.
(2) When recognised? At purchase or when items are issued for use, depending on materiality and inventory policy.
(3) Why / what? Stationery and small consumables do not create future economic benefit over multiple periods, so they are normally expensed rather than capitalised.
Q13 – Travel advance for staff trip
Scenario: Staff receives IDR 15 million travel advance for an official trip and later submits receipts of only IDR 13 million.
Accounting treatment:
(1) P&L or BS? Advance is BS until acquittal; then the eligible portion becomes P&L.
(2) When recognised? Expense is recognised upon approval of travel settlement.
(3) Why / what? The unspent IDR 2 million must be returned or offset against the staff advance. The company only recognises the actual, supported travel cost.
Q14 – Refund from supplier after overpayment
Scenario: FHF pays a supplier IDR 20 million by mistake, but the correct invoice value is IDR 18 million. Supplier refunds the excess IDR 2 million later.
Accounting treatment:
(1) P&L or BS? The excess payment is BS as a receivable / prepayment until refunded or offset.
(2) When recognised? When the overpayment is identified, then cleared once cash is returned or deducted from future invoice.
(3) Why / what? The organisation should not expense the overpaid portion because it is not a cost of the service. It is an amount recoverable from the supplier.
Q15 – Grant receivable from donor after approved report
Scenario: A donor accepts the financial report for a completed milestone and confirms reimbursement of USD 12,000.
Accounting treatment:
(1) P&L or BS? P&L revenue if the grant condition is met, with receivable in BS until cash arrives.
(2) When recognised? When donor approval is received and the right to receive reimbursement becomes enforceable.
(3) Why / what? The approved report creates a valid claim against the donor. Cash receipt later only settles the receivable; it does not determine when the revenue is earned.
Q16 – Capital expenditure paid by donor directly to vendor
Scenario: Donor pays a vendor directly for equipment used by FHF project activities, and FHF never touches the cash.
Accounting treatment:
(1) P&L or BS? Usually fixed asset in BS if the asset is controlled by FHF and meets capitalisation criteria; corresponding donor contribution is recognised according to grant policy.
(2) When recognised? When control of the equipment passes to FHF and recognition criteria are met.
(3) Why / what? Even if cash never passes through FHF, the organisation may still control the asset and must record it properly. The accounting follows substance, not only the cash flow.
Q17 – Inventory of medical consumables
Scenario: FHF buys boxes of sterile consumables for outreach activities and stores them for future use.
Accounting treatment:
(1) P&L or BS? BS inventory until issued for use, then P&L expense.
(2) When recognised? At purchase as inventory, and at issue as expense.
(3) Why / what? Consumables held for future use should remain on the balance sheet until consumed. This avoids overstating project expense before the items are actually used.
Q18 – Exchange rate effect on USD funding
Scenario: FHF receives USD funding and later spends part of it after the exchange rate has changed.
Accounting treatment:
(1) P&L or BS? Foreign currency balance is recorded in BS and retranslated as required; exchange differences may go to P&L depending on policy and accounting standard.
(2) When recognised? On receipt, at each reporting date, and when settlement or conversion occurs.
(3) Why / what? Foreign currency transactions create exchange gains or losses because the rupiah value changes over time. The books should reflect the current carrying value of the foreign currency balance.
Q19 – Fixed asset depreciation
Scenario: FHF buys a laptop for project use with a useful life of 3 years.
Accounting treatment:
(1) P&L or BS? Fixed asset in BS, depreciation in P&L.
(2) When recognised? Capitalise when ready for use; depreciation starts when the asset is available for use and continues over useful life.
(3) Why / what? The laptop provides future benefit beyond one year, so it is not a one-time expense. Cost is allocated systematically over the period of use.
Q20 – Accrued utility bill at month-end
Scenario: Electricity for May has been used but the bill arrives in June.
Accounting treatment:
(1) P&L or BS? P&L expense in May with accrued liability in BS.
(2) When recognised? At 31 May based on service consumed.
(3) Why / what? Utilities follow accrual accounting: expense belongs to the month of consumption, not the invoice date. When the bill arrives in June, the accrual is cleared.
