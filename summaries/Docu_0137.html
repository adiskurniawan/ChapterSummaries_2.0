What a routing number is 9-digit identifier for a U.S. bank or credit union used to route ACH, check clearing, and some wire transfers. Find on your checks (first 9 digits), bank website, or bank statement.
What ACH is Automated Clearing House — a U.S. electronic network that moves credits (deposits) and debits (withdrawals) between bank accounts. Payroll direct deposit (credit); subscription fee (debit).
Who maintains / issues routing numbers The bank or credit union (assigned by the American Bankers Association in the U.S.). Contact bank customer service or check bank documentation.
Who legitimately knows them Your bank, you (account holder), authorized payers (employer, biller), and other banks during transfers. Provide to employer HR, mortgage servicer, or a third-party payment processor you trust.
Who should provide them Bank provides routing number; account holder provides routing + account number to authorized payers. Employer requests your routing + account number for payroll setup.
Who to never provide them to Unsolicited contacts, unknown private sellers, social media requesters, callers asking for “verification” without prior relationship. Do not give routing+account to random callers, DMs, or unknown forms.
Minimum required data for ACH Routing number + account number; often requires account type (checking/savings) and account holder name. Employer form or secure online payment form will request these fields.
How to share securely Use your bank’s secure portal, encrypted upload, sealed paper form delivered in person, or a verified company’s secure enrolment system. Avoid plain email/unsolicited phone. Upload via employer payroll portal; provide a voided check on HR paper form.
How payers verify accounts Micro-deposits (small test credits), instant bank verification (tokenized OAuth), voided check or bank letter. Approve two micro-deposits shown in your account to confirm setup.
ACH credit vs ACH debit Credit = other party sends money into your account (you receive). Debit = other party pulls money from your account (you owe/authorize). Employer uses ACH credit; utility company uses ACH debit for autopay.
Authorization required Written or electronic authorization from account holder is required before most third parties can initiate ACH debits. Keep copies of authorizations. Signed direct-deposit authorization; electronic consent during signup.
How to revoke or stop ACH Revoke authorization with the company and request stop with your bank; follow bank’s procedure for unauthorized debits. Contact company to cancel autopay and place a stop payment or ACH block at your bank.
Common risks Unauthorized debits if authorization is given fraudulently; account takeover if combined with other personal info; exposure in scams. Fraudulent “payment unlock” scam requesting your bank details.
Mitigations Provide details only through verified channels; use account numbers dedicated to external payments when possible; enable account alerts; use positive pay/ACH filters if offered. Set SMS/email alerts for all transactions; ask bank about ACH debit blocks.
Monitoring & detection Regularly review transactions; enable real-time alerts; check payee names/channels for unfamiliar debits. Reconcile monthly statements; enable mobile push notifications.
If misuse occurs Contact your bank immediately, dispute unauthorized ACH transactions, ask about provisional credit and fraud investigation, and consider changing account number if needed. Call bank fraud line and file a formal dispute.
Legal / consumer protections (summary) U.S. consumers have electronic funds protections and dispute processes (e.g., for unauthorized electronic transfers); specifics vary—check your bank’s disclosures and applicable law. Read your bank’s Electronic Funds Transfer Agreement; ask about deadlines and required forms.
Differences from wire transfers Wires are often faster, irreversible, and use different routing (wire routing or SWIFT/IBAN for international). ACH is batch-processed and usually lower cost. Use wire for time-sensitive international transfer; ACH for payroll and recurring U.S. payments.
International equivalents SWIFT/BIC and IBAN (international bank identifiers) or local clearing codes in other countries; these are used for cross-border transfers rather than U.S. ACH. For a SEPA/Euro transfer provide IBAN; for U.S. domestic provide routing+account.
Practical checklist before sharing 1) Confirm recipient identity and need.
2) Use secure channel.
3) Prefer micro-deposit verification.
4) Keep records of authorization.
5) Enable monitoring/alerts. 		Only provide details after completing this checklist.

Why It Matters
How routing numbers are structured 9 digits: (1–4) Federal Reserve prefix, (5–8) bank identifier, (9) checksum digit. Helps detect invalid or mistyped routing numbers.
Where routing numbers differ Banks have separate routing numbers for ACH, wires, and checks. Using the wrong one can cause transfer failures.
Typical places you find your routing number Bank website, mobile app, paper checks, bank statements, customer service. Ensures you’re using an official source, not a scam message.
ACH timing ACH credit: 1–2 business days. ACH debit: same-day or next-day depending on bank. Important for payroll timing, bill payments, and avoiding late fees.
ACH transaction types PPD (personal), CCD (corporate), WEB (online), TEL (phone), ARC/BRC (check conversion). Indicates how authorization was obtained and affects dispute rules.
Required details for ACH setup Routing number, account number, name on account, account type, authorization. Missing fields cause rejected setup or failed transfers.
Unauthorized ACH red flags Small test debit by unknown company, repeated small debits, sudden subscription debits. Early detection prevents larger unauthorized withdrawals.
Dispute windows Consumers: generally 60 days for unauthorized transactions (varies by bank agreement). Missing the window reduces protections and refund options.
Recommended account hygiene Use alerts, reconcile statements, create a dedicated “payments account,” keep a buffer. Reduces fraud impact and simplifies monitoring.
When to use wire instead of ACH Large amounts, urgent payments, time-critical transfers. Wires are faster but cost more and are usually irreversible.
When ACH is preferable Payroll, recurring bills, routine domestic transfers, low-fee movement of funds. ACH is cheaper and automated for predictable payments.
Precautions for online forms Check HTTPS, verify the organization, avoid public Wi-Fi, never send via plain email. Reduces risk of credential theft or interception.
