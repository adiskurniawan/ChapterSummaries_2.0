Audit Preparation
Prepare the file so reviewers can trace the population, tests, support, and conclusions without reconstruction.
Scope and control intent: Assemble reconciliations, ledgers, policies, contracts, grant support, board materials, prior-year findings, and current-year schedules before fieldwork begins so the audit starts from a complete and internally consistent record. The objective is not to impress the auditor with volume, but to provide a file that can be navigated from transaction to trial balance to report without gaps in the chain of evidence. Confirm the population, the period, the preparer, the reviewer, the source system, and the tie-out logic for each major schedule. If a schedule is derived from multiple systems, document the extraction method and indicate how duplicates, voids, adjustments, credit memos, and timing differences were handled. If a supporting item is unavailable, isolate the gap immediately and identify whether the missing item is a true absence, a delayed retrieval, or a mislabeled record.
Primary focus: Confirm completeness, ownership, and tie-out before any request is answered, and make sure every schedule can be traced back to source records. Each schedule should have a named owner who can explain the numbers, the assumptions, the source data, and the approval path. Reviewers should know exactly what was checked, what was not checked, and what remained open at the time the file was released. A well-prepared audit file is not a collection of documents; it is a controlled evidence package that explains how each figure was built, how exceptions were handled, and where management judgment was applied. For any estimate, disclose the basis, the review performed, and the sensitivity of the figure to alternative assumptions.
Escalation rule: Escalate missing support early so open items do not become exceptions, delays, or avoidable findings. Escalation should be documented in the request log with the date, issue, owner, expected completion date, and any interim workaround. Do not allow informal verbal explanations to substitute for missing support. If the underlying transaction cannot be rebuilt from source records, document the limitation, the effect on the schedule, and the corrective action to prevent recurrence. Use the escalation path to protect the audit timeline and to avoid reconstructing the file under pressure after fieldwork has started.

Operational controls and workflow
Use a pre-fieldwork checklist that confirms the population, the period, the owner, and the source system for every major schedule. The checklist should also verify that reconciliations are current, sign-offs are complete, exceptions are documented, and all key support is indexed in a way that another person can retrieve it without direct guidance. Require preparer and reviewer sign-off before the file is released to the auditor. Reviewers should confirm not only arithmetic accuracy but also whether the schedule aligns with policy, funding terms, and prior-period treatment. Document all reconciliations so the audit trail is complete even if the original preparer is unavailable later. Keep one request log for the entire engagement so duplicates and missing items are visible quickly. If the same request is made more than once, note whether the second request reflects a true follow-up, a revised scope, or a missed response, and close the loop in the log.
Evidence, recordkeeping, and audit trail
Retain source documents, reconciliations, approved schedules, and final outputs together in one indexed file structure. Preserve version history for all schedules and narratives. Store confirmations, tie-outs, and management review notes with the corresponding audit request. Keep audit support in a single logical structure by year, process, and request number so search and retrieval remain manageable under review pressure. Where electronic approvals are used, preserve the time stamp, approver identity, and document version that was approved. Where a schedule changed after review, keep the prior version, the revised version, and the reason for the change. The objective is to preserve an unbroken chain from source to conclusion.

External Audit
Support independent auditors with complete schedules, management responses, and clear points of contact.
Scope and control intent: Define the audit boundary, timeline, deliverables, and responsible owners in advance so there is no ambiguity over who supplies what. Align the request process, evidence flow, and response review to the external auditor’s scope so that no item is answered from memory or reconstructed ad hoc during fieldwork. Establish a single source of truth for the engagement, including the audit request log, the evidence repository, management response drafts, and the final issuance package. The boundary should clearly identify which entities, funds, programs, locations, and periods are in scope and which are excluded, including any shared services or pass-through activity that may require separate support. If there are multiple service providers, fiscal agents, or related entities, document the intercompany or interorganizational dependencies early so the audit team can see where support originates.
Primary focus: Align financial statements, internal control evidence, and compliance support to the auditor’s scope without leaving unexplained gaps. The finance team should anticipate the likely audit topics, identify the source records for each topic, and pre-clear the most likely tie-outs before the first formal request arrives. Where estimates or allocations affect the statements, keep the methodology, assumptions, and reviewer sign-off readily available. If a matter is sensitive or unresolved, document the factual record carefully and avoid speculative explanations that could create confusion later. Keep the audit package internally consistent so the statements, footnotes, supporting schedules, and management letters all point to the same underlying records and conclusions.
Escalation rule: Route unresolved issues to management and governance promptly so deadlines are not missed and disagreements are recorded early. Escalation should occur when a request cannot be completed on time, when a response requires judgment beyond the preparer’s authority, or when there is disagreement over the completeness or interpretation of the evidence. Record the issue, the owner, the due date, the decision path, and any interim accommodation granted to the auditor. Do not wait until the close of fieldwork to surface a dispute that could have been discussed earlier. Prompt escalation protects the deadline, preserves the audit trail, and reduces the risk that the same unresolved item appears as a recurring finding in later cycles.

Where the auditor requested a sample, preserve the population definition, sample selection method, item-level support, and the explanation for any deviations or exclusions. The aim is to make the audit record self-contained so the file can be reconstructed without informal recollection.

Internal Audit
Use internal audit to test controls, verify compliance, and identify breakdowns before they become findings.
Scope and control intent: Cover high-risk processes, repeated exceptions, and areas with weak oversight or heavy manual work so the review is risk-led rather than routine-only. Internal audit should be structured to give management and governance an independent view of where control design is fragile, where operating performance is inconsistent, and where remediation has not truly changed the underlying condition. The scope should include process design, control execution, supporting evidence, exception handling, and the sustainability of corrective actions. High-risk areas often include procurement, payables, cash handling, payroll, grants, journal entries, related-party activity, and any process with manual overrides or decentralized approval practices. A risk-based audit universe should be refreshed when systems, funding, leadership, or operating model changes create new exposure.
Primary focus: Keep testing independent, documented, and tied to risk priorities, and make the results usable by management and governance. Every review should produce findings that are clear enough to support action and precise enough to support retesting. Internal audit workpapers should show why the sample was chosen, what was tested, how the control should operate, and whether the observed performance matched the stated control objective. Findings should be written to distinguish between design issues, operating failures, isolated exceptions, and evidence gaps. Avoid conclusory language without support. Management should be able to use the results directly to assign owners, set due dates, prioritize remediation, and report progress to the board or committee.
Escalation rule: Report issues with clear ratings, owners, due dates, and remediation expectations so they can be tracked to closure. Escalation should occur when a repeat issue appears, when remediation is overdue, when a control failure has a financial or compliance effect, or when a higher-level decision is needed to correct a design weakness. If management does not accept a finding, record the basis for disagreement and the evidence supporting each position. If the issue has enterprise-level significance, elevate it to governance rather than leaving it at the process owner level. The objective is not merely to identify problems, but to ensure the response is real, measured, and validated.

Operational controls and workflow
Maintain an audit universe with processes, systems, grants, and controls listed clearly. Perform risk-based planning using exposure, complexity, volume, and prior findings. Evidence, recordkeeping, and audit trail

Store the remediation plan and retest evidence with the original finding. Each file should show the chain from planning to test step to conclusion to management response to retest. If a control owner provides alternate evidence or a supplemental explanation, keep that response linked to the original test result. The purpose of the file is to let an external reader understand the basis for each conclusion without relying on oral explanation. Version control and date stamps are essential because internal audit often spans several months and multiple reporting cycles.

Audit Response Management
Manage audit requests, evidence, follow-up questions, and responses through a controlled workflow.
Scope and control intent: Keep one source of truth for requests, owners, due dates, and final answers so no auditor question is answered from memory alone. The response process should be treated as a controlled workflow with intake, assignment, drafting, review, approval, release, and closure. Responses should be complete, consistent, and supported by linked evidence. If more than one department may answer the same question, the organization should designate a single coordinator to prevent contradictory messaging. The coordinator should also keep a running note of where each piece of evidence is stored and whether the response requires legal, grant, or governance review.
Primary focus: Prevent inconsistent responses by routing all auditor communications through designated owners and a single review channel. A response should never be sent directly from the preparer if the matter is material, sensitive, or depends on judgment. Each final answer should be checked against the request text to make sure the question was fully addressed. Where the auditor asks for clarification, treat the follow-up as a new tracked item rather than an informal continuation. This protects the history and makes it easier to show how the final answer evolved. If a request is revised midstream, preserve the original version, the revised version, and the reason for the change.
Escalation rule: Elevate stalled items quickly so response delays do not create findings by default. The response log should flag any item that is blocked by missing records, unavailable personnel, disputed facts, or unresolved interpretation. Escalate stalled items to the appropriate manager or governance contact before the due date becomes a missed deadline. If an answer cannot be finalized because evidence is still being gathered, send an interim status update that states the expected completion path without making unsupported commitments. Keep the escalation path visible to leadership so delayed responses are recognized as operational risks rather than isolated clerical issues.

Audit Findings Follow-Up
Track findings from receipt through remediation, validation, and formal closure.
Scope and control intent: Assign each finding a cause, corrective action, owner, and completion date so remediation remains visible from start to finish. Follow-up should not be limited to collecting status updates. It should verify that the underlying condition is resolved, the control has changed in a durable way, and the documented evidence supports closure. The finding register should distinguish between open, in progress, pending retest, closed, and reopened items so the organization can see the real status of corrective action. Each issue should also note whether it affects a single transaction, a recurring process, or a broader control environment weakness.
Primary focus: Distinguish between one-time exceptions and control failures that need redesign, retraining, or new monitoring. A one-time exception may require only limited correction and explanation, while a recurring issue may require process redesign, system changes, stronger approvals, or supervisory intervention. The follow-up owner should state the root cause clearly enough that the corrective action targets the cause rather than the symptom. If the root cause is not known, the issue should remain open until the cause is documented or until the organization can show why the cause cannot be fully determined. For findings tied to grants or regulatory obligations, include the potential consequence of non-closure in the tracking file.
Escalation rule: Retest remediation and document closure evidence before marking an item complete. Closure should require confirmation that the corrected process is operating as intended, not merely that a policy was rewritten or a memo was circulated. If the retest fails, the item should be reopened with a revised due date and a revised remediation plan. If the issue is overdue, escalate it through the governance chain. The follow-up file should preserve all prior versions of the finding, the management response, the retest work, and the closure approval so there is a full history of what changed and why.

If a finding is later reopened, preserve the closure evidence and the reopening explanation together. This allows later reviewers to understand whether the original closure was premature or whether the process changed after closure.

Audit Trail Documentation
Maintain a complete trail from source document to ledger entry, report, and approval.
Scope and control intent: Preserve the evidence needed to explain who did what, when, and why so that every transaction can be reconstructed without guesswork. The audit trail should allow a reviewer to move from the original source document to the journal entry, to the ledger account, to the report line, to the final approval without losing context. For complex items, include the narrative that explains why the treatment was chosen and what alternative treatment was considered. The control objective is not merely retention; it is reconstructability. Any transaction that cannot be traced easily should be treated as an exception until it is corrected or documented.
Primary focus: Make every adjustment, override, and exception traceable to support and approval. Manual entries should have support that explains the business reason, the calculation, the approver, and the period in which the entry should be recognized. Overrides should be rare, visible, and reviewed. Where a report line is built from multiple inputs, preserve the source data, transformation logic, and any manual correction applied at the end of the process. The trail should be strong enough for a later reviewer to confirm that the report reflects the underlying activity accurately and consistently.
Escalation rule: Stop backfilling missing evidence after the fact; document the gap and the correction separately. If a missing item must be rebuilt, the original absence should still be disclosed and the new support should be identified as reconstructed rather than original. Avoid mixing reconstructed materials with original source records without a clear label. If repeated backfilling occurs, treat it as a control weakness and escalate it to management. A healthy audit trail is created at the time of transaction, not assembled later under audit pressure.

Supporting Documents Management
Organize invoices, approvals, contracts, reports, and correspondence so support is easy to retrieve.
Scope and control intent: Keep documents linked to the transaction, program, and period they support so review does not depend on tribal knowledge. A strong document management practice ensures that support is indexed, searchable, current, and clearly associated with the transaction it validates. The file should show not only the final document but also the approvals, clarifications, and attachments that establish context. Documents should be grouped in a manner that reflects how the organization works in practice, while still allowing quick retrieval by auditors, managers, or compliance staff. Support should not be stored in scattered email folders or individual desktops where institutional knowledge is lost when people leave.
Primary focus: Standardize naming, indexing, access, and version control across the file set. Consistent naming should identify date, vendor or counterparty, transaction type, account, program, and status. Indexing should allow a reviewer to locate the supporting document without asking the preparer where it was stored. Access controls should protect sensitive records while still allowing authorized reviewers to perform their work. Version control should make clear which document is final, which is draft, and which was superseded. The aim is to avoid ambiguity about which record supports the accounting treatment or business decision.
Escalation rule: Flag missing, duplicate, or conflicting documents before the file is closed. If support cannot be matched to the transaction, the item should remain open until the discrepancy is resolved or formally explained. Duplicate or conflicting documents should be reviewed to determine which version is authoritative and whether the discrepancy reflects a processing error, a timing issue, or a source-system problem. Escalate repeated filing problems as a process issue rather than treating them as isolated clerical mistakes. If document quality deteriorates, the retrieval process will fail when it matters most.

Recordkeeping and Archiving
Retain records long enough to satisfy audit, tax, grant, and governance requirements.
Scope and control intent: Set retention periods by record type, not by convenience or file location, so critical evidence is preserved for the correct period. Retention rules should reflect legal, regulatory, contractual, and operational needs, including grant terms, donor restrictions, payroll obligations, tax filing support, board materials, and litigation holds. Records should remain accessible for as long as they are needed to prove compliance, substantiate transactions, and support decision-making. Archiving should not mean hiding records; it should mean storing them in a stable, controlled, and retrievable state.
Primary focus: Protect the history of approvals, transactions, payroll, grants, and board actions. The archive should preserve not only the final record but also the context needed to understand it. For example, board actions may require minutes, exhibits, vote records, and related policy changes; payroll records may require wage data, withholding evidence, approvals, and adjustments; grant records may require award letters, budgets, period support, correspondence, and closeout documentation. The archive should be organized so these related materials remain grouped together rather than split across unrelated folders.
Escalation rule: Preserve records under hold when an audit, inquiry, or dispute is pending. Once a legal hold, audit hold, or investigative hold is in place, no destruction should occur until the hold is formally lifted. Hold notices should identify the affected record classes, the reason for the hold, and the responsible contact. If a destruction cycle is paused, note the date and basis for the pause. An effective retention system makes it impossible to destroy needed evidence by accident.

Document Retention Policy
Write a retention policy that specifies what is kept, where it is stored, and when it is destroyed.
Scope and control intent: Cover finance, payroll, grants, board records, tax filings, and supporting evidence, and align retention with actual legal and operational obligations. The policy should define the universe of records, the retention period for each class, the storage medium, the access model, the destruction trigger, and the approval authority. It should also define what happens when a record is both a working document and an official record, and how to identify the official version. A retention policy is only useful if staff can follow it without guessing. For that reason, it should be written in operational language and tied to actual workflows.
Primary focus: Align retention with legal, audit, and funding requirements instead of a single fixed schedule. Different records often require different retention periods based on law, grant terms, donor restrictions, or organizational need. The policy should make those distinctions visible so that low-risk drafts are not retained forever and high-risk records are not deleted too soon. If there is uncertainty, the policy should default toward preservation until the requirement is clarified. The policy should also clarify whether digital scans are acceptable substitutes for paper originals and when originals must be retained.
Escalation rule: Suspend destruction when records may be needed for audit or investigation. The policy should define hold conditions, hold authority, and release authority. It should also describe how destruction is paused, logged, and resumed after the hold is lifted. If a record class is implicated in a recurring issue or a pending examination, its retention treatment should be reviewed before any routine destruction cycle proceeds. Policy exceptions should require documented approval and an explanation of why the exception is necessary.

Filing Systems
Use a filing structure that makes returns, approvals, support, and board records easy to locate.
Scope and control intent: Organize files by year, process, program, and document type with consistent naming so another person can find the same evidence quickly. A filing system is effective only if it supports retrieval under pressure, cross-reference across functions, and long-term consistency even as staff change. The system should be designed around how records are actually used in audit, compliance, management review, and governance rather than around the convenience of the original preparer. The key question is whether a new reviewer can find the needed item without tribal knowledge.
Primary focus: Make the filing system auditable so another person can find the same evidence quickly. To do that, the system should have a master index, a standard folder map, naming conventions, restricted edit rights, and a small set of rules that staff can remember and follow. Searchability matters as much as storage. The structure should separate final records from drafts, working papers from official documents, and current-period files from archived material. If the structure is too complex, users will stop following it consistently and the file will lose control.
Escalation rule: Fix broken indexing and misfiled records before they become recurring access problems. Repeated filing errors indicate a process problem, a training problem, or a system-design problem. Escalate if important documents are stored inconsistently, if the same item appears in multiple places without a single authoritative version, or if staff cannot locate records without direct help. The filing system should be corrected at the structural level, not merely cleaned up after the fact.

Ethical accounting depends on the willingness to stop, ask, review, and document before proceeding.

Compliance Culture
Build a culture where policies are followed, questions are raised, and issues are escalated early.
Scope and control intent: Make compliance part of ordinary work rather than a separate exercise so control performance is visible in normal operations. Culture determines whether controls are actually used when people are busy, uncertain, or under pressure. A compliance culture is evident when staff ask before acting, document what they did, stop when support is missing, and escalate concerns without fear of reprisal. The visible behaviors of management are especially important because they define what is considered normal.
Primary focus: Reinforce training, reviews, and accountability for repeated exceptions. Training should not stop at policy distribution. It should explain what good control behavior looks like in daily work, what constitutes an exception, and what staff should do when the rule cannot be followed. Managers should review exception trends and respond to recurring issues with root-cause actions, not just reminders. If a team repeatedly bypasses the process, the issue may be structural or cultural rather than merely procedural.
Escalation rule: Treat repeated noncompliance as a culture and control issue, not just a one-off error. Repeated exceptions suggest that the process is too hard to follow, the training is ineffective, the controls are impractical, or the culture does not support compliance. Escalation should therefore go beyond the individual error and assess whether the problem requires redesign, retraining, or stronger oversight. A healthy culture makes noncompliance visible and fixable before it becomes normalized.

Financial Stewardship
Protect assets, document use of funds, and show that resources were used for authorized purposes.
Scope and control intent: Link stewardship to budgets, approvals, restrictions, and reconciliations so money is controlled, explained, and defensible. Financial stewardship requires not only proper accounting but also disciplined resource use, awareness of funding restrictions, and evidence that assets were safeguarded. Stewardship should be visible in approvals, reconciliations, monitoring, and documented explanations for material spending decisions. It should be especially strong where funds are restricted, grant-based, donor-designated, or public in nature.
Primary focus: Monitor cash, reserves, grant terms, and spending authority. The organization should know what it can spend, when it can spend it, and what evidence shows the spending was authorized and allowable. Stewardship reviews should compare actual usage to budget, restriction, grant terms, and operational need. If spending patterns are changing, the organization should identify whether the change is strategic, temporary, or concerning. Stewardship also includes protecting assets from misuse, waste, and poor documentation.
Escalation rule: Escalate overspending, unsupported costs, or misuse of funds immediately. If a cost lacks support, appears unallowable, exceeds authority, or violates a restriction, the issue should be treated urgently. Escalation should identify the amount, the impacted fund or program, the likely corrective action, and the owner of the response. Repeated stewardship failures may indicate a process, training, or oversight problem and should be reviewed accordingly. Stewardship is demonstrated by how quickly the organization corrects and explains exceptions.

Operational controls and workflow
Confirm allowability before spending and again before posting major costs. Use budget to actual review as a control, not just a report. Require documented approval for unusual or high-value transactions. Watch cash levels, reserve use, and restricted-fund spending continuously. Include funding source and restriction review in the purchasing or payment workflow. Reconcile actual usage to approved budgets on a scheduled basis. If an exception is expected to recur, document the basis and the approved treatment.
Evidence, recordkeeping, and audit trail
Retain approvals, reconciliations, budget analyses, and spend support with the transaction file. Document why a cost was allowable and under what funding source it was charged. Preserve exception logs and corrective action for any overspend or unsupported charge. Keep grant or donor correspondence when it affects allowability or use. If a cost is moved between funding sources, preserve the original charge, the reason for reclassification, and the approving authority. This creates a transparent stewardship record for later review.
