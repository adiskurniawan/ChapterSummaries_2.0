PART 1 — BACKGROUND (Extremely comprehensive summary)
Introduction & Background
- Purpose of Afrobarometer (AB): AB conducts comparative public-opinion surveys across Africa on democracy, governance, economics, social development, and civil society to give citizens’ views a consistent, credible voice in policy and public debate.
- History & scale: Launched in 1999; to date (as described in Part 1) AB has interviewed over 350,000 respondents in 39 countries, covering more than four-fifths of Africa’s population. Results are widely disseminated (public events, publications, website, social media) and inform media debate, parliamentary hearings, cabinet meetings, and policy decisions.
- Impact & public role: Findings are used by policy actors and civil society; AB positions public voice as an important pillar of democracy and governance in Africa. The manual emphasises the reputational and fiduciary responsibilities that follow from AB’s public profile.
Transition to new legal entity & financial conventions
- Legal status change (Jan 2020): AB became an independent legal entity with full capacity to act and enter transactions without routing through CDD-Ghana; this formal change underpins separate financial and contractual authority.
- Accounting / currency convention: Project expenditure is recorded in a computerized accounting system. The standard currency for budgets, forecasts, recording expenses, and reporting is the U.S. dollar (USD) unless a donor explicitly requires a different reporting currency.
AB Network structure (summary & roles)
- Secretariat: Central coordination, oversight and custodian of network-level processes (including finance).
- Core Partners (CPs): Higher-level regional partners with greater responsibilities (listed in the manual as: University of Nairobi — Institute of Development Studies (UON-IDS); Institute for Justice and Reconciliation (IJR); Ghana Centre for Democratic Development (CDD-Ghana)). Core Partners supervise National Partners and perform substantial technical/managerial roles.
- Support Units (SUs): Specialist technical support (examples in manual: University of Cape Town (UCT), Michigan State University (MSU)).
- National Partners (NPs): Country-level implementing organisations responsible for national survey implementation; list of current NPs is maintained on the AB website (referenced in the manual).
- Network implication: Because AB operates through this networked model, the FMM must provide harmonised financial policies that can be applied across differing institutions, jurisdictions and local accounting regimes. (Appendix references: Organisational Structure at Appendix 10).
Purpose & Structure of the Financial Management Manual (FMM)
- One-stop reference: The FMM consolidates AB’s financial policies and procedures for the entire network (Secretariat, CPs, SUs, NPs, consultants/collaborators). It defines orderly procedures for authorisation, payment, accounting treatment and internal control.
- Incorporation of related policy: Where other formal documents exist (e.g., Procurement Manual), the FMM draws them in by reference to create a cohesive framework.
- Content scope: Policies, procedures, templates, appendices, operational controls, reporting requirements and audit expectations that together govern how AB uses, records, protects and reports financial resources across the network.
Objectives of the FMM (detailed list)
a. Operational guidance: Give existing and new staff clear guidance on recording transactions, monitoring and controlling expenditures, producing timely/accurate financial and management reports, and meeting statutory obligations.
b. Safeguard assets & people: Describe required controls to protect AB assets and employees.
c. Continuity: Document operational/business procedures so continuity is possible when responsible personnel change or are unavailable.
d. Consistency: Ensure uniform application of AB financial policies and procedures across the network.
e. Reference for oversight: Provide auditors, consultants and stakeholders a single reference to evaluate AB’s financial controls and compliance.
f. Institutional consolidation: Reinforce institutional structures and procedures, consolidating previous investments and gains.
g. Network standardisation: Document accounting, record-keeping and reporting rules that apply across AB and partner institutions to ensure consistent capture of income/expenditure.
h. Tools & templates: Provide standard templates and tools (budget templates, control sheets, reporting formats, procurement forms, etc.) to operationalise financial management across the network.
- Primary users: Finance staff are the main users, but the manual also serves Board members, managers, and other staff as a governance and operational reference.
Application & Interpretation (authority & reach)
- Authorising body: The FMM is issued with authority of the Board of Directors (BoD) and therefore applies network-wide.
- Scope of applicability: Mandatory for all employees and network members (Secretariat, CPs, SUs, NPs), and to be interpreted/administered by the Board or its authorised agents.
- Design principle: Policies should reflect effective internal control and accounting practices ensuring AB resources are managed in furtherance of the network’s objectives.
Non-Compliance (policy on breaches)
- Mandatory compliance: All network members must comply with the FMM from the effective date of the current version.
- Remedies & enforcement: Non-compliance is dealt with per AB’s Ways of Working (WoW) document — i.e., policy sets out that violations will be handled under established disciplinary and remedial processes (WoW referenced as the mechanism).
Changes & Version Control (detailed procedure & responsibilities)
- Dynamic document: The FMM must be periodically updated to remain relevant to operational and regulatory changes.
- User responsibility: All users must ensure that documented procedures reflect actual operations and that adequate controls exist; users should flag and propose updates where processes have changed.
- Coordination & owners: The manual identifies relevant staff responsible for coordinating updates and new procedures (these roles are assigned in the Responsibility Matrix).
- Change process: All changes follow the procedures in the Responsibility Matrix — updates are authorised and recorded, and the manual is maintained as a controlled document (versioning and approval steps are required).
- Reference requirement: Board, CMT and partners must consult the FMM when transactions are likely to have financial impact for AB.
Responsibility Matrix — explicit entries from Part 1
- Purpose of matrix: Assigns who does what for preparing, approving, and reviewing the FMM.
- Matrix entries (as stated in Part 1):
• Overall responsibility to oversee preparation of FMM: Board / CMT — marked X (Board/CMT holds overall oversight).
• Approval of FMM and policy amendments: Board and CEO / COO — marked X X (both Board and Executive approve amendments).
• Preparation and annual review of FMM: CEO / COO — marked X (operational responsibility for preparing and conducting annual reviews rests with CEO/COO).
- Operational practice: Those listed in the matrix must follow the documented change and review procedures; the COO and Finance Manager act as custodians for implementation and day-to-day upkeep.
Cross-references & where to find supporting content (as noted in Part 1)
- Appendices & annexes: The FMM references numerous appendices (e.g., Organisational Structure — Appendix 10, Chart of Accounts — Appendix 7, Budget templates and partner tools), and these form part of the full manual.
- Related policies mentioned: Procurement Manual, Travel Policy, Ways of Working (WoW), Data Protection/Privacy policy, etc. (some are included as appendices in later Parts of the manual).
- Distribution: Current version available on Google Drive in “Policies” folder and on the AB website (distribution list referenced in front matter of the manual).
Practical implications for network staff and partners
- Mandatory reading & training: Finance staff, project managers, CPs, SUs and NPs should familiarise themselves and use templates and controls in the FMM; supervisors must ensure teams are trained and aware.
- Evidence & audit readiness: The FMM sets expectations for documentary support, record retention and audit packs — partners must retain source documentation per the retention rules specified later in the manual.
- Operational alignment: Units must align their local financial practices with the FMM; where local statutory or donor requirements differ, the FMM provides that donor/ statutory rules take precedence but any deviation must be documented and approved per the manual’s change control process.
Summary statement (why Part 1 matters)
Part 1 establishes the legal, organizational and governance context for Afrobarometer’s financial operations: it defines AB’s network structure, the reason for a unified Financial Management Manual, the manual’s objectives, mandatory nature, the process for keeping it current, and who owns and approves it. This foundational chapter sets expectations for consistent, auditable, and donor-compliant financial management across the entire Afrobarometer network.

PART 2 — POLICIES & PROCEDURES (Detailed, single-column summary)
Donor funds — classification & management
Project activities are funded by a mix of core and supplemental donors. Core donors are those who (a) sign on for a multi-year phase, (b) fund all categories of expenses, and (c) contribute more than USD 0.5M. Supplemental donors fund single-country surveys, specific questionnaire modules, or have limitations on allowable expenditure categories. Donor funds are managed centrally by the Afrobarometer Secretariat in Accra (No. 95 Nortei Ababio Loop). The FMM establishes centralised control, donor compliance, single audit reporting where appropriate, and alignment of local partner agreements with donor terms.
Consolidated Fund — purpose & operation
Since Round 4 (2008) donors were encouraged to pool contributions into a single Afrobarometer Consolidated Fund managed by the Secretariat. Purpose: (a) minimise cash-flow variability when donor disbursements are staggered; (b) enable consolidated financial reporting and a single annual audit rather than multiple donor-specific audits; (c) reduce administrative burden from decentralised donor management. Donors that prefer not to pool funds may have stand-alone accounts (see Bank accounts).
Bank accounts — types, use & bank addresses
Afrobarometer maintains multiple bank accounts (Standard Chartered Ghana & Stanbic Bank Ghana): six USD accounts and two Ghana-cedi accounts (described as Consolidated Fund FCA, donor stand-alone FCA accounts, locally-generated foreign currency current accounts, and local cedi accounts for Ghana operations). Account design and permitted transactions are described (e.g., FCA consolidated account accepts foreign transfers/cheques and does not accept over-counter cash deposits). Bank addresses for both banks are recorded in the manual.
Signatories, account opening & operating rules
Bank signatories are designated by AB subject to Board ratification. Account opening/closure requires Board approval and documentary records (board resolutions, signatory changes) be retained in minutes. Two signatories are required for cheques (preferably with dissimilar occupations). Signatories should not normally both authorise payments and record them; they must ensure payment requests have appropriate supporting documentation and be alert to duplicate payment risk. Overdraft authority requires specific approval and must be within the organisation’s constitution and controlling authority (CMT).
Operating bank accounts — monthly controls & reconciliations
Bank accounts must be reconciled monthly by the Finance Officer and reviewed/approved by the Finance Manager; where impracticable, reconciliations prepared by cashier must be signed by a responsible official and retained. Cheque books are stored securely; cash and cheque receipts are controlled. Paying-in slips are prepared in duplicate, itemising cheques with payer and cheque number. Bank paying slips must be periodically verified. Online banking releases follow the same internal approval process as cheques.
Petty cash — authorised floats, controls & replenishment
Secretariat petty cash floats are set at GHS 20,000 and US$10,000 (floats may vary by country). The COO or FM approves petty cash levels. Controls: individual disbursement limits; secure storage (fireproof safe); full supporting documentation (petty cash voucher, authorisation); signature of payees; monthly or float-depletion reconciliations with two-person counts and senior finance approval; no staff loans or cashing of personal cheques via petty cash; no IOUs; record payments in cash book when made.
Cheque & online payments — approvals, supporting docs & signatory guidance
All payments (cheque or online) require documented internal approvals from the spending functional director or partner director and must be supported by supplier invoices, purchase orders, comparative quotes, approvals and procurement documentation. A full list of authorised approvers and their restrictions must be provided to bank signatories. Signatories must confirm that supporting documentation is present and that internal controls have been completed before signing. Cancellations/returns are investigated and GL entries corrected promptly.
Stale cheques & prohibition of undated/blank cheques
Cheques uncashed for more than six months are considered expired; banks will not honour cheques older than six months. Finance Manager must review reconciliations periodically and reverse stale cheques while contacting vendors. Blank or undated cheques are strictly prohibited.
Payment procedure — voucher, checks & attachments
Before payment the processing officer raises a payment voucher recording date, voucher number, beneficiary, amount (including taxes withheld), narration, GL account code, initials/signature of preparer, approver, and recipient (if cash). The officer verifies arithmetic accuracy and links amounts to supporting invoices/agreements, issues cheque or online payment request, and attaches supporting documentation (invoices, approvals, procurement evidence, purchase orders, award letters). Bank signatories or authorised officers sign/release payments only after satisfying themselves payments are valid and evidenced.
Statutory payments & deadlines
Statutory remittances (taxes, social security, pensions) must be made by statutory deadlines each month; penalties for late remittances are unacceptable as donor operating expenses (i.e., not chargeable to donors). Proof of remittance must be retained and filed.
Budget, Forecasts, Work Plans & Partner Agreements — currency & process
Annual budgets, rolling forecasts and work plans are coordinated by the Secretariat with input from partner institutions and budget centres. Currency: USD is the default for budgets, forecasts and agreements unless donors require another currency. The Afrobarometer Budgeting Guidelines & Framework (Appendix 4) provides operational guidance. Annual budgets should be finalised by 31 December; quarterly forecasts are finalised in the third week following quarter end. Core Partner and Support Unit agreements are sub-grant style (Sida-compatible) tied to SOW/TOR and payment schedules; National Partner contracts are fixed-price fixed-period with deliverables and payment schedules tied to deliverables. Partners must submit quarterly financial and narrative reports, and disclose accounting policies when necessary.
Financial Management Procedures — purpose & partner responsibilities
The FMM provides a blueprint for internal controls across the network. Each partner institution is responsible for establishing reliable internal controls tailored to local accounting regulations and ensuring compliance with FMM policies. The manual covers procurement, recording, reconciliations, approvals and reporting.
Accounting basis & policy — IPSAS & historical cost
Financial statements are prepared in accordance with International Public Sector Accounting Standards (IPSAS) under historical cost convention unless an IPSAS specifically applies otherwise. Statements are presented in USD, rounded to whole numbers. If no IPSAS applies, management must adopt policies that produce relevant, faithful, neutral, prudent, and complete financial information reflecting economic substance over legal form.
Expenditure approval — documentary evidence & invoice controls
All approvals must be documented and retained with accounting paperwork. Approvals come from the appropriate functional director or partner director. Expenditure must be within approved budget lines or covered by formal amendments. Invoice handling must prevent loss, support verification (grid stamping, arithmetic checks), record allocation, and evidence goods/services receipt. Approvers should not be those responsible for receiving goods. Invoices are posted to the GL after approval with supplier-order filing or paid periodically via payment schedules.
Chart of Accounts & coding
The Finance Manager maintains the Chart of Accounts (COA), reviewed periodically by the COO; changes require COO sign-off. All transactions must be coded before authorisation/payment to enable consistent reporting and apportionment across cost centres and donors. The practice of lumping expenses as “other direct costs” is discouraged without adequate supporting detail and analysis.
Bank, cash book & GL reconciliations — frequency & controls
- Reconcile cash book balances to general ledger control accounts monthly; prepare bank reconciliation statements monthly.
- Cash book adjustments must be reviewed by a responsible official before posting.
- Investigate unusual reconciling items and review reconciliations by a supervisor other than preparer.
- Record adjustments sequentially in journals; ensure journal entries balance and are prepared by independent personnel, checked against supporting documents before processing.
- Keep upload previews and reconciliation evidence for any GL batch uploads.
Reporting — quarterly monitoring, annual financial statements & narrative
Financial reports prepared quarterly to monitor funds: objectives include reliable stakeholder reporting and compliance with IPSAS/statutory rules. Annual reporting period is calendar year (1 Jan to 31 Dec); annual financial statements (prepared within three months of year end) include statement of comprehensive income, statement of financial position, cash flow, statement of changes in equity, notes, and narrative report of activities funded during the year. Finance Manager and finance staff ensure transactions are coded to the approved COA.
CP & SU reporting (partner reporting rules)
Core Partners and Support Units must submit quarterly financial reports in an agreed format showing actuals vs budget and fund balances. Subsequent disbursements are conditional on receipt and vetting of the quarterly reporting pack. Partners must retain supporting documentation and provide narrative Word report alongside financial submissions.
Annual financial reports & audit preparation
Within three months of year-end, the COO through the Accounts Manager prepares annual financial statements for external audit. These include required financial statements and accompanying notes and narratives. The audit pack should contain reconciliations, bank advices, payroll packs, sample payslips, statutory remittance proofs, apportionment schedules, and any donor-specific documentation. Journal narratives must include PayrollRun ID, period, country, and totals for traceability.
AB Travel policy — purpose, allowable costs & per diem
AB pays reasonable travel expenses directly related to AB business (flights, hotel, ground transport, per diem). Travellers must exercise prudence and document expenses. Per diem: flat US$75/day for all AB-related travel (derived as 75% of USG M&IE across 18 AB countries). This per diem does not apply where lodging/meals are prearranged by hosts or for special fellowships — those are determined case-by-case. Travellers must submit mission reports within one week of return.
Travel definitions, approvals & procedures
Authorized travel must be approved by the travel-approving official (department head/functional director or partner director). Travel requests for overnight trips must be submitted 42 days prior with supporting information (invitation, outline of costs). Travel Desk handles bookings; travellers must use most economical routes. Travel approvals, TRF completion, visa and insurance checks, and travel conduct rules are stipulated (including dress code and local cultural advice). The Travel Request Form (TRF) is mandatory and fields are described in the manual.
Modes of transportation & ticketing rules
Primary modes: economy class commercial air travel for foreign travel, commercial rental vehicles where authorised, and local car/bus for in-country travel. Exceptions (e.g., higher class) require documented management approval. Missed flights due to traveller negligence may lead to salary deductions for costs of changed/ forfeited tickets. Travel Desk must be informed of no-shows to preserve tickets when possible.
Travel advances & accountable imprests
Secretariat may provide travel advances for per diem, lodging, and miscellaneous reasonable expenses. All advances are treated as accountable imprest; receipts/unused change must be returned upon arrival. Unapproved or unreconciled expenditures may be deducted from salaries.
Travel expense categories: lodging, M&IE, professional transport & other
- Lodging: reimbursed for actual medium-priced single room or at host’s specified capped rate; exceptions with justification allowed.
- M&IE: standard daily US$75 covers meals and incidental personal expenses; higher amounts reimbursable with receipts and circumstantial evidence.
- Professional transport & communications: not included in M&IE; a basic rate of US$30/day generally covers local taxi/communications — higher actuals reimbursed with receipts and justification.
- Other reimbursable expenses (with receipts): visa fees, airport transfers, photocopy expenses; individual items of USD7.50+ require receipts. Alcohol and medical costs are excluded (medical insurance should be traveller’s responsibility unless agreed otherwise).
Indirect costs / overheads
An indirect cost recovery (overhead) rate of 15% is applied across the board unless CMT approves exceptions. Indirect costs cover general overheads not explicitly budgeted in sub-grant agreements. Any exception must be approved by the CMT.
Procurement — policy objectives & delegated processes
Procurement supports survey operations across many countries; procurement seeks value for money, transparency, competitiveness and compliance with donor rules. The COO manages procurement with support from functional directors. Requisitions require formal email requests with date required, justification, item/service details and approval. Local procurement officers obtain three quotes from approved suppliers for competitive procurement; sole-source requires documented justification and approval.
Procurement process — quotations, evaluation & award
- Requisition approved by overseeing functional director (Surveys, Communications, Capacity Building, Analysis, Secretariat) or CP/SU director.
- Local procurement obtains three quotes where practical; quotes passed to approving director for value-for-money evaluation.
- Where cost exceeds budget limits, price competitiveness must be evidenced and the justification recorded.
- Pre-award due diligence may be required for vendors lacking performance records (request past performance for last three years).
- Final payment only after goods inspection or service certification by approving functional director. All procurement documentation retained for a minimum audit period (six years typical).
National Partner procurement & evaluation
National Partner procurement must be transparent and open, following the network procurement plan (AB.Partner.Procurement.Plan.21.Aug18). Core Partners and Support Units undergo periodic evaluation (at least once per survey round) to assess performance and continued suitability (referenced procurement plan).
Internal financial review — Secretariat oversight & partner checks
The Secretariat conducts quarterly financial reviews of partner quarterly reports and end-of-round financial reviews at partner sites to assess controls and compliance. Surprise or periodic checks may occur. Partners must maintain effective systems and supply impartial analyses/recommendations to the Secretariat. The internal review checklist follows Sida-agreed procedures and includes trial balances, bank statement verification, bank reconciliation checks, procurement sample testing (20% sample), asset register checks and NP payment verification against contracts and deliverables. Findings are issued in a report and followed up.
Internal review procedures — key steps
Steps include obtaining up-to-date trial balances, verifying inflows from Secretariat remittances via bank statements, reviewing bank reconciliations prepared by persons other than preparer, checking payments against contract terms/amendments, ensuring procurement rules applied and supporting documentation exists, inspecting asset purchase documents and inventory, verifying NP payments are backed by approved budgets and deliverables, cross-linking ledgers to financial reports, and issuing findings with required remediation actions.
External audit — scope, standards & reporting
An annual external financial audit is required (per donors such as Sida). Audits are conducted by independent qualified auditors in accordance with International Standards on Auditing (ISA) and local accounting standards. Where indirect costs are charged or donors require, the auditor examines organisation-wide general purpose financial statements. Audited statements and management letters must be submitted to donors. The auditor follows agreed-upon procedures (ISRS 4400 where applicable) to review specific items (e.g., follow up on internal management control recommendations, payroll verifications, budget vs actual comparisons, supporting documentation testing, foreign exchange gain/loss disclosure, procurement compliance). The auditor provides an independent auditor’s report (ISA 800/805 format), management letter with prioritized recommendations, and factual findings reports for agreed-upon procedures.
External audit — partner audits & chain of audited reports
Auditors must verify that sub-grantees/partners have signed agreements and that audit requirements in partner agreements align with AB’s donor obligations. Auditors should test an unbroken chain of audited financial reports for partner disbursements (verification of a minimum percentage of contributions both by value and number — e.g., review contributions totalling at least 65% of disbursed funds and 100% of the number of contributions). Auditor reporting must flag observations donors should be informed about.
Financial capacity building
The Secretariat offers training and assistance to partners on AB financial processes and procedures, including budgets, reporting, procurement, and internal control expectations. Capacity building helps partners strengthen compliance, reporting quality, and fiduciary management.
Policy on selecting & contracting network partners — objectives & approach
AB prioritises local ownership and capacity building; partners (National Partners) are selected after country assessment missions by teams that identify institutions with financial, administrative, logistical and technical capacity. The manual outlines the selection steps: pre-mission identification of potential partners and informants; in-country interviews with key informants; assessment of organisational capability to conduct national representative surveys (administrative/financial capacity, survey experience, national coverage, political neutrality, quantitative analysis capacity). Core Partners lead the request-for-bids process in countries where surveys proceed.
Selection process details & procurement of NP
AB aims for at least three bids where possible, though in some countries the candidate pool is small. Bids are evaluated on technical quality and cost, with emphasis on adherence to AB methodology and standards to enable long-term multi-round partnerships. In some countries two partners may be contracted (one for data collection and one for analysis/supervision). Core Partners must document reasons for partner selection and ensure contracts are fixed-price with deliverables and reporting requirements.
New National Partner financial assessment
As part of selection, AB reviews organisational and financial documents: certificates of incorporation, organisational structure, CVs of key personnel, audited financial reports, tax filings. The supervising Core Partner (in consultation with senior management) makes the final selection decision based on these documents and the proposal. Partners must keep receipts for at least three years after contract completion.
Contracting — NP & CP contract form & accountability
Contracts are fixed-price fixed-period for survey rounds, tying payments to deliverables to minimise financial risk. Core Partners will contract NPs or, where CP cannot, Secretariat may contract on their behalf. Currency of contracts is USD unless otherwise agreed. Contracts require that NPs provide quarterly financial reports and retain receipts for audit. Sub-grants to CPs and SUs follow similar sub-grant formats and accountability rules (see Part 3 for sub-grantee management updates introduced from Round 8 onward).
New Core Partner financial assessment — scope & procedures
Elevation to Core Partner status triggers a thorough technical, managerial and financial capacity review because Core Partners sub-contract to NPs and face higher reporting demands. An assessment team (including finance manager/project accountant) examines financial statements, internal controls, bookkeeping, budgetary control, revenue sources, asset registers, liabilities, creditors, and verifies the existence and physical status of assets. Audited financials and internal control evidence are required; evaluators may perform test verifications against records.
Policy on AB-Funded Consultancy Services — purpose & rules
AB engages consultants (often drawn from National Partners) for technical assistance. Consultants must not already receive salary from AB for the same services (i.e., AB pays consultancies only to individuals not already paid by AB for those duties). Engagements require Core Partner director consultation and approval. Requests to engage consultants must be submitted in writing at least 10 days prior to contract start with CVs, TOR, duration and justification.
Consultancy professional fees & conditions
Maximum daily rates: national investigator USD 300/day; data manager USD 225/day; other personnel rates negotiated with CP but should not exceed USD 200/day. Weekend or holiday rates only paid if work is confirmed. Number of days and rates must be agreed before travel; increases require Core Partner agreement. Consultant roles/responsibilities, client responsibilities (logistics), deliverables and reimbursements must be defined in advance.
Exchange Rate Policy Guidance — Highest Rate (HR) principle
Where host country conversion of grant funds to local currency is required, AB policy requires use of the Highest Rate (HR) that is lawful and market-determined at the time. HR ensures budgets and local costing are not understated/overstated by use of artificially low official rates. The guidance gives examples: unified market rate where available; auction rates where auctions exist; commercial bank or parallel/free market rates where those are lawful; where parallel markets are substantial (≥10% of transactions) and 10%+ above official rate HR may be parallel rate; HR excludes illegal black-market rates or special privileged government rates for specific objectives.
Exchange rate flexibility & exceptions
Not every transaction must use the exact HR — commercial bank spot transactions and negotiated arm’s-length transactions may use negotiated rates (acceptable as long as not significantly and persistently different from HR). “Significantly and persistently” is defined as ±10% and three months duration. Donors recognise exceptions where partners move in good faith toward market rates; deviations must be documented. In hyper-inflation contexts, dollar disbursement schemes (gradual dollar releases) are recommended to reduce depreciation risk. Donors may approve exceptions in special cases.
Cash-flow control — estimates & projections
Annual budgets must be supported by monthly or quarterly projected cash-flow statements showing receipts and payments. Short, medium and long-term cash projections must be regularly updated and compared to original estimates. Where drawdowns from lenders are needed, drawdown notices must be timely and in required form. Core Partners must provide cash flow projections to Secretariat quarterly.
Asset management — purchases, insurance & depreciation
Asset purchases require approval of the functional or partner director and must be supported by at least three quotations prior to order (repeat purchases from reputable vendors like MSU Tech Store or Amazon may be exempt). Assets must be insured. Computer equipment is depreciated over four years (full depreciation period specified). Asset registers, purchasing documentation and inventory records must be maintained for audits and physical verification.
Payroll standards — controls, recording, changes & reconciliations
Payroll payments only to authorised employees at approved rates. Controls include pre-numbered payroll change forms, director authorisation for payroll changes, reconciliation of gross pay period-to-period for additions/removals and standing data, checks of authorising initials, correct donor allocations, sample calculation checks, reconciliation of payroll cheques to payroll register, and prompt recording of unclaimed wages. Payroll data changes (new hires, terminations, pay changes, voluntary deductions) require written authorisation. Payroll must be reconciled to GL and bank regularly.
Time sheets, overtime & payroll data maintenance
Hourly employees must complete daily time sheets indicating days worked, cost centre and leave. Originals submitted to finance on a fixed monthly date and signed by employee and supervisor. Overtime requires manager approval. Payroll processing must ensure accuracy of hours and ensure all payroll disbursements are for valid services performed. Payroll control sheet and sample verification procedures described elsewhere in the manual.
Coding / expense allocation — apportionment & detail
All expense documents must be coded before authorisation/payment (partner, expense type). Apportionments must be supported by schedules (FTE, % time, fixed splits, project-coded timesheets) and approved by budget holders. Donor-required effort reporting must be retained and reconciled to apportionment schedules. Lumping expenses as “other direct costs” is discouraged without detailed analysis and supporting backup.
Control — serial documents & accountable forms
Accountable documents must be serially numbered and controlled (purchase order books, cheque books, goods received notes, invoice books, receipt books, etc.). Receipt and issuance records are maintained; unused/returned books are kept under lock and key. A register of controlled documents is maintained by authorised Finance/Administration officers.
Record retention policy — periods & categories
Financial records and supporting documentation retained in partner archives. General retention: contracts, grants, project financial records retained for six years after final report, final audit and closeout (or per donor/local statutory requirement if longer). The manual lists specific retention guidance by category: accounting (bank statements, payroll, vouchers), expense reports, subsidiary ledgers, GL and journals, audit reports — generally six years; purchasing docs (POs, supplier contracts) — six years; corporate records (bylaws, charters, minutes, registration documents, tax returns) — indefinitely; insurance policies/claims & group disability records — 10 years; personnel expired contracts, leave records, disability records — 10 years or indefinitely for personnel files (terminated) per manual. Where donor or local law requires longer retention, that governs.
Financial templates, systems & integration (summary)
The FMM references and requires use of standard templates (payroll control sheet, GLACOS upload template, payslip template, timesheet/effort report, advance request & recovery form, apportionment schedule, payroll reconciliation worksheet, payroll authorisation checklist). Use HROS (or equivalent) for master data and GLACOS/approved templates for GL uploads; retain upload preview and reconciliation files. Systems integration and validation rules are recommended to reduce manual error (HROS-payroll-GL integration, automated bank feeds, validations to block missing tax IDs or mismatches).
Controls to minimise fraud, errors & misuse (summary)
Key controls: segregation of duties (preparer/reviewer/payment operator), independent authorisers, authorisation of master data changes with two-step approvals for payee additions, dual signatory for bank transfers, bank positive pay/validation services where available, restricted access to payroll files with audit logs, periodic surprise spot checks, analytics for unusual payment patterns (duplicate accounts, round salaries), whistleblower channels and incident response plans. Automated pre-post validation checks (tax ID presence, totals match, tax bands applied) and exception reporting are recommended.
Audit expectations & audit pack (summary)
Auditors expect full traceability between source documents and GL: payroll control sheet reconciliations, sample payslip + contract + timesheet evidence, bank advices corresponding to net pay payments, statutory remittance proofs, apportionment schedules and evidence of donor effort reporting, master data change logs, exception approvals, advances reconciliation, provider SOC reports for outsourced payroll, access logs, variance analysis for material movements and status of prior audit recommendations. Audit packs must be indexed and cross-referenced for efficient review.
Special/payment exceptions & escalation thresholds (summary)
Material exceptions (manual payments, severance, large adjustments) require higher approvals per delegated authority schedule (see Part 4). Example thresholds (illustrative): minor adjustments < USD 500 — payroll manager approval; medium USD 500–5,000 — CFD approval; high > USD 5,000 or unusual items — CD + CFD + HR/legal sign-off. Write-offs of unrecovered advances require DoA authority and documentation of recovery efforts. All exceptions require documentary justification and retention in payroll or financial pack.
Implementation & continuous improvement guidance (summary)
Immediate actions: publish payroll and financial calendars/cut-offs, restrict and review access to master data, reconcile current payroll control sheet to bank and GL, ensure apportionment schedules approved for donor-funded staff, review advances ledger and recovery plans, run sample payroll checks documenting findings, ensure statutory remittance proof filed and indexed, confirm outsourced provider SLAs and control attestations. Medium term: implement automated validation checks, integrate HROS to payroll and GL, configure GL to require PayrollRun ID, conduct payroll health checks, provide role-specific training and update SOPs/checklists. PART 4 — DELEGATED AUTHORITY LIMITS (Extremely comprehensive single-column summary)
Purpose & rationale
Part 4 defines and documents the Delegated Authority (DoA) framework for Afrobarometer (AB). Its purpose is to devolve decision-making so routine operational decisions are taken quickly at the right level while preserving Board oversight of material strategic, legal and financial matters. The DoA framework: (a) protects AB by ensuring commitments are made by authorised persons within set limits; (b) enforces segregation of duties and auditability; (c) requires that decisions align with approved budgets, donor agreements, and statutory rules; and (d) establishes escalation and approval pathways for exceptions and higher-risk matters.
Scope & application
Applies across the AB organisation and network partners. Matters outside specified authority levels or not explicitly covered are referred upwards (CEO/CMT/Board) as set out. Delegations are subject to budget limits, donor/statuary constraints and the FMM; donor or legal requirements that are stricter override the DoA.
Governing principles
- Delegation is permitted but ultimate accountability remains with the Board.
- Delegations are documented in writing and communicated to Finance and relevant staff.
- Segregation of duties: maker/checker/approver must be separate where practical.
- Monetary limits apply per transaction and cannot be split artificially to circumvent limits.
- Emergency approvals are allowed but must be documented and ratified retrospectively by appropriate authority.
- Temporary delegations (e.g., during leave) must be time-bound and within delegator’s own limits.
Hierarchy of authority (roles referenced in DoA)
Board of Directors (BoD) → Central Management Team (CMT) → Chief Executive Officer (CEO) → Chief Operating Officer (COO) → Finance Manager (FM) → Functional/Programme/Core Partner Directors (HoD) → Project Accountants / Finance Officers. Each role carries defined scopes and monetary ceilings described in subsequent sections.
Matters reserved to the Board / Board’s powers
The Board retains approval authority for high-level strategic, legal and financial matters, such as: approval of the FMM and DoA; organisational strategic plan and annual company plan (after CMT review); major acquisitions/disposals or business closures above Board thresholds; establishment of new businesses where projected three-year expenditure exceeds Board thresholds; borrowings/guarantees above Board thresholds; opening/closing bank accounts and appointment of signatories; capital commitments beyond delegated thresholds; appointment/removal of external auditors; and any other matter explicitly reserved to the Board. The Board must be consulted on material changes and receives escalation for extraordinary matters.
CMT (Central Management Team) authorities & responsibilities
CMT exercises delegated authority for strategic operational matters within Board-set limits and is responsible for: approving projects/transactions below Board thresholds but above CEO/COO thresholds; approving exceptions to standard policies; oversight of the DoA framework; review and approval of annual company plans prior to Board submission; approval of borrowing/guarantees below Board thresholds where permitted; and corporate restructuring matters not captured by Board reserved powers. CMT is also the controlling authority when specified in the DoA schedules.
CEO — executive authority
The CEO is the primary executive authorised to commit AB to contracts and expenditures within the CEO’s stated monetary limits and per budget. CEO responsibilities include signing donor and sub-grant agreements (within limit), approving spend and contract awards up to CEO thresholds, approving high-level personnel decisions subject to Part 4 HR rules, and escalating matters beyond CEO limits to CMT/Board. The CEO may further delegate specific authorities in writing (e.g., to COO) but cannot delegate beyond their own limits.
COO — operational authority
COO handles day-to-day operational approvals and controls within COO limits: operational procurements, contracts and payments (subject to segregation of duties and budget availability), oversight of procurement and payment processes, approval of travel and operational advances in line with policy, management of operational exceptions for referral to CEO/CMT, and supervision of FM and finance functions to ensure compliance with DoA.
Finance Manager (FM) — control & payment authority
FM’s authority is focused on financial control (not strategic commitments): verifying expenditures are within approved budgets and properly coded; approving payments, journals and reconciliations within FM monetary limits once vouchers are authorised; authorising petty cash replenishments; maintaining Chart of Accounts and enforcing coding discipline; monitoring compliance with DoA and reporting breaches; and escalating anomalies. FM cannot commit funds by contract but can refuse processing when approvals are absent or exceed limits.
Functional / Programme / Core Partner Directors (HoD)
Directors may initiate and approve expenditures, procurements and contractual engagements within their delegated limits and approved budgets. Their responsibilities include certifying receipt of goods/services, confirming value for money, approving travel and activity expenses for their units, and ensuring program expenditures align with donor terms and approved work plans. Directors must obtain higher-level approvals for items above their authorised ceilings.
Project Accountants & Finance Officers — operational restrictions
Operational finance staff process payments, record transactions, prepare reconciliations and reports and manage petty cash within specified operational limits. They must not authorise commitments or sign contracts. They are empowered to refuse processing of any transaction lacking proper authorisation or that exceeds delegated limits.
Monetary thresholds & sample table of delegated approvals (explicit thresholds from Part 4)
— Acquisition, disposal or closure of a business or interest: US$0.1 million (≥) — Board; below US$0.1 million — CMT.
— Establishment of a new business (including JV/partnership): estimated expenditure over three years > US$0.5 million — Board; < US$0.5 million — CMT.
— Annual company plan: Board approval (after CMT review). Significant variations to forecasts must be notified to CEO/COO.
— Capital investment / disposal of tangible assets (incl. software): Unbudgeted > US$100,000 — Board; US$10,000–US$99,999 — CMT; Below US$10,000 — relevant functional director.
— Borrowings by AB from unrelated third parties / guarantees / letters of comfort: ≥ US$0.2 million — Board; < US$0.2 million — CMT.
— Borrowings by related entities from unrelated third parties / guarantees from other related entities: where underlying transaction ≥ US$0.2 million — Board; < US$0.2 million — CMT.
— Borrowings or grants of credit facilities between related entities: ≥ US$0.2 million (aggregate) — CMT.
— Increase/reduction of authorised or issued capital by an operating subsidiary: CMT approval required for all values (note: capitalisation of non-distributable profits does not require reference).
— Unbudgeted redemption or early repayment of loans/facilities: CMT approval required.
— Corporate restructuring not covered above: CMT approval required.
(Note in Part 4: where borrowings/guarantees are part of an approved program for acquisitions/investments previously approved by the Board, only CMT approval may be required.)
Notes & additional directions associated with thresholds
a) Change of name of related companies and establishment of new companies should be approved by CMT and notified to the Board.
b) Establishment or closure of companies, representative offices or branch offices outside home country should be agreed with the Board and involve external tax advice.
c) Appointments of directors (operating companies) should be notified to the Board; audit committee appointments should be referred to the audit committee chair with notification to the Company Secretary.
Human resources matters (referral & approval rules)
- Board-level referrals: succession planning for all senior managers and higher must be referred to the Board.
- CMT referrals (matters to be considered by CMT chaired by CEO): revisions to incentive schemes affecting senior managers; general changes to salary, benefits, contracts or T&Cs for all employees; major changes to pension/benefit schemes following Remuneration Committee recommendation; merit/performance ratings for senior managers; proposed appointments/promotions/regradings/salary revisions/dismissals/redundancies/early retirement of senior managers; immediate notification of any employee fatalities; proposed appointments of directors to boards of group entities (non-trading/dormant excluded).
- Appointment of heads of department: CEO appoints heads of departments; heads consult CEO when appointing team members.
Agreements & contracts — signing authority
- Authority to sign contracts within the delegated limits and approved budget is vested in the CEO. Contracts or agreements that exceed delegated limits must be approved by the Board. Contracts include procurement, service agreements, consultants, asset transfers, leases, licences, MOUs, and other legal arrangements. Contract amendments follow the same approval thresholds as original contracts.
Amendments to the Delegated Authority schedule
- Amendments to the DoA matrix must be presented to the Board for review and ratification. The COO is the custodian of the delegated authorities and maintains the schedule.
Authority Matrix (legend & purpose)
- Legend: A = Final approval; B = Must agree/approve; C = Must be consulted (before); D = Must be informed (after); E = Originates.
- The Authority Matrix in Part 4 provides a mapped list of routine transactions and which role(s) are responsible, must approve, consult or be informed (examples include grant agreements, sub-grants, annual budgets, asset transfers, insurance, litigation, purchases, staff appointments, expenses claims). The matrix enforces segregation of duties and records who executes vs who approves.
Examples from the Authority Matrix (selected entries and role mapping)
- Grant agreements with donors: CMT = A (final approval), CEO/COO = C/E, FM = C/E, other roles informed as shown in the full matrix.
- Sub-grant agreements: Board = A, CMT = B, CEO = E/Originates, COO/FM = C/D depending on item.
- Annual work plans and budgets: Board = A, CMT = B, CEO/COO = B/E, FM = C (refer to full matrix for complete role mapping).
- Budgeted CAPEX > US$100k: Board = A; < US$100k: CMT/CEO depending on whether budgeted or unbudgeted (check exact matrix cells).
- Purchases / purchase orders: low-value purchases delegated to HoDs/Project Managers; purchases > US$5k <100k require higher level approvals; > US$100k typically referred to Board/CMT as per procurement rules. (The full Authority Matrix in the manual lists each line item and the corresponding approvers — consult that matrix for precise role-by-role responsibilities.)
Payments, advances, write-offs & recoveries
- Payments must be authorised in line with the DoA limits and processed according to segregation rules (preparer ≠ approver ≠ payment signatory where practicable).
- Advances (travel, operational, sub-grant) must be approved at the delegated level and recorded as receivables until accounted for.
- Write-offs of unrecovered advances or losses require higher-level approvals depending on value: small write-offs may be within CFO/FMs limits but larger write-offs require CMT or Board approval per schedule and DoA thresholds and must follow documentation/attempted recovery rules. Staff loans are generally prohibited unless Board-approved.
Procurement & contract award rules in relation to DoA
- Procurement approvals require both appropriate procurement procedure compliance (quotes, competitive processes, sole-source justification) and signature by persons with the delegated monetary authority.
- Waivers of competitive procurement must be approved by a higher authority level and documented in the procurement record. Contract amendments follow the same delegated thresholds as original awards.
Delegation, revocation, temporary delegations and notification
- Delegations must be documented; the Finance/HR/Company Secretary must be notified of changes.
- Temporary delegations (e.g., for leave cover) must be written, time-limited and cannot exceed the delegator’s own authority.
- The Board, CEO or COO may revoke delegations at any time for non-compliance or organisational need; revocation must be documented and communicated.
Monitoring, compliance, audit & sanctions
- FM must monitor compliance with DoA via transaction reviews, periodic reporting and routine reconciliations.
- Internal and external audits will assess adherence to DoA; audit findings on non-compliance must be remediated promptly.
- Breaches of DoA are treated as non-compliance under the Ways of Working (WoW) and may trigger disciplinary measures, demand for recovery of unauthorised commitments, or other sanctions depending on severity.
Relationship to other FMM parts & donor/legal precedence
Part 4 links to Part 2 (policies & procedures — budgeting, procurement, payments, accounting controls) and Part 3 (sub-grantee rules). Specific templates, approval forms and reporting formats are in the appendices. Where donor agreements or statutory rules impose stricter requirements, those take precedence over the DoA; deviations must be documented and approved as required.
Practical implementation notes & governance practice
- All approvals and delegations should be recorded, dated and retained for audit trails.
- Finance must maintain a current register of authorised signatories and delegation letters.
- Delegated limits should be reflected in ERP/finance system role permissions where possible to prevent unauthorised processing.
- Regular training for approvers is recommended so approvers understand limits, PO/procurement rules, and segregation requirements.
Summary — role of Part 4 in AB financial governance
Part 4 is the authoritative rulebook for who may commit AB legally and financially and up to what amounts. It balances the need for timely operational decision-making with Board and donor assurance by formalising monetary thresholds, escalation routes, HR referral rules, contract signing limits, procurement sign-off rules, and monitoring/sanction mechanisms. Correct implementation ensures accountability, audit readiness, and control over organisational and donor funds.

Budget content & required outputs: Revenue budget by source; survey operational budget (incl. technical assistance & dissemination); communications/publications budget; capacity building budget; overheads and special projects; capital expenditure budget; consolidated budget booklet/summary and supporting schedules (apportionments, payroll, travel, procurement). Assumptions & constraints: Use standard FX, inflation, airfare and salary assumptions provided by Secretariat; budgets must be realistic and evidence-based; managers must highlight material variances and provide justification; budgets must align with donor terms and allowable cost categories.
Quarterly Budget Reviews (QBR): Quarterly forecast and reforecast to reset budgets and approve material changes; a ±10% threshold triggers CMT approval for variations. QBR schedule: QBR1 Week 1 April, QBR2 Week 1 July, QBR3 Week 1 October, Annual Budget November/December.
Governance & escalation: CMT reviews resource allocation proposals and approves final budgets prior to Board submission. Exceptions not in budget require documented justification and CMT/Board approval per DoA. Training and capacity building for budget owners is recommended.
Appendix 5 — Partner Risk Assessment Questionnaire (full content & use)
Purpose: Standard pre-award/contracting disclosure and risk questionnaire to assess National Partners’ organisational, financial, governance and political exposure before contracting. Used to inform selection, due diligence, and monitoring decisions.
Key sections & sample questions:
• Legal & incorporation status — “Is your organisation incorporated? Provide registration documents.”
• Political exposure & neutrality — “Is your organisation or principal officers involved in partisan politics or politically exposed activities that could put AB’s non-partisan status at risk?”
• Tax & statutory compliance — “Has your organisation complied with tax obligations and can it obtain a valid tax clearance certificate?” (proof requested).
• Financial viability — “Is your organisation solvent and does it have audited financial statements?” (three years requested).
• Past conduct — “Any corruption or bribery cases in the last year?” and request auditor confirmation of independence.
• Controls & policies — “Does your organisation have financial policies and procedures?”
• Risk relationships — “Does your organisation have relationships with groups that engage in political violence?”
• Staff political involvement & funding of political parties — questions on staff partisan activities and organisational funding to political parties.
Declaration & sign-off: Executive Director/CEO signs a declaration certifying responses and absence of criminal proceedings related to theft, corruption or fraud. Questionnaire results inform CP/Core Partner decisions and contract terms.
Appendix 7 — Afrobarometer Chart of Accounts (COA) — purpose & usage
Purpose: The COA provides the standard ledger structure and coding required for consistent recording across the network: expense categories, revenue lines, project cost centres, payroll, statutory liabilities, advances, asset accounts, intercompany clearing, and donor/fund codes. It ensures consistent classification for consolidated reporting, donor reporting and audit. The Finance Manager maintains the COA and revisions require COO sign-off. All partners must map and use the COA for GL posting, GLACOS uploads and reconciliation.
Appendix 8 — Sub-Grantee Reporting Template (embedded file, required elements)
Purpose: Standard quarterly financial report template CPs/SUs/NPs must use when submitting financial accountability for instalments. Template (embedded) includes: summary of funds received and expenditure by budget line, narrative on activity progress, reconciliation of advances, bank statement extracts, copies of key supporting documents and schedules (payroll, procurement, asset purchases), and certification/signatures.

Sub-grantees must follow file naming conventions and retention rules. Submissions are prerequisites for subsequent instalments.
Appendix 9 — Budgeting Template & Sample Instructions (embedded file)

The instructions describe assumptions, allowable cost rules, required supporting schedules (headcount, apportionment worksheets), and how to populate FX and inflation assumptions.

Appendices — cross-cutting administrative rules & templates
- File naming conventions & retention: Appendices prescribe standard file naming (Payroll_PRYYYYMM_Country_YYYYMMDD_ControlPack.pdf etc.) and retention periods (minimum six years for financial records unless donor/local law requires longer).
- Templates & version control: All templates maintained centrally with version control; partners must use current templates from the Policies folder on Google Drive.
- Audit pack composition: Appendices specify audit pack items (payroll control sheets, sample payslips, timesheets, bank advices, statutory remittances, GL upload previews, apportionment schedules, advance recovery schedules, procurement documentation).
- Operational links: Appendices link to sections in Parts 1–4 for governance, budgeting, procurement and delegated authority to ensure consistent interpretation and application.
Implementation & compliance expectations for appendices
- Mandatory adoption: All network members must adopt the appendices where relevant — e.g., Appendix 3 travel rules apply to all AB-funded travel; Appendix 1 controls apply where cards are issued; Appendix 9/10 templates must be used for budgets; Appendix 8 reporting template is mandatory for quarterly NP/CP/SU submissions.
- Training & capacity building: Secretariat provides guidance and hands-on training to partners for templates, budgeting procedures, and financial reporting to reduce repeat errors and ensure audit readiness.
- Change control: Any updates to appendices must follow the FMM change/version procedures and be approved by the custodians (COO/FM) and where material, ratified by the Board.
Summary — the role of Part 5 appendices in AB financial control architecture
Part 5 operationalises the FMM by providing concrete, enforceable instruments: card usage rules, an ethical code, travel/advance rules, a detailed budgeting process and templates, partner risk screening, donor-specific audit terms, the COA, and required reporting templates. These appendices enable consistent implementation, auditability, donor compliance and central oversight; they reduce ambiguity by standardising formats, deadlines, approvals and evidence requirements, and they form the backbone of AB’s network-wide financial governance and control environment.
