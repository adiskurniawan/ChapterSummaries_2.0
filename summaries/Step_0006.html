Overall model
1. Overall model — how the three sheets work together
Staff is the master employee reference, SC_Allocation is the month-by-month approval-code assignment map, and SCA_Data is the monthly cost output by person and approval code. Together, they form a full staff costing and allocation workflow. The model begins with identity, moves through allocation rules, and ends with costed output.

This is a classic master-to-assignment-to-fact design. It is efficient because each sheet has one main responsibility. That separation keeps the workbook easier to understand and reduces duplication.
2. Overall model — practical interpretation
In operational terms, the workbook first identifies who the staff member is, then determines how that staff member is split across approval codes in a given month, and finally calculates the payroll cost lines for that split. That is the practical logic of the file. It is not merely storing payroll data; it is transforming staff identity and allocation logic into financial detail.

When the workbook is used correctly, the user can start with a person in Staff, see that person’s monthly routing in SC_Allocation, and then trace the resulting monthly amounts in SCA_Data. That creates a clear end-to-end audit path.
3. Overall model — key dependency chain
If Staff is incorrect, identity and tax data become unreliable. If SC_Allocation is incorrect, the cost split by month will be wrong. If SCA_Data is incorrect, the financial output will not reconcile. Each sheet has a distinct role, but all three depend on each other. A failure in any one layer can reduce the integrity of the others.

This dependency chain is what makes the workbook both powerful and sensitive. It can generate detailed monthly staffing cost data, but only if the reference data, allocation rules, and output records are all aligned.
4. Overall model — why the design is sensible
The design is sensible because it separates stable data from changing data. Staff changes relatively slowly, SC_Allocation changes by month or by assignment decision, and SCA_Data changes every period as the payroll result is generated. That separation makes maintenance easier and supports different business users: HR can manage staff data, operations can manage allocation, and finance can consume the cost output.

In a business workbook, that kind of division of responsibility is one of the strongest indicators of a workable structure. It makes the model easier to audit, easier to update, and easier to explain.
5. Overall model — where errors usually appear
Most errors in a workbook like this usually come from mismatched names, inconsistent codes, stale mappings, or split allocations that are not interpreted properly. Those issues do not always break the workbook visibly, but they can quietly distort the output. That is why the model should be reviewed across all three sheets rather than in isolation.

A clean Staff sheet without a clean SC_Allocation sheet is still risky. A clean allocation sheet without a clean output sheet is still incomplete. The reliability of the final payroll cost depends on all three layers working together.
6. Overall model — control takeaway
The overall model is a staff costing pipeline. Staff establishes the person, SC_Allocation establishes the monthly charge mapping, and SCA_Data records the charged cost. That pipeline is simple to describe but operationally important because it controls how payroll data becomes management reporting data.

In that sense, the workbook is not just a spreadsheet; it is a structured cost allocation system. Its correctness depends on consistent master data, consistent monthly assignment logic, and consistent transactional output formatting.
Staff sheet
1. Staff — what this sheet is
This sheet is the employee master data table. It is the central reference layer for a staff costing workbook because it holds the stable identity, payroll, tax, and administrative attributes that other sheets depend on. In practical terms, this is the sheet that answers the question: “Who is this employee, and what fixed attributes define them for payroll and reporting purposes?”

The role of a master data sheet is different from the role of a transaction sheet. A transaction sheet records what happened in a specific period, but a master sheet defines the people and attributes that stay relatively consistent over time. That distinction matters because payroll logic usually needs both: a stable employee reference and a changing monthly cost output. Staff is the stable reference point in that structure.
2. Staff — row grain
Each row represents one staff record. That means the row grain is employee-level, not month-level, not cost-line-level, and not approval-code-level. The sheet is organized around one person per row, which is the normal design for a human resources or payroll master table. This structure makes it easier to look up a person, validate a code, and maintain payroll-specific attributes in one place.

Because each row is a single staff entity, the sheet can be used as a lookup base for downstream formulas, validation rules, and allocation logic. If a workbook later needs to pull a name, tax ID, or gross-up flag into a payroll report, this is the sheet that should be queried. The one-row-per-person design also makes audit review simpler because each staff member has one canonical record instead of scattered information across many transactional lines.
3. Staff — key identifiers
Staff Code, D365 Staff Code, Name, and Tax ID Number are the main identity fields. They serve different but related purposes. Staff Code is likely the internal human resources key, D365 Staff Code is the ERP or system integration key, Name is the readable human identifier, and Tax ID Number is the statutory identifier used for tax compliance and payroll documentation. Together, these fields create a multi-layer identity profile for each employee.

In operational use, these identifiers are not interchangeable. A workbook may display the name to users, but use the staff code for stable joins and the tax ID for legal filings. That is why master data sheets almost always carry more than one identity field. They reduce dependency on any single identifier and make it possible to reconcile the same person across payroll, accounting, tax, and ERP systems.
4. Staff — D365 mapping behavior
D365 Staff Code appears to be the ERP or Dynamics 365 reference. A value of #N/A in that field suggests the staff member is not mapped cleanly to D365 or the lookup failed. In a payroll workbook, that is an important signal because it means the employee exists in the local HR or payroll context but may not have a successful cross-system mapping yet.

This kind of mapping issue is common in real business files. Sometimes the staff member is new, sometimes the ERP record has not been created, and sometimes the lookup formula is pointing to a code that no longer exists in the source system. A #N/A should therefore be treated as a data control exception rather than ignored. It is a warning that the employee record may need reconciliation before the workbook can be trusted for downstream posting or reporting.
5. Staff — payroll attributes
Gross Up (Y/N) and Marital Status are payroll and tax treatment fields. They influence how salary, allowances, and withholding are calculated, especially when a workbook needs to distinguish between employees who receive gross-up treatment and those who do not. Marital Status is also important because tax withholding formulas often depend on family or dependency status, which can affect the taxable base or withholding category.

These fields are not just descriptive labels. They are decision inputs. In a payroll engine, a field like Gross Up (Y/N) can determine whether a tax burden is borne by the company or passed through to the employee, while marital status can affect the tax rate or allowance category used in payroll calculations. That means this sheet is not only storing HR details; it is storing computational attributes that may drive formulas elsewhere in the model.
6. Staff — bank and reporting fields
Name in BNI is likely the bank-ready display name used for payment processing or bank transfer formatting. This field matters because payment systems often require a name format that matches the bank record more closely than the internal HR display name. Notes Sisca and Notes on Staff Code are operational comment fields that can capture exceptions, special handling, legacy references, or internal instructions for people who maintain the workbook.

These kinds of fields are common in production spreadsheets because the workbook must support both calculation and administration. A sheet that only stores strict payroll fields often becomes difficult to maintain without a place for annotations. The notes columns provide that flexibility. They help operators remember why a code exists, why a mapping is unusual, or why a staff record needs special review.
7. Staff — business role
This sheet functions as the master reference behind the allocation and payroll costing sheets. It provides the stable staff identity data that the monthly costing logic depends on. If SCA_Data is the month-by-month result and SC_Allocation is the month-by-month assignment map, then Staff is the base reference that defines the people being assigned and costed in the first place.

The business role of this sheet is therefore foundational. It is the layer that supports lookup, validation, and compliance. Most problems in downstream sheets trace back to either a missing master record, a mismatch in naming, or an incorrect staff code. For that reason, the quality of the Staff sheet has a direct effect on the reliability of the entire workbook. A clean staff master table is usually the first requirement for a trustworthy cost allocation model.
8. Staff — relationship to the other sheets
SCA_Data uses Name and Approval code for cost allocation, while SC_Allocation uses Name and monthly approval mapping. The Staff sheet should be the source used to validate names, codes, tax IDs, and payroll flags before those costs are distributed. In other words, Staff supplies the identity and attribute base, SC_Allocation supplies the monthly routing logic, and SCA_Data records the costed output.

This relationship is important because the workbook needs consistency across all three layers. If the same person is written slightly differently in one sheet, the join may fail or duplicate records may appear. If a tax flag is wrong in the master data, payroll treatment downstream can be wrong even if the allocation logic is correct. The Staff sheet therefore acts as the anchor for data governance across the entire model.
9. Staff — data quality point
Because the costing and allocation sheets rely on names, code consistency is important. Any mismatch between Staff, SC_Allocation, and SCA_Data can create duplicate records, missing mappings, or incorrect payroll charging. That means data quality in this sheet is not cosmetic; it is structurally significant. A spelling difference, an outdated staff code, or an unmapped ERP code can change how downstream formulas behave.

In practice, a payroll model like this benefits from strict maintenance rules. Names should be standardized, codes should be unique, and exceptions should be documented. If the workbook is used repeatedly each month, the staff master should be treated as controlled reference data rather than a free-form list. That is the simplest way to keep the allocation and costing outputs stable over time.
10. Staff — why this sheet is usually maintained separately
Keeping employee master data in a separate sheet is a sign of good workbook design. It avoids repeating the same identity, tax, and payroll attributes inside the monthly cost table. If each monthly row had to carry all employee metadata, the workbook would become harder to maintain and easier to corrupt. By separating master data from transactional data, the model reduces duplication and improves consistency.

This separation also makes updates easier. If a tax ID changes, or a staff member’s gross-up status changes, the correction can be made once in Staff rather than in many monthly records. That supports both accuracy and efficiency. A well-managed master sheet is one of the simplest ways to reduce errors in long-form payroll and allocation workbooks.

Blank cells in this sheet are not always an error, but they are always worth interpreting carefully. A blank Address field, for example, may simply mean that the workbook does not use addresses for this process. A blank Notes field may mean no exception is needed. But blank identity fields such as Staff Code, Name, or Tax ID Number would be a much more serious concern because those fields affect matching and compliance.

The important point is that not every missing value has the same business meaning. Some columns are mandatory for system logic, while others are optional administrative metadata. A good reviewer should distinguish between the two. The Staff sheet likely contains both core fields and supporting fields, so data review should focus first on the identifiers and payroll-driving attributes.

13. Staff — operational takeaway
At a practical level, this sheet is the control center for “who is who” in the workbook. It is where the workbook establishes identity, tax treatment, ERP alignment, and reporting labels before any allocation or cost calculation happens. If the Staff sheet is clean and current, the rest of the model has a much better chance of being accurate.

SC_Allocation
1. SC_Allocation — what this sheet is
This sheet appears to be the allocation mapping table that drives how each staff member’s cost is assigned across months. It is a crosswalk between employee names and monthly approval codes, and it effectively tells the workbook how to route each person’s payroll cost for every month of the year. That makes it a planning and distribution sheet rather than a transactional cost sheet.

In a payroll allocation model, a sheet like this is essential because the same staff member may belong to different approval codes over time, or may need to be split between more than one code in a single month. The SC_Allocation sheet stores that routing logic in a simple year-long matrix.
2. SC_Allocation — row grain
Each row represents one person. Each month column shows which approval code applies for that person in that month. The sheet is therefore a time-based assignment matrix. The row grain is employee-level, but the values inside the row change by month, which means the sheet captures both identity and time variation in one place.

This structure is very practical because it lets a reviewer scan a person’s annual allocation pattern in a single line. It becomes easy to see whether someone remains consistently assigned to one approval code all year, or whether their charge needs to shift partway through the year. The model is therefore readable both by month and by person.
3. SC_Allocation — column structure
The columns run from JAN Approval Codes through DEC Approval Codes. That makes the sheet a full-year reference table rather than a transactional table. The monthly structure is especially useful because it aligns with payroll cycles and allows the workbook to convert a calendar month into the correct cost allocation code for that month.

The month-by-month layout also gives the sheet a planning dimension. Instead of storing only the current month’s assignment, it stores the year’s assignment pattern. That means the workbook can be used for both current operations and forward-looking allocation review.
4. SC_Allocation — single-code behavior
For many employees, a month contains one approval code repeated across the year. That means the person is fully assigned to a single approval bucket for those months. This is the simplest form of allocation and usually reflects a stable organizational relationship, such as one employee being tied to one cost center or one approval stream for a long period.

Single-code behavior is easy to maintain and easy to interpret. It produces a clean downstream record because the payroll cost can be assigned without needing to split percentages. In a downstream costing table, a single code in the allocation sheet often translates into a single row per person per month in the result sheet.
5. SC_Allocation — split-code behavior
Some cells contain multiple approval codes with percentages, such as OCT2529080 | 90%; 247GR | 10%. That indicates a monthly split allocation, where one person’s cost is divided between two approval codes in fixed proportions. This is a more advanced allocation pattern and usually reflects a person who works across two funding streams, two departments, or two operational responsibilities in the same month.

Split allocations are operationally important because they require the downstream costing logic to preserve percentages accurately. A 90/10 split is not just a label; it is a computational instruction. If the workbook interprets the cell incorrectly, total cost by approval code will be wrong. That is why the formatting of these cells needs to be consistent and machine-readable.
6. SC_Allocation — operational role
This sheet likely acts as the source of truth for how monthly costs should be distributed when building SCA_Data. It tells the model whether a person belongs to one approval code or must be split across more than one. In effect, it is the bridge between the employee master data and the monthly payroll cost output.

Because of that role, the sheet is both an operational document and a control document. Users can inspect it to understand where the payroll cost will go, and formulas or macros can read it to generate the allocated output. A sheet with this function needs to be maintained carefully because it affects financial reporting and internal charge allocation.
7. SC_Allocation — relationship to Period
The month columns should line up with the Period values used in SCA_Data. For example, 202605 in SCA_Data should correspond to the May column in this allocation sheet. That alignment is the temporal link between the assignment map and the cost fact table. If the period mapping is off by one month, the entire costing result can be mis-routed.

This also means the sheet is likely used by formulas or automation that translate a numeric period into a specific month column. That relationship has to be consistent and unambiguous. A well-structured workbook should therefore treat the month headers as the authoritative monthly route map for cost allocation.
8. SC_Allocation — why this sheet matters
Without this mapping, the cost rows in SCA_Data would not know how to distribute staff cost by approval code and month. This sheet is the allocation engine behind the payroll costing structure. It provides the business rule that says, for each month, which approval code or split should receive the cost.

That makes the sheet a critical control point. Any error in this sheet is not just a clerical issue; it changes financial distribution. If an employee is assigned to the wrong code, cost may be posted against the wrong budget or project. For that reason, this sheet should be reviewed with the same care as any costing control schedule.
9. SC_Allocation — data quality point
The format inside each month cell must be consistent. If some cells use plain codes and others use split notation, any downstream automation must be able to parse both patterns correctly. This is important because the workbook may be used by formulas, VBA, Power Query, or manual review, and each tool has different tolerance for mixed text formatting.

Consistency also improves auditability. When every month cell follows a known pattern, it is easier to validate and easier to maintain. If the notation changes without a corresponding logic update, the downstream result can become unreliable. This is especially important in a year-long allocation matrix where one small text inconsistency can affect many monthly outputs.
10. SC_Allocation — why names are the row anchor
The person name appears to be the row anchor rather than the staff code. That is practical because names are the most immediately recognizable way for users to scan the table. A reviewer can quickly find a person and inspect the monthly pattern without having to decode a staff code first. This supports usability, especially when finance or HR staff are the primary maintainers.

At the same time, using names as the visible row anchor means the workbook must maintain name consistency very carefully. If a person’s name is written in two different ways across sheets, the mapping can fail. That is why the Staff master sheet should remain the canonical source for identity references, even if the allocation sheet is named-based for readability.
11. SC_Allocation — what a split cell implies operationally
A split cell implies that one employee’s monthly cost is not fully charged to a single bucket. That can happen for many business reasons: part-time project work, shared responsibility across departments, temporary reassignment, or budgetary sharing between approval streams. The sheet does not need to explain the business reason in every case, but it must preserve the split clearly enough for the costing logic to use it.

This kind of arrangement is common in organizations with matrix structures. The allocation sheet therefore acts as a practical record of business complexity. It allows the payroll model to represent reality more accurately than a single-code-only system would.
12. SC_Allocation — operational takeaway
Overall, this sheet is the monthly allocation bridge. It shows who is charged where, when, and in what split. It is the sheet that turns a stable employee reference into a time-dependent charge allocation. If it is accurate, SCA_Data can be generated cleanly. If it is wrong, the cost distribution will be wrong even if all payroll calculations are otherwise correct.

This sheet is likely used for cost reporting, budget allocation, and managerial chargeback by approval code. It is the detailed output sheet that downstream reports would use for monthly staff cost analysis. In other words, it is where payroll becomes finance-ready for consumption by analysts, controllers, and project owners.

Because of that purpose, the sheet must be structured in a way that supports aggregation and audit. It is not enough to show a total; users need to be able to trace how the total was built. The detailed component columns support that traceability.
12. SCA_Data — data quality point
Because the same person can appear multiple times, consistency between Name, Approval code, and FTE in Month is critical. Any mismatch here would distort monthly cost allocation. If the code is wrong, the cost is charged to the wrong bucket. If the FTE is wrong, the split amount is wrong. If the name is inconsistent, the record may not reconcile cleanly to the allocation or master sheets.

For that reason, this sheet should be checked as both a financial output and a control output. The totals may look plausible even when the allocation logic is wrong, so the row-level details matter.
13. SCA_Data — why the period is important
Period is the time anchor of the entire table. The workbook is not simply recording employee cost in the abstract; it is recording cost for a specific month. That makes the sheet suitable for monthly reporting, rolling forecasts, and month-end reconciliation. The period value also gives the sheet a natural connection back to the allocation matrix in SC_Allocation.

Having a period field in the fact table also allows the workbook to be filtered or summarized over time. That is especially useful for year-to-date reporting, monthly trend analysis, and period-based cost comparisons.
14. SCA_Data — what the sample rows suggest
The sample rows suggest that the same employee can have both full and split allocations within the same period. That is a strong indication that the model is built for real-world workforce complexity rather than a simplified one-line-per-person structure. It also suggests the workbook may need to handle a variety of allocation patterns, from single-code staff to multi-code staff.

That flexibility is valuable, but it also means the workbook should be carefully validated. A table like this can accumulate subtle discrepancies if the split logic is not applied consistently.
15. SCA_Data — operational takeaway
This sheet is the numeric outcome layer of the staff allocation model. It is the place where payroll components, statutory costs, payables, and allocation percentages come together. If the Staff sheet defines the employee, and SC_Allocation defines where the cost goes, SCA_Data defines how much cost lands in each place.
