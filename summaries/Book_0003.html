Chapter 1 • Intuition
Chapter 1: Intuition
Opening vignette — salespeople, museum, and sudden change:
A group of long-tenured salespeople—stalled, defensive, repeatedly citing “relationships” and time—were taken to a museum and given a short guided exercise: pick a surprising painting, imagine it rewinded and fast-forwarded, then return to work after a ten-minute drill. Two months later roughly 60% had risen from poor to average or better; 40% remained unchanged or were let go. The difference was not training content per se but the capacity to notice and internalize one exceptional detail. Those who improved could vividly recall a unique image; those who didn’t could not. That recall signaled reactivated intuition.
Definition — intuition as detection of exceptions:
Intuition here is not mere pattern recognition. Instead of matching precedent, it often originates from exceptional information: a fact, event, or anomaly that violates prevailing assumptions and opens a new causal story. Exceptional information is specific, immediately consequential, and requires initiative to surface. When acted upon, exceptions can lead to disproportionate advantage by revealing new rules rather than confirming old ones.
Army Special Operations — trained intuition and initiative:
U.S. Special Operators exhibit heightened, trainable intuition. Their method emphasizes running ahead of data and deliberately hunting exceptions. A veteran Operator in Baghdad (2003) met an Iraqi engineer who spoke near-perfect American English and warned that restoring utilities was essential to control—an observation that contradicted optimistic official narratives. The Operator’s initiative to report that exceptional detail anticipated geopolitical outcomes that others missed.
Exceptional information vs. algorithmic recognition:
Behavioral economists frame intuition as recognition (pattern match). The Army’s framing flips this: intuition detects ruptures—outliers that signal new possibilities. Machines and routine algorithms regress to the mean and dismiss flukes; trained human intuition suspends that dismissal and uses exceptions to hypothesize new dynamics.
Van Gogh — an artistic intuition of exception:
Van Gogh noticed chromatic clashes that conflicted with RYB orthodoxy (red vs green, yellow vs purple). He discovered that green–purple, yellow–blue, and (importantly) cyan–red produce stronger perceptual conflicts than classic rules predicted. His insight anticipated the RGB understanding later essential to electronic displays. Van Gogh’s “exception” reframed color relationships and preluded technological consequences.
Marie Curie — scientific intuition from a peculiar ray:
Curie observed faint emissions from uranium compounds that did not comport with known chemical bonding explanations. By treating the anomaly as significant rather than noise, she discovered radioactivity: energy emitted from the atom itself. That exceptional observation led to foundational shifts across physics, medicine, and technology.
Wozniak & Jobs — technological intuition and spotting exceptions in people:
Woz saw value in the Altair’s tiny, unconventional microcomputer and built the Apple I; that exception to the prevailing belief (bigger was always better) catalyzed personal computing. Jobs’s intuition specialized in spotting exceptional people—he read for asymmetrical talent and invested in outliers like Woz. Both forms of intuition (object and person) illustrate how exceptions beget new industries and organizations.
The childhood default — assume everything is special:
Children assume uniqueness: two spoons are not identical; every encounter matters. Modern adults default to pattern-driven cognition—efficient but blind to subtle anomalies. Restoring the childlike stance—treating things as possibly exceptional—reopens curiosity, empathy, and the capacity to spot nonobvious opportunities or threats.
Pineland and the training of exception noticing:
Pineland is a purpose-built Special Operations training landscape full of roleplayers and improbable backstories. Immersion in an environment where everything is exceptional forces the brain to relearn how to parse unique signals rather than default labels. Mind-travel—real immersion that breaks habitual cognitive anchors—is essential; superficial travel or routine exposure won’t reset perception.
Shift to Narrative — a practical habit to toggle off judgment:
Labeling and rapid judgment kill curiosity. The Pineland technique “Shift to Narrative” converts labels into concrete stories: when you catch yourself labeling (“serious,” “bro,” “soccer mom”), ask “Where did that judgment come from?” and then narrate the specific event or behavior that generated it. Replacing tags with story detours attention from category to cause and invites imagination and hypothesis.
Museum exercise distilled — why it worked:
Art presents curated exceptions—visual anomalies, compositional surprises, narrative gaps—so the viewer naturally imagines before judging. Asking subjects to pick a surprising detail and to imagine its past and future breaks habitual labeling and reactivates the brain’s capacity for exceptional detection. The follow-up ten-minute paired exercise taught questioning that multiplies surprise and restrains premature Why-questions.
The five-minute paired exercise — structure and rules:
Partner A asks five minutes of Who/What/When/Where/How questions about Partner B’s favorite nonwork activity. Crucially: do not ask Why. Why prompts abstract judgment and stops curiosity; specific Wh-questions excavate surprising facts. When a surprising detail appears, probe it with more Wh-questions (not Why). Resist premature self-identification (e.g., “me too”)—that return to self ends discovery.
Scoring and sales outcome — two-point hypothesis test:
After five minutes, the questioner hypothesizes why the partner engages in that activity. Agreement or surprise each give a point; two points (surprising but agreed) indicate the questioner uncovered exceptional, personally valid information. Top salespeople reach this target ~70% of the time—this is sales as knowing the customer better than they know themselves.
Spy-hunter interviews — confession and coherence as truth signals:
Special Operations interrogators seek confessions that are stranger than fiction yet internally coherent. Unexpected admissions that fit into a consistent narrative are taken as indicators of authenticity and exceptional information. The method reveals that the truth often appears peculiar; training oneself to accept and probe peculiarities produces deeper intelligence.
Operational rules for spotting exceptions:
1. Assume potential exceptionality: treat observations as possibly unique.
2. Delay Why; use Who/What/When/Where/How to refine specifics.
3. Convert labels into narratives—ask for concrete moments that produced the label.
4. Resist egocentric shortcuts ( “I’d do the same” ).
5. Immerse or simulate immersion to reset perception (museum, Pineland, roleplay).
Why modern cognition resists exceptions—and how to counter it:
Efficiency incentives (education, corporate KPIs, AI systems) reward rapid categorization, which reduces cognitive load but blinds us to low-frequency, high-impact anomalies. Countermeasures: deliberate novelty (museums, roleplay), structured questioning protocols (no-Why Wh-chains), and deliberate practice of narrative conversion to preserve bandwidth for exception noticing.
Practical applications — sales, science, design, leadership:
— Sales: use the paired Wh exercise to reveal latent customer needs.
— Science: treat data outliers as potential new mechanisms rather than measurement noise.
— Design: seek perceptual exceptions that suggest new affordances (van Gogh → RGB).
— Leadership: spot the Wozes—people whose outlier behaviors imply asymmetrical future value.
Simple training routine to reactivate intuition:
1. Weekly museum/walk of anomalies (or curated media binge).
2. Daily 5-minute paired Wh exercise with a colleague or friend.
3. Habit: when a label forms, write the event that generated it and imagine what happens next.
4. Periodic immersion: roleplay, unfamiliar communities, or structured simulations.
Limits and cautions — not all anomalies are signal:
Not every exception is a new rule; many are noise. The method requires disciplined triage: surface exceptions, probe with specific Wh questions, hypothesize a coherent Why, then test consequences rather than leaping to wholesale revision. Maintain measurement and iterative validation to separate signal from ephemera.
Transition to imagination (next chapter):
Once exceptions are surfaced, imagination turns them into concrete futures. Chapter 2 will treat imagination as the engine that converts exceptional cues into actionable visions—how to rehearse, detail, and scale insights that began as surprising Wh-facts.

Chapter 2 • Imagination
CHAPTER 2: IMAGINATION
Opening vignette — the bomb in the fog:
Special Operators are briefed for a complex human-intelligence mission; a simulated bomb explodes in misty pines, a child screams, and the unit has almost no reliable data. Despite the shock and uncertainty, trained Operators respond rapidly and effectively — each in a different but successful way: one leads, one follows, one delegates. The common factor is not superior sensors or faster computation but trained imagination: the ability to plan and improvise from very little information.
What imagination is not — and what it is:
Two simple hypotheses fail to explain the Operators’ performance. It is neither mere hallucination (false perception) nor brute-force, faster data processing. The Operators succeed with very low data; therefore imagination must be a different cognitive faculty. Historically the word pointed to mental imagery, but imagery alone is downstream of a deeper faculty: story. Story — an ordered sequence of actions and causes — is the script the brain runs to generate imagined possibilities. Story is therefore the biological engine of imagination.
Story as planning, not just communication:
Modern education treats story primarily as a communicative device (Cicero’s narratio and rhetorical tradition). But evolutionary and developmental evidence suggests story preceded language and served cognition: children naturally think in narrative, and animals (crows, chimps, mice) exhibit storylike planning. Story creates possible futures rather than computing probabilities; it invents sequences that conform to environmental rules and so enables action under uncertainty. In short: story → imagination → plan.
Two modes of storythinking — past (causal) and future (counterfactual):
Operators use two complementary narrative modes. Past storythinking asks why (causal analysis of what happened); future storythinking asks what if (branching counterfactuals). These modes form a feedback loop: richer causal hypotheses generate more inventive counterfactuals; more creative counterfactuals allow stronger causal inferences when outcomes resolve. The trained imagination integrates a coherent past (a single, clarifying why) with many branched futures (a forest of what-ifs).
Mental shape of effective imagination — one why, many what-ifs:
The Operators’ internal model looks like a long spine (an integrated, prioritized objective) with branching options radiating forward. This shape supplies two complementary capacities simultaneously: directional persistence (momentum from an integrated past/clear strategic why) and nimble adaptability (many tactical branches to respond instantly to new information). The pattern explains how improvisers — athletes, surgeons, ad-lib comedians — stay on purpose while adapting spontaneously.
Training imagination — “planning, planning, planning”:
Instructors who were Operators insist the single most practical method for training imagination is disciplined planning: repeatedly generate a single long-term objective and then build many plausible, concrete tactical routes to that objective. The paradox: imagination is exercised by hard, systematic planning. Planning broadens the set of what-ifs and teaches spotting exceptional signals that seed alternate paths.
Defined strategy, unlimited tactics — operational principle:
Two core rules emerge: (1) Define a single, prioritized strategic objective (one mountaintop). Avoid multiple primary goals that conflict under pressure. (2) Multiply tactics: cultivate many concrete, distinct routes to the objective rather than a single Plan A and a vague fallback. Together these produce “defined strategy, unlimited tactics.” When executed, the operator can commit to a direction yet flex tactics instantly as the environment changes.
How the Operators apply this in the field — a worked example:
At Cape Fear: the shared objective was “build rapport with the guerrilla chieftain.” When the bomb detonated, Operators generated different tactics tied to that one strategy: personally rescue the child (demonstrate altruism), follow the chieftain’s lead (signal trust), or prompt the chieftain to rescue solo (demonstrate confidence in his agency). Each divergent tactic preserved the strategic why while exploiting an exceptional datum (the wounded child).
Historical and cultural models of the method:
Operators studied planners across domains. Admiral Horatio Nelson exemplified a unified strategic aim coupled with decentralized tactical autonomy (“the Nelson touch”). Ludwig van Beethoven exemplified strict structural unity combined with bold local innovations (motif + improvisatory key shifts). Both models show the same cognitive architecture: tight strategic intent plus flexible local invention.
Why story outperforms pure logic under low data:
Logic computes probabilities from sufficient historical data; story constructs plausible sequences that respect environmental constraints even when data are sparse. In volatile, low-data situations — battlefield, early-stage innovation, crisis management — the possible (generated by story) matters more than the merely probable. Story enables initiative, fast adaptation, and creative recombination where statistical methods stall.
Biological and developmental grounding — Anna Craft and beyond:
Research and observation (e.g., Anna Craft’s preschool studies) show children naturally engage in storythinking; other species show similar capacities. Storythinking is ancient and conserved because it conferred survival advantages: the ability to imagine novel, permissible actions accelerated adaptive behavior and evolutionary opportunity.
Training recipe — practical steps to develop Operator-grade imagination:
1. Pick one primary objective. State it in a single sentence.
2. Generate many concrete tactics. Write 8–12 distinct “what if” actions that reach the same objective. Make each tactic operational, not abstract.
3. Run rapid simulations. Test tactics against possible obstacles; update tactics from outcomes.
4. Hone signal-spotting. Practice identifying exceptional information that changes available tactics.
5. Tighten your life story. Integrate recent events into a coherent why so choices align quickly.
6. Practice in low-data drills. Use timed exercises that force action with sparse inputs (role plays, tabletop drills).
7. Debrief to enrich causal inference. After each exercise, elicit diverse why hypotheses to expand future what-ifs.
Common cognitive errors and mitigations:
- Diffused objectives: fix with strict prioritization exercises (rank and eliminate).
- Plan-A bias: force at least five radically different tactics before committing.
- Overreliance on probability: rehearse low-data, high-uncertainty scenarios where story solutions succeed.
- Failure to learn from outcomes: adopt short, disciplined after-action reviews that map causes to branching tactics.
Exercises to train storythinking (practical examples):
- One-mountain exercise: choose a weekly priority; each morning invent five distinct routes to advance it; act on one daily.
- Bomb-in-the-fog drill: with a partner, simulate an unexpected disruptive event and require each participant to produce three viable tactics in 90 seconds tied to the same strategic aim.
- Causal enrichment: after a failure, list ten possible causes (no fewer), then extract three counterfactual tactics you would have tried had you seen each cause earlier.
Integration with emotion and identity — why life story matters:
Your personal narrative (life story) functions as your planning scaffold. An integrated past supplies clarity of why; emotional valence anchors choices and accelerates commitment. When your past is coherent and your future richly branched, decision speed and creative reach increase. Emotion is therefore not noise but a primal tool for integrating memory and motivating adaptive exploration.
Synthesis — imagination as engineered practice:
Imagination is not a mysterious gift reserved for a few geniuses. It is a practiced cognitive architecture rooted in story: define a single, prioritized purpose; systematically generate and rehearse many concrete tactics; train the perceptual habit of spotting exceptional signals; and institutionalize rapid debriefs that convert outcomes into richer causal models. Apply this regimen across domains — leadership, medicine, teaching, parenting — and you convert low data into decisive action.
Operational checklist (quick reference):
1. State one primary objective.
2. Produce ≥8 concrete, distinct tactics.
3. Simulate under time pressure.
4. Execute one tactic.
5. Debrief: capture causes and update tactic set.
6. Repeat and archive playbooks.
Forward note — emotion as the third primal power:
Story and planning make imagination usable; emotion binds the plan to identity and priority. Emotion supplies the motivational torque that sustains commitment to the chosen why while enabling rapid selection among branched what-ifs. (The role of emotion is introduced here and developed further in the subsequent chapter.)

Chapter 3 • Emotion
CHAPTER 3: EMOTION
Opening vignette — the CEO, the singleton, and the failure of EQ-as-logic:
The CEO who rose by trusting his feelings faces a skeptical executive team that prefers metrics. A weeklong EQ course turns emotion into identifications and labels (EQ-as-logic) but fails to change behavior. The author has an alternative, field-tested in Special Operations: emotion is a biological signal tightly coupled with story — an internal narrative that organizes past and future. The key contrast: EQ-as-identification treats emotion as interpersonal data; primal emotion treats it as an inward monitor of your life story.
Singletons and self-assessment — Lucy Gray's mechanism:
Singletons (operators who deploy alone) cannot rely on teammates; they must self-assess with unerring clarity. Lucy Gray reports she “does it through feel.” This is not mysticism: emotion is tracking the shape of the operator’s mental narrative. When the narrative stays integrated (one coherent past, branching future), action proceeds; when it fractures or narrows, emotion flashes warnings that prompt corrective moves.
Emotion as narrative monitor — how feelings map to story-shape:
Emotions are not random biases. They signal specific structural failures in your story: fear indicates a missing or broken plan (future collapsed); anger indicates an overconfident single-plan tunnel (future narrowed to one branch); grief and shame signal splintered pasts (past split into contradictory stories). Reading these signals as story-feedback turns emotion into usable intelligence.
Fear — the brain’s warning that you have no plan:
Fear evolved to flag the absence of an actionable plan: your future has ceased to branch. Fear’s bodily features (weak knees, mind blank) push you toward receptivity — a biological default to accept others’ plans as an emergency backup. Rather than dismissing fear as irrational, treat it as evidence: your narrative has lost its directional spine. The right response is to recover a first-step plan.
First-step plan — push your gaze to the horizon:
Operators manage acute fear by moving their gaze outward toward a strategic horizon, which restores narrative breadth and enables a hasty initial move. That first step feeds back to produce further steps and clearer planning. Practical preparation improves speed: priming a single specific strategic objective in calm moments makes first-step plans immediate under stress.
Practical priming guidance:
Do not prime abstract goals ("happiness"). Pick a concrete, tangible objective (e.g., "finish the manuscript," "close the acquisition"). Concreteness produces concrete first steps that your brain can invent even when panic narrows perception. Priming reduces paralysis and lets competence snowball from the first imperfect step.
Anger — the brain’s signal that you have one plan:
Anger signifies that your future has narrowed to a single path. It energizes commitment and force, which can be adaptive (rapid decisive action) but comes at steep social and physiological costs. The smarter use of anger is to pair its assertive energy with operational flexibility: search for exceptional information and generate alternative plans so you gain anger’s intensity without its rigid downside.
Emotion Reset — recalling past improvisations to defuse anger:
When anger locks you into a single plan, trigger an Emotion Reset: vividly recall a past moment when you improvised successfully under pressure. Make the memory specific and sensory. The memory tells your brain: you can form new workable plans, which calms aggression and reopens exploratory intuition. Prepare a few such resets ahead of time so they can be accessed instantly.
Why fear and anger are complementary adaptive signals:
Fear recruits external plans when you have none; anger forces decisive execution when you have one. Both are intelligent but dangerous if uncorrected: fear can make you submissive; anger can make you catastrophically rigid. The corrective moves are symmetric — recover a horizon + first step for fear; create exception-driven second plans for anger.
Grief and shame — signals of a fractured past:
Grief tells you the world story has split (the world is not reliably good); shame tells you the self-story has split (you are not reliably good). Both fracture the past that supplies your strategic why. They shrink motivation and scatter attention. Rather than suppressing these feelings, use them diagnostically: identify the specific event or action that produced the fracture and then apply your overarching purpose to integrate or correct the memory.
From growth-orientation to specific purpose:
A biological default — growth — is a helpful but abstract orienting purpose. For grief and shame remediation you need a specific personal purpose. Singletons cultivate such purposes via two positive diagnostic emotions: dumb pride and maverick gratitude. These positive signals reveal which past acts and external validations actually anchor your real why.
Dumb pride — the rebel marker of true commitment:
Dumb pride is a steady, nonconformist pride in acts others label foolish. It signals a past action you would repeat even in the worst outcome. Those acts pass the “would I do it again if I lost everything?” stress test and thus reveal primal commitment. Collect these acts as clues to your core mission: when a remembered action survives that counterfactual, it points to the fight worth sustaining.
Maverick gratitude — noticing thanks that confirm your mission:
Maverick gratitude arises when a thank-you slides past expectation and lands deep because someone recognized the unconventional value of what you did. Small-but-meaningful thanks are diagnostic: if a tiny recognition lights you up, you were advancing your own purpose, not someone else’s. Use those moments to confirm and refine your private why.
Training emotional self-assessment — practices singletons use:
- Review your past in detail, from childhood to present, to surface shame, grief, dumb pride, and the moments that produced surprising gratitude.
- Prime a single specific strategic objective in calm moments and rehearse first-step plans for it.
- Prepare vivid Emotion Resets (memories of prior hasty-plan success) for anger moments.
- Practice horizon-shifting drills: when stress narrows your attention, deliberately identify a long-term objective and name a single next step.
Behavioral rules for emotion-informed decisions:
1. When you feel fear → ask: "Do I have a plan?" If no, recall your strategic objective and force a first-step plan.
2. When you feel anger → ask: "Do I have only one plausible path?" If yes, look for exceptional data and invent at least one alternative plan; trigger an Emotion Reset if needed.
3. When you feel grief or shame → identify the concrete past event, then use your purpose (or, if unclear, the growth principle) to reframe, integrate, or correct.
Operational checklist (quick reference, preserve line breaks):
1. State one concrete strategic objective.
2. Prime and visualize it in a calm moment.
3. Prepare 2–3 Emotion Resets (specific past improvisations).
4. Practice first-step plans under time pressure.
5. Catalog dumb-pride moments and maverick gratitudes.
6. When fear hits: recall the objective — make a first step.
7. When anger hits: trigger an Emotion Reset — look for exceptions — craft a second plan.
8. When grief/shame appears: name the event — apply purpose to integrate or repair.
Exercises to build emotion-as-data skills:
- Horizon drill: In pairs or alone, simulate a sudden upsetting constraint and force a one-sentence strategic objective plus a 60-second first-step plan. Debrief on where fear appeared and how the horizon technique shifted it.
- Emotion Reset practice: Write three vivid memories of times you improvised under pressure; rehearse sensory detail until recalling them rapidly reduces aggression.
- Dumb-pride inventory: List five acts you would repeat even if punished; test each with the "would I do it again if I lost everything?" stress probe. Use the survivors to refine your personal purpose.
- Maverick-gratitude spotting: For one week, note every thank-you that moves you unusually; record context and what it validated. Use it to triangulate your mission.
Integration with intuition and imagination:
Emotion supplies a verdict on whether your story is intact; intuition proposes rapid candidate moves; imagination generates many what-ifs. Together they form the practical engine of Primal Intelligence: emotion diagnoses (why is the story failing?), imagination invents candidate routes (what ifs), and intuition selects plausible next moves rapidly. Emotion therefore seeds both corrective action and creative search.
Why this approach outperforms EQ-as-labeling:
Label-based EQ trains people to identify others’ feelings, useful for social smoothing. Primal-emotion training makes emotion an internal compass for narrative integrity that directly adjusts behavior under pressure. It produces faster, more resilient decision-making because it ties feelings to concrete corrective operations (first-step plans, resets, second plans) rather than to abstract empathy categories.
Limits, cautions, and social costs:
Emotion-driven operations can be misread socially (anger’s force harms teams; dumb pride can look antisocial). Use the techniques publicly with humility: explain your purpose, invite exception-finding from trusted colleagues, and institutionalize post-action reviews so emotion-driven risks are corrected rapidly. Never weaponize fear or anger to manipulate others’ plans.
Synthesis — emotion as operational instrument:
Emotion is not a flaw to be engineered away; it is a real-time diagnostic of the shape of your life story. Read it precisely: fear means no plan; anger means one plan; grief and shame mean a fractured past. Treat these signals as prompts for narrow, practical interventions: recover the horizon and a first step, invent exceptions and alternate plans, integrate past fractures through purpose and corrective acts. Cultivate dumb pride and maverick gratitude as internal beacons of your true why. Systematize these practices, and emotion becomes a disciplined tool for resilient, purpose-driven action.
Forward note — prepare before you launch:
Singletons do heavy pre-mission work: they prime objectives, catalogue resets, rehearse first steps, and clear out past demons. Do the same in your domain. Slay your demons on your terms so they don't ambush you in crisis. With narrative clarity, emotion will not be a liability but the most reliable internal signal you possess.

Chapter 4 • Commonsense
CHAPTER 4: COMMONSENSE
Opening vignette — the stock picker and the Franklin test:
The chapter opens with a successful stock picker who made his fortune by sensing when to trust durable patterns and when to leap on novel opportunity. He could beat algorithmic traders not by superior math but by a mental habit Benjamin Franklin exemplified: knowing when you don’t know. Commonsense, as the chapter frames it, is the practical faculty that tells you whether the present rules still apply or whether the world has turned.
What commonsense is — knowing when you don't know:
Commonsense is not a collection of facts; it is the meta-skill of detecting environmental novelty and volatility. A child’s hesitation in an unfamiliar house reveals the core operation: the mind recognizes its own ignorance. Logic—even sophisticated machine logic—cannot do this intrinsically. Story-based cognition (why / what-if) detects when current causal narratives fail; that detection is commonsense.
Why commonsense matters more than raw calculation:
Computers extrapolate from historical data and, when confronted with novelty, will bluff (confidently). Humans succeed in messy environments because commonsense prompts doubt when the past no longer guarantees the future. The brain’s job is to falsify current rules, not verify them; commonsense is the instrument that signals: [There is relevant information you don’t know]. In stable domains this yields fast, confident action; in volatile domains it triggers rapid adaptation.
Two functional faces of commonsense — negative alarm and positive selection:
Primarily commonsense plays a negative role: it makes you doubt a plan when conditions change. Positively, it can help choose the new plan that best fits the emergent situation. The chapter emphasizes training both halves: (1) sharpening the alarm for true novelty, and (2) cultivating the judgment to pick useful alternative branches.
Benjamin Franklin and editorial commonsense:
Franklin learned to swivel between conventional comfort and surprising news as a newspaper writer—an editorial commonsense that approved steady prose when fundamentals held and provoked novelty when warranted. His aphorisms (e.g., "Be slow in choosing a friend," vs. "You may delay, but time will not") encode the same paradox commonsense resolves: when to be cautious and when to be fast.
How Operators teach commonsense — the recycle course:
U.S. Special Operations teach commonsense by alternating routine and novelty in training, forcing recruits to detect when standard rules fail. The training deliberately exposes students who live in classroom certainty to environments that generate proper anxiety—enough to notice unknowns but not enough to freeze. The foundational lesson: anxiety is a diagnostic instrument, not a defect.
Tuning anxiety — three-step method (Operators' card):
1) Get appropriately anxious (Past: NONE, Future: NEAR): anxiety signals volatility; its presence is essential for vigilance.
2) Rid the future of the past: ask when worry is a replay of old failures; if so, process it into an SOP rather than letting it hijack the present.
3) Focus on Now + 1: concentrate anxiety on the immediate next action, not on distant hypotheticals. This narrows productive 'what-if' generation to the piece of future you can most influence.
Why anxiety helps when tuned correctly:
Anxiety indexes the density of branching futures your imagination produces. Light, well-directed anxiety keeps you alert to exceptions without scattering attention. Excessive anxiety either (a) collapses you into avoidance by weaponizing past traumas or (b) floods you with far-future fantasies that paralyze action. Tuning removes past ghosts and concentrates worry on the near future where corrective moves are tractable.
How to rid the future of the past — convert worry into SOPs:
Most future-oriented fears are actually unresolved past events. The practical remedy is to convert those past anxieties into updated Standard Operating Procedures. If you can design and test a new SOP that demonstrably improves outcomes, adopt it; if not, preserve the durable SOP and chalk the fear up to luck. This preserves the advantage of long-tested rules while allowing measured, evidence-based improvement.
Now + 1 — the operational horizon for commonsense:
Look one step ahead. Scan for plausible near-term exceptions, imagine immediate responses, but wait to execute until the exception appears. This practice keeps you prepared without preemptively abandoning fundamentals. Now + 1 is the cognitive locus where imagination, intuition, and anxiety most effectively produce actionable plans.
Case study — Warren Buffett (Past Anxiety: NONE):
Buffett’s edge came from converting past anxieties into SOPs (Dale Carnegie routines, investment rules from Graham) so that he could exploit market fear rather than be governed by it. Processing past anxiety let him stick to durable fundamentals (“be greedy when others are fearful”) and capture long-term growth with minimal emotional noise.
Case study — James Simons (Future Anxiety: NEAR):
Simons built a different edge by obsessing over Now + 1. His Renaissance team tuned models and human attention to detect immediate market anomalies and react fast. When algorithmic models went wrong, Simons heeded his own anxiety and changed course—unplugging a failing model, recruiting fresh intuitions, and adopting a near-term, volatility-aware approach that restored and amplified returns.
Where logic and commonsense diverge in practice:
Logic seeks confirmation; commonsense seeks falsification. Logic asks "Does the data support?" Commonsense asks "What could falsify this?" The latter is superior in contested, adaptive environments where historical trends break. This is why Franklin-style aphorisms and Operator SOPs beat algorithmic complacency in many real-world settings.
Training recipes — exercises to develop commonsense:
- Recycle course simulation: alternate high-certainty routines and surprise scenarios; after each surprise, debrief: what rules failed, which signals were missed?
- Now + 1 drills: take a routine task and list three plausible near-term exceptions plus immediate contingencies; rehearse these contingencies under time pressure.
- SOP retrofit: pick a recurring failure in your life/work; design a specific SOP that would have changed the outcome; test it in low-stakes settings.
- Anxiety tuning logs: for one week, record anxieties, mark whether each stems from past episodes or future uncertainty, and apply either SOP design or Now + 1 focus accordingly.
Behavioral rules for commonsense-informed action:
1. When you feel a spike of anxiety → ask: "Is this a past ghost or a near-future unknown?"
2. If it's past → convert into an SOP or accept durable plan if you cannot improve it cost-effectively.
3. If it's near-future → limit scope to Now + 1, imagine one strong contingency, prepare, and wait.
4. When calm prevails → trust tested rules; when sharp novelty arrives → escalate Now + 1 vigilance.
Operational checklist (quick reference):
1. Note the emotion: is your worry past or future?
2. If past → design or update an SOP; test it.
3. If future → narrow attention to Now + 1.
4. Imagine one immediate contingency and rehearse it.
5. Do not over-correct long-term fundamentals for single anomalies.
6. Archive effective playbooks; discard reactive fads.
Common errors and mitigations:
- Mistaking flashbacks for foresight: treat vivid past replay as an SOP-design opportunity, not a reason to avoid the task.
- Paralysis by far-future imagination: resist solving hypothetical problems beyond Now + 1.
- Overfitting to anomalies: test whether exceptions are durable before discarding fundamentals.
- Anxiety starvation (no worry at all): cultivate light vigilance; lack of concern is often the true risk signal.
Exercises to practice with a team:
- Editorial pivot: simulate running a news desk; assign half the team to produce "conventional" coverage and half to invent plausible disruptive stories. Rotate roles and debrief on when to pivot editors’ policies.
- Market Now + 1 table-top: present a sudden market shock; force each trader to produce a one-step contingency and a trigger for execution.
- SOP garage: bring three failed projects and draft specific SOP changes that would have materially reduced the risk next time.
Integration with the other Primal powers:

Technique 1 — start in the middle (middle → beginning → end):
Story-primed brains treat surprising middles as triggers. An unexpected event compels an audience to rewind (why did this happen?) and then fast-forward (what if this leads to …?). Technique 3 — write in riddle (contradiction → suspense → completion):
Riddles create cognitive tension by juxtaposing contradictions. That tension accelerates scanning for resolution and invests the audience emotionally in the answer. Shakespeare’s witches and paradoxes, and Lincoln’s Gettysburg rhetoric, are examples of riddle-as-engagement. Opening vignette — the rookie, the coaches, and the paradox of control:
World-class football coaches fret over the rookie quarterback: enormous upside, enormous risk. The rookie can invent game-changing plays or crumble under pressure. Core rule — unleash the rookie:
Special Operations (and other high-performing domains) intentionally place novices in consequential roles and then let them proceed, permitting chains of mistakes to form. Experts resist early intervention; instead they let the rookie's errors unfold and then run forward from those errors rather than trying to rewind. The result is a forced creation of novel solutions and an expansion of the expert’s capability.
Why this works — constructive disruption of expertise:
Experts tend to plateau because knowledge breeds habit: once you know, you stop learning. Handing control to a rookie breaks the loop. When the rookie drives the system into unanticipated failure modes, the expert is forced to improvise new routes forward, thereby inventing fresh techniques and pushing personal limits. Over time repeated exposure to these disruptions increases the expert’s range and resilience.
Field evidence — pilots, showrunners, sales, code, medicine:
SOAR helicopter pilots give rookies real controls on difficult missions (often in simulators or supervised real sorties). Showrunners let junior writers and actors take creative control of episodes; sales leaders let inexperienced sellers attempt unconventional deals; tech leaders staff teams with junior coders. In each case, the rookie’s liberties produce novel approaches and force seniors to solve unexpected problems, elevating the whole team.
Mechanics — run with the mistake, don't rewind it:
The teaching invariant is forward ownership. Experts do not rewind and correct earlier rookie choices; they accept the trajectory and make minimal changes necessary to land. This forward-only posture prevents the expert from defaulting to their prior template and instead compels real-time invention that becomes codified as new expertise.
Neural and storythinking basis — branching imagination and reverse-branching:
The neuron’s tree-like anatomy mirrors imagination’s branching. Experts anticipate many rookie branches while simultaneously reverse-branching from a desired endpoint to find intersections with the rookie’s forward branches. Letting the rookie go creates novel intersections that the expert then inspects and adopts—this is how new plans are discovered in flight.

A strong improv troupe pairs creative, risky openings (from rookies) with experienced actors who steer to satisfying endings by plotting backwards from an imagined finish. The rookie supplies dramatic possibility; the expert supplies directional closure. That pairing produces shows that are both exciting and coherent.
Diagnostic for expertise — how to test if you’re an expert:
Hand control to a rookie. If your apprehension lessens the more the rookie flies into the unknown, you are likely an expert: you can rapidly imagine solutions and relax into adaptive improvisation. If your anxiety rises or never abates, you are not yet expert at imagining fluid future intersections and should practice with lower-risk simulations until you can anticipate and invent fast.
What’s best for the rookie — paradox resolved:
The method serves both purposes: it accelerates rookie growth and preserves long-term team performance. Rookies learn faster by doing consequential work; seniors learn by being forced into novelty. The coaches’ worry about handing over control masks the deeper requirement: cultivate the coach’s trust in their own ability to rescue and learn from what the rookie creates.
Historical and institutional exemplars — Noyce, Apollo, Osler:
Robert Noyce entrusted million-dollar projects to recent graduates; Apollo staffed mission control with very young engineers, producing rapid innovation; Dr. William Osler transformed medical education by releasing students into wards to treat real patients and by urging them to listen. Each example shows institutional acceleration when novices are empowered with responsibility.
Constraints and safety — where to unleash, how much risk to accept:
You should not hand novices full control in unmediated, unconstrained environments. Use graded exposure: simulators, staged missions, supervised real tasks, or well-scoped projects. Preserve safety nets that allow learning without catastrophic public harm. The principle is controlled risk—grant latitude while ensuring recoverable boundaries.
Coaching posture — trust, forward ownership, minimal reversal:
Coaches must cultivate trust in their own capacity to salvage outcomes and to learn from them. The operative behaviors:
(1) resist early correction,
(2) avoid retroactive rewrites,
(3) take ownership of forward fixes, and
(4) deliberately look for what the rookie invented that you can adopt. This posture models humility and invites psychological safety.

1. Start in simulation or constrained environments.
2. Give the rookie a real stake and let them choose approaches that excite them.
3. Refrain from immediate correction; observe the error chain.
4. When necessary, assume control and move forward from the rookie's trajectory rather than reversing it.
5. Debrief with the rookie, extracting lessons for both parties.

Provide psychological safety—clearly communicate the learning purpose to the rookie and the team—so risk-taking is honored, not punished.
Cultural enablers — systems that support unleashing:
Organizational norms that accelerate this method include: simulation infrastructure, explicit "safe-to-fail" missions, post-action reviews that focus on learning, reward systems for creative risk (not just flawless execution), and leadership exemplars who publicly model forward ownership.

- Too-early intervention: resist the urge to correct minute errors—set explicit thresholds for intervention.
- Rewind temptation: avoid undoing rookie choices; instead take forward ownership.
- Punitive culture: don't punish failure; treat recoverable failures as experiments.

Control-based onboarding protects short-term metrics but flattens long-term capability. Unleashing accelerates learning, generates novel tactics, and scales institutional adaptability. The short-term cost—messy experiments—yields durable benefits: a deeper bench, fresher strategies, and continuous innovation.

Coaching’s primary goal should be expanding collective capability, not minimizing immediate errors. Hand novices real responsibility, accept recoverable messes, run forward from rookie-created problems, and harvest the inventions that result. This is how teams remain adaptive, experts keep improving, and institutions renew themselves.

Leadership vs. management — the root distinction:

CHAPTER 11: MOTO
Opening vignette — Fort Bragg, the crude site, and Special Operators:
The author visits an unexpectedly primitive covert training camp at Fort Bragg and meets Special Operators from elite units. The site is low-tech yet rigorous — driftwood pits, temples made of wrecked helicopters, sweat, and grubby practical training. The author promises a new explanatory science for action under uncertainty: Primal Intelligence. This chapter presents the core idea: a motor-based, narrative-thinking faculty—moto—that evolved in the Cambrian and still powers our creative action.
Two ancient problems that shaped two minds — Eat vs. Don't-get-eaten:
In the Cambrian explosion life faced two survival pressures: find food (Eat) and avoid predators (Don't-get-eaten). These pressures produced two complementary neural solutions: (1) sensory-symbolic, pattern-based computation (the origin of logic and the visual cortex), and (2) motor-based, exception-driven action (the origin of story and motor intelligence). The combined architecture is why humans can both compute regularities and invent novel actions.
Logic (A = B) vs. Action (A → B) — the fundamental distinction:
Logic reduces phenomena to interchangeable symbols (A = B), enabling powerful pattern computation but limited causal imagination. Action-thinking treats events as directional causes (A → B), chaining moves into narratives that anticipate outcomes. Computers excel at A = B; the brain evolved machinery for A → B. Conflating the two erases what makes action creative and adaptive when data are scarce.
Why "galloping horses on a screen" aren't acting for the machine:
A computer animation looks like motion to human viewers, but the machine processes discrete frames (A = B snapshots). Humans convert the frames into A → B narratives (movement, intent) via motor imagination. The screen's illusion is visible to us; the machine only handles symbols. This asymmetry explains why present-day AI can mimic surface action without truly inventing it.
The synapse and motor intelligence — moto as biological mechanism:
Neurons connect through biochemical synapses, not electrical MOS transistors. Whereas a transistor implements binary logic (off/on) suitable for A = B computation, synapses instantiate contingent, causal linkages that function like A → B. That physical difference is not cosmetic: it underpins motor intelligence's capacity to instantiate actions that anticipate unknown causes.
Why software simulations can't fully replicate moto:
You can simulate the neuron's behavior in software, but the simulation remains an A = B artifact unless a human encodes the exceptional causal rules. To simulate A → B in practice requires boiling unknown causes into explicit algorithms — a process that already transfers the creative work back to human neurons. A simulation therefore reproduces appearance but not the embodied capacity to invent actions from sparse, exceptional information.
Moto's advantages — action under low data and a bias to experiment:
Moto runs on feedback and a bias to act. It functions effectively with very little input, inventing initial steps and refining them by testing in the world. This property explains adaptive ingenuity in novel situations: surgeons improvising in an operating theater, athletes inventing a new move midplay, or inventors prototyping radical devices without large datasets. Moto trades perfect certainty for actionable initiation.
Moto is mostly nonconscious — learn by imitation and story, not logic:
Motor skills and many improvisational patterns remain below conscious articulation (watch-and-imitate learning). Still, moto can be sharpened consciously through narrative practice: imagining sequences, rehearsing first steps, and training contexts that encourage exception-spotting. Story is the high-level interface for shaping moto: narrative constrains which actions motor systems generate and which habits they consolidate.
Story as the operating system of moto:
Story is a chain of causes and effects — a sequence of actions that moto can run. When you imagine a story, you engage motor-pattern generation: possible steps are composed into trajectories the motor system can test. Storythinking thus converts imagined futures into motor-ready plans (what-ifs that become hasty plans and then iterative refinements).
Narrative cognition vs. symbolic cognition — two conscious kinds:
Simple consciousness aligns with vision and symbolic processing (spatial, present-oriented). Self-consciousness is temporal and narrative-driven (you as continuous across time). This distinction illuminates why humans can introspectively rehearse life-stories, integrate past mistakes, and commit to future strategies in ways AI cannot. Narrative cognition stitches identity to action.
Shakespeare as a cultural pedagogy for storythinking:
Shakespeare exemplifies storythinking: characters break patterns, create exceptions, and act with original causal sequences. Great innovators, artists, and leaders (Einstein, Tesla, Beethoven, Lincoln, Keynes, etc.) drew on Shakespeare because his plays train the mind to handle exception-laden causality. Reading such narratives accelerates the brain’s capacity to imagine novel action chains.
Historical arc — story's exile and return:
Renaissance experimenters privileged intuition and narrative over scholastic logic. The Enlightenment revived logic and law, suppressing narrative exception. Romanticism and thinkers like Blake restored story and imagination. Keynes later wove narrative insights into economics, recognizing imagination's role in markets. The twentieth century's programmers and ENIAC re-centered optimization and statistics, delegitimizing narrative as primary for invention.
Guilford, ideation, and generative AI — limits of computational creativity:
Mid-20th-century psychology reframed creativity as divergent (random association) plus convergent (logical selection). This idea fed design thinking and, later, generative AI. But generative systems are simultaneously more random and more correlational than humans — producing many recombinations but lacking causal motor invention. AI can ideate, but lacks the synaptic, motor-driven capacity to instantiate genuinely novel A → B action in the world.
Why AI can't replace moto-driven innovation:
AI optimizes patterns from data and recombines symbols. It cannot instantiate genuinely new action chains that anticipate unknown, contingent causalities with the embodied-bias-to-act that moto supplies. Practical leadership, field improvisation, engineering leaps, and artistic breakthroughs depend on the motor-narrative faculty that evolved to handle exceptions. AI augments but does not obviate human moto.
Practical consequences — what to train and why:
To amplify moto, cultivate:
(1) story practice (realistic scenario fiction, narrative rehearsal),
(2) motor rehearsal (small, fast experiments; improvised hasty plans),
(3) feedback loops (rapid real-world testing), and
(4) emotional scaffolding (use pride, gratitude, and focused purpose to fuel sustained risk-taking). These elements convert imagined sequences into motor patterns and vice versa.

- First-step drills: Under a time limit, pick a single strategic objective and produce three distinct first steps; execute the quickest.
- Motor mimicry labs: Practice watching an expert movement (video or in person) and immediately imitating it to capture embodied heuristics.
- Narrative debriefs: After each experiment, narrate what happened as a causal story; extract the exceptional cues that changed the outcome.

CHAPTER 12: STORYTHINKING — PIXAR, WILLIAM JAMES, AND THE SCIENCE OF SINGULAR MINDS
Opening vignette — from synapse rigs to a new science of story:
My unconventional research into human intelligence started in the late 1990s, two decades before I met U.S. Special Operations. In those days, I worked in a University of Michigan Medical School neurophysiology lab researching the synapse---the A → B machine that generates original action. On a handmade microscope rig held together by rolls of tape and mismatched screws, I plugged glass instruments into individual synapses to track what they were thinking. Then I engineered mutant synapses, changing their machinery to make them think differently.
Natural history and the little green pamphlet — a different method of knowing:
When I discovered, on a gently rusting shelf at the back of the lab, a little green pamphlet titled A Naturalist's Field Guide, I was enchanted. Handmade in the 1960s by a college instructor on an early photocopier, it employed a mix of splotchy typewriter and elegant sketches to explain the science of natural history. Natural history's method is deceptively simple: Sit in nature, waiting patiently for things that grab your curiosity. Curiosity is sparked in your brain by the unexpected---a new species, an original behavior, or some other exception to biology's known laws. Keep your eyes on that surprise, watching where it comes from, what it does, and how it interacts, learning the bigger story of the exception and its role in the environment.
Natural history vs. statistics — a clash of scientific cultures:
The lab director plucked the pamphlet from my hands and tossed it in a trash chute. Natural history, he told me, was dead. The dying had begun in the wake of Einstein. His enormous fame had infected biologists with "physics envy," the desire to do research with the infallible rigor that the general public associated with mathematics. The result was the rise of big-data ecology and of cognitive science (which treated neurons as transistors performing Bayesian computation). This statistical approach made natural history seem outdated, and it had another more directly destructive consequence: It mass-produced itself. Where decades of painstaking fieldwork were required to train a natural historian, mathematical biologists could be manufactured via a single statistics course. Equipped with a hundred-dollar calculator and a knowledge of standard deviations, they could study butterflies, oceans, emotions, or anything: it was all the same because it was all numbers.
Naturalism’s complement to math — Faraday, James, and the lab director:
I read about natural history's rise and fall and digested it into three short narratives:
(1) Michael Faraday discovered electromagnetic laws without math, cultivating a "feel" for nature that later found expression in Maxwell's equations;
(2) William James inaugurated modern psychology by closely studying individual cases and developing a feel for the human brain;
(3) my lab director taught patient, hands-on mentorship in synapse experiments.
These narratives reveal natural history's deeper scientific root: naturalism. Naturalism is the careful observation of natural rarities. It's driven by curiosity and also by imagination, because the naturalist must speculate creatively on the unseen physical mechanisms that cause the happening. That creative speculation produced the nineteenth-century revolutions that yielded modern biology, modern physics, and modern psychology. Although it rejects the brute generalizations of statistics, it is not contrary to math; it is complementary, generating hypotheses that can be tested by later calculations.
Core theoretical move — the brain thinks in narrative:
Moved by the stories of Faraday and James, I decided that I would become a naturalist. I would probe human intelligence by studying brains that excelled at low-information processes—imagination, emotion, commonsense, intuition—that couldn't be discovered by the statistical methods of modern psychology. I would study singular intelligence of individual brains. I had an epiphany: A brain was a group of synapses, which was a group of A → B, which was a group of actions. Training in literature — Yale, Shakespeare, and practical humility:
Although this was my theoretical breakthrough, it took many years to make practical gains because I knew lots about microscopes and neurons—and almost nothing about novels, myths, and plays. To take the next step I needed to learn how story worked. Rather than running populational studies, I wanted to observe an exceptional producer of narratives, immersing myself for years in his creations. I struck upon the notion of studying Shakespeare. I hadn't read Shakespeare since ninth grade. I was familiar with his legend: country boy with little education goes to the big city and transforms the world. I thought I could learn from Shakespeare what was special about human intelligence. So off I went to Yale to get a PhD in literature, bringing my lone copy of Shakespeare: a paperback Henry V from high school.
Shakespeare as method — invention of story that works:
I developed a simple but original theory about Shakespeare: He had devised a method for inventing stories that worked. He was more than a great wordsmith: he was a nimble innovator who changed the narrative—and kept on changing it. After Yale I lectured at Stanford, and a student pointed out that Pixar movies were doing what I said Shakespeare had done: telling new stories that succeeded. Fifteen minutes later I called Pixar. In the archives I learned the rich and wild natural history of movies like Toy Story, and I realized Pixar was using a version of Shakespeare's method. Then Disney purchased Pixar, threatening that experimental ecosystem; the life inside might be preserved, but not alive. Needing a new ecosystem I went south to Los Angeles, joining USC and working in film, TV, and theater. I wrote screenplays, sold some, failed at others, and kept returning to my theory.
Project Narrative and applied naturalism — broad industry work:
Project Narrative, headquartered at Ohio State University, funded me to start a lab. I reconnected with neuroscience and psychology, and I began exploring the singular intelligences of engineers, entrepreneurs, doctors, and leaders. Companies and institutions across tech, healthcare, fashion, insurance, fast food, defense, and startups invited me in. At first they wanted marketing help; I agreed only if I could enter their strategy and operations. I argued that strategy was the story of what the organization wanted to do, operations the story of what it actually did, and marketing the story of what it wanted people to think it was doing. In successful organizations the three stories aligned; in dysfunctional ones they diverged, creating inauthentic marketing.
Three executive tribes — rationalists, rational humanists, AI futurists:
As I poked through C-suites I noticed three tribes:
(1) classic rationalists who believed in rational-choice theory and quantitative economics;
(2) rational humanists who stressed empathy and design;
(3) AI futurists who foretell computers replacing human leaders.
All three rely on rational decision-making and on data. They believed in programming people toward correct behavior through nudges and choice architecture. This struck me as creepy: data is inherently backward-looking, and when the future differs from the past, imagination—not statistics—creates new futures.
Imagination vs. data — why new plans come from story:
Data optimizes within existing spaces; Shakespeare’s method invents new options. Imagination can generate novel strategies when historical data fail. New plans are new stories; if you can invent a story that works, you can change the future. Shakespeare’s plots taught audiences to think like plotters—learning to ask "What would an ambitiously inventive plotter do?"—and thereby trained real-world planning. This method powered the breakthroughs of artists and leaders across history.
From theory to practice — screenwriting, consulting, and sales:
I tried to teach executives to read Shakespeare; they instead paid me thousands of dollars an hour to consult. They said I was a natural at strategy. I knew the truth: I was not a natural. My instinct was atrocious. The only thing I was good at was multiple choice. I had hacked the literature GRE, analyzed old tests into diagrams and spreadsheets, and ticked the right boxes. I had been a fake savant. To convince executives that story method could be taught, I had to make myself expendable: codify the know-how so teams could carry it forward.
Personal backstory and impostor confession — family, tests, and arrival at Yale:
The secret starts with my dad, a brilliant mind who never learned to cope with reality. He lost jobs, drank, and my family lived with the consequences. I devoted myself to school, won a scholarship, and told extravagant lies to cover my difference. At college I met a Marine recruiter, took the ASVAB, and scored astronomically. I failed the physical initially but trained and passed. I used test-taking skill to enter Yale, not innate literary mastery. This confession illustrates why brilliance on standardized tests does not equal brilliance at life.
Boot camp vignette — smashed glasses and near-disaster:
On a flight to Quantico my recruiter's "indestructible" glasses were smashed by a falling suitcase. Ashamed, I tossed the pieces in a trash can and boarded the bus blind, pretending I did not need glasses. In boot camp I guessed at instructions, misloaded my rifle, mis-ironed my hat, and developed a blister that turned septic; I was evacuated to Walter Reed and pumped full of antibiotics. That was the end of my time at boot camp. I kept the story mostly to myself for years. Special Operations later uncovered the truth.
Special Operations as naturalists of action — shared method and validation:
When I first met U.S. Special Operations, they told me their mission was to solve the problems no one else could. To find fixes they embed for years in foreign environments, using patient immersion to spot opportunities. Special Operators are thus naturalists. They recognized that my training—if it worked on someone who wasn't a natural like me—would be legitimate and scalable. They asked me to hand over my methods so teams could carry on the mission if an operator was killed: make yourself expendable by teaching.
Teaching and scaling the method — externalizing story techniques:
I got the know-how out of my head and became a teacher. I learned to do for others what Shakespeare had done for me: pass on a method for activating the brain's primal power to create new plans that work. With that method you can adapt to change and win in chaos. It can be taught to thousands: third graders, college students, MFAs, engineers, salespeople, doctors, teachers, athletes, MBAs, social workers, CEOs, and Special Operators.
Organizational diagnosis — aligning three organizational stories:
Successful organizations align strategy (intended story), operations (actual story), and marketing (perceived story). When they misalign, the result is inauthenticity. Storywork aligns incentives, behavior, and public narrative. Leaders who want teams that improvise under pressure should supply realistic scenario practice, reward quick experimental moves, model private competence, and coach by framing narratives rather than prescribing micro-steps.
Practical promise and empirical testing — lab, consulting, appendix:
My lab at Project Narrative, my consulting across industries, and my work with Special Operations produced testable interventions. The methods include immersion in exemplary narratives, scenario practice, writing Shakespeare-style sequels for organizations, translating imagined plots into hasty first steps, and rehearsing motor-first plans.
