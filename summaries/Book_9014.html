Table 1 — Benchmarks
Step
Action (Best Practice)
Key Purpose / Benchmark Insight
1. Define Audit Objective Identify whether audit is statutory, donor-required, program-specific, or consolidated. Specify scope, timeline, and deliverables. Ensures alignment with funder expectations and national regulations. Prevents overlapping or missing audit obligations.
2. Form Audit Committee Create or assign a board-level committee to oversee auditor selection and performance. Guarantees governance independence and accountability. Avoids management dominance over auditor choice.
3. Prepare Audit Terms of Reference (ToR) Write a clear ToR describing objectives, scope, deliverables, timelines, and required standards (e.g., ISA, IFRS for NGOs). Provides all bidders identical information; ensures transparent and comparable proposals.
4. Map Donor Requirements Summarize each funder’s audit and reporting rules (periods, formats, disclosures). Multi-funder NGOs must align one audit to satisfy all donors’ conditions, avoiding duplicate audits and extra cost.
5. Establish Selection Criteria Set weighted criteria before receiving bids: competence (30%), NGO experience (25%), independence (15%), cost (15%), resources & tech capacity (10%), communication (5%). Objectifies evaluation; prevents bias. Supports justification to board and donors.
6. Issue Request for Proposal (RFP) Send RFP to at least 3–5 qualified firms. Include ToR, background, and donor complexity description. Creates competitive environment and documentation trail of fairness.
7. Check Eligibility and Credentials Confirm firm is legally registered, has CPA/chartered status, complies with professional oversight authority. Protects NGO from unqualified or blacklisted auditors. Builds donor confidence.
8. Verify NGO/Donor Audit Experience Ask for references of prior NGO or foundation clients with similar multi-donor setups. Prior exposure to donor rules (USAID, EU, DFAT, UN) reduces onboarding time and audit errors.
9. Assess Independence Confirm no financial, management, or advisory ties to your NGO. Disclose prior services in writing. Maintains objectivity. Many funders reject audits from firms providing paid consultancy to same client.
10. Evaluate Technical Approach Review proposed audit methodology, sampling, risk assessment, materiality, and reporting structure. Reveals whether firm truly understands grant-based accounting and restricted funds.
11. Assess Team Composition Require list of key personnel, their credentials, NGO experience, and continuity plan. Ensures stable team and prevents loss of institutional memory during multi-year engagements.
12. Examine Audit Tools and Technology Determine whether firm uses secure portals, data analytics, or remote audit software. Important for NGOs with dispersed field offices and multiple funder data systems.
13. Evaluate Cost Proposal Compare total cost and hourly rates versus scope. Identify extra costs (travel, translations, additional donor schedules). Focus on value, not lowest bid. Cheap audits may fail donor compliance checks.
14. Conduct Due Diligence Check references, quality control reviews, and peer review results. Search regulatory body for sanctions. Confirms reputation and quality assurance history.
15. Score and Rank Bidders Use predefined matrix. Document each evaluator’s score and comments. Ensures transparency and traceability for internal audit trail and donor review.
16. Interview Shortlisted Firms Hold structured interviews on understanding of NGO operations, risk areas, and donor complexity. Allows assessment of communication skill, cultural fit, and comprehension of NGO realities.
17. Negotiate Terms Clarify deliverables: financial opinion, management letter, donor-specific schedules, deadlines, publication rights. Prevents disputes and late donor reporting.
18. Draft Engagement Letter Must include scope, standards (ISA), timeline, fees, staff list, confidentiality, right of access to records. Formal legal protection for NGO and compliance with auditing standards.
19. Board Approval Submit final selection and contract to board for endorsement. Confirms governance oversight and satisfies donor due-diligence expectations.
20. Auditor Orientation Provide organizational chart, donor list, grant agreements, accounting policies, internal control manuals. Speeds up fieldwork and ensures auditor understands complex fund flows.
21. Internal Audit Coordination Align external auditor with internal audit findings to avoid duplication. Strengthens control environment and reduces cost.
22. Pre-Audit Readiness Check Finance team prepares “Prepared by Client” list, reconciliations, grant tracking reports, donor schedules. Reduces audit delays and management letter points.
23. Fieldwork Communication Assign one audit liaison. Hold weekly status meetings. Track open issues. Prevents miscommunication in multi-office NGOs.
24. Management Letter Review Discuss findings, agree on corrective actions, assign responsible persons, set deadlines. Converts audit into learning tool and governance improvement mechanism.
25. Donor Report Alignment Ensure final audited statements meet each donor’s reporting format. Avoids donor rejection or re-submission requests.
26. Post-Audit Evaluation Committee rates auditor on quality, timeliness, professionalism, cost, and added value. Basis for future renewals or rotation. Builds institutional memory.
27. Auditor Rotation Policy Replace lead partner every 3–5 years or firm every 5–7 years. Maintains independence and fresh perspective.
28. Continuous Improvement Implement management letter recommendations and track closure status annually. Demonstrates accountability and builds donor trust.
29. Transparency and Disclosure Publish audited financial statements and summary of key findings on website or annual report. Enhances stakeholder confidence and donor visibility.
30. Knowledge Retention Archive full audit documentation, RFP, scoring sheets, contracts, reports, and board approvals. Provides institutional continuity and audit trail for future funders.

Table 7 — Best Practices
Step
What to do
Why it matters (especially with multi-funders)
1. Clarify audit needs & objectives • Determine whether you need a statutory audit, funder-specific audit (grant audit), program-level audit, or combination.
• Define scope: which funds, programs, donor restrictions, geographical operations, internal controls.
• Establish timeline, deliverables, reporting lines (to board, audit committee, donors). 		With multiple funders, each may have different audit/assurance requirements (e.g., donor A requires full financial audit, donor B requires compliance with specific grant conditions). Clear scope prevents surprises or gaps.
2. Governance & procurement process • If you have an audit committee or board sub-committee, assign them responsibility.
• Use a formal process (Request for Proposals – RFP) to invite multiple audit firms.
• Set criteria, ranking methodology upfront. 		A structured process helps ensure independence, transparency, and fairness; essential when multiple funders expect accountability.
3. Pre-selection / shortlisting • Identify auditors/firms with nonprofit/NGO experience.
• Check that they are licensed/registered in your jurisdiction.
• Ask for peer review or quality control reports. 		NGO auditing has specific challenges (restricted funds, donor conditions, multiple grants). Experience avoids “learning on your case”.
4. Develop and apply selection criteria Common criteria include:
– Technical competence (audit standards, non-profit accounting)
– Understanding of multiple donor fund accounting & compliance
– Independence / ethical record
– Cost/fee structure
– Communication skills / partner fit
– Technology/tools used
– Ability to handle complexity (multiple funds, programs, international operations)
– References and reputation
– Turnover/continuity of audit team

Then rank them. 		With multi-funders you must ensure the auditor can handle multiple funding streams, varying donor restrictions, internal control reviews, and possibly international aspects. Ordinary audit firms may not have that depth.
5. Evaluate independence and conflict of interest • Ensure the auditor is independent of management/board and does not provide conflicting services (e.g., consulting) that impair objectivity.
• Consider whether you should rotate lead partner or firm after a number of years (to keep “fresh eyes”). 		Funders often look for strong independence. If the same audit firm also does other non-audit work for you (or is too entrenched), risk of bias or slowed challenge increases.
6. Request and compare proposals • Prepare a request (RFP) that includes: organizational background, funds/funders, scope of audit, deliverables, timeline, any donor-specific audit/reporting requirements.
• Ask for detailed proposals: audit methodology, timeline, staff assigned, fees, references.
• Compare proposals not only on fee but on value, fit, and risk. 		With multiple funders you may have complex reporting obligations (multiple schedules, donor compliance audits). You need to know how the auditor will handle that. Cheap quotes might skip critical elements.
7. Appointment & engagement agreement • Once firm selected, sign an engagement letter or contract specifying scope, fees, responsibilities, deliverables, timeline, rights/access, confidentiality.
• Include provisions for dealing with multiple funders (e.g., delivering separate schedules, donor specific reports). 		Formalising this ensures both parties understand obligations. With multi-funders the complexity means clarity up front avoids later disputes.
8. Audit logistics & preparation • Provide to the auditor the “Prepared by Client” (PBC) list.
• Ensure your records are audit-ready: donor agreements, grant contracts, segregation of duties, restricted fund tracking, internal control documentation.
• Ensure staff understand their role in audit (provide access, answer questions). 		Multiple funders imply many restricted funds, separate reporting lines, different compliance rules. If you don’t prepare, the audit cost/time rises significantly.
9. Monitor & communicate during the audit • Maintain ongoing communication with auditor: discuss approach, timing, what’s needed.
• Have a central liaison (finance director or similar).
• Ensure audit committee/board is briefed.
• Track any issues or delays early. 		Multi-funders complicate the audit (multiple sites, programs, funds). Good communication prevents surprises and ensures deliverables match funder expectations.
10. Post-audit evaluation & reporting • Receive and review the audit report: financial statements, management letter/observations, donor-specific schedules/compliance reports.
• Board/audit committee should evaluate auditor performance: quality, responsiveness, independence, cost-effectiveness.
• Address recommendations and monitor implementation of corrective actions.
• Report to funders: provide them the audited report + any additional donor-required schedules. 		With multiple funders, you must ensure that each fund’s reporting obligations are met, and that donors receive what they expect. Evaluating the auditor ensures future cycles go smoother and your next selection/refinement is better.
11. Rotation / renewal strategy • Decide how long an auditor will serve (e.g., 3-5 years) and whether you will rotate out to maintain fresh perspective.
• At each renewal or change, repeat RFP or re-evaluate criteria. 		As complexity grows (more funders, programs, geographies), you may need fresh skills and perspective. Rotation also supports independence and donor confidence.

Understanding donor restrictions and compliance Each funder may have unique conditions: eligible costs, milestones, reporting schedules, currencies, or in-kind contributions. The auditor must understand how the organization segregates and tracks each fund. Weak fund segregation risks non-compliance or loss of donor trust. —

Best practice includes monthly or quarterly reconciliation of restricted fund balances. BDO (+1)

Higher risk profile Multiple funders increase audit complexity and risk. Auditors should adopt a risk-based approach with stronger internal control focus and larger sample testing. Corporate audit experience alone is insufficient. —
Cost versus value trade-off Multi-funder audits cost more due to complexity. Lowest-cost auditors may lack donor-compliance expertise. Weigh cost against long-term benefits such as fewer findings and stronger donor confidence. —
Integration with governance and audit committee Governance must embed the audit process. The audit committee should oversee auditor selection, monitor progress, review management letters, and ensure corrective actions, demonstrating strong governance to funders. National Council of Nonprofits

Table 9 — Benchmarks
Core Benchmark
Key Purpose / World-Class Standard Insight
1. Global RFP and Scoring Framework Use a standardized international RFP template with weighted evaluation matrix (e.g., 30% technical, 25% NGO experience, 20% independence, 15% quality control record, 10% price). Aligns with IFAC and Accountancy Europe recommendations for audit transparency.
2. Documented Independence and Ethics Declaration Require auditors to sign an annual independence and ethics statement compliant with IESBA Code of Ethics. Ensures audit credibility under globally recognized professional standards.
3. Proven Track Record in Multi-Donor Audits Select firms with verifiable experience auditing complex multi-funder NGOs (e.g., UN, USAID, DFAT, EC-funded projects). Demonstrates mastery of grant compliance frameworks and cross-donor reconciliation.
4. Active Oversight by Audit and Risk Committee Establish committee charter defining authority, meeting frequency, and reporting line to board. Modeled on OECD and COSO governance standards. Guarantees board-level monitoring of audit integrity.
5. Comprehensive Engagement Agreement Engagement letter to include ISA references, data protection clauses (GDPR or local equivalent), and confidentiality obligations. Global best practice for legal and data compliance.
6. Donor Compliance Matrix Integration Maintain a live compliance matrix mapping every donor’s audit requirements, reporting format, and submission deadline. Enables unified audits accepted by multiple donors worldwide.
7. Auditor Rotation and Peer Review Policy Rotate lead partner every 3 years, firm every 5–7 years. Require proof of recent external peer review by oversight body (e.g., PCAOB, ACCA, ICAB). Strengthens independence and audit quality.
8. Full Transparency and Public Disclosure Publish audited financial statements, key ratios, and management response summary on NGO website within 120 days of year-end. Meets highest transparency expectations (e.g., International Aid Transparency Initiative).
9. Structured Corrective Action Tracking Establish system to track implementation of auditor recommendations, with quarterly status reports to board. Converts audit findings into measurable governance improvements.
10. Institutional Audit Memory System Maintain digital archive of RFPs, contracts, working papers, reports, management letters, and closure status. Provides organizational audit history, facilitating donor verification and new staff orientation.

Table 10 — Benchmarks
Strategic Benchmark
Global Best Practice Justification
Integrated Risk-Based Audit Planning Require auditors to apply a documented risk assessment model linking materiality, donor exposure, and fraud risk. Complies with ISA 315 and ISA 330.
Cross-Donor Consolidation Protocol Auditor to test reconciliation between donor-specific ledgers and master GL. Ensures unified reporting integrity for complex NGOs operating in multiple currencies.
Digital Audit Platform Requirement Select auditors using secure digital audit tools with encrypted file exchange and analytics dashboards. Reduces fieldwork time and data exposure risk.
Quality Control under ISQC 1 / ISQM 1 Require evidence that firm applies International Standard on Quality Management (ISQM 1). Confirms professional rigor in planning, supervision, and review.
Capacity-Building Component Include requirement for annual management workshop on donor compliance, fraud prevention, and internal control enhancement. Embeds knowledge transfer as part of audit value.
Global Benchmarked Fees Reference regional median audit fee/hour rates from IFAC or donor-published benchmarks. Avoids under-bidding that compromises quality.
Conflict-of-Interest Registry Maintain registry listing any current or past engagements with related parties. Updated annually. Reduces reputational and ethical risk.
Audit Quality Rating System Internal rating (1–5) based on timeliness, accuracy, independence, and insight quality. Benchmark against previous years. Drives continuous improvement.
International Donor Audit Harmonization Use shared frameworks like OECD-DAC audit standards or UN Harmonized Approach to Cash Transfers (HACT). Reduces redundant audit effort.
Ethical Transparency Pledge Publish NGO’s ethical procurement policy and auditor selection rationale. Meets transparency standards set by global foundations and regulators.
