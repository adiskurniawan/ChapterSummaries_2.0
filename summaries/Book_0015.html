Accounting Rules and Principles (PSAK-based)
Purpose & objective
The purpose of accounting rules and principles under Indonesian Financial Accounting Standards (PSAK) is to ensure that financial statements provide information that is relevant, reliable, comparable, and understandable for users such as investors, creditors, regulators, management, and other stakeholders.
Accounting standards aim to faithfully represent an entity’s financial position, financial performance, and cash flows, support economic decision-making, ensure accountability for resources entrusted to management, and enable consistency across entities and reporting periods.
PSAK is largely converged with International Financial Reporting Standards (IFRS), supporting international comparability while reflecting Indonesian legal, economic, and regulatory conditions.
Scope & applicability of PSAK
PSAK applies primarily to profit-oriented entities preparing general-purpose financial statements intended for external users.
The standards are issued by the Indonesian Accounting Standards Board (DSAK-IAI) and are mandatory for entities within scope, subject to effective dates and transition provisions.
Entities outside PSAK scope (e.g., micro, small, or shariah entities) may apply alternative frameworks such as SAK EMKM or Sharia Accounting Standards, but PSAK remains the dominant framework for large and listed entities.
Conceptual Framework (foundation of PSAK)
The Conceptual Framework underpins all PSAK standards and guides standard-setting, interpretation, and application where no specific PSAK applies.
It defines the objective of financial reporting, qualitative characteristics of useful financial information (relevance and faithful representation, supported by comparability, verifiability, timeliness, and understandability), and the elements of financial statements (assets, liabilities, equity, income, and expenses).
It also establishes recognition criteria, measurement bases (historical cost, current value, fair value, value in use), and concepts of capital and capital maintenance.
Underlying accounting assumptions
Financial statements are prepared on a going concern basis unless management intends or is required to liquidate the entity or cease trading.
The accrual basis of accounting is applied, meaning transactions and events are recognised when they occur, not when cash is received or paid, except for cash flow information.
Consistency of presentation and classification is required from period to period unless a change results in more reliable and relevant information or is required by a PSAK.
Presentation of financial statements – PSAK 1
PSAK 1 prescribes the overall requirements for the presentation of financial statements, including structure, minimum content, and general features.
A complete set of financial statements includes: statement of financial position, statement of profit or loss and other comprehensive income, statement of changes in equity, statement of cash flows, and notes comprising significant accounting policies and explanatory information.
Entities must present comparative information for prior periods and disclose judgements, estimates, and sources of estimation uncertainty that have a significant risk of material adjustment.
Non-current assets held for sale and discontinued operations – PSAK 58
PSAK 58 applies to non-current assets or disposal groups classified as held for sale and to discontinued operations.
An asset is classified as held for sale when its carrying amount will be recovered principally through a sale transaction rather than through continuing use, and the sale is highly probable.
Such assets are measured at the lower of carrying amount and fair value less costs to sell, are presented separately on the statement of financial position, and are not depreciated.
Accounting policies, estimates and errors – PSAK 25
PSAK 25 governs the selection and application of accounting policies, accounting for changes in estimates, and correction of errors.
Accounting policies are applied consistently unless a change is required by a PSAK or results in more reliable and relevant information.
Changes in accounting estimates are recognised prospectively, while material prior-period errors require retrospective restatement of comparative information unless impracticable.
Interpretations related to accounting policies – ISAK 32
ISAK 32 provides interpretative guidance for specific applications of PSAK 25 where judgement is required.
It assists entities in distinguishing between changes in accounting policies and changes in estimates and in applying retrospective or prospective treatment appropriately.
This interpretation enhances consistency and comparability across entities applying PSAK 25.
Measurement bases and fair value – PSAK 68
PSAK 68 establishes a single framework for fair value measurement and related disclosures across all PSAK standards that require or permit fair value.
Fair value is defined as the price that would be received to sell an asset or paid to transfer a liability in an orderly transaction between market participants at the measurement date.
The standard introduces a fair value hierarchy (Level 1, Level 2, Level 3) based on the observability of inputs and requires extensive disclosures, particularly for Level 3 measurements.
Financial instruments – overall framework
Financial instruments accounting under PSAK encompasses classification, measurement, impairment, hedge accounting, presentation, and disclosure of financial assets and liabilities.
The framework aims to reflect an entity’s business model for managing financial instruments and the characteristics of contractual cash flows.
Relevant standards include PSAK 50 (presentation), PSAK 55 (recognition and measurement, largely superseded), PSAK 60 (disclosures), and PSAK 71 (current comprehensive standard).
Financial instruments – PSAK 71 (classification and measurement)
PSAK 71 classifies financial assets based on the entity’s business model and whether contractual cash flows represent solely payments of principal and interest (SPPI).
Financial assets are measured at amortised cost, fair value through other comprehensive income (FVOCI), or fair value through profit or loss (FVPL).
Financial liabilities are generally measured at amortised cost, except for certain liabilities measured at fair value, with presentation rules aligned to economic substance.
Financial instruments – impairment (expected credit loss model)
PSAK 71 introduces a forward-looking expected credit loss (ECL) model, replacing the incurred loss model.
ECLs are recognised on initial recognition and updated at each reporting date to reflect changes in credit risk.
The model distinguishes between 12-month ECL and lifetime ECL, requiring more timely recognition of credit losses and enhanced credit risk disclosures.
Financial instruments – hedge accounting and disclosures – PSAK 60
Hedge accounting under PSAK 71 aligns accounting treatment with risk management activities and allows greater flexibility compared to previous standards.
PSAK 60 requires extensive qualitative and quantitative disclosures about the significance of financial instruments, risks arising from them, and how those risks are managed.
Disclosures include credit risk, liquidity risk, market risk, and sensitivity analyses.
Foreign currency transactions – PSAK 10
PSAK 10 governs accounting for foreign currency transactions and foreign operations.
Foreign currency transactions are initially recorded using the spot exchange rate at the transaction date; monetary items are retranslated at the closing rate at each reporting date.
Exchange differences are generally recognised in profit or loss, except for certain items recognised in other comprehensive income.
Hyperinflationary economies – PSAK 63
PSAK 63 applies when an entity’s functional currency is that of a hyperinflationary economy.
Financial statements are restated to reflect changes in purchasing power using a general price index before being translated into another currency.
The objective is to ensure that financial information remains meaningful and comparable in conditions of extreme inflation.
Revenue recognition – PSAK 72
PSAK 72 establishes a comprehensive five-step model for recognising revenue from contracts with customers.
Revenue is recognised when control of goods or services is transferred to the customer, either over time or at a point in time.
The standard requires identification of performance obligations, determination and allocation of transaction price, and recognition of revenue based on satisfaction of obligations, with extensive disclosure requirements.
Government grants – PSAK 61
PSAK 61 addresses accounting for government grants and disclosure of government assistance.
Grants are recognised only when there is reasonable assurance that conditions will be met and the grants will be received.
Grants related to assets are presented either as deferred income or deducted from the carrying amount of the asset, while income-related grants are recognised systematically in profit or loss.
Operating segments – PSAK 5
PSAK 5 requires entities to disclose information about operating segments based on internal reports reviewed by the chief operating decision maker (CODM).
Segment reporting reflects management’s view of the business, enhancing relevance for users assessing performance and resource allocation.
Disclosures include segment revenue, profit or loss, assets, liabilities (if reviewed), and reconciliations to entity-wide totals.
Employee benefits – PSAK 24
PSAK 24 governs accounting for employee benefits, including short-term benefits, post-employment benefits, other long-term benefits, and termination benefits.
Defined contribution plans recognise expense as contributions are due, while defined benefit plans require actuarial valuation using the projected unit credit method.
Remeasurements of defined benefit obligations are recognised in other comprehensive income and are not recycled to profit or loss.
Share-based payment – PSAK 53
PSAK 53 requires entities to recognise the fair value of goods or services received in share-based payment transactions.
Equity-settled transactions are measured at grant-date fair value and recognised over the vesting period, while cash-settled transactions are remeasured at each reporting date.
The standard ensures that compensation cost is reflected in financial performance, even when no cash outflow occurs.
Income taxes – PSAK 46
PSAK 46 prescribes accounting for current and deferred income taxes using the balance sheet approach.
Deferred tax assets and liabilities arise from temporary differences between the carrying amount of assets and liabilities and their tax bases.
Deferred tax assets are recognised only to the extent that it is probable that taxable profit will be available against which deductible temporary differences can be utilised.
Earnings per share – PSAK 56
PSAK 56 sets principles for calculating and presenting basic and diluted earnings per share (EPS).
Basic EPS is calculated by dividing profit or loss attributable to ordinary shareholders by the weighted average number of ordinary shares outstanding.
Diluted EPS reflects the potential dilution from convertible instruments, options, and other potential ordinary shares.
Disclosure, transparency and comparability
PSAK emphasises comprehensive disclosure to enable users to understand the financial statements and assess an entity’s financial position and performance.
Disclosures include significant accounting policies, judgements, estimates, risk exposures, and sensitivity analyses where required.
Consistency, clarity, and comparability across periods and entities are fundamental principles reinforced throughout PSAK standards.
Governance, judgement and compliance
Management is responsible for selecting appropriate accounting policies, making judgements and estimates, and ensuring compliance with PSAK.
Those charged with governance oversee the financial reporting process, internal controls, and compliance with applicable standards.
Non-compliance with PSAK may result in qualified audit opinions, regulatory sanctions, or reduced credibility of financial information.

Balance Sheet and Related Notes (PSAK)
Purpose & objective.
The balance sheet (statement of financial position) and related notes provide a structured, faithful representation of an entity’s financial position at the reporting date to enable users to assess liquidity, solvency, capital structure and the nature and measurement of assets, liabilities and equity. The notes explain accounting policies, judgments, estimates, risks and detailed line-item breakdowns necessary for transparency, auditability, regulatory compliance and informed economic decision-making. The balance sheet is prepared on a going concern basis and under accrual accounting unless PSAK permits otherwise.
Scope & structure of balance sheet and notes.
The balance sheet covers all recognised assets, liabilities and equity at period end; related notes include accounting policies, supporting schedules, reconciliations, commitments, contingencies and post-reporting disclosures. Presentation follows PSAK 1 and is supported by specific standards (PSAK 19, 16, 13, 48, 73, 14, 57, 8 and related ISAKs). Comparative information for the prior period is required unless an exemption applies.
Core recognition & measurement principles.
Assets are recognised when future economic benefits are probable and measurable reliably; liabilities are recognised when a present obligation exists and settlement is probable and measurable. Initial measurement is at cost unless a PSAK requires fair value or another basis. Subsequent measurement depends on the applicable standard (cost, amortised cost, revaluation, fair value). Materiality and substance over form guide recognition, classification and disclosure decisions.
Classification & presentation rules.
Classify assets and liabilities as current or non-current based on operating cycle and expected realisation/settlement within 12 months unless liquidity presentation is more relevant. Present minimum line items on the face of the statement with further disaggregation in notes. Offsetting is prohibited unless explicitly permitted by a PSAK.
Judgements, estimates & uncertainty.
Disclose significant judgements (e.g., lease classification, functional currency, CGU identification) and key sources of estimation uncertainty (e.g., impairment assumptions, useful lives, provisions). Provide sensitivity disclosures where estimation uncertainty could lead to material future adjustments.
Key definitions & core recognition principle.
• Carrying amount: asset amount after accumulated amortisation/depreciation and impairment.
• Recoverable amount: higher of fair value less costs of disposal and value in use (PSAK 48).
• Right-of-use asset: lessee’s asset representing use-right under PSAK 73.
• Investment property: property held to earn rentals or for capital appreciation (PSAK 13).
• Provision: present obligation from past events with probable outflow (PSAK 57).
Core principle: recognise items only when respective PSAK recognition criteria are met and measure using the model required by that PSAK.
Intangible assets — recognition & measurement (PSAK 19).
Intangibles are identifiable non-monetary assets without physical substance (separable or contractual/legal rights). Recognise separately acquired intangibles at cost. Internally generated intangibles: research expensed; development capitalised only if technical feasibility, intention to complete, ability to use/sell, probable future benefits, resource availability and reliable measurement are all demonstrated. Subsequent measurement: cost or revaluation model (active market required). Finite-lived intangibles are amortised; indefinite-lived intangibles are not amortised and are tested annually for impairment.
Intangible assets — disclosures.
Disclose useful lives, amortisation methods, gross carrying amounts, accumulated amortisation and impairment, reconciliation of movements, amounts capitalised for development costs and the basis for recognition, and the line items in profit or loss where amortisation is included.
Property, plant & equipment — recognition & initial measurement (PSAK 16).
PPE are tangible assets held for use in production, supply, rental or administrative purposes and expected to be used for more than one period. Initial cost includes purchase price, directly attributable costs and initial estimates of dismantling/restoration obligations. Capitalise borrowing costs per PSAK 26 where applicable. Commence depreciation when the asset is available for use.
PPE — subsequent measurement, componentisation & derecognition.
Choose cost model (cost less accumulated depreciation and impairment) or revaluation model applied consistently to entire classes. Component accounting: split parts with materially different useful lives and depreciate separately. Capitalise subsequent expenditure only if it enhances future benefits; routine maintenance is expensed. Derecognise on disposal or when no future benefits expected; recognise gain/loss in profit or loss.
PPE — disclosures.
Disclose measurement bases, depreciation methods and rates, reconciliation of carrying amounts (additions, disposals, revaluations, depreciation, impairment), restrictions on title, pledged assets, capital commitments and revaluation surplus movements (if any).
Investment property — recognition & measurement (PSAK 13).
Investment property is land or buildings held to earn rentals or for capital appreciation. Initial measurement at cost. Subsequent measurement: either fair value model (changes to profit or loss) or cost model (depreciation + impairment, with fair value disclosed). Transfers to/from investment property require evidence of change in use, not merely management intention.
Investment property — disclosures.
Disclose chosen measurement model, fair value determination methods, rental income, direct operating expenses, restrictions on realisability and contractual obligations to purchase, construct or develop investment property.
Impairment of assets — overview & triggers (PSAK 48).
Assess at each reporting date whether indicators of impairment exist. If present, estimate recoverable amount (higher of fair value less costs of disposal and value in use). Impair when carrying amount exceeds recoverable amount. Goodwill and indefinite-lived intangibles: annual testing regardless of indicators. Perform CGU-level testing where cash inflows are not independent.
Impairment — recognition, reversal & disclosures.
Recognise impairment losses in profit or loss; allocate first to goodwill then to other assets of the CGU pro rata. Reversals are permitted for assets other than goodwill when estimates change. Disclose impairment losses and reversals, key assumptions (discount rate, growth rates), and sensitivity analyses for material CGUs.
Lease accounting — recognition & measurement (PSAK 73).
Lessees recognise a right-of-use asset and a lease liability at commencement date for most leases. Lease liability measured at present value of lease payments; ROU asset measured at cost (including initial direct costs) and depreciated. Exemptions for short-term leases and low-value assets may be elected. Lessors classify leases as finance or operating and account accordingly.
Leases — presentation & disclosures.
Disclose maturity analysis of lease liabilities, variable lease payments, extension/termination options and expense related to exempt leases. Present ROU assets separately or within the same line item as underlying assets with reconciliation of movements.
Inventories — recognition & measurement (PSAK 14).
Inventories are assets held for sale, in production, or to be consumed in production. Measure at the lower of cost and net realisable value (NRV). Cost includes purchase, conversion and other costs incurred to bring inventories to present condition and location. Permitted cost formulas: FIFO or weighted average; LIFO prohibited.
Inventories — write-downs & disclosures.
Write down inventories to NRV when damaged, obsolete or when selling prices decline; reverse write-downs if circumstances improve. Disclose accounting policies, carrying amounts by category, amount recognised as expense (cost of sales), write-downs and reversals, and inventories pledged as security.
Provisions & contingencies — recognition & measurement (PSAK 57).
Recognise a provision when a present legal or constructive obligation from a past event exists, an outflow is probable and the amount can be estimated reliably. Measure at the best estimate of expenditure required, discounted when the time value of money is material. Contingent liabilities are not recognised but disclosed unless remote; contingent assets disclosed when inflow probable.
Provisions — specific applications & disclosures.
Special guidance for restructuring provisions, onerous contracts, warranties and reimbursements. Disclose nature, timing, uncertainties, movement during the period and expected reimbursements. For reimbursements, recognise asset only when reimbursement virtually certain and not exceeding the related provision.
Events after the reporting period — PSAK 8.
Classify post-reporting events as adjusting (evidence of conditions existing at reporting date — adjust recognised amounts) or non-adjusting (arising after reporting date — disclose if material). Disclose date of authorisation for issue, proposed dividends, significant non-adjusting events and material post-period commitments.
Share capital & reserves — recognition & presentation.
Share capital recorded at proceeds received net of transaction costs; equity instruments not remeasured after initial recognition. Reserves include retained earnings, revaluation surplus, translation reserve, hedging reserve and other OCI components. Disclose authorised, issued and paid shares, par value, rights/restrictions, treasury shares and reconciliation of movements in the statement of changes in equity; present non-controlling interests separately within equity.
Related notes — general disclosure requirements.
Notes must clearly present accounting policies, significant judgments and estimates, and detailed supporting information for balance sheet line items with cross-references to primary statements. Notes must be complete, neutral and free from material error and must preserve verbatim numeric references to PSAK/ISAK where applicable.
Comparatives, consistency & restatements (PSAK 25).
Provide comparative information for all amounts. Changes in presentation, accounting policies or error corrections require restatement and disclosure of nature, rationale and quantitative effects per PSAK 25. Apply consistent policies unless a change improves relevance and reliability.
Linkage with other PSAKs.
Balance sheet items interact with PSAK 68 (fair value), PSAK 71/50 (financial instruments), PSAK 26 (borrowing costs), PSAK 10 (foreign currency), PSAK 1 (presentation) and relevant ISAKs. Ensure cross-standard consistency and disclose where multiple standards apply to the same item.
Required inputs & supporting documentation (minimum evidence).
• Purchase invoices, purchase orders and contracts for cost evidence.
• Fixed asset register with unique IDs, locations, custodians, cost, accumulated depreciation/amortisation and useful lives.
• Construction contracts and capitalisation schedules for CIP.
• Lease contracts, payment schedules, options and discount rate support.
• Valuation reports and assumptions for fair value/revaluation models.
• Impairment files: cash flow forecasts, discount rates, assumptions, sensitivity analyses and CGU allocations.
• Inventory costing worksheets, NRV calculations and stock count records.
• Provision support: legal opinions, claim letters and best-estimate calculations.
• Board approvals and minutes for policy changes, impairments and revaluations.
Recognition & initial measurement — operational summary.
• PPE: recognise when control and probable future benefits exist; initial measurement at cost including directly attributable costs and restoration obligations.
• Intangibles: recognise separately acquired intangibles at cost; capitalise development only when criteria met.
• Investment property: recognise at cost where applicable; measure subsequently per chosen model.
• Inventories: recognise at cost when control and payment obligations exist.
• Leases: recognise ROU asset and lease liability at present value of lease payments at commencement.
• Provisions: recognise when present obligation, probable outflow and reliable estimate exist.
Subsequent measurement & models — operational guidance.
• PPE: cost or revaluation model (apply consistently to class).
• Intangibles: amortise finite lives; test indefinite lives annually.
• Investment property: fair value model (FV changes to P&L) or cost model (depr + impairment + FV disclosure).
• Inventories: lower of cost and NRV; test at each reporting date.
• Fair value inputs: follow PSAK 68 Level 1–3 hierarchy and disclose levels.
Depreciation, amortisation & componentisation — rules.
• Methods must reflect consumption pattern (straight-line, units-of-production, reducing balance where justified).
• Component accounting required where parts have materially different useful lives.
• Reassess useful life and residual value annually and apply changes prospectively.
Impairment testing procedures (PSAK 48) — stepwise.
1. Identify indicators (external/internal) at each reporting date.
2. Determine CGU structure where individual asset cash flows not separable.
3. Estimate recoverable amount (higher of FV less costs to sell and value in use) with DCF support.
4. Recognise impairment loss in P&L; allocate across CGU assets.
5. For non-goodwill assets, consider reversal if recoverable amount increases.
6. Retain sensitivity analyses, discount rate derivations and management approvals in working papers.
Derecognition & disposal rules.
• Derecognise assets when control transfers or no future benefits expected; for disposal groups apply PSAK 58 held-for-sale criteria.
• For sale-and-leaseback apply PSAK 72/73 to assess whether sale qualifies and recognise gain limited to rights transferred; account for retained ROU appropriately.
• Recognise gain/loss as proceeds less carrying amount and disposal costs.
Lease operational steps (PSAK 73) — checklist.
• Identify leases and separate non-lease components unless policy combines them.
• Determine lease term including reasonably certain extension/termination options.
• Determine discount rate (implicit or incremental borrowing rate) and calculate present value of lease payments.
• Recognise initial lease liability and ROU asset; capitalise initial direct costs to ROU as applicable.
• Subsequently record interest on liability and depreciation on ROU; apply exemptions for short-term/low-value leases if elected.
Inventories operational rules & controls.
• Classify inventories into raw materials, WIP and finished goods.
• Apply and document consistent cost formula across similar inventory types (FIFO or weighted average).
• Maintain perpetual or periodic records and perform cycle counts; test NRV and record write-downs with market evidence.
Provisions, contingencies & onerous contracts — operational guidance.
• Recognise provisions when present obligation, probable outflow and reliable estimate exist.
• For reimbursements, recognise asset only when reimbursement virtually certain and cap at provision amount.
• For onerous contracts, recognise provision equal to unavoidable costs; include direct incremental and allocable costs as appropriate.
• Document legal opinions and management rationale supporting measurement and recognition.
Required note disclosures (minimum details).
• Accounting policies and rationale for measurement model choices.
• Reconciliations of opening and closing carrying amounts for PPE, intangibles, investment property and ROU assets including additions, disposals, depreciation/amortisation, revaluations and impairment losses.
• Carrying amounts by class, useful lives, depreciation methods and fully depreciated assets still in use.
• Detailed impairment disclosures: losses, reversals, key assumptions and sensitivity analysis.
• Lease disclosures: maturity analysis of lease liabilities and reconciliation of ROU movements.
• Inventories: carrying amount by category, write-downs and pledges.
• Provisions: movement schedule, timing and uncertainties.
• Material non-adjusting post-period events and date of authorisation for issue.
Accounting treatment & sample journal entries.
1. Capitalisation of PPE at cost: Dr PPE — Cr Bank/Payables.
2. Accrual of construction-in-progress: Dr CIP — Cr Accrued liabilities.
3. Depreciation: Dr Depreciation expense — Cr Accumulated depreciation.
4. Impairment loss (CGU): Dr Impairment loss (P&L) — Cr Accumulated impairment / carrying amount reduction.
5. Disposal: Dr Bank/Receivable — Cr PPE carrying amount; recognise gain/loss in P&L.
6. ROU & lease liability initial: Dr ROU asset — Cr Lease liability; Dr initial direct costs — Cr Bank (capitalised to ROU).
7. Lease subsequent: Dr Interest expense — Cr Lease liability; Dr Depreciation expense — Cr Accumulated depreciation (ROU).
8. Inventory write-down: Dr Inventory write-down expense — Cr Inventory allowance.
9. Provision recognition: Dr Expense (or asset) — Cr Provision liability.
10. Revaluation surplus (revaluation model): Dr Asset — Cr Revaluation surplus (OCI) to extent not reversing previous deficits.
Controls, reconciliations & operational checklists.
• Maintain detailed fixed asset register reconciled regularly to GL.
• Segregate duties across acquisition, tagging, custody and accounting entries.
• Perform annual physical verification and reconcile variances.
• Reconcile CIP roll-forwards and review period-end capitalisation cut-offs.
• Maintain lease register and reconcile to lease liability schedule.
• Perform inventory cycle counts and reconcile to perpetual records.
• Implement provision approval matrix with legal and board sign-offs for material amounts.
Templates, filing & audit pack — minimum contents.
• Fixed asset register with supporting invoices and capitalisation approvals.
• Lease schedule and contract copies with amortisation/interest calculations.
• Impairment workpapers and sensitivity analyses.
• Inventory valuation worksheets and NRV support documentation.
• Provision movement schedule with legal opinions and settlement scenarios.
• Reconciliations of asset ledgers to GL and bank confirmations for disposals.
• Board minutes for revaluations and accounting policy changes.
Common errors & mitigations.
• Capitalising non-qualifying costs — mitigation: enforce capitalisation checklist and pre-approval.
• Missing impairment triggers or delayed recognition — mitigation: automated trigger lists and periodic screening.
• Improper lease classification or ignoring extension options — mitigation: lease register reviews and legal sign-off.
• NRV mis-estimation for inventories — mitigation: market testing and sample-based NRV support.
• Under/over provisioning — mitigation: obtain legal confirmation and use best-estimate ranges with management sign-off.
Operational implementation checklist (pre-close & post-close).
Pre-close: ☐ Verify invoices for major capex and obtain approvals; ☐ Update asset register for dispositions/additions; ☐ Identify leases and confirm discount rates; ☐ Perform inventory cut-off counts and reconciliations.
Post-close: ☐ Reconcile asset registers to GL and correct postings; ☐ Finalise impairment test files with management sign-off; ☐ Compile note disclosures and audit pack reconciliations.
Disclosure drafting guidance & checklist for notes.
• State chosen accounting policy for each asset class and rationale for any changes.
• Provide reconciliations of carrying amounts showing additions, disposals, depreciation, revaluations and impairment.
• Disclose significant estimates and judgements (useful lives, residual values, discount rates, CGU composition).
• For fair value, disclose level (1/2/3) and sensitivity to key unobservable inputs.
• For provisions, disclose nature, timing and uncertainties of outflows.
Audit readiness & evidence expectations.
Auditors expect source invoices/contracts, asset tagging evidence, valuation/impairment models, lease contracts and amortisation schedules, inventory count records and provision support including legal correspondence. Maintain a cross-reference index linking each disclosure line to supporting working papers.
Special situations & ISAK interactions.
• ISAK 36 (Land Rights): assess whether land rights are asset purchase vs lease (interaction of PSAK 16 and PSAK 73).
• ISAK guidance and transition rules when new standards affect balance sheet measurement.
• PSAK 63 (hyperinflation): restatement requirements where functional currency becomes hyperinflationary.
Record retention & naming conventions.
Retain primary supporting documents (invoices, contracts, valuations, legal opinions) per statutory and organisational policy (commonly 6–10 years or longer where required). Use consistent file naming including asset ID, period and doc type for auditability.
KPIs & management information.
• % fixed assets physically verified in last 12 months.
• Number/value of asset disposals awaiting support.
• Inventory write-downs as % of carrying amount.
• Provision movement volatility and ageing of contingent liabilities.
• Time to close impairment workpapers from period-end to management sign-off.

Consolidated and Separate Financial Statements (PSAK)
Purpose & objective
Consolidated and separate financial statements provide a faithful representation of an entity’s economic activities when it controls, jointly controls, or significantly influences other entities. The objective is to present financial information that reflects the substance of group relationships rather than legal form, enabling users to assess the financial position, performance, and cash flows of a group as a single economic entity, as well as the parent entity on a stand-alone basis where required. Consolidation principles ensure comparability, transparency, elimination of intra-group effects, and consistent application of accounting policies across the group.
Scope & applicability
These standards apply to parent entities preparing consolidated financial statements, investors with subsidiaries, associates, and joint arrangements, and entities presenting separate financial statements. PSAK 65 governs consolidation based on control; PSAK 4 governs separate financial statements; PSAK 22 and PSAK 38 address business combinations; PSAK 58 covers disposals and held-for-sale classifications; PSAK 15 governs equity accounting; PSAK 66 addresses joint arrangements.
Core consolidation principle
An entity consolidates another entity when it controls it. Control exists only when the investor has power over the investee, exposure or rights to variable returns from its involvement, and the ability to use its power to affect those returns. All three elements must be present simultaneously and continuously reassessed. Control is assessed based on substance, not merely legal ownership.
Consolidated financial statements — PSAK 65.
Consolidated financial statements present the parent and its subsidiaries as a single economic entity. All assets, liabilities, equity, income, expenses and cash flows of subsidiaries are combined line by line with those of the parent.
- Uniform accounting policies must be applied across the group; reporting dates must be aligned (or adjusted if different).
- Intra-group balances, transactions, income and expenses are eliminated in full to avoid double counting.
- Non-controlling interests (NCI) are presented separately within equity and profit or loss.
Definition and assessment of control.
Control assessment considers substantive rights, potential voting rights, contractual arrangements, de facto control, and structured entities.
- Power arises from rights that give the current ability to direct relevant activities.
- Variable returns include dividends, residual interests, synergies, fees, and other benefits.
- Continuous reassessment is required when facts and circumstances change.
Non-controlling interests (NCI).
NCI represent equity interests in a subsidiary not attributable, directly or indirectly, to the parent. NCI are recognised at acquisition either at fair value or at the proportionate share of the acquiree’s identifiable net assets (policy choice applied consistently).
- NCI share of profit or loss and other comprehensive income is attributed even if this results in a deficit balance.
Loss of control.
When a parent loses control of a subsidiary, it derecognises the subsidiary’s assets, liabilities and NCI, recognises any retained investment at fair value, and recognises the resulting gain or loss in profit or loss.
- Amounts previously recognised in OCI are reclassified or transferred directly to retained earnings as required by relevant PSAKs.
Separate financial statements — PSAK 4.
Separate financial statements are those presented by a parent, investor or venturer in which investments in subsidiaries, associates and joint ventures are accounted for on a basis other than consolidation or equity method.
- In separate financial statements, investments are measured either at cost, in accordance with PSAK 71 (fair value), or using the equity method (policy choice applied consistently by category of investment).
Purpose of separate financial statements.
Separate financial statements provide information about the parent entity’s own financial position and performance, independent of group consolidation.
- They are often required by regulators, shareholders or legal statutes and are presented in addition to, not instead of, consolidated financial statements (except where consolidation exemption applies).
Business combinations — PSAK 22.
A business combination is a transaction or event in which an acquirer obtains control of one or more businesses. PSAK 22 requires the acquisition method to be applied.
- Identify the acquirer and acquisition date.
- Recognise and measure identifiable assets acquired and liabilities assumed at fair value.
- Recognise goodwill or a gain from a bargain purchase.
Goodwill accounting.
Goodwill is measured as the excess of consideration transferred plus NCI and previously held interest over the fair value of identifiable net assets acquired.
- Goodwill is not amortised but tested annually for impairment under PSAK 48 and whenever indicators of impairment exist.
Business combinations under common control — PSAK 38.
Transactions between entities under common control are accounted for using the pooling-of-interests method.
- Assets and liabilities are recognised at their carrying amounts.
- No goodwill is recognised; any difference is presented within equity as additional paid-in capital or similar reserve.
Disposals and assets held for sale — PSAK 58.
Non-current assets or disposal groups are classified as held for sale when their carrying amount will be recovered principally through a sale transaction and the sale is highly probable.
- Assets held for sale are measured at the lower of carrying amount and fair value less costs to sell and are not depreciated.
Discontinued operations.
A discontinued operation is a component of an entity that has been disposed of or classified as held for sale and represents a separate major line of business or geographical area.
- Results are presented separately in profit or loss to enhance comparability of continuing operations.
Equity accounting — PSAK 15.
The equity method is applied to investments in associates and joint ventures in consolidated financial statements.
- The investment is initially recognised at cost and subsequently adjusted for the investor’s share of post-acquisition profit or loss and OCI of the investee.
- Distributions received reduce the carrying amount of the investment.
Associates and significant influence.
An associate is an entity over which the investor has significant influence, generally presumed with 20%–50% of voting power, unless clearly demonstrated otherwise.
- Significant influence includes participation in policy decisions, representation on the board, and material transactions.
Impairment of investments accounted for using equity method.
Investments accounted for using the equity method are tested for impairment under PSAK 48 when objective evidence of impairment exists.
- Any impairment loss is recognised in profit or loss and reduces the carrying amount of the investment.
Joint arrangements — PSAK 66.
A joint arrangement exists when two or more parties have joint control, requiring unanimous consent over relevant activities. PSAK 66 classifies joint arrangements as joint operations or joint ventures.
Joint operations.
In a joint operation, parties have rights to the assets and obligations for the liabilities.
- Each operator recognises its share of assets, liabilities, revenues and expenses in its own financial statements.
Joint ventures.
In a joint venture, parties have rights to the net assets of the arrangement.
- Joint ventures are accounted for using the equity method in consolidated financial statements.
Uniform accounting policies & reporting periods.
Group entities must apply uniform accounting policies for like transactions and events.
- If reporting dates differ, adjustments are made for significant transactions between dates, or statements are prepared to the same reporting date where practicable.
Intra-group transactions and unrealised profits.
All intra-group balances, transactions, income and expenses are eliminated in full on consolidation.
- Unrealised profits arising from intra-group transactions are eliminated against the carrying amounts of related assets.
Disclosure requirements.
Entities must disclose information that enables users to evaluate the nature of, and risks associated with, interests in other entities and the effects of those interests on financial position, performance and cash flows.
- Disclosures cover subsidiaries, NCI, associates, joint arrangements, structured entities and significant judgements made in determining control or influence.
Exemptions from consolidation.
A parent may be exempt from presenting consolidated financial statements if it is a wholly-owned or partially-owned subsidiary and specific criteria are met, including availability of IFRS-compliant consolidated statements by an ultimate or intermediate parent.
Presentation & consistency.
Consolidated and separate financial statements must be clearly identified and presented consistently from period to period.
- Comparative information is required and changes in group structure must be fully disclosed and explained.
