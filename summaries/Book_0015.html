Accounting rules and principles — Comprehensive summary (PSAK Pocket Guide 2023)
Introduction
• Purpose: summarise recognition, measurement and presentation requirements of Indonesian Financial Accounting Standards (IFAS/PSAK) effective for periods beginning on/after 1 Jan 2023 unless noted otherwise.
• Convergence: DSAK-IAI continues alignment with IFRS — notable issuance: PSAK 74 (IFRS 17) effective 1 Jan 2025 (early application permitted).
• SAK International: May 2023 release — word-for-word IFRS translations forming a new pillar; implementation details pending regulator decisions.
Accounting principles & applicability of PSAK
• Authority: DSAK-IAI issues PSAKs and ISAKs; IFAS intended for profit-oriented entities preparing general purpose financial statements for broad user decisions (shareholders, creditors, employees, public).
• Complete set of financial statements: statement of financial position; statement of comprehensive income; statement of cash flows; description of accounting policies; notes; and opening statement of financial position when retrospective application/restatement occurs.
• Conceptual Framework: status/purpose; objective of GPFR; qualitative characteristics; reporting entity; elements; recognition/derecognition; measurement; presentation & disclosure; capital concepts; defined terms.
Presentation of financial statements — PSAK 1 & PSAK 58
• Objective: provide information useful for economic decisions; ensure comparability across periods and entities.
• Basis: going concern unless management intends/has no realistic alternative to liquidate or cease trading; accrual basis for all except cash flow statement.
• Format: no single prescribed format but minimum line items required on face of statement of financial position (PPE, investment property, intangibles, financial assets, equity-accounted investments, biological assets, deferred/current tax, inventories, receivables, cash & equivalents; issued capital & reserves; NCI; liabilities including provisions and payables; assets/liabilities held for sale).
• Current/non-current presentation: separate unless liquidity-based presentation provides reliable/relevant information.
• Statement(s) of profit or loss & OCI: single or two-statement approach permitted; present revenue, finance costs, impairment losses, share of profit of associates/JVs, tax expense, discontinued operations as minimum items; present material items separately; allocate profit and OCI between owners and NCI.
• Other comprehensive income: present components by nature and grouped into those recyclable to profit or loss and those not recyclable; disclose reclassification adjustments and related tax effects (either net of tax or before tax with aggregate tax shown).
• Statement of changes in equity: present total comprehensive income attributable to owners/NCI; retrospective application effects; reconciliations for each component of equity and dividends disclosed.
• Notes: integral — basis of preparation, significant accounting policies, critical estimates/judgments, required disclosures.
Accounting policies, accounting estimates & errors — PSAK 25 & ISAK 32
• Selection of policies: apply relevant PSAK; where none exists, use similar/related PSAK guidance, the Framework, recent pronouncements of other standard-setters, or industry practice (provided no conflict).
• Reliability qualities: faithful representation, substance over form, neutrality, prudence, completeness.
• ISAK 32: clarifies definition & hierarchy of standards; warns that conflicting non-PSAK rules prevent an explicit PSAK compliance statement.
• Consistency: apply policies consistently to similar items unless PSAK permits otherwise.
• Changes in accounting policies: account for new standard transitions per transitional provisions; if none, apply retrospectively (restating comparatives) unless impracticable; special exception for initial adoption of revaluation model for PPE/intangibles (accounting in year of change).
• Voluntary policy changes: allowed when resulting information is more reliable/relevant.
• IFRIC agenda decisions: may prompt voluntary policy changes; disclose forthcoming change if decided but not implemented.
• New/revised standards not yet effective: disclose and provide known/estimable impact information.
• Accounting estimates: recognise prospectively; changes affect current and future periods unless they change assets/liabilities/equity in which case adjust carrying amounts in the period of change.
• Errors: prior-period errors (material) restated retrospectively unless impracticable; errors include mistakes, oversights, misinterpretation, and fraud.
Fair value measurement — PSAK 68
• Definition: fair value = price that would be received to sell an asset or paid to transfer a liability in an orderly transaction between market participants at the measurement date (exit price).
• Principle: perspective of market participants, not the entity; not affected by entity’s intentions for the asset/liability.
• Required determinations: (1) unit of account (specific asset/liability), (2) highest & best use for non-financial assets, (3) principal (or most advantageous) market, (4) valuation technique.
• Scope: provides the measurement framework where another PSAK requires/permits fair value; does not itself mandate when to use fair value.
Financial instruments — PSAK 50, PSAK 55, PSAK 60, PSAK 71 (overview)
• Standards set: PSAK 50 (presentation), PSAK 55 (recognition & measurement historic), PSAK 60 (disclosures), PSAK 71 (current recognition, classification, measurement, impairment, hedge accounting), ISAK 28 (extinguishing liabilities with equity instruments).
• Objective: cover classification (debt vs equity), offsetting, recognition/derecognition, measurement, hedge accounting and disclosures for risks (credit, liquidity, market).
• Wide scope: receivables, payables, investments, borrowings, derivatives, some contracts to buy/sell non-financial items if net settlement in cash/financial instruments possible.
Classification & measurement under PSAK 71
• Three measurement categories for financial assets: amortised cost; fair value through other comprehensive income (FVOCI); fair value through profit or loss (FVPL).
• Classification drivers: entity’s business model for managing assets and contractual cash flows test (solely payments of principal and interest — SPPI).
• Irrevocable election: at initial recognition to designate assets to FVPL to eliminate accounting mismatches.
• Hybrid contracts: for financial asset hosts within PSAK 71 scope, no separate separation of embedded derivatives — apply SPPI to whole contract; if fails SPPI, classify whole contract as FVPL.
• Equity instruments: normally FVPL; irrevocable election on instrument-by-instrument basis to present FV changes in OCI (OCI election) if not held for trading — no subsequent recycling of OCI to profit/loss on disposal for equity OCI election (subject to standard specifics).
• Financial liabilities: generally measured at amortised cost unless designated FVPL or required otherwise; changes in fair value due to own credit risk presented in OCI when FV option elected.
Embedded derivatives, recognition & derecognition
• Embedded derivatives: assess whether economic characteristics and risks are closely related to host; if not and not within PSAK 71 scope, separate and measure at FVPL.
• Recognition: recognise financial assets/liabilities when entity becomes a party to the contract.
• Derecognition financial assets: when rights to cash flows expire or transferred and risks/rewards substantially transferred, or control transferred per detailed rules.
• Derecognition liabilities: extinguished when discharged, cancelled or expired; debt restructuring guidance distinguishes settlement vs modification based on substantially different terms.
Impairment (Expected Credit Losses) — PSAK 71
• Model: forward-looking ECL model with three stages based on credit deterioration since initial recognition:
– Stage 1: no significant increase in credit risk (or low credit risk) → recognise 12-month ECL; interest on gross carrying amount.
– Stage 2: significant increase in credit risk (not credit-impaired) → recognise lifetime ECL; interest on gross carrying amount.
– Stage 3: credit-impaired → recognise lifetime ECL; interest on net carrying amount (after allowance).
• Trade receivables/contract assets without significant financing component: measure loss allowance at lifetime ECL at initial recognition.
• Low credit risk exemption: if asset has low credit risk at reporting date, entity may measure impairment using 12-month ECL.
• Judgment: significant increase assessment and forward-looking ECL estimation require substantial judgment and data; PSAK 71 adds extensive disclosure requirements.
Hedge accounting — PSAK 71 & ISAK 13
• Purpose: reflect economic substance of risk management by aligning timing of recognition of gains/losses on hedging instruments and hedged items.
• Qualifying requirements: formal designation & documentation at hedge inception (identify instrument, hedged item/transaction, hedged risk, hedge ratio, hedge effectiveness assessment); economic relationship between hedging instrument and hedged item; credit risk must not dominate value changes; hedge ratio consistent with risk management strategy.
• Effectiveness testing: no 80–125% bright line: retrospective 80–125% test removed — however ineffectiveness must still be measured and recorded in profit or loss.
• Types of hedges: fair value hedge; cash flow hedge; net investment hedge.
• Accounting outcomes: fair value hedge → adjust hedged item for hedged risk, recognise in income statement; cash flow hedge → effective portion to OCI and reclassified to profit/loss when hedged item affects profit/loss; net investment hedge → similar to cash flow hedge.
• ISAK 13: additional guidance on hedging foreign-currency net investments.
Disclosure (PSAK 60 & PSAK 68 interaction)
• PSAK 60: requires disclosures enabling users to evaluate significance of financial instruments for financial position/performance and nature/extent of risks (credit, liquidity, market) and how risks are managed.
• PSAK 68: three-level fair value hierarchy and required quantitative disclosures especially for Level 3 measurements.
• Scope: Disclosures apply to all entities with financial instruments (not only financial institutions).
IBOR reform — reliefs & amendments
• Project phases: two-phase project providing reliefs for hedging & contract cash-flow changes due to benchmark interest rate reforms.
• Phase 1: temporary reliefs so reform does not generally cause hedge accounting to terminate (but record hedge ineffectiveness); reliefs around risk components, 'highly probable' requirement, prospective assessments, retrospective effectiveness tests and recycling rules.
• Phase 2: reliefs for contract modifications to replace old IBORs with alternative rates — changes necessary and economically equivalent to IBOR reform should not cause immediate income/expense recognition; hedge accounting reliefs continue most hedges but may require additional ineffectiveness; additional disclosure requirements about exposure and transition progress.
Foreign currencies — PSAK 10 & PSAK 63 (hyperinflation)
• Two issues:
(1) translating foreign-currency transactions into an entity’s functional currency;
(2) translating financial statements of foreign operations into presentation currency.
• Initial recognition: use exchange rate at transaction date; monetary items retranslated at reporting date (recognise exchange differences in profit or loss).
• Non-monetary items: measured at historical cost use transaction date rate; if measured at FV, use rate at date fair value determined. ISAK 33 clarifies date of transaction for advance consideration.
• Translation of foreign operations: assets/liabilities at closing rate; income statement at transaction dates or average rate (if approximates); exchange differences recognised in OCI; hyperinflationary economies: restate financial statements to measuring unit at end of reporting period per PSAK 63 before translation; ISAK 19 guidance on applying restatement when hyperinflation is newly identified.
Revenue & government grants — PSAK 72 & PSAK 61
• PSAK 72 core principle: recognise revenue to depict transfer of goods/services to a customer at an amount reflecting consideration expected in exchange.
• Scope: contracts with customers except those in scope of other standards (leases, financial instruments, insurance).
• Five-step model:
(1) identify contract with a customer,
(2) identify separate performance obligations,
(3) determine transaction price (including variable consideration and time value of money),
(4) allocate transaction price to performance obligations (based on stand-alone selling prices),
(5) recognise revenue when/as performance obligations satisfied (control transfer — point in time or over time).
• Contract combination and modifications: combine contracts if negotiated together or interrelated; treat modifications as separate contract only if it adds a distinct performance obligation and price reflects stand-alone selling price.
• Variable consideration: include only if highly probable it will not cause significant revenue reversal; measured via probability-weighted or most-likely amount approach; judgment indicators for unpredictability listed.
• Allocation: allocate transaction price using observable stand-alone selling prices; use estimation techniques (cost plus margin, residual approach) when stand-alone price not observable; discounts/variable amounts may be allocated entirely to one or more obligations if conditions met.
• Satisfying performance obligations: transfer of control — indicators include unconditional payment obligation, legal title, physical possession, risks/rewards transfer, customer acceptance; when over time, measure progress by input or output methods that best depict transfer.
• Contract costs: capitalise incremental costs of obtaining a contract if recoverable (expense if amortisation period <1 year or other PSAK applies); fulfilment costs capitalised only when criteria met.
• Licensing: determine whether licence is distinct; distinguish right to access IP (recognise over time) vs right to use IP (recognise at point in time); special rules for sales/usage-based royalties for licences.
• Principal vs agent: assess whether entity controls specified good or service before transfer; indicators provided; assessment by specified good/service.
• Government grants — PSAK 61: grants recognised when reasonable assurance of compliance and receipt; grants related to income recognised systematically to match related costs (offset expense or presented as income); grants related to assets either reduce carrying amount of asset or presented as deferred income and recognised over asset useful life; ISAK 18 for assistance not related to operating activities.
Operating segments — PSAK 5
• Management approach: segments identified based on internal reports regularly used by CODM to allocate resources and assess performance.
• Which entities require disclosure: entities with public equity/debt, or seeking listing; voluntary disclosure permitted but labelled appropriately.
• Reportable segments: provide profit/loss measure (as per CODM) and assets/liabilities if regularly provided to CODM; aggregation allowed where economic characteristics are similar — significant judgment required.
• Required segment disclosures: revenue by customer groups; revenue by geography; dependence on major customers; reconciliation of segment totals to primary financial statements for revenue, profit/loss and other material items.
Employee benefits — PSAK 24
• Scope: short-term benefits (wages, bonuses, paid leave), post-employment benefits, other long-term benefits, termination benefits.
• Defined contribution plans: employer expense = contribution payable for period; no actuarial assumptions necessary.
• Defined benefit plans: complex measurement requiring actuarial valuations for demographic & financial assumptions (turnover, mortality, discount rate, salary growth, medical costs); recognised on balance sheet as net defined benefit liability or asset (difference between defined benefit obligation and plan assets subject to asset ceiling rules).
• Measurement: defined benefit obligation discounted using high-quality corporate bond rates (or government bond rate if appropriate) in currency of liabilities; plan assets measured at fair value.
• Components of benefit expense: service cost (current service cost, past service cost, gain/loss on settlement) and net interest (unwinding of discount & theoretical return on plan assets).
• Remeasurements: (actuarial gains/losses, return on plan assets excluding amounts in net interest, effect of asset ceiling changes) recognised in OCI.
• Past service cost: recognised when plan amendment/curtailment occurs; settlement gains/losses in profit/loss when settlement occurs.
• ISAK 15: guidance on asset ceiling, minimum funding requirements and interaction.
Share-based payment — PSAK 53
• Scope: transactions where goods/services received in exchange for equity instruments or cash amounts based on equity instrument price (includes employee share schemes and non-employee arrangements).
• Measurement: equity-settled measured at grant-date fair value of instruments for employee services (or fair value of goods/services for non-employees if reliably measurable); cash-settled measured at fair value of liability and re-measured each reporting date with changes in profit/loss.
• Equity-settled: no subsequent remeasurement after grant date; expense recognised over vesting period; extensive disclosures required.
• Complex valuation: option pricing models and vesting conditions require management judgment and inputs; results reduce reported profit in many users.
Taxation — PSAK 46 (current & deferred tax) + ISAKs
• Current tax: liability/asset measured at amounts expected to be paid/recovered using enacted or substantively enacted tax rates/laws at balance sheet date; recognise liability/asset for current & prior periods to extent unpaid/overpaid.
• Deferred tax: recognise for temporary differences between tax base and carrying amounts using enacted/substantively enacted tax rates expected when asset realised/liability settled; exceptions: initial recognition of goodwill (DTL only), initial recognition of asset/liability in non-business-combination transaction affecting neither accounting nor taxable profit, and certain investment in subsidiaries/associates when criteria met.
• Measurement: reflects manner entity expects to recover/settle asset/liability (use of sale vs use assessment).
• Deferred tax assets: recognised only to extent probable that taxable profit will be available to utilise deductible temporary differences/unused tax losses; discounting of deferred tax not permitted.
• Presentation: tax in profit/loss unless arises from business combination or items recognised outside profit/loss (OCI or equity) — then present tax in same component.
• ISAK 34: guidance on uncertainty over income tax treatments (recognition & measurement when tax treatment may not be accepted by authority).
• ISAK 20: guidance on accounting for tax consequences of change in tax status of entity or shareholders.
Earnings per share — PSAK 56
• Purpose: provide standardised measure of profitability per ordinary share to investors/analysts.
• Required entities: those with publicly traded or in process of issuing ordinary shares; disclose basic & diluted EPS with equal prominence.
• Basic EPS: profit attributable to ordinary shareholders divided by weighted average number of ordinary shares outstanding (adjust for bonuses/rights issues).
• Diluted EPS: adjust profit and weighted average shares to reflect conversion of dilutive potential ordinary shares (convertibles, options, employee share plans) using prescribed methods; present continuing & total operations; disclose separate EPS for discontinued operations.
(Cross-cutting & practical matters highlighted)
• Judgement & disclosure emphasis: many standards require significant management judgment, assumptions and forward-looking information — extensive disclosures needed for users to assess estimates, risks and management strategies.
• Transition & early adoption: new standards often permit early application; transitional provisions must be followed; disclose known impacts of not-yet effective standards.
• Interaction between standards: several ISAKs clarify interaction between standards (e.g., ISAK 32 on hierarchy, ISAK 9 on decommissioning liabilities & PPE, ISAK 36 on land rights vs leases).
• Documentation & controls: robust policies, documentation of judgements, accounting manuals and reconciliations essential to support positions and disclosures, particularly for financial instruments, revenue, tax uncertainties, employee benefits and impairment assessments.
Key references (standards & interpretations covered in this section)
• Standards referenced: PSAK 1 Presentation of financial statements; PSAK 25 Accounting policies, estimates & errors; PSAK 46 Income taxes; PSAK 48 Impairment of assets; PSAK 50 Presentation (debt/equity); PSAK 53 Share-based payment; PSAK 55/60 (legacy) & PSAK 71 Financial instruments; PSAK 63 Hyperinflation; PSAK 68 Fair value measurement; PSAK 72 Revenue; PSAK 73 Leases (interaction); PSAK 24 Employee benefits; PSAK 56 EPS; PSAK 61 Government grants; PSAK 74 Insurance contracts (transition); and relevant ISAKs (9,11,13,14,15,16,17,18,19,20,28,29,30,31,32,33,34,35,36).
Practical implications for preparers
• Planning & transition: ensure consistent application of Framework and PSAKs; maintain clear transition plans for new standards (notably PSAK 74 and financial instruments/impairment implications).
• Models & documentation: strengthen judgment documentation, models and sensitivity analyses for ECL, impairment, revenue estimates, actuarial valuations and fair value measurements.
• Disclosures: improve disclosures: risk management, fair value hierarchy, ECL explanations, revenue judgments, tax uncertainties and significant accounting policy choices.
• Coordination: coordinate finance, tax, actuarial and legal inputs for complex areas (insurance, employee benefits, tax uncertainty, IBOR reform).
• Audit readiness: maintain audit-ready packs for material areas: models, estimates, reconciliations, and transition workpapers.

Balance sheet and related notes — Comprehensive single-column summary (PSAK Pocket Guide 2023)
Overview & purpose
This section summarises recognition, measurement, presentation and disclosure requirements for assets, liabilities, equity and related notes that appear in the statement of financial position (balance sheet) and its supporting notes. It covers tangible and intangible non-current assets, investment property, impairment, leases, inventories, provisions and contingencies, events after the reporting period, and equity (share capital and reserves). The guidance explains initial recognition, subsequent measurement (cost, revaluation, fair value), depreciation/amortisation, impairment testing, derecognition, related journal examples, and required disclosures to enable users to assess the entity’s financial position and future cash flows.
Intangible assets — PSAK 19 (recognition, measurement, subsequent accounting)
Definition & scope
Identifiable non-monetary assets without physical substance (separable or arising from contractual/legal rights).
Initial recognition
• Separately acquired: recognised at cost (purchase price + directly attributable costs including import duties and non-refundable taxes).
• Internally generated: split into research (expense) and development (capitalise only if criteria met). No capitalisation of research costs. Development costs capitalised when the entity can demonstrate technical feasibility, intention to complete, ability to use/sell, probable future economic benefits, availability of resources and reliable measurement of expenditure.
Internally generated restrictions
Certain internally generated items (brands, mastheads, customer lists, goodwill from internal growth) are not recognised. ISAK 14 provides guidance for website development costs.
Acquired in a business combination
Intangible assets that meet the identifiability criteria are recognised separately at fair value on acquisition (measurement criteria automatically satisfied in a business combination).
Subsequent measurement & amortisation
• Finite life: amortise on a systematic basis over useful life; review for indicators of impairment when appropriate.
• Indefinite life: not amortised; test for impairment annually and whenever indicators arise.
• Useful life, amortisation method, residual value are reviewed at least annually.
Derecognition
On disposal or when no future economic benefits are expected; recognise gain or loss in profit or loss.
Disclosure requirements
Accounting policy, amortisation methods, useful lives (or indefinite life), carrying amounts by class, reconciliation of movements, impairment losses and reversals. For assets from business combinations, disclose additional information about assumptions used in valuation.
Property, plant and equipment (PPE) — PSAK 16
Definition & recognition
PPE recognised when cost can be reliably measured and future economic benefits probable. Cost includes purchase price (net of discounts), directly attributable costs to bring asset to working condition, import duties and non-refundable taxes, and estimated dismantling/restoration costs (where recognised as a provision).
Measurement at recognition
Initial measurement at cost. Subsequent measurement choice: cost model (cost less accumulated depreciation and impairment) or revaluation model (fair value at revaluation date less subsequent depreciation and impairment). Apply consistently to a class of assets.
Subsequent expenditure
• Capitalise subsequent expenditure only if it meets recognition criteria (future economic benefits > cost).
• Parts with different useful lives should be accounted for separately (component accounting).
• Replaced parts: derecognise carrying amount of replaced part and capitalise new part if criteria met.
Depreciation
Depreciable amount = cost (or revalued amount) less residual value. Depreciate on systematic basis over useful life. Depreciation methods and useful lives reviewed regularly. Borrowing costs directly attributable to acquisition/construction of a qualifying asset are capitalised per PSAK 26.
Decommissioning & land rights
• Provision for dismantling/restoration: recognise present value as liability and capitalise to PPE; unwind discount as finance cost. ISAK 9 clarifies changes in such provisions.
• Land rights in Indonesia: account as PPE or lease depending on substance (ISAK 36 guides interaction with PSAK 73).
Derecognition & disposals
On disposal or when no future benefits expected: derecognise and recognise gain/loss = proceeds less carrying amount in profit or loss.
Practical points & examples
• Proceeds from items produced while preparing asset for intended use are recognised in profit or loss; cost of testing not reduced by proceeds.
• Journal examples:
– Acquire PPE: Dr PPE (cost) / Cr Bank or AP.
– Capitalise dismantling estimate: Dr PPE / Cr Provision for dismantling.
– Depreciation (period): Dr Depreciation expense / Cr Accumulated depreciation.
– Disposal: Dr Bank (proceeds) / Dr Accum. depreciation / Cr PPE; recognise gain/loss.
Disclosure requirements
Major classes of PPE, measurement basis, depreciation methods and rates, reconciliation of carrying amounts, restrictions, commitments for purchase, revaluation details (date, valuer, methods) if revaluation model used.
Investment property — PSAK 13
Definition & scope
Property (land or building) held to earn rentals and/or for capital appreciation. Includes property under construction or development if held to earn rentals/capital gain. Excludes owner-occupied property (PPE) and inventory held for sale in ordinary course of business.
Initial & subsequent measurement
Initial at cost. Subsequent measurement policy choice: fair value model (preferred for many) or cost model. If fair value model chosen, measure to fair value each reporting period and recognise changes in profit or loss. If cost model chosen, measure at cost less accumulated depreciation and impairment; disclose fair value in notes.
Properties under construction
If fair value can be measured reliably, investment property under construction may be measured at fair value; otherwise cost model applies.
Transfers
Transfer to/from investment property permitted only when there is an evidence of change in use (a change in intention alone is insufficient). ISAK 31 interprets scope and physical structure aspects.
Disclosure
Measurement basis, restrictions, rental income, direct operating expenses, fair value gains/losses (if fair value model), reconciliations, commitments. For cost model, disclose fair value in notes.
Impairment of assets — PSAK 48
Objective & recoverable amount
Ensure assets are not carried above recoverable amount. Recoverable amount = higher of fair value less costs of disposal and value in use (present value of expected future cash flows from asset in its current use).
When to test
Test whenever indicators of impairment exist. Assets with indefinite useful lives, intangible assets not yet available for use, and goodwill must be tested annually whether or not indicators exist.
Cash-generating units (CGUs)
Where individual asset recoverable amount cannot be estimated, allocate to smallest identifiable CGU that generates largely independent inflows. Goodwill allocated to CGUs or groups of CGUs expected to benefit from synergies. Maximum aggregation for goodwill testing is the lowest level of operating segment before aggregation.
Impairment loss allocation & recognition
Impairment loss first reduces any goodwill allocated to the CGU, then pro rata to other assets in the CGU on a consistent basis. Recognise loss in profit or loss. Reversals of impairment (except for goodwill) recognised if indicators show recoverable amount increased — reversal limited to carrying amount that would have been determined (net of depreciation) had no impairment been recognised.
Indicators
External: market, economic, technological, increases in market interest rates. Internal: obsolescence, underperformance vs budgets, physical damage.
Disclosure
Events and circumstances leading to impairment, amount recognised or reversed, assets affected, valuation methods and key assumptions for value in use, discount rates, sensitivity analyses.
Lease accounting — PSAK 73 (balance sheet effects)
Definition & lessee accounting
Lease = contract conveying right to use asset for period in exchange for consideration. Lessees recognise a right-of-use (ROU) asset and corresponding lease liability for almost all leases (except short-term and low-value exemptions).
Initial measurement (lessee)
• Lease liability: present value of lease payments not yet paid, discounted using rate implicit in lease (if determinable) or incremental borrowing rate.
• ROU asset: initial measurement = lease liability + lease payments made at or before commencement less incentives received + initial direct costs + restoration costs.
Subsequent measurement (lessee)
• Lease liability: measured using effective interest method; increase for interest and decrease for payments; remeasure on lease modifications.
• ROU asset: depreciated (usually on same basis as underlying asset) and tested for impairment (PSAK 48).
Income statement & cash flow classification
Lessee recognises interest on lease liability (finance cost) and depreciation on the ROU asset; cash payments split between principal (financing activities) and interest (operating or financing depending on policy).
Lessors
Classify leases as finance or operating depending on transfer of substantially all risks and rewards. Finance lessor derecognises underlying asset and recognises a receivable; operating lessor continues to recognise the asset and recognise lease income over term.
Subleases & sale and leaseback
• Sublease classification references the ROU asset; head lease short-term exemption leads to sublease classification as operating.
• Sale and leaseback: assess whether transfer qualifies as a sale under PSAK 72. If sale qualifies, seller-lessee measures ROU asset as proportion of previous carrying amount retained and recognises only portion of gain relating to rights transferred; if not a sale, treat transaction as financing.
Modifications
Specific rules for lessee and lessor accounting for lease modifications.
Disclosure
Maturity analysis, ROU asset carrying amounts, lease liabilities, expense items for short-term and low-value leases, variable payments, practical expedients used, significant judgements and discount rates.
Inventories — PSAK 14
Definition & measurement
Inventories measured at the lower of cost and net realisable value (NRV). Cost includes purchase price, import duties, non-refundable taxes, transport, handling and other directly attributable costs less discounts and rebates. NRV = estimated selling price in the ordinary course of business less estimated costs of completion and selling expenses.
Cost formulas & allocation
• For interchangeable items or items not specifically identifiable, use FIFO or weighted average cost formula. LIFO is prohibited.
• Items that are not interchangeable or segregated for specific contracts measured on an individual item basis.
Specific measurement issues
• Costs of conversion include fixed and variable production overheads allocated on a systematic basis. Abnormal amounts of wasted materials, labour or production overheads are expensed.
• For finished goods and work in progress, capitalise costs consistently and avoid double counting costs covered by other standards.
Write-down & reversal
• Write down to NRV when cost exceeds NRV; reversal of write-downs recognised when NRV recovers (limited to original cost basis).
Disclosure requirements
Accounting policy, carrying amounts by classification (raw materials, WIP, finished goods), cost formulas used, amount of any write-downs recognised or reversed, and carrying amount pledged as security.
Provisions, contingent liabilities and contingent assets — PSAK 57
Definitions
• Provision: present obligation from past events where an outflow of resources is probable and amount can be estimated reliably (liability of uncertain timing or amount).
• Contingent liability: possible obligation depending on uncertain future events or present obligation not recognised because outflow not probable or amount unreliably measurable — disclosed unless remote.
• Contingent asset: possible asset from past events not recognised but disclosed if inflow probable.
Recognition criteria for provisions
Recognise provision when: (a) present obligation exists from past obligating event; (b) probable outflow of resources (more likely than not); and (c) reliable estimate of amount. Do not recognise provisions for future operating losses alone.
Measurement
Best estimate of expenditure required to settle obligation; where time value of money material, discount the expected cash flows using a pre-tax rate reflecting current market assessments of the time value of money and risks specific to liability. Unwinding of discount recognised as finance cost.
Constructive obligations & restructuring
Constructive obligation arises from established practice or published plan that creates a valid expectation. Restructuring provision recognised only when present obligation exists (detailed formal plan and reliance by those affected). Include only direct expenditures necessarily entailed by restructuring. Expected gains on sale not included.
Reimbursements
Where reimbursement is virtually certain, recognise reimbursement as separate asset; present net or gross depending on accounting policy; ensure reimbursement does not exceed the related provision.
Levies & decommissioning
• ISAK 30 addresses levies where obligating event is the activity that triggers payment.
• ISAK 9 clarifies changes in decommissioning provisions recognised in PPE carrying amounts and presentation of unwinding of discount.
Onerous contracts
Recognise provision when unavoidable costs of meeting contract exceed expected economic benefits; recognise impairment on dedicated assets before recognising onerous contract provision.
Disclosure requirements
Nature of obligation, expected timing, uncertainties, assumptions and sensitivities, movements in provisions reconciliation (opening to closing), and for contingent liabilities (describe nature and estimate or range of possible outcomes unless remote).
Events after the reporting period — PSAK 8
Objective & classification
Distinguish between adjusting events (evidence of conditions existing at balance sheet date) and non-adjusting events (conditions arising after balance sheet date). Adjust financial statements only for adjusting events; disclose non-adjusting events if material.
Examples
• Adjusting: settlement of a court case providing evidence that the entity had a present obligation at balance sheet date; determining consideration for assets sold before year-end.
• Non-adjusting: major business combinations, destruction of major plant by fire after year-end, issue of shares after year-end — disclose nature and estimate of financial effect.
Going concern
If events after the reporting period indicate the going concern basis is not appropriate for whole entity, financial statements must reflect this. If non-adjusting events indicate going concern is inappropriate, disclose accordingly.
Dividends
Dividends declared or proposed after balance sheet date are not recognised as liabilities at balance sheet date; disclose details.
Financial commitments & significant post-period events
Disclose significant commitments or contingent liabilities arising after period end; include date financial statements authorised for issue and identity of authorising persons.
Held for sale & discontinued operations — PSAK 58 (balance sheet implications)
Classification & criteria
Non-current assets or disposal groups classified as held for sale when available for immediate sale in present condition and sale highly probable (management committed, active marketing at reasonable price, expected completion within 12 months and unlikely to change). Assets held for distribution to owners similarly treated when distribution is highly probable.
Measurement & presentation
Assets (or disposal groups) classified as held for sale measured at the lower of carrying amount and fair value less costs to sell. They are not depreciated or amortised while classified as held for sale. Present separately in the balance sheet: assets held for sale and liabilities in disposal group separately disclosed.
Discontinued operations
A component classified as discontinued when it is a separate major line of business or geographical area, part of single coordinated plan to dispose of a major line/geography, or acquired exclusively for resale. Present discontinued operations separately in the income statement and restate comparative income statement items. Disclose post-tax profit or loss of discontinued operations and gain/loss on disposal.
Share capital, reserves and equity presentation (PSAK 50 & PSAK 1 cross-references)
Definition & composition
Equity is residual interest in assets after deducting liabilities. Components: issued capital, share premium/Additional Paid-in Capital (APIC), retained earnings, revaluation reserves, hedging reserves, foreign currency translation reserve, other statutory reserves and non-controlling interests in consolidated statements.
Equity instruments & classification
Classification as equity or liability depends on substance: instruments that impose on the issuer an obligation to transfer cash or financial assets are liabilities. Features such as redemption, mandatory dividends, or puttable rights may require liability classification unless specific criteria for equity classification are met. PSAK 50 provides detailed tests and examples (including puttable instruments and components representing option to convert).
Treasury shares
Deducted from equity. No gain or loss recognised in profit or loss on purchase, resale, issue or cancellation of an entity’s own equity instruments; changes go to equity.
Non-controlling interests (NCI)
Presented as a component of equity separately from parent owners’ equity. NCI measured either at fair value or at proportionate share of identifiable net assets (policy choice at acquisition).
Reserves & movements
Statement of changes in equity shows total comprehensive income attributable to owners and NCI, effects of retrospective application/restatement, transactions with owners, dividends and reconciliations for each component of equity. Disclose dividends per share and total dividends recognised as distributions.
Disclosures (PSAK 1)
Detailed reconciliation for each equity component, nature and purpose of reserves, restrictions on distributions, number and class of shares authorised/issued/outstanding and details of treasury shares. Also capital management policies and objectives.
Share capital & specific PSAK interactions (tax amnesty, business combinations, equity method)
Tax amnesty & APIC (PSAK 70)
Specific optional approach for assets/liabilities recognised under tax amnesty where difference between tax amnesty recognised amounts and carrying values may be recorded in APIC and not recycled to profit or loss. Redemption money (tax paid) charged to profit or loss in the period of approval; outstanding balances related to tax disputes written off when tax amnesty approval received. Subsequent measurement follows IFAS. (PSAK 70 summary referenced in guide).
Business combinations under common control (PSAK 38)
These are accounted for using pooling (predecessor) method — amounts presented in equity (APIC adjustments) rather than re-measurement to fair value; prior period results restated. The difference between transfer price and carrying value recorded in equity, not recycled to profit or loss.

Consolidated and Separate Financial Statements
Purpose & objective
1) Explain the principles, scope and accounting for consolidated financial statements when an entity controls one or more other entities.
2) Explain the principles, scope and accounting for separate financial statements when an entity presents investments in subsidiaries, associates and joint ventures on an entity-by-entity basis.
3) Ensure users understand which entities must be consolidated and how control is determined.
4) Explain how business combinations are measured and presented.
5) Explain how interests in associates and joint ventures are accounted for using the equity method.
6) Explain how disposals, assets held for sale and discontinued operations are treated.
7) Promote faithful representation of group performance, financial position and cash flows.
8) Promote consistent accounting for acquisitions and disposals.
9) Promote transparent disclosure of relationships with other entities.
Scope & when the standards apply
PSAK 65 applies when an entity controls one or more entities and therefore must present consolidated financial statements. PSAK 4 governs accounting in separate financial statements (presented as supplementary information to consolidated FS where elected). PSAK 22 (and PSAK 38 for entities under common control) apply to business combinations. PSAK 58 applies to non-current assets held for sale and discontinued operations. PSAK 15 covers equity accounting for associates and joint ventures. PSAK 67 contains the required disclosures about interests in other entities.
Key definitions & core principles
1) Control (single definition): control exists when an investor has (a) power over the investee, (b) exposure to, or rights to, variable returns from its involvement with the investee, and (c) the ability to use its power to affect those returns. Consolidation is required only when all three elements are present.
2) Associate: an entity over which the investor has significant influence but not control or joint control (presumed if ≥20% voting power; presumed not if <20%).
3) Joint venture / joint operation: a joint arrangement where parties have joint control; classification depends on the rights to net assets (joint venture) or rights to assets and obligations for liabilities (joint operation).
4) Disposal group / held for sale: a group of assets to be disposed together and related liabilities; classification criteria and measurement are specified under PSAK 58.
Assessing control — practical tests and considerations
Control assessment is principle-based and requires consideration of facts and circumstances:
1) the purpose and design of the investee;
2) which activities are the relevant activities that significantly affect returns and whether the investor has rights to direct them;
3) whether the investor’s rights are substantive (protective rights normally do not constitute control);
4) the effect of voting rights and potential voting rights (including options, convertible instruments);
5) whether the investor acts as principal or agent; and
6) relationships between investors and whether rights over specified assets alone indicate control.
There are no bright-line tests; judgement is required.
Consolidation procedures & accounting mechanics (PSAK 65)
• Consolidated financial statements present the financial position, performance and cash flows of the group as a single economic entity under the control of the parent.
• Identify the parent (acquirer), identify subsidiaries and consolidate using uniform accounting policies and consistent reporting dates and periods (adjust when necessary).
• Eliminate intragroup balances, intragroup transactions and unrealised profits/losses on intragroup transactions in full, unless offset is required by a standard.
• When a parent owns less than 100%, present non-controlling interest (NCI) within equity separately from owners of the parent.
Non-controlling interests (NCI)
When the parent acquires less than 100% of an acquiree, NCI represents the equity in a subsidiary not attributable to the parent.
On acquisition the acquirer may measure NCI either at:
1) fair value (full goodwill method); or
2) the NCI’s proportionate share of identifiable net assets (proportionate method).
NCI is presented separately in equity and profit or loss and OCI are allocated between parent and NCI.
Disclosure – where to find it (PSAK 67)
PSAK 65 does not itself contain all disclosure requirements. Detailed disclosure requirements about interests in other entities (including the amounts and nature of subsidiaries, associates and joint arrangements, and summarised financial information) are set out in PSAK 67. Preparers should plan consolidation processes to capture information required by PSAK 67.
Investment entity exemption
Entities that meet the definition of an investment entity are exempt from consolidating particular subsidiaries; instead they measure those controlled investments at fair value through profit or loss (PSAK 71). This exemption recognises that for investment-entity business models, FVTPL measurement better reflects the economics.
Separate financial statements (PSAK 4)
Separate financial statements are the reporting entity’s financial statements presented on an individual-entity basis (often presented as supplementary information alongside consolidated statements). In separate financial statements, investments in subsidiaries, associates and joint ventures may be accounted for either at cost, in accordance with PSAK 71 (financial instruments), or using the equity method (where permitted). The chosen policy is applied consistently and disclosed.
Business combinations — acquisition method (PSAK 22)
All business combinations (except those under common control) are accounted for using the acquisition method, which requires:
1. Identify the acquirer.
2. Determine the acquisition date.
3. Recognise and measure identifiable assets acquired, liabilities assumed and any NCI — generally at fair value on the acquisition date (PSAK 68 guidance).
4. Recognise and measure consideration transferred — fair value of assets given, liabilities incurred and equity instruments issued. Discount deferred consideration if material.
5. Recognise goodwill or a bargain purchase gain — goodwill equals consideration transferred + NCI fair value + fair value of any previously held equity interest less the fair value of identifiable net assets. A bargain purchase results in an immediate gain recognised in profit or loss.
Consideration transferred & acquisition costs
Consideration may include cash, assets, liabilities incurred, and equity instruments (all at fair value). Deferred or contingent consideration is measured at fair value at acquisition (classification as liability or equity per PSAK 50). Acquisition-related costs (transaction costs) are expensed as incurred and not included in consideration. Amounts paid to settle pre-existing relationships or payments contingent on future employee services are treated separately.
Contingent consideration — classification & subsequent measurement
Contingent consideration recognised at acquisition date is classified as either:
1) a liability (or other financial instrument) — remeasured at fair value through profit or loss after initial recognition; or
2) part of equity — not remeasured.
Classification follows PSAK 50. Subsequent measurement and profit or loss effects depend on that classification.
Goodwill & impairment
Goodwill represents future economic benefits from assets not individually identified and recognised separately. Goodwill is not amortised; it is tested for impairment at least annually, or more frequently if indicators exist, at the cash-generating unit (CGU) level (or groups of CGUs) expected to benefit from the business combination. For goodwill impairment testing, the maximum aggregation permitted is the lowest level of operating segment (pre-aggregation) consistent with the acquisition reporting.
Business combinations between entities under common control (PSAK 38)
Combinations of entities under common control are outside the scope of the acquisition method in PSAK 22. PSAK 38 requires the pooling of interests method: the acquirer incorporates the acquiree’s assets, liabilities and results as if the entities had always been combined. Prior periods are restated; assets and liabilities are not fair-valued in principle; the difference between consideration and carrying value is recorded in equity (often APIC), not recycled to profit or loss. Judgement is required and note disclosure of accounting policy is important.
Recognition of provisions & contingent liabilities on acquisition
Liabilities and contingent liabilities of the acquiree are recognised at fair value at the acquisition date if they meet recognition criteria (PSAK 57). For contingent liabilities not recognised under other standards, apply PSAK 57 or ISAK interpretations as relevant.
Disposal of subsidiaries, held for sale and discontinued operations (PSAK 58)
• A non-current asset (or disposal group) is classified as held for sale when it is available for immediate sale and its sale is highly probable (management committed, actively marketed, expected to complete within 12 months, and unlikely to be withdrawn).
• Held-for-sale assets (or disposal groups) are measured at the lower of carrying amount and fair value less costs to sell; they are not depreciated or amortised; presented separately on the face of the balance sheet.
• A discontinued operation is a component of an entity that has been disposed of or classified as held for sale and represents a separate major line of business, major geographical area, part of a single coordinated disposal plan, or a subsidiary acquired exclusively for resale. Results of discontinued operations are presented separately in the income statement and comparatives are restated.
• The gain or loss on disposal equals proceeds less carrying amounts of net assets (including attributable goodwill and amounts in OCI) at the date control passes. The consolidated income statement includes results up to date of disposal.
Equity method — investments in associates & joint ventures (PSAK 15)
• Significant influence is the power to participate in financial and operating policy decisions (not control); the 20% voting presumption is a starting point but may be rebutted.
• Under the equity method an investment is initially recognised at cost and adjusted thereafter for the investor’s share of profit or loss and other comprehensive income of the investee, and reduced for dividends received. Changes in the investee’s equity post-acquisition affect the carrying amount.
• If the investor’s share of losses exceeds the carrying amount of the investment (including long-term interests that form part of the net investment), the carrying amount is reduced to nil and additional losses are recognised only to the extent the investor has incurred obligations or provided guarantees; otherwise further losses are not recognised.
• Associates and joint ventures are tested for impairment in accordance with PSAK 48 and accounted for as single assets for impairment purposes where required.
Joint arrangements (PSAK 66) — classification & accounting
• Joint control exists only where unanimous consent of the parties sharing control is required for decisions about the relevant activities.
• Joint arrangements are either joint operations (rights to assets and obligations for liabilities) or joint ventures (rights to net assets). Classification depends on the parties’ rights and obligations, not only the legal form.
• Accounting: joint operators recognise their share of assets, liabilities, revenues and expenses as appropriate; joint ventures are accounted for using the equity method. Where a separate vehicle exists, a deeper assessment of contractual terms and facts may be needed because legal form alone may be misleading.
Interaction with financial instruments, fair value and impairment standards
• Measurement of identifiable assets and liabilities on acquisition follows PSAK 68 (fair value measurement principles).
• Financial instruments held by an investor (including long-term interests that form part of the net investment in an associate or joint venture) are accounted for under PSAK 71 where the equity method is not applied to those instruments.
• Impairment testing of goodwill and investments follows PSAK 48; the equity-accounted investment is treated as a single asset for impairment if indicators exist.
Presentation, disclosures and audit considerations
• Consolidated financial statements must clearly present the group position, allocate profit or loss and OCI between parent owners and NCI, and provide reconciliations and required disclosures (PSAK 67).
• Auditors and preparers focus on control assessment documentation, consolidation adjustments (eliminations), fair value measurements used in acquisitions, the measurement and disclosure of NCI, treatment of contingent consideration, impairment testing of goodwill and equity-accounted investees, the accounting for business combinations under common control, and disclosures for discontinued operations.
• Entities should maintain processes to collect subsidiary/associate/joint venture financials, intercompany eliminations, and supporting valuations early in the acquisition/integration process to meet disclosure requirements.
Practical implications & judgement areas
Key judgement areas include: control assessment (power & substantive rights), classification of joint arrangements, determining the acquirer and acquisition date, fair value measurement of acquired assets and liabilities, classification and measurement of contingent consideration, measurement of NCI (fair value vs proportionate), whether a transaction qualifies as a business (vs asset acquisition), accounting for combinations under common control, impairment testing of goodwill and equity investments, and the determination of whether an operation is a discontinued operation. Each area requires detailed supporting documentation and strong governance.
Cross-references to related standards & interpretations
Relevant cross-references include PSAK 22/PSAK 38 (business combinations), PSAK 57 (provisions and contingent liabilities), PSAK 68 (fair value measurement), PSAK 71 (financial instruments), PSAK 48 (impairment), PSAK 58 (non-current assets held for sale and discontinued operations), PSAK 15 (equity accounting), PSAK 66 (joint arrangements) and PSAK 67 (disclosure of interests in other entities). ISAKs and interpretations provide additional application guidance for complex situations (for example, accounting for levies, decommissioning obligations, and service concession disclosures where relevant).
Checklist — essential controls & documentation for preparers
• Document control assessments (rights, voting, relevant activities and agent/principal analysis).
• Maintain acquisition files: acquirer identification, acquisition date, purchase price allocation, fair value workings, contingent consideration terms, acquisition-related costs, and NCI measurement basis.
• Track intragroup eliminations and reconciliations (balances, transactions and unrealised profits).
• Preserve impairment assessments: CGU allocation, cash-flow models, key assumptions and sensitivities.
• Prepare PSAK 67 disclosure pack: list of subsidiaries/associates/joint arrangements, summarised financial information, and reconciliations to the consolidated numbers.
• For disposals: evidence of ‘highly probable’ sale, marketing, approvals, measurement to lower of carrying amount and fair value less costs to sell, and discontinued operation restatements where applicable.
Summary reminder
Consolidation and separate financial statement accounting are judgment-intensive areas that require careful control assessments, rigorous application of the acquisition method (or pooling for common control), correct measurement and disclosure of NCI, consistent treatment of investments in separate financial statements, correct classification and measurement of disposals/held-for-sale and robust impairment testing. Preparers must align accounting policies, maintain audit-ready acquisition and consolidation files, and disclose required information under PSAK 67.

Related-party disclosures — PSAK 7
Objective & rationale: Provide users with information about the existence of related parties and the effect of related-party relationships and transactions on the financial statements so users can assess possible risks, bias or transactions not concluded on an arm’s-length basis.
Identification & scope: identify parents, subsidiaries, associates, joint ventures, fellow subsidiaries, key management personnel (KMP), KMP close family members, post-employment plans, entities under common control and entities providing KMP services. Consider both direct and indirect relationships (e.g., holdings via intermediaries).
Required disclosures: nature of relationships; balances at period end (receivables, payables, loans, guarantees, commitments) with comparative amounts; nature/amount of transactions (sales, purchases, transfers, leases, financial arrangements) with terms and conditions (rates, security, repayment terms); KMP compensation by category (short-term, post-employment, other long-term, termination benefits, share-based payments); provisions for doubtful related-party receivables and specific impairment notes.
Measurement & recognition guidance: apply the same measurement bases as for comparable transactions with unrelated parties unless special treatment is mandated by another PSAK. For intragroup elimination in consolidated statements, follow consolidation rules (PSAK 65) but disclose externally the nature and extent of intragroup transactions where relevant to users of separate financial statements.
Practical control points: maintain up-to-date related-party register; require disclosures in contracts of any potential related-party status; pre-approval and recording processes; reconciliation of related-party balances monthly; review of related-party pricing and terms by independent governance (audit committee).
Common errors & red flags: omitted related parties, understatement of balances, unrecorded guarantees, inconsistent disclosure formats, transactions presented without economic substance. Audit focus: completeness tests, board minutes, legal confirmations, bank confirmations and third-party confirmations.
Cash flow statements — PSAK 2
Objective & rationale: show sources and uses of cash and cash equivalents, enabling assessment of liquidity, solvency, cash generation and stewardship of cash resources; provide reconciliation between opening and closing cash/equivalents.
Structure & content: operating, investing and financing activities. Management may present operating cash flows using direct or indirect method (indirect method common) — disclose which is used and the reasons if significant.
Classification principles:
• Operating — receipts from customers, payments to suppliers/employees, interest/dividends received/paid (classification choice disclosed), taxes paid (operating unless attributable to investing/financing activities).
• Investing — acquisition/disposal of PPE, intangible assets, subsidiaries (net of cash acquired), long-term loans, and investments not qualifying as cash equivalents.
• Financing — proceeds/repayments of borrowings, equity contributions, distributions (dividends) and repayments of lease liabilities principal portion (interest element classification per entity policy).
Cash equivalents & restricted cash: define and disclose policy; disclose balances not available for general use (collateral, escrow) and present separately or disclose in notes.
Non-cash transactions & disclosures: describe material investing/financing non-cash transactions (e.g., asset purchases via finance leases, share issues for acquisition) either in notes or as supplementary schedules.
Foreign currency & translation: cash flows arising from foreign currency transactions translated at exchange rates at the dates of cash flow or using average rates if approximates actual rates; disclose effect of exchange rate changes on cash/equivalents (reconciliation table).
Controls & reconciliations: reconcile cash flow to GL and bank statements; maintain supporting schedules for cash movements by activity; document allocation rules for mixed-purpose receipts and payments; ensure cut-off controls for cash receipts and payments around period ends.
Presentation examples & journal mapping: provide sample journal-to-cash flow mapping (e.g., GL cash receipts → operating cash receipts; purchase of PPE → investing outflow; dividend received → investing or operating per policy).
Audit expectations: reconcile bank confirmations, bank reconciliations, cash movement schedules, and ensure non-cash transactions are adequately disclosed.
Interim financial reporting — PSAK 3
Objective & purpose: provide timely, relevant information for decision-making during the reporting year while preserving comparability with annual financial statements.
Required content (minimum): condensed statement of financial position as at interim period end (and comparative year-end), condensed statement(s) of profit or loss and other comprehensive income for the interim period and year-to-date, condensed statement of changes in equity (YTD), condensed cash flow statement (YTD) and selected explanatory notes addressing significant events and transactions during the interim period.
Recognition & measurement: apply same accounting policies as annual financial statements; use year-to-date accumulation for income, and present comparative interim periods for the prior year; use consistent estimates with annual reporting except where new information requires estimates to be updated.
Materiality in interim reporting: assess materiality in the context of the interim period; present items that are material to understanding changes in financial position; do not rely on immateriality because amounts are small relative to annual figures if they are individually significant to interim users.
Disclosure focus: significant changes in estimates, tax effects for interim periods (use estimated annual effective tax rate where necessary), seasonality or cyclical nature of operations, changes in financing or covenant waivers, events occurring after interim date but before interim report issuance, and any correction of prior-period errors discovered in the interim period.
Presentation choices & practical guidance: optional to present fuller explanatory notes if considered necessary; interim statements may be condensed but must not omit material information; include reconciliation where condensed disclosures omit detail found in annual statements; ensure consistent comparative periods.
Controls & governance: interim close calendar, cut-offs aligned with monthly close, interim disclosure checklist, senior approval of interim estimates and material items, audit committee review where required. Auditor involvement varies: review or limited-scope assurance engagements may be required by regulators or lenders.
Service concession arrangements — ISAK 16 & ISAK 22
Objective & rationale: clarify accounting for operators in public-private infrastructure arrangements where grantor retains control of the service and significant residual interest; ensure consistent classification between financial asset and intangible asset models and appropriate disclosures.
Key identification steps: determine whether the grantor controls/ regulates services and residual interest; identify whether operator has an unconditional contractual right to receive cash independent of usage (financial asset model) or a right to charge users (intangible asset model); evaluate contractual substance over legal form.
Accounting models & practical application:
• Financial asset model: recognise a financial asset measured at the present value of consideration receivable; revenue and finance income recognised using effective interest method; derecognition rules when consideration is received.
• Intangible asset model: recognise an intangible asset representing the right (license) to charge users; capitalise construction costs; amortise over concession term; recognise revenue per PSAK 72 for any construction-service bundled arrangements.
• Hybrid cases: some arrangements may combine both models (partial rights to cash plus user charges). Apply judgment and disclose basis.
Construction & operation phases: capitalise qualifying costs during construction where the intangible model applies; recognise revenue under construction contracts guidance (PSAK 72) if applicable; operating phase accounting depends on model selected.
Maintenance & upgrade obligations: recognise provisions for contractual obligations to maintain or restore infrastructure per PSAK 57; capitalise upgrades that meet recognition criteria; disclose nature of obligations and timing.
Disclosure requirements (ISAK 22): nature and terms of arrangement; significant obligations (maintenance, restoration); revenue recognition policy; contract term and renewal conditions; basis for choosing the accounting model; key estimates and risks; amounts recognised as financial assets/intangible assets and movements in period.
Practical controls: contract inventory, contractual clause register (residual interest, tariff regulation, renewal options), scenario models for traffic/tariff risks, separate ledgers for concession accounting, evidence of user-charge vs grant receivable flows. Obtain legal and tax input for public-sector nuances.
Retirement benefit plans — PSAK 18

(Note: employer accounting for pensions covered under PSAK 24; PSAK 18 focuses on plan reporting.)
Plan classification & primary reporting requirements:
• Defined contribution plans: report statement of net assets available for benefits, contributions receivable/paid, and reconciliation of changes in net assets; expense recognition limited to contributions payable.
• Defined benefit plans: report actuarial present value of promised retirement benefits (distinguish vested and non-vested), net assets available for benefits, deficit/surplus and resulting reconciliation; include actuarial assumptions, sensitivity analysis, maturity profile of obligations, and description of plan funding policy.
Measurement & actuarial requirements: present value calculations using best-estimate demographic and financial assumptions (discount rates based on high-quality corporate bond yields where available), disclosure of methodology, and remeasurement recognition rules (how actuarial gains/losses are treated in plan statements).
Plan asset measurement: fair value measurement at reporting date; disclosure of asset classes, investment strategy, concentration, and fair value hierarchy for plan assets; explain investment risks and policy for asset-liability matching.
Disclosures & transparency: funding policy, contributions expected, surplus/deficit treatment, allocation to plan members vs sponsoring employers, governance of plan assets, and any restrictions on asset availability. Include reconciliation of movements in benefit obligations and plan assets during the reporting period.
Governance & controls: independent actuarial valuations at prescribed intervals, investment policy documents, trustee minutes, segregation of duties between record-keeper and asset manager, and periodic oversight by actuary/audit committee. Audit focus: actuarial assumptions, valuation model, existence and valuation of plan assets, and related party transactions involving plan assets.
Tax amnesty assets and liabilities — PSAK 70
Objective & background: prescribe accounting for assets and liabilities recognised under a tax amnesty regime by aligning recognition with sound accounting principles while acknowledging special legal/tax treatment. Applies where a tax amnesty law provides recognition or transfer of tax-related items at prescribed values.
Initial recognition & measurement: recognise tax amnesty assets at the amounts declared/approved under amnesty documentation (deemed cost) when control is obtained and recognition criteria under applicable PSAK are satisfied. Recognise corresponding liability for redemption or payment obligations where applicable.
Equity treatment for adjustment differences: differences between carrying amounts and redemption (or tax amnesty payment) often recorded as additional paid-in capital (APIC) rather than profit or loss, per PSAK 70 guidance (subject to local rule specifics). Document the accounting policy chosen and rationale.
Subsequent measurement & disclosure: measure in accordance with underlying PSAKs relevant to asset class (PSAK 16, PSAK 19, PSAK 71 etc.). Disclose the nature of the amnesty, basis for measurement, reconciliation of amounts recognised, any remaining contingencies, tax liabilities settled or outstanding and the effect on deferred tax positions. Disclose treatment of prior tax losses, tax credits or uncertain tax positions settled by amnesty.
Practical considerations & controls: maintain amnesty application files and official approval notices, reconcile tax ledger to statutory filings and financial records, evidence of payments made, and approvals for equity adjustments. Coordinate with tax advisors and legal counsel. Audit focus: existence of official approval, correctness of amounts declared, treatment of residual tax liabilities and proper classification in equity vs profit or loss.
Presentation of not-for-profit oriented entity financial statements — ISAK 35
Objective & rationale: align PSAK presentation principles to the stewardship and accountability needs of not-for-profit oriented entities (NFPEs), reflecting resource stewardship, donor restrictions and program reporting rather than profit maximisation.
Key presentation differences & recommended formats:
• Statement of financial position: distinguish net assets with donor-imposed restrictions and net assets without such restrictions; present classes of funds (e.g., restricted, unrestricted, endowment) as applicable.
• Statement of activities / comprehensive income: present changes in net assets rather than profit; present income by source (donations, grants, fees) and expenses by program/activity and by nature where useful for donors.
• Statement of cash flows & changes in equity: include donor-restricted cash flows separately where significant; reconcile fund movements and activity-level changes.
Recognition & restrictions: recognise contributions/donations when probable and measurable; apply deferral for donor-restricted funds until conditions are met; disclose terms and nature of restrictions. Distinguish exchange vs non-exchange transactions and apply appropriate recognition timing.
Fund accounting & program reporting: where NFPEs use fund accounting, reconcile fund-level presentation to consolidated entity-level statements; disclose purpose, conditions and performance metrics at program level where material; provide narrative about program outputs and outcomes where relevant to financial stewardship.
Disclosure focus: donor restrictions, purpose and timing conditions, grant agreements, contingencies specific to grants (e.g., clawbacks), measures of fund utilisation, related-party funding and governance arrangements (trustees, fund custodians).
Controls & compliance: maintain grant management systems, donor agreement register, reporting packs for major donors, restrictions tracking, and internal controls for earmarked fund use. Audit focus: compliance with donor terms, separability of restricted funds, and whether presentation adequately reflects stewardship obligations.

Industry-specific topics — Comprehensive single-column summary (PSAK Pocket Guide 2023)
Purpose & objective

Scope & overall approach
Industry topics summarised here include: exploration and evaluation of mineral resources (PSAK 64), stripping costs in production phase of surface mines (ISAK 29), agriculture and biological assets (PSAK 69), and insurance contracts (PSAK 62 transitional / PSAK 74 new model). For each topic the Pocket Guide: (a) defines scope and exclusions; (b) sets recognition and initial measurement rules; (c) describes subsequent measurement choices (cost/revaluation/fair value); (d) explains impairment and unit-of-account / CGU issues; (e) highlights presentation, disclosures and transition requirements; and (f) lists practical implications, controls and audit evidence expectations. Cross-cutting standards that frequently interact include PSAK 16/19 (PPE/intangible), PSAK 48 (impairment), PSAK 57 (provisions), PSAK 68 (fair value), PSAK 71 (financial instruments), PSAK 72 (revenue) and PSAK 25 (accounting policies & errors).
PSAK 64 — Exploration for and evaluation of mineral resources
Objective & scope: Accounting for exploration for and evaluation (E&E) expenditures after legal rights to explore have been obtained and before technical feasibility and commercial viability of extracting resources are demonstrable. Excludes pre-acquisition and post-commercial production activities (which follow other PSAKs).
Recognition & initial measurement: Capitalise E&E expenditures that meet recognition criteria as exploration and evaluation assets and measure them at cost on initial recognition. Costs capitalised typically include acquisition of exploration rights, geological, geophysical, exploratory drilling, sampling, assay, feasibility studies (development-phase costs excluded), directly attributable staff and contractor costs, and other directly attributable costs. Costs written off in the research phase are not reinstated unless subsequent criteria are met.
Classification (tangible vs intangible): Determine whether an E&E asset is tangible (equipment, certain infrastructure) or intangible (licences, exploration rights); classify consistently and apply PSAK 16 or PSAK 19 accordingly for subsequent accounting.
Subsequent measurement options: After initial recognition, apply either cost model or revaluation model consistent with PSAK 16/19 depending on asset nature. Revaluation model requires active market evidence and robust valuations.
Reclassification to development/production: When technical feasibility & commercial viability are demonstrated (for example, proved reserves supported by feasibility studies and permits), reclassify E&E assets to development assets (PPE or intangible) — test for impairment before reclassification and transfer at carrying amount. Document the judgment and supporting technical evidence (reserves, feasibility, permits).
Impairment & recoverability: Test E&E assets for impairment when indicators exist and always before reclassification. Allocation to CGUs or groups of CGUs no larger than a reportable segment; impairment measurement follows PSAK 48 (recoverable amount = higher of fair value less costs to sell and value-in-use). Use technical inputs (reserve volumes, commodity price assumptions, recovery rates, mine life) in value-in-use models.
Decommissioning & environmental liabilities: Consider provisions for restoration and decommissioning under PSAK 57 if legal/constructive obligations exist; initial estimates may be capitalised to the extent they meet recognition criteria and form part of the asset cost (see ISAK 9 interaction).
Disclosure & audit evidence: Disclose accounting policies, E&E asset balances and movements (additions, transfers, impairment losses, disposals), amounts capitalised by category, judgements on reclassification, significant assumptions for impairment (commodity prices, discount rates), and investing cash flows. Auditors expect technical reports (geological/reserve studies), board approvals, permit evidence and reconciliations to tax/regulatory filings.
ISAK 29 — Stripping costs in the production phase of a surface mine
Objective & scope: Interpretation on accounting for waste removal (stripping) costs in the production phase of surface mining when such activity yields improved access to ore and provides future economic benefits beyond the current reporting period.
Core principle: Capitalise the portion of stripping costs that provides a future economic benefit by creating a separable asset (a “stripping activity asset”), and expense the remainder. Capitalised stripping activity assets are amortised over the life of the portion of the orebody accessed or on a systematic basis that reflects the benefit transfer (often unit-of-production).
Recognition criteria for capitalisation: Capitalise stripping costs only where: (1) the component of the orebody that becomes more accessible can be identified (unit of access), (2) the cost can be measured reliably, and (3) future economic benefits are probable. If conditions not met, expense as incurred.
Measurement & amortisation: Initial measurement = directly attributable stripping costs allocated to the stripping activity asset. Subsequent amortisation commonly uses unit-of-production (UoP) based on proven/probable reserves associated with the accessed portion or another systematic basis that reflects the consumption of benefit. Review for impairment under PSAK 48.
Presentation & disclosures: Disclose capitalised stripping assets, amortisation method, impairment testing approach and sensitivity of key assumptions (reserves, strip ratios). Provide movement schedules distinguishing capitalised vs expensed stripping costs and disclose accounting policy in notes.
Practical issues & examples: Determine the appropriate unit of account (pit benches, sectors), maintain operational records linking costs to specific mining areas, and document methodology. When a stripping campaign benefits multiple reporting periods, ensure allocation across periods and auditability of the basis.
PSAK 69 — Agriculture (biological assets & agricultural produce)
Objective & scope: Accounting for biological assets (living plants and animals) and agricultural produce harvested from them. Applies to all entities engaged in agricultural activity (management of biological transformation and harvest).
Key definitions: Biological asset = living animal or plant; Agricultural produce = harvested product of a biological asset (e.g., harvested crops, harvested livestock). Bearer plants = plants used solely to grow produce over multiple periods (e.g., orchard trees, vines, plantation trees).
Measurement—bearer plants vs other biological assets:
• Bearer plants: Measured either at cost (cost model) or at revalued amount less accumulated depreciation/impairment (revaluation model) under PSAK 16. Depreciate over useful life.
• Other biological assets & produce: Measured at fair value less costs to sell at each reporting date with changes recognised in profit or loss (reflects biological transformation). Agricultural produce at harvest is measured at fair value less costs to sell at the point of harvest and thereafter treated as inventory under PSAK 14.
Fair value measurement principles: Fair value per PSAK 68 — use prices in the principal market; if none, the most advantageous market. Use observable market prices where available; otherwise apply valuation techniques (discounted cash flows, market comparables). Exclude financing and re-establishment costs from fair value measurement. Document valuation techniques and inputs (market prices, yields, growth rates, discount rates).
Recognition timing & profit/loss: Changes in fair value (biological transformation) recognised in profit or loss as part of operating activities. Harvested produce first recognised at fair value less costs to sell (becomes inventory) and later measured per PSAK 14 (cost or NRV).
Impairment & bearer plants: Indefinite-life biological assets and bearer plants with indefinite lives are tested annually for impairment; bearer plants with finite lives are tested when indicators exist. Apply PSAK 48 impairment framework where required.
Disclosures & sensitivity: Disclose measurement bases, reconciliation of opening and closing carrying amounts, maturity profiles, nature of biological activities, valuation techniques and significant unobservable inputs, sensitivity to price/yield/discount rate changes. Auditors expect market data, harvest records, production schedules and third-party valuations where used.
Practical & tax considerations: Ensure separation of bearer vs consumable biological assets, document assumptions about harvest timing and yields, appraise the impact of subsidies, quotas or government schemes on valuation, and consider how biological changes affect revenue recognition and inventory costing.
Insurance contracts — PSAK 62 (interim) & PSAK 74 (new)
Context & timing: PSAK 74 (issued Nov 2020 in Indonesia) replaces legacy approaches (including PSAK 62) and introduces a comprehensive measurement model consistent with IFRS 17. PSAK 74 is effective for annual periods beginning on or after 1 Jan 2025 (early application permitted). Entities implementing should plan data, systems and controls changes.
PSAK 62 — interim approach: Allowed continuation of local insurance accounting policies where they met minimum criteria (including liability adequacy tests). Emphasis on disclosures that explain insurance contract amounts and risks. Useful in jurisdictions awaiting full PSAK 74 adoption or where legacy practices remain for non-insurers.
PSAK 74 — measurement overview (three approaches):
• General Model (Building Block Approach): Measure contracts at initial recognition as the sum of (a) fulfilment cash flows (best-estimate future cash flows + time value of money + risk adjustment) and (b) contractual service margin (CSM) representing unearned profit. Fulfilment cash flows remeasured each period; CSM adjusted for services rendered and certain changes in estimates and recognised over coverage period.
• Premium Allocation Approach (PAA): Simplified approach permitted for short-duration contracts (coverage period typically ≤ 1 year) or when it approximates the general model.
• Variable Fee Approach (VFA): Applies to contracts with direct participation features (insurer’s share of underlying returns). The insurer’s share of changes in underlying items is included in the CSM, altering profit timing and volatility treatment.
Key measurement elements explained:
• Fulfilment cash flows (FCF): unbiased current estimates of future cash flows related to the contract (inflows/outflows), plus an explicit risk adjustment for non-financial risk and discounting to reflect time value of money.
• Risk adjustment: Reflects compensation the entity requires for bearing the uncertainty about the amount and timing of cash flows.
• Contractual Service Margin (CSM): The unearned profit that is released to profit as the insurer provides coverage and services; CSM absorbs future favourable/unfavourable changes in estimates (subject to specific adjustments).
Presentation & profit recognition: Remeasurement of FCF is recognised through CSM (if related to future services) or in profit or loss (if relating to past services). P&L presentation distinguishes insurance service result, insurance finance result and investment components where relevant.
Transition approaches & practical expedients: Full retrospective application preferred but where impracticable, modified retrospective or fair value approaches permitted. Classification overlay option available where PSAK 71 is adopted simultaneously to reduce accounting mismatches in comparatives. Detailed transition disclosures are required.
Interaction with reinsurance, investment components & financial instruments: Reinsurance contract accounting may mirror underlying insurance measurement; financial assets backing insurance liabilities are measured under PSAK 71 and the entity must manage recognition/measurement mismatches (classification overlay helps). Investment-related service components are assessed carefully (PSAK 72 interactions).
Disclosures & governance: Extensive disclosures required: CSM reconciliations, FCF sensitivity, assumptions (discount rates, mortality/morbidity, lapse, expense assumptions), insurance risk exposures, age/term profiles, concentration risk, transition reconciliations, and the effects on key ratios (solvency, capital). Strong governance, model validation, actuarial sign-offs and audit trails are essential. Auditors require actuarial reports, model documentation, experience studies and control evidence.
Key measurement interactions across industry standards
• PSAK 68 (Fair value): central for biological assets (PSAK 69), for revaluation of certain E&E assets, and for some insurance assets and liabilities valuation techniques; establishes market-based measurement principles (principal/most advantageous market, valuation techniques, input hierarchy).
• PSAK 48 (Impairment): applies to E&E assets, PPE reclassified from E&E, bearer plants and goodwill in extractive CGUs; value-in-use modelling requires robust cash flow forecasts tied to reserves and operational plans.
• PSAK 16/19 (PPE/Intangibles): determine depreciation/amortisation, capitalisation of directly attributable costs and treatment of decommissioning; interacts with stripping cost capitalisation and asset reclassification.
• PSAK 57 (Provisions): decommissioning, restoration, environmental liabilities and onerous contracts arising from industry activities are recognised per PSAK 57 and, if capitalised, included in asset cost per PSAK 16; ISAK 9 clarifies changes in decommissioning liabilities.
Impairment, CGU allocation and unit-of-account practicalities
• E&E assets: allocate to CGUs no larger than a reportable segment; where individual asset recoverable amounts cannot be estimated, group to smallest cash-generating unit. Use geological/reserve splits to define CGUs.
• Bearer plants & biological assets: estimate recoverable amount at asset level where possible; indefinite-life assets must be tested annually. Use harvest cycles and biological growth models in value-in-use calculations.
• Goodwill & combined CGUs: goodwill from business combinations in extractive acquisitions must be allocated to CGUs or groups of CGUs benefiting from synergies (lowest level permissible is usually operating segment before aggregation).
• Insurance portfolios: group contracts into portfolios managed together for risk/pricing; CSM and liability measurement generally performed at portfolio level.
Disclosure expectations (industry consolidated view)
Disclosures should enable users to assess the amount, timing and uncertainty of future cash flows arising from industry activities. Required disclosure themes include: accounting policies and measurement bases, reconciliations of opening and closing balances (E&E, biological assets, insurance liabilities, CSM), movement analyses (capitalised costs, remeasurements, transfers, impairments), key assumptions and sensitivity analyses (commodity prices, yields, discount rates, mortality), nature and extent of risks (credit, liquidity, commodity price, insurance underwriting), governance over estimates and model validation, and reconciliations to regulatory or tax filings. Disclose transition choices and quantitative impacts when applying PSAK 74.
Practical implementation checklist (industry-wide)
1. Scope mapping: map operational activities to PSAK/ISAK scope (exploration vs development vs production; biological asset types; insurance contract types).
2. Documented policies: select and document measurement policies (cost vs revaluation, fair value policies, stripping capitalisation rules, CSM policy) and ensure consistent application.
3. Units of account & CGU definitions: define units for capitalisation, amortisation and impairment testing (pit benches, fields, orchards, insurance portfolios).
4. Data & systems: design systems to capture operational data (drill logs, production rates, strip ratios, harvest volumes, claims run-off) and valuation inputs (market prices, actuarial tables).
5. Valuation models & governance: develop valuation models (DCF, actuarial models, fair-value techniques), establish model governance, independent review and validation, and maintain audit trails.
6. Technical support: obtain geological/reserves reports, feasibility studies, actuarial reports and independent valuations where required; retain approvals and board minutes for reclassification decisions.
7. Controls & reconciliations: implement reconciliations between operational and accounting data, capitalisation authorisation, and routine impairment/valuation review cycles.
8. Audit-ready documentation: prepare indexed audit packs containing technical evidence, model inputs, sensitivity runs, migration/transition workpapers and reconciliations to statutory filings.
Common pitfalls & control mitigations
Pitfalls: inappropriate capitalisation of exploration or stripping costs; weak linkage between operational metrics and accounting units; insufficient technical evidence for reclassification; inadequate fair value inputs (using financing or replanting costs); poor documentation of assumptions for insurance CSM; failing to test bearer plants for impairment; inadequate provisions for decommissioning; and poor traceability between field-level costs and accounting entries.
Mitigations: maintain traceable cost allocation systems, require pre-approved technical feasibility studies, independent technical reviews, robust model governance and sensitivity testing, mandatory pre-reclassification impairment test, segregation of duties for capitalisation approvals, and centralised registers for regulatory permits and tax positions. Conduct periodic external valuations/actuarial reviews.
