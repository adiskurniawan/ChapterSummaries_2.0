Chapter 11 — Cash Management
Purpose & objective
Ensure robust, auditable control over all organisational cash holdings and movements to protect organisational funds, minimise reliance on cash, reduce theft/loss and FX exposure, ensure timely and accurate accounting and reconciliation, maintain donor and statutory compliance, and provide clear traceability for audits and incident response. Bank transfers are the preferred payment method; cash is permitted only when operationally necessary and where controls can be applied. All cash-related activities must be documented, independently reviewed, reconciled promptly, and retained according to retention policy. Reconciling items older than 30 days must be escalated and may only be posted to suspense with documented CD/CFD authorisation and a defined resolution plan.
Scope
All cash activities across country offices and sub-offices, including safe holdings, petty cash, programme advances (on- and off-system), payroll cash disbursements, cash-in-transit, third-party cash collections, mobile money, cash refunds, foreign currency cash holdings, cash insurance arrangements, and all supporting records (vouchers, withdrawal slips, receipts, reconciliations). Excludes bank account controls (covered in Treasury/Bank Policy) except where bank→cash movements intersect with cash controls. Contractor/vendor payments are covered under procurement unless explicitly paid in cash as a programme exception.
Core principles
• Minimisation: prefer electronic payments; limit cash holdings and frequency of withdrawals.
• Segregation of duties: separate roles for custody, issuance, recording, and independent review (maker-checker).
• Secure custody: safes, access controls, key management and physical security protocols.
• Timely reconciliation: weekly counts and reconciliations; mandatory reconciliation on last working day of each financial period.
• Traceability: pre-numbered vouchers/receipts, bank withdrawal evidence, and complete audit packs.
• Accountability & escalation: prompt investigation of discrepancies and formal escalations for unresolved items >30 days.
• Donor & statutory compliance: ensure donor restrictions and local regulations (currency controls, cash reporting) are respected.
Definitions & GL mapping
• Main cash (operational): GL 1050 Cash on hand.
• Petty cash / Imprest: GL 1055 Petty cash (imprest).
• Programme advances — on-system: GL 1141 Staff programme advances.
• Off-system programme advances / beneficiary cash: GL 1142 Off-system advances (restricted).
• Bank: GL 1000 Bank.
• Cash suspense / clearing: GL 1999 Cash suspense (use only with CD/CFD written authorisation).
• FX revaluation (cash): GL 7435 FX gain/loss.
• Cash-in-transit / deposit in transit: GL 1059 Cash-in-transit (if used).
Note: Map GL account numbers to local chart of accounts where different; maintain mapping table in treasury guidance.
Governance & responsibilities (RACI)
• Country Director (CD): Approve country-level cash policy exceptions, hand-carry limits, material write-offs and final sign-off on high-risk incidents.
• Country Finance Director (CFD): Owns cash policy implementation, reviews reconciliations, approves top-ups, escalates issues, authorises suspense postings with CD where required, and reports to Regional Finance.
• Treasury/Centre: Provide bank transfer support, cash forecasting guidance and centrally managed insurance/pooled arrangements if used.
• Cashier / Petty Cash Custodian: Daily custody, prepare reconciliations, maintain cash books, issue cash against authorised vouchers, record receipts and deposit unspent funds promptly.
• Payments Officer / Bank Officer: Prepare bank withdrawal/transfer instructions, ensure correct bank mandates/signatories are used and file withdrawal proofs.
• Independent Reviewer / Approver (not cashier): Perform independent cash counts, review reconciliations, sign-off, and review top-up requests.
• Budget Holders / Programme Managers: Approve programme advances and certify expenditure against advances.
• Security Officer / Logistics: Advise on secure transit, safes placement, and staff safety during cash movements.
• Internal Audit: Periodic spot checks and thematic reviews of cash controls.
Authorisations, thresholds & delegation
• Country hand-carry limit: Documented and signed by CD & Regional Finance; reviewed annually.
• Top-up approval: Budget holder (for purpose) + CFD sign-off for amounts above petty thresholds; bank withdrawal must be authorised per bank mandate (two authorised signatories where required).
• Off-system advance approval: Regional approval required for any off-system advances above set threshold (e.g., USD 1,000 or local equivalent).
• Write-off & loss approval: Small losses (e.g., < USD 500) may be approved by CFD; medium losses require CD; large losses escalate to Regional HQ per DoA. All write-offs require a documented investigation report and evidence of recovery attempts. Define thresholds in country delegation matrix.
Safe & physical security — minimum standards
• Safes should be dual-control (two-key or combination plus courier key stored separately) and meet local physical security standards (fire-rated, bolted to concrete floor where possible).
• Safe location: locked room with restricted access, minimal visibility, and appropriate environmental controls.
• Key/combo control: maintain a key-holder register; two authorised holders only (primary cashier and alternate non-room holder).
• Access logs: sign-in/out for key access; immediate reporting and replacement on loss.
• CCTV/alarm: recommended where feasible; document procedures for alarm activation and response.
• No personal or non-work items to be stored in payroll or cash safes. Conduct monthly review of safe access list.
Cash counting & reconciliation procedures
• Frequency: at minimum weekly and on the last working day of the accounting period; increase frequency based on risk/volume.
• Counting method: primary counter (cashier) prepares float; independent verifier (not cashier) performs count and both sign a cash count form with opening balance, receipts, disbursements and closing balance.
• Reconciliation formula: Opening balance + Bank top-up(s) + Receipts collected − Disbursements = Closing balance. Reconcile closing balance to GL 1050 (including petty and off-system advances where relevant).
• Documentation: cash count form, supporting vouchers/receipts, bank withdrawal evidence and top-up requests attached to reconciliation.
• Variance handling: immediate recount, review supporting documents; if variance persists, initiate formal investigation and escalate to CFD within 24 hours. Record findings and corrective actions in incident log.
Top-up / replenishment — detailed workflow
1. Forecast & requisition: Cashier prepares top-up request with projected needs, current reconciliation and list of pending vouchers/advances; include currency breakdown and expected dates.
2. Authorisation: Budget holder certifies purpose; CFD reviews amounts and approves per thresholds. For high-risk payments, CD approval required.
3. Bank instruction: Payments officer prepares bank withdrawal/transfer instruction referencing top-up ID and attaches reconciliation and authorisation evidence. Bank instruction follows bank mandate signatories and approval processes.
4. Withdrawal & transit: Two authorised staff collect cash; use secure transport; avoid predictable routes/times.
5. Receipt & verification: On return, independent verifier conducts count; both cashier and verifier sign top-up confirmation and attach to top-up request.
6. Posting & filing: Finance posts GL journal (Dr 1050 / Cr 1000) with PayrollRun/TopUp ID in narration; attach bank withdrawal slip and reconciliation to electronic file; update cashbook and safe ledger.
7. Follow-up: Retain physical bank withdrawal slips for filing; perform post-top-up review within 3 business days.
Issuing cash — controls by payment type
• Programme advances to staff: Require written advance request with purpose, budget code, recovery date, and approver signature. Record on programme advance control sheet and GL 1141. Recover via receipts or payroll recovery; reconcile within agreed timeframe (normally 4 weeks).
• Beneficiary payments (cash to third parties): Require beneficiary lists, proof of identity, signed acknowledgement of receipt, and photo/field officer confirmation where permissible. Use sequentially numbered payment vouchers and maintain beneficiary registers. Consider using vouchers or mobile money where possible to reduce cash handling risk.
• Payroll cash disbursements: Use numbered payslip lists; pre-verify ID at payment; perform batch counting and reconciliation; prefer bank where feasible. Maintain strict confidentiality of payroll details.
• Emergency payments: Require CD+CFD approval and retrospective documentation within 5 business days; record exception in emergency log and include security mitigations.
Off-system advances & higher-risk cash — additional controls
• Off-system advances are an elevated risk and require Regional approval if above threshold. Keep off-system advances short-duration (max 2–4 weeks) and limited value.
• Maintain off-system advance ledger with beneficiary, purpose, dates, returned amount, outstanding balance and supporting evidence.
• Increase oversight: weekly reconciliation, supervisor visits, and independent validations for off-system funds in remote areas.
• Convert to on-system advances where project conditions demand long-term use.
Foreign currency cash & FX management
• Minimise foreign currency holdings; convert to local currency promptly when operationally feasible.
• Record currency type and balances per safe; maintain separate reconciliations by currency.
• Period-end revaluation: revalue foreign currency cash using agreed system/Agresso exchange rates; post FX gains/losses to GL 7435 with supporting calculation and source of rate.
• For donor-funded cash in foreign currency, track donor currency balances separately and report in donor currency where required. Include FX policy reference and document exchange rates used.
Cash-in-transit & transit security
• For high-value withdrawals, use insured cash-in-transit providers where cost-effective and available.
• When staff transport cash: minimum two-person rule, pre-agreed route/time, use unmarked containers if safety protocol requires, carry only authorised amounts, and use emergency contact plan.
• Log transit details: staff names, vehicle registration, route, time out/return and handover checklist. Post-incident review if any security event occurs.
Bank linkages, deposits & proof of withdrawal
• All bank→safe movements must be evidenced by bank withdrawal slips, bank advices, teller receipts, or SWIFT/BIC where applicable.
• Attach bank evidence to top-up request and reconciliation; file both electronic and, if required by law/donor, scanned physical copies.
• Deposits collected (third-party cash) must be banked promptly; maintain deposit slips and reconcile bank deposit to cash receipts. Define deposit timelines (e.g., next banking day unless security issues).
Recording, GL postings & sample journal entries
• Top-up (bank → safe): Dr 1050 Cash on hand / Cr 1000 Bank. Narration: “Top-up SAFE — TOPUP-YYYYMMDD — Country — TopUpID — BankRef”.
• Issue programme advance: Dr 1141 Programme advances / Cr 1050 Cash on hand. Narration: “Advance — ADV-YYYYMMDD — Beneficiary — ProjectCode”.
• Settlement of advance (expense): Dr ProjectExpense / Cr 1141 Programme advances. Narration: “Advance settlement — ADV-XXXX — Beneficiary”.
• Return of unspent advance: Dr 1050 Cash on hand / Cr 1141 Programme advances.
• FX revaluation: Dr/Cr 1050 / Dr/Cr 7435 as required with calculation attached. Ensure narration includes period, TopUpID/AdvanceID and preparer.
File naming, indexing & retention
• Standard file naming: Cash_<DocType>_<Country>_<YYYYMMDD>_<ID>.pdf (examples: Cash_Topup_CountryX_20260301_TOPUP-20260301.pdf, CashCount_CountryX_20260226_Week9.pdf).
• Maintain an indexed cash register (electronic) recording TopUpID, date, preparer, reviewer, GL refs, and links to scanned documents.
• Retention: retain cash records per statutory and donor requirements (commonly minimum 7 years; longer where local law/donor mandates). Apply secure storage and PII protection to beneficiary data.
Audits, reviews & sample audit pack
• Maintain a cash audit pack per period containing: cash count forms, top-up requests, bank withdrawal proofs, payment vouchers, payee acknowledgements, petty cash reconciliations, advance control sheets, GL postings and narrative, incident reports and resolution evidence, and sign-off checklist.
• Internal audit will expect segregation evidence, independent verification signatures, reconciliations demonstrating safe cash = GL, and justification for any suspense postings. Provide auditors with indexed packs and cross-reference table.
Investigations, incidents & write-off procedures
• Immediate response: secure remaining funds, quarantine affected records, capture witness statements and CCTV if available, and notify CD/CFD and security.
• Investigation: log incident, assign investigator, collect evidence, quantify loss, identify root cause and responsible parties, and recommend recovery or disciplinary action.
• Write-off: authorisation per DoA after investigation; document recovery attempts and donor notification if funds were donor-restricted. Maintain incident register and lessons-learned log.
• Disciplinary & legal action: follow HR disciplinary policies and involve local law enforcement where theft/fraud suspected. Consider civil recovery action where appropriate.
Controls to mitigate risk & fraud (practical list)
• Pre-numbered vouchers and secure custody of blank stationery.
• Strict segregation of duties: different individuals for custody, disbursement, recording and reconciliation.
• Mandatory independent weekly counts and unannounced spot checks by Internal Audit/Finance Manager.
• Positive pay / bank validation for cheque usage and dual signatory for bank withdrawals per mandate.
• Rotate cash duties periodically and require mandatory periodic vacations for cash custodians.
• Maintain automated exception reports (large single cash payments, frequent petty top-ups, repeated voided receipts).
• Insure cash-in-transit and consider local political/security risk insurance for high-risk environments.
Petty cash: controls & linkage to petty cash policy
• Imprest petty cash floats must be authorised, recorded in GL 1055, and reconciled before replenishment; custodian accountable.
• Limit petty cash transaction values per country petty policy; use petty cash vouchers with receipts attached.
• Periodic unannounced checks and annual certification of custodian by supervisor. Integrate petty cash procedures with Chapter 12 (Petty Cash Policy).
Remote, field & mobile money cash arrangements
• Remote cash disbursal: Apply enhanced controls—use field trip authorisation, route plans, independent beneficiary verification and photographic evidence where permitted by data protection rules.
• Mobile money / e-wallets: Prefer where bank access is limited; maintain vendor contracts, KYC of mobile money providers, transaction logs and reconciliation to GL. Ensure mobile money wallet custodians maintain PIN confidentiality and device security.
• Contingency: document fallback cash arrangements and pre-authorised emergency drawdown procedures with strict post-event reporting.
Donor requirements & restricted funds
• Track donor-funded cash separately; ensure donor-specific rules on eligible expenditures, procurement and evidence are applied.
• Retain donor-level supporting documentation and apportion cash where multi-funded activities occur. Notify donors per award terms if loss involves donor-funded amounts.
Legal, tax & regulatory considerations
• Comply with local currency control regulations, cash transaction reporting thresholds, anti-money laundering (AML) rules and statutory filing requirements.
• Maintain KYC records for significant cash payees where required and cooperate with statutory audits/inquiries.
• Seek tax/legal advice for cross-border cash transfers and for payroll cash where withholding/tax reporting obligations exist.
Systems & automation opportunities
• Integrate cashbook with GL/Agresso/GLACOS to automate GL postings and reduce manual errors.
• Use electronic templates for top-up requests, with mandatory fields and attached scanned evidence enforced by system validation.
• Implement dashboards showing cash balances by safe/currency, aged advances, and exception indicators (variances, overdue recoveries).
• Automate FX revaluation calculations and attach rate source in system.

Training, capacity building & awareness

Common errors & remediation actions (expanded)
Common errors:
• Failure to perform weekly counts or independent verification.
• Cashier reconciling own float without independent review.
• Missing bank withdrawal slips or unfiled top-up evidence.
• Off-system advances unrecorded or unreconciled.
• Excessive cash on hand at month-end.
Remediations:
• Enforce weekly schedule and mandatory independent sign-off.
• System validation to require attachments for top-ups before GL posting.
• Monthly aged report of advances to prompt clearance.
• Immediate reduction in safe holdings where excessive, and retraining of staff on top-up forecasting.

Incident handling & remediation flow

5. Approve write-off per DoA if required and implement recovery actions; notify donors/stakeholders as required.
6. Implement corrective actions and monitor effectiveness; update SOPs and training.

Chapter 12 — Petty Cash
Purpose & objective
Provide a robust, auditable framework for management of small-value cash disbursements where bank transfers are impractical or impossible. Objectives: maintain an imprest petty-cash float with strict custody and replenishment controls; minimise cash-on-hand exposure and fraud risk; ensure every voucher is supported, authorised and coded to the correct project/SOF/donor; preserve segregation of duties; ensure timely reconciliation and GL posting; provide complete audit and donor evidence; define escalation and disciplinary routes for losses or irregularities. Petty cash must never substitute for supplier AP, payroll, or regular recurring payments that should be processed through banking or procurement.
Scope
All petty-cash activity across country offices, sub-offices, field posts and events: establishment and top-up of imprest floats, day-to-day petty purchases, petty refunds and petty reimbursements, event floats, cash collections/third-party collectors, remote/off-system arrangements, emergency small payments, custodian handovers, and reconciliations. Applies to centrally provided floats and locally authorised floats. Excludes supplier payments, staff salary payments (except documented emergency exceptions) and programme advances which follow Chapter 13.
Key definitions & core recognition principle
• Petty-cash float / Imprest: fixed cash balance established by transfer from bank to cash-on-hand for small payments; replenished to restore the float against submitted vouchers.
• Petty-cash voucher: sequentially numbered voucher supporting each disbursement with required fields and receipt.
• Custodian / Petty-cash holder: the person responsible for physical custody and record keeping.
• Top-up: bank transfer/cheque to replenish the float back to agreed level after vouchers submitted and approved.
• Independent reviewer: person different from custodian who verifies cash on hand and vouchers.
• Core principle: operate petty cash on an imprest basis; recognise expense at point vouchers are submitted and supported; replenish by bank transfer only after independent verification and authorised approval; maintain clear audit trail and segregation of duties.
Core principles / Key principles
1. Petty cash operates as an imprest float with a fixed maximum value and a defined per-payment ceiling.
2. Each disbursement must be supported by an original receipt/invoice and a completed petty-cash voucher; missing receipts require formal Missing Receipt Affidavit and higher-level approval where material.
3. Segregation of duties: custodian ≠ approver ≠ top-up approver ≠ bank/payments officer. Where segregation impossible, documented compensating controls must be applied (surprise counts, mandatory independent monthly review, dual custody for collection).
4. All vouchers coded to GL project/SOF/donor lines with supporting upload attachments for audit.
5. Regular reconciliations (monthly minimum) and unannounced cash counts.
6. No personal loans/ salary payments/ capital purchases via petty cash unless documented, approved and recorded as exception.
7. Immediate investigation, disciplinary and recovery action for shortages or theft.
Governance & responsibilities (RACI)
• Country Director (CD) / Sub-office Manager: approve petty-cash scheme, float ceilings and per-payment limits; approve exceptions above defined thresholds; sign-off on material write-offs.
• Country Finance Director (CFD): design and enforce petty-cash controls and SOPs; approve top-ups; perform periodic spot checks and reconciliations; maintain petty-cash register; approve custodian changes and compensating controls.
• Petty Cash Custodian: secure custody, maintain cash book and sequential vouchers, issue cash against authorised vouchers, prepare top-up requests and submit vouchers for approval and filing.
• Budget Holders / Requestors: pre-authorise petty-cash expenditure as required and confirm appropriate coding.
• Independent Reviewer / Verifier: different person to custodian who performs physical counts, reviews vouchers, signs reconciliations and countersigns handovers.
• Bank/Payments Officer: executes bank transfer/top-up upon receipt of approved supporting documents and GL postings; posts GL journal when required or upload to GL system.
• Internal Audit / Compliance: periodic audits, incident investigations, testing of controls and reporting of findings.
• Programme/Operations Managers: ensure petty cash is used only for permitted purposes at field events; co-operate with reconciliations.
RACI mapping: Establish a formal RACI matrix locally that maps each task (establishment, top-up, reconciliation, count, custody transfer, approval of exceptions, write-off) to roles.
Systems & security (recording & attachments)
Use the finance system/ledger to record petty-cash GL codes and attach scanned vouchers and reconciliation worksheets to journal entries. Maintain a petty-cash register (spreadsheet or system) capturing float ID, currency, custodian, location, float amount, per-payment ceiling, opening date, bank GL reference and status. Restrict access to electronic copies to finance users and auditors; protect scanned vouchers with appropriate folder permissions; apply version control and naming conventions. Where possible use a secure e-petty solution or scanned voucher upload portal to reduce PII exposure in email. Maintain encrypted backups of petty-cash files and custodial handover records.
Petty cash calendar & cut-offs
Publish a petty-cash calendar: monthly reconciliation cut-off date for vouchers to be submitted (example: cut-off 3rd working day), top-up processing dates (example: weekly or as required but no more than monthly), and scheduled surprise count windows. Align petty-cash calendar with monthly GL close to ensure expenses posted in correct accounting period. Communicate cut-offs to budget holders and custodians. Include contingency arrangements for emergency top-ups outside calendar with pre-authorised sign-off.
Required inputs & supporting documentation
• Completed, sequential petty-cash voucher for every cash payment, with required fields (see sample).
• Original supplier/retailer receipts or invoices attached to voucher.
• Authoriser signature (budget holder or delegated approver) and custodian signature.
• Top-up request including float balance, total vouchers submitted and requested replenishment amount.
• Independent count/sign-off sheet for top-up.
• Bank deposit/transfer evidence when cash is returned to bank or float reduced.
• Custodian handover form for any change in custodian.
• Missing Receipt Affidavits for unsupported payments with approver comments.
• GL posting reference and scan of uploaded journal or GLACOS preview file.
Petty cash calculation components & permitted uses (detailed)

Chapter 13 — Programme Advances (Floats)
Purpose & scope
Programme advances (floats) are short-term cash advances issued to staff or authorised agents to meet legitimate programme-related expenditures where immediate supplier or bank payment is impractical. This chapter prescribes comprehensive controls covering authorisation, issuance, custody, settlement, FX treatment, recordkeeping, reconciliations, off-system use, cross-office and inter-country returns, recovery, sanctions for non-compliance, and audit expectations. Applies to all country offices, sub-offices, field missions and emergency operations.
Core control objectives
• Minimise outstanding advances and overall cash exposure.
• Ensure every advance is pre-authorised, traceable and spent strictly on approved purposes.
• Enforce timely liquidation supported by original documentation; hold advance holders personally accountable.
• Record all advances in the finance system (GL) and reconcile balances monthly.
• Restrict off-system advances and apply heightened controls where unavoidable.
Governance & roles — detailed RACI
Advance holder (staff or designated agent): requests advance; safeguards cash; spends strictly per approval; collects, numbers and retains original receipts; returns residual cash; submits complete settlement on time.
Budget holder / Programme Manager: reviews and authorises advance requests; confirms budget availability; approves settlement, coding and purpose compliance.
Finance (Cashier / Accountant): verifies no prohibited outstanding advance exists; records issue and settlement journals in Agresso/GLACOS; assigns AdvanceID; maintains advance register; follows up overdue items; performs monthly reconciliations and escalates ageing balances.
Country Finance Director (CFD): enforces policy compliance; reviews outstanding advance reports and exceptions; approves receipt-missing forms and reclassifications per threshold; escalates persistent non-compliance.
Country Director (CD): approves exceptional advances, threshold breaches and exemptions; approves write-offs and recovery actions on escalation.
Regional Finance / Treasury: approves off-system or unusually large advances per delegated authority; advises on inter-country returns and FX treatment.
Authorisation rules, limits & eligibility
• All advances require prior written authorisation by the appropriate budget holder and finance approval.
• Standard limit: an individual advance must not exceed the staff member’s monthly net salary unless explicitly approved by the CD (or higher delegated authority).
• Normally only one outstanding advance per person at any time.
• No advances to consultants, casual vendors or staff in their final contract month unless exceptional written CD approval is obtained.
• Local thresholds for CD and Regional approvals must be documented in the country SOP.
Advance request & pre-issue checks (step-by-step)
1. Staff completes Programme Advance Request form detailing purpose, activity description, budget breakdown, currency, amount requested, expected settlement date and beneficiary details where cash distributions apply.
2. Budget holder signs to confirm legitimacy and budget availability.
3. Finance checks the advance holder’s existing outstanding balance, ageing and settlement history; requests are rejected if policy conditions are not met.
4. CFD or designated approver authorises per threshold.
5. Finance assigns a unique AdvanceID and prepares the GL journal for issuance.
Accounting entries — standard treatment
On issue:
Dr 1141 Staff programme advances (or member-specific advance GL) — advance amount.
Cr 1050 Cash on hand or Cr 1000 Bank — advance amount.
On settlement:
Dr relevant programme expense GLs — supported expense totals.
Dr 1050 Cash on hand — residual cash returned (if any).
Cr 1141 Staff programme advances — total advance amount.
All journals must reference AdvanceID and holder name in the narrative.
Settlement & supporting documentation requirements
Settlement must include: completed Advance Return form; original numbered receipts/invoices for every expenditure; signed beneficiary lists and delivery confirmations where cash distributions occur; proof of bank or transfer payments made on behalf; petty cash vouchers where applicable; and evidence of residual cash return. Receipts must be legible and identify suppliers. Missing receipts require a completed Receipt Missing Form with CFD or CD approval; repeated occurrences trigger investigation.
Timing standards & ageing rules
• Normal settlement deadline: within 4 weeks of issue (or shorter if defined in local SOP).
• Finance produces a monthly outstanding advance report; items >4 weeks require written justification and documented follow-up.
• Advances outstanding >8 weeks are escalated to CD and Regional Finance; persistent non-compliance may lead to suspension of further advances and disciplinary action.
Foreign exchange (FX) treatment
Advances should be issued and settled in the same currency wherever practicable. If expenditure occurs in another currency, record the actual exchange rate applied (attach exchange receipt) or apply the approved company rate for settlement date if receipts are unavailable; document the method. Post realised FX gains or losses to GL 7435 (or designated FX GL) with correct SOF/DEA. Outstanding foreign-currency advances are revalued at period-end per system policy and disclosed transparently.
Off-system advances — exceptional high-risk use
Off-system advances (not immediately recorded in the GL) are permitted only for urgent, short-term contexts (e.g., 24–72 hour emergency procurement) with prior written Regional Finance approval. Enhanced controls apply: strict time limits, designated custodians, Programme Advance Control Sheet tracking receipts and serials, independent cash counts, and mandatory posting to the GL at the earliest possible time and within the same accounting period where feasible.
Cross-office and inter-country returns & clearing
If residual cash is returned to another office within the same country, record the return against the issuing office cost centre and retain transfer documentation. For inter-country returns, use inter-company control accounts: recipient posts Dr Cash / Cr Inter-country control; issuing office clears via Dr Inter-country control / Cr 1141 or Cash as applicable. Reconcile clearing balances monthly and retain SWIFT or transfer confirmations.
Unauthorised spending & reclassification controls
Expenditure outside the approved purpose requires post-facto written approval by the budget holder or CD. Without approval, amounts are charged to a suspense or staff liability account and recovery or disciplinary procedures initiated. Any reclassification (e.g., capitalisation) requires CFD approval and documented justification.
Limits, prohibitions & special cases
• Normally one outstanding advance per staff member.
• No advances to ineligible persons without explicit written CD approval.
• Advances exceeding monthly net salary or involving high-risk activities require CD and Regional Finance approval with documented business case.
• High-risk distributions require independent counting and verification at receipt point.
Follow-up, recovery & disciplinary measures
Finance issues written reminders at 1-week, 3-week and 4-week milestones. Unresolved advances may be recovered through payroll deduction where lawful or other approved mechanisms. Persistent non-compliance can result in suspension from future advances, disciplinary action or legal recovery. Write-offs require approval per delegation (CD/Regional/Member HQ).
Monthly reconciliation & management reporting
Maintain an Advance Register by holder and AdvanceID. Monthly, reconcile the register to GL 1141, investigate variances and produce an ageing analysis (0–4 weeks, 4–8 weeks, >8 weeks). Include an outstanding advance summary and action plan in the monthly finance pack for CD/CFD review.
Documentation, naming, filing & retention
Each advance file must include: request and authorisation, issue evidence, AdvanceID, return form, receipts, beneficiary lists, GL references, correspondence and any Receipt Missing Forms. Audit expectations & audit pack
Auditors will test the advance register against GL balances; review sampled advance issue and settlement files; verify authorisation, budget availability, FX treatment and escalation of overdue items; and review evidence of recovery or disciplinary action. Maintain a ready audit pack including recent samples and the aged outstanding listing.
Templates, system entries & tools
Standard templates include: Programme Advance Request form, Advance Return form, Advance Register, Receipt Missing Form, ageing report and off-system control sheet. All Agresso/GLACOS journals must reference AdvanceID, holder name and correct SOF/DEA.
Controls for distributions & beneficiary payments
Cash distributions require an approved distribution plan, signed beneficiary lists or alternative verification, independent witness signatures, and reconciliation of quantities and values distributed. High-risk distributions require additional verification such as spot checks, photos or third-party monitoring.
High-value or sensitive advances — enhanced safeguards
For advances above defined thresholds: dual-custodian custody, secure transport arrangements, insurance or armoured courier where feasible, independent verification at receipt, and mandatory post-activity spot checks by senior management.
Payroll interface & recovery coordination
Where recovery via payroll is required, document the deduction schedule, obtain written consent where applicable, attach authorisation to the advance file, and coordinate with payroll to ensure correct and timely execution, including at staff exit.
Special scenarios — lost receipts, theft, emergencies
Lost receipts require a Receipt Missing Form with detailed explanation and approval. Theft or loss must be reported immediately to CD, CFD and internal audit, with incident reports and engagement of security or law enforcement as required. Emergency operations may allow streamlined issuance within pre-approved limits but require enhanced post-facto controls and frequent reconciliation.
Cross-checks & verification techniques
Reconcile receipt totals to GL and bank records; verify unit prices against market benchmarks; sample beneficiary confirmations via visits or calls; and use geo-tagged photos or third-party monitors for high-risk contexts.
Common errors & mitigations
Errors include issuing advances without pre-checks, missing AdvanceID in journals, poor receipt quality, late settlement, unrecorded off-system advances and undocumented FX. Mitigate through strict SOP enforcement, mandatory templates, automated ageing alerts, receipt scanning and regular training.
Operational control checklists
Pre-issue: request complete; budget approved; outstanding balance checked; AdvanceID assigned; authorisation obtained.
On issue: GL posted; withdrawal evidence attached; file created.
Settlement: return form completed; receipts attached; coding approved; GL settlement posted; residual cash recorded; register updated and file archived.

Reporting, KPIs & management information
Monthly reports show holder, AdvanceID, amount, currency, issue date, expected return, ageing and follow-up status. Quarterly reviews analyse policy exceptions and off-system usage. Track exposure metrics: total outstanding, % >4 weeks and % >8 weeks.
Training, SOPs & continuous improvement
Mandatory training for budget holders, advance holders and finance staff; maintain lessons-learned logs; conduct periodic internal audits and spot checks focusing on high-risk and off-system advances.
Immediate actions for offices
1. Reconcile the Advance Register to GL 1141 for the current month.
2. Identify and follow up advances >4 weeks.
3. Ensure every outstanding advance has an AdvanceID and complete file; suspend new advances where gaps exist.
4. Review off-system approvals and controls.
5. Refresh staff training on receipt standards.

Chapter 14 — Currencies and Foreign Exchange
Purpose & scope
This chapter establishes rules, procedures and controls for managing multiple currencies and foreign exchange (FX) across operations. It governs transaction and cash request currencies, posting in transaction currency, translation to functional currency and SCI/USD in Agresso, month-end revaluations, FX gains and losses, donor-currency monitoring, in-country FX procurement, coordination with Treasury, and controls to minimise FX exposure, donor risk and audit findings. It applies to all country offices, sub-offices and transactions involving foreign currency.
Key definitions & core recognition principle
• Transaction currency: currency shown on the invoice/contract/payment instrument — use for posting.
• Functional currency: the currency in which the country office reports locally.
• SCI/USD functional currency: the consolidation/SCI reporting currency (USD) used in Agresso/central reporting.
• Agresso system rate: exchange rates centrally maintained in the ERP for automatic translation.
• SOF exchange-rate budget entry: donor-prescribed or project-prescribed rate entered into budget/SOF fields when required.
• FX realised gain/loss: gain or loss recorded when a conversion/settlement occurs and actual rate differs from book/system rate.
• FX unrealised gain/loss: gain or loss arising from revaluation of FX-denominated balance-sheet items at period end.
Core recognition principle: post transactions in their actual transaction currency; use system translations for reporting; recognise realised FX at conversion/settlement date and revaluation FX at period end per central revaluation process.
Core principles & control objectives
• Record every transaction in the actual transaction currency.
• Use Agresso transaction-currency fields; avoid manual pre-translation.
• Keep foreign-currency cash & bank balances low at month-end to minimise revaluation volatility and audit risk.
• Treasury (centre) manages FX procurement, exchange-rate setting and any hedging; offices must follow Treasury guidance and notify Treasury of onshore conversions.
• Maintain complete audit trail: quotes, confirmations, commissions, bank advices, journal narratives and SOF entries.
• Obtain minimum competitive quotations for local FX procurement and clearly document exceptions.
Governance & responsibilities (RACI — detailed)
• Treasury (centre): sets/updates Agresso exchange rates; executes central FX procurement & hedging; runs central month-end revaluation; consolidates FX reporting; issues FX policy & guidance.
• Country Director (CD): approves country-level currency usage exceptions and material FX decisions outside policy.
• Country Finance Director (CFD): requests FX from Treasury; monitors FX exposures; ensures correct posting, SOF/DEA tagging and justification of material FX movements; approves local corrective journals.
• Payments/Banking Officer: sources FX locally when authorised; obtains & files exchanger receipts, SWIFTs and bank advices; attaches evidence to GL journals.
• Budget Holders / Programme Managers: request cash in required currency and reforecast when FX affects delivery.
• Regional Finance: oversight on donor-specific FX treatment, approval of material exceptions and liaison with Treasury for large exposures.
• Internal Audit: periodic review of FX controls and adherence to policy.
Transaction currency & posting mechanics
• Confirm transaction currency from contract/invoice/award documentation prior to processing.
• Enter original transaction currency and date in Agresso — do not manually translate before posting.
• Use SOF exchange-rate budget entries if donor prescribes a rate/method; otherwise rely on central Agresso rates.
• Every FX-related journal must include: transaction currency, exchange rate (or system-rate reference), transaction date, narrative, PayrollRun/PaymentBatch ID (where relevant), and attachments (bank/exchanger evidence).
• For partial currency transactions (e.g., split payments), post each line in its actual transaction currency.
Agresso exchange-rate updates & communication
• Treasury centrally maintains Agresso exchange rates and publishes update frequency and cut-offs. Typical cadence: 0–3 updates/month depending on volatility.
• Country offices must notify Treasury promptly when: (a) onshore conversions occur; (b) actual conversion rates materially differ from system rates; (c) planned large purchases/transfers are scheduled.
• Retain exchanger/bank evidence for actual rates; provide supporting documentation to Treasury for rate validation and system update.
Donor-currency translation & SOF exchange-rate budgets
• If donor/member prescribes an exchange rate or methodology, capture that instruction in award files and enter the prescribed rate into the SOF exchange-rate budget before posting related transactions.
• If no donor rate, use central Agresso rate on transaction date.
• Reconcile donor reports to Agresso donor-currency outputs; retain calculation workpapers and email/award confirmation of prescribed rates.
Budgets, proposals & reforecasting
• Prepare budgets in transaction currencies where feasible; translate to USD/functional currency using SCI templates or agreed conversion methods.
• Explicitly document FX assumptions and triggers for reforecasting in budget narratives.
• Reforecast donors and programmes when FX movements exceed governance thresholds (recommended ~5% by default) or where material impact on delivery exists. Notify Treasury for any funding/cash timing decisions.
Programme advances, petty cash & FX handling
• Issue advances in the currency required for field expenditure when practicable.
• Liquidation must reflect actual exchange rate used at conversion or actual currency spent, supported by exchanger/bank receipts.
• Record FX commissions/fees as actual expenses with receipts.
• Realised/unrealised FX on advance liquidation must be posted to FX GL (e.g., 7435) or member-specified GL with correct SOF mapping. Keep advance-subledger reconciled monthly.
In-country FX procurement (exceptional cases)
• Local procurement of FX is permitted only when Treasury cannot source the currency or market/legal constraints exist. Obtain minimum two quotes where practical and document selection rationale.
• Do not enter hedges, forwards or derivatives locally without written Treasury approval.
• Retain exchanger details, rate, commission, date, and bank confirmation; attach to GL journal and to the FX quote log.
FX gains & losses — month-end revaluation
• Central month-end revaluation is performed over FX-denominated balance-sheet items (bank, cash, receivables, payables, advances) and produces FX gain/loss entries recorded to FX GL (e.g., 7435) or member-specific GLs.
• Country offices should: minimise FX balances at period-end, clear advances where possible, and provide accurate outstanding item schedules.
• Material revaluation movements must be explained in monthly finance packs with supporting schedules and narratives.
Award & donor FX risk — identification & monitoring
• Primary FX risk sources: (1) mismatch between budgeted and actual conversion rates; (2) timing differences between income conversion and expenditure reporting rates; (3) donor-prescribed rates causing unrealised variances.
• Maintain award-level FX risk registers, monitor donor-currency budgets, and include FX sensitivity analysis in award risk assessments. Escalate material FX exposure to Regional Finance and Treasury.
Practical mitigation actions for offices
• Request cash in the currency that will actually be spent.
• Minimise foreign-currency cash and bank balances at month-end.
• Clear programme advances before period-end.
• Provide frequent and accurate cash forecasts (30–90 day).
• Reforecast donor budgets when FX moves beyond thresholds.
• Retain conversion evidence and attach to GL journals.
Inter-office, inter-company transfers & clearing
• Use Treasury-approved channels for inter-office and cross-border transfers; include currency, rate, SWIFT and commission details.
• Use inter-country control/clearing accounts for reimbursements and reconcile clearing balances monthly with supporting documentation.
• All inter-office FX transactions must carry clear narration identifying sender/receiver, currency and rate.
Hedging, forwards & derivatives
• No local hedging, forwards or derivatives without explicit written Treasury approval.
• Any centrally arranged hedge requires exposure analysis, economic rationale, governance approvals and full audit trail (contracts, confirmations and valuation schedules).
Working with implementing partners (IPs)
• Contracts must specify payment currency and FX responsibilities.
• Obtain Regional Finance approval for multi-currency subawards or where partner payment currency differs from award currency.
• Require partners to provide invoices and receipts in transaction currency or documented conversion evidence.
VAT, tax & statutory FX requirements
• Comply with local tax rules, including invoice-currency and conversion rules for VAT reporting.
• Seek professional tax advice for cross-border or complex FX tax treatments and retain supporting positions.
System controls & Agresso practical notes
• Use transaction-currency fields and transaction dates accurately.
• Apply SOF exchange-rate entry for donor-prescribed rates before posting.
• Ensure every FX journal has a clear narrative and attachments.
• Pre-clear local manual FX or correcting journals with the CFD and retain approvals.

Chapter 16 — Year-End Financial Procedures
Purpose, objective & risk context
Year-end financial close is a critical, high-risk control point underpinning organisational consolidation, statutory financial statements, donor accountability, governance oversight and external audit assurance. The year-end period concentrates a high volume of estimates, judgement-based entries, manual journals, partner and sub-office submissions, donor close-outs and audit scrutiny into compressed timelines. This chapter establishes a mandatory, end-to-end framework governing year-end planning, execution, documentation, review, approval, audit support and post-close remediation to ensure all balances are complete, accurate, properly authorised, fully supported and consolidation-ready. It defines required actions, schedules, controls, timelines, segregation of duties, evidence standards and escalation thresholds that every country office must follow before, during and after year-end close.
Scope & applicability
This chapter applies to all country offices, sub-offices, field missions and project locations, including interactions with regional finance, centre finance, treasury, HR, procurement, logistics, award management and global assurance. Scope covers: year-end planning and readiness; soft and hard close processes; accruals and prepayments; balance-sheet reconciliations; provisions (including severance, terminal benefits and contingent liabilities); Gifts in Kind (GiK); fixed assets; payroll and leave liabilities; donor and award close-outs; partner and sub-award accounting; bank, cash and advances; inter-office balances; statutory and donor reporting inputs; audit engagement management; post-close corrections; record retention; transition, ATA and indemnity treatment. Key definitions & core recognition principles
• Soft close: preliminary year-end close per centre timetable where all material accruals, provisions and reconciliations must be complete for consolidation review.
• Hard close: final locked close following centre confirmation; no further postings permitted without formal approval.
• Accrual: recognition of expenses and income in the period in which they are incurred or earned, irrespective of cash movement.
• Provision: liability of uncertain timing or amount recognised where a present obligation exists and can be reasonably estimated.
• Year-end action plan: detailed project plan covering all close activities, owners, deadlines, dependencies and escalation points.
• Audit pack: indexed, cross-referenced set of reconciliations, schedules, journals and source documents supporting all balances.
Core principle: all year-end balances must be recognised on an accrual basis, supported by auditable evidence, reviewed independently and approved in line with Delegation of Authority (DoA) before consolidation.
High-level accounting & control principles
1. Completeness before consolidation: all required schedules, reconciliations, confirmations and supporting evidence must be completed, reviewed and filed before soft close submission.
2. Accrual discipline: recognise income, expenses, assets and liabilities in the correct period based on economic substance, not cash timing.
3. Consistency & comparability: methodologies for estimates, provisions and valuations must be applied consistently year-to-year unless formally approved changes are documented.
4. Segregation of duties: preparation, review/technical validation, approval and posting must be performed by distinct roles.
5. Centre primacy: group revaluations, consolidation entries and centrally mandated adjustments are executed only by centre unless instructed otherwise.
6. Audit-ready documentation: every GL balance must be traceable GL → reconciliation/schedule → source documentation with clear narratives and sign-offs.

Purpose & objectives
Financial planning and budgeting translate strategic and programmatic plans into quantified, resourced and time-phased financial commitments. This chapter establishes a comprehensive framework to ensure that budgets are: realistic, complete and aligned to approved strategies; compliant with donor, statutory and organisational requirements; prepared on a full-cost basis; phased to support effective monitoring and cashflow management; and supported by clear assumptions and an auditable trail. Scope
Applies to all country offices and covers: annual country master budgets; award (donor-funded) budgets; non-award funded SOFs (unrestricted or centrally funded); partner and sub-award budgets; budget uploads and maintenance in Agresso; quarterly forecasting and reforecasting; budget monitoring and variance analysis; exchange rate application; and linkage to cashflow planning. Key definitions & core budgeting principles
• Master budget: consolidated annual country budget covering all awards, non-award funded SOFs and shared costs.
• Award budget: approved budget for a specific donor award, aligned to donor format and reporting requirements.
• SOF (Source of Funds): internal funding structure in Agresso representing a donor award or unrestricted funding stream.
• DEA (Donor Expense Account): donor-defined budget or reporting line mapped to SCI account codes.
• Transaction currency: currency in which expenditure will actually occur.
• Donor currency: currency required for donor proposal, reporting or contract.
• Forecast: updated estimate of expected outturn for the year, incorporating actuals and revised assumptions.
Core principles: budgets must be complete (full cost), realistic, phased, documented, approved, and entered into Agresso promptly; forecasts must reflect best available information and be updated when assumptions change materially.
Core principles / Key principles
1. Budgets are prepared on a full-cost basis, including direct, shared and indirect/support costs.
2. Budgets must reflect approved operational plans and realistic implementation capacity.
3. All budgets are prepared in transaction currency and translated consistently for donor and USD reporting.
4. Budgets are phased monthly to enable meaningful budget-versus-actual (BvA) analysis.
5. Assumptions, methodologies and exchange rates are clearly documented and retained.
6. Budgets and forecasts are authorised by accountable budget holders and approved per Delegation of Authority (DoA).
7. Agresso is the system of record; budgets must be uploaded accurately and on time.

• Budget Holders / Programme Managers: prepare detailed budgets for their areas; justify assumptions; ensure completeness and realistic phasing; review monthly BvA; propose corrective actions.
• Support Functions (HR, Logistics, IT, Operations): provide cost inputs, procurement lead times, salary scales, asset and maintenance plans.
• Members / Awards Management: agree donor award budgets, donor-specific rates and assumptions; lead donor negotiations on budget changes.
• Regional Office / Centre: issue budgeting instructions, templates and treasury rates; review and challenge country budgets and forecasts.
Annual budgeting cycle & timetable
• Preparation phase: typically Q3 (guidance: August–September). Programme priorities and targets confirmed; budget guidance issued; support function inputs collected.
• Consolidation & review: September–October. Finance consolidates, reviews completeness and funding coverage; management review and challenge.
• Approval: October. CD approves master budget; member/centre approvals where required.
• System upload: within 30 days of approval. Budgets posted to Agresso and verified.
• In-year monitoring: monthly BvA; quarterly forecasts completed within 30 days of period end (per country financial calendar).
Master budget — purpose, structure & formula
The master budget provides a consolidated financial view of the country programme for the year. It is used to assess affordability, funding gaps and resource allocation decisions.
Formula: Master budget = Total planned programme and support costs − Confirmed funding − Likely funding (defined as funding with >85% probability).
Funding gaps identified must be escalated for fundraising, scope adjustment or reprioritisation. The master budget must reconcile to the sum of all SOFs (award and non-award) in Agresso.
Award budgeting process — detailed steps
1. Define programme objectives and outputs per proposal or operational plan.
2. List activities required to achieve objectives.
3. Identify inputs (staff, goods, services, assets, partner costs).
4. Estimate quantities and unit costs using current market data and organisational benchmarks.
5. Assign SCI account codes, cost centres, projects and donor DEA lines.
6. Determine transaction currency for each cost line.
7. Phase costs monthly based on implementation plan and procurement timelines.
8. Apply exchange rates for donor currency conversion per donor or treasury guidance.
9. Prepare detailed notes explaining assumptions, risks and sensitivities.
10. Review collaboratively with finance and support functions; revise as needed.
11. Obtain budget holder and required management approvals.
Transaction currency vs donor currency
• All budgets must be entered into Agresso in the transaction currency(ies) where expenditure will occur.
• For donor proposals and reports, transaction currency budgets are converted to donor currency using donor-specified rates/methods or, where not specified, the latest Agresso/centre treasury rates.
• If a donor-specified rate differs by more than 5% from Agresso rates or recent experience, finance must notify centre treasury and document the rationale and risk implications.
• USD translation is required for consolidated reporting using approved rates.
Exchange rate guidance for budgeting
• Use donor-mandated rates where required by agreement.
• Otherwise, use latest treasury/Agresso budget rates issued by centre.
• Rates should be updated close to proposal submission or budget finalisation.
• Material movements (>5%) require reforecasting and communication to RO/centre and members where donor reporting is impacted.
Phasing & budgeting assumptions
Budgets must be phased monthly to reflect expected timing of expenditure. Phasing should consider recruitment plans, procurement lead times, seasonality, partner disbursement schedules and activity sequencing. All assumptions (staff numbers, inflation, unit rates, exchange rates, implementation timing) must be documented in a working paper retained with the budget. Line-level calculation sheets must be available for audit and review.
Shared / non-thematic costs & full-cost recovery
Shared costs (e.g. country office support, rent, utilities, logistics, HR, IT) must be identified and allocated transparently either to awards or to a designated non-award funded SOF. Allocation bases (e.g. headcount, FTEs, direct cost share, square metres) must be consistent, documented and applied monthly to reflect true project costs and donor full-cost recovery principles.
Forgotten costs — standard checklist
Budget preparers must explicitly consider and, where applicable, include: recruitment and onboarding costs; staff training and capacity building; staff insurance and medical costs; vehicle purchase, fuel, maintenance and insurance; generator fuel and servicing; IT hardware and licences; warehousing and storage; partner management and monitoring; audit and donor compliance costs; evaluations and close-out costs.
Partner involvement & sub-award budgeting
Where programmes are delivered through partners, partner workplans and budgets must be obtained and reviewed. Partner budgets must align with SCI activity plans and be incorporated into award budgets. Contract currency must match payment currency. Sub-award codes must be used in Agresso uploads to enable tracking. Partner phasing and reporting schedules must be consistent with SCI budgets and donor requirements.
Support services input & integration
HR, logistics, IT and other support functions must provide timely and realistic cost and timing inputs, including salary scales, benefit costs, recruitment timelines, procurement lead times, asset lifecycles and maintenance plans. Finance must integrate these inputs to avoid under-budgeting and implementation delays.
SOF & DEA mapping
For donor formats, SCI account codes must be mapped to donor DEA codes. Each SCI budget line must carry cost centre, project, account code, DEA description, transaction currency and explanatory notes. Mapping must be reviewed and validated to ensure donor-compliant reporting and avoid reclassification later.

5. Upload budget via post-back into Agresso.

Material variances must be analysed and explained in narrative reports provided to management and RO/centre as required.
Variance analysis — triggers & corrective actions
Variance drivers include timing differences, miscoding, implementation changes, FX movements and estimation errors. Corrective actions may include re-phasing, recoding, operational adjustments, donor budget realignment requests (via members), cost controls or fundraising actions. FX-driven variances exceeding 5% require reforecasting in donor currency.
Quarterly forecasting process
Countries must prepare phased forecasts for the remainder of the year at least quarterly. The first forecast is typically in April (per country calendar), with subsequent forecasts each quarter. Forecasts incorporate actuals to date and updated assumptions. Iterative review with RO/centre is required where risks or funding gaps emerge.

• CFD consolidates, reviews and uploads budgets and forecasts.
• CD approves the master budget and material reforecasts.
• For donor awards, members must agree budgets and donor-specific rates and assumptions before upload.

Maintain clear working papers, standard templates and documented assumptions. Ensure segregation of preparation and review. Avoid last-minute uploads without review. Chapter 18 — Donor Financial Reporting
Overview & governance framework

Donor financial reporting is governed by the Funds Summary which defines responsibilities across members, country offices and centre. Members retain ultimate accountability for donor submissions; country offices ensure accurate implementation and recording of award expenditure. Financial and programmatic reporting must be consistent and mutually reconcilable.
Key control principles

All donor reports are generated from Agresso after hard close and rely solely on system data. All financial adjustments must be posted before hard close of the final reporting period. Off-system adjustments are not permitted unless corresponding Agresso entries are made and fully documented.
Standard donor report from Agresso

The centre automatically issues the “standard donor report by member” after hard close. This report shows actual versus budgeted expenditure by award and contains SCI costs only. Members and country offices use this report as the authoritative base for donor reporting.
Division of responsibilities

Members are responsible for combining SCI figures with member-level costs and submitting donor reports. Country offices are responsible for implementing programmes, coding expenditure accurately, and ensuring awards are spent as planned. Both parties must collaborate to ensure donor compliance and financial integrity.
Member reporting adjustments

Members may adjust the Agresso report to include: member expenses, indirect cost recovery (ICR), bank interest declarations, and donor-specific formatting. All member adjustments must reconcile back to the Agresso SCI figures. Any explanation of underspend or variance must be clearly documented in the donor submission.
Funds summary vs donor budget

SCI reports expenditure only against the funds summary agreed with the member. Member-incurred costs and ICR recovery are excluded from SCI reporting but included by the member in donor reports. The member reports the full donor budget; SCI reports only its funded portion.
Award management during implementation

Country offices must deliver activities in line with the funds summary and donor-currency budget. Expenditure must be coded accurately and promptly in Agresso. Monthly review of expenditure against budget is mandatory throughout the award term.
Monthly expenditure review requirements

Monthly reviews must identify miscoding, over-spend and under-spend. Corrective actions must be taken promptly to address identified issues. Ongoing monitoring reduces the risk of significant adjustments at reporting deadlines.
Match funding management

Match requirements must be tracked, documented and maintained throughout the award. This includes off-system match such as community in-kind contributions. Members are responsible for sourcing match funds, and any intra-member match must be agreed in advance.
Corrective actions following review

Country offices must reallocate miscoded transactions without delay. Activities may be adjusted to ensure appropriate donor-currency spend where this does not compromise programme objectives. Potential budget reallocations must be discussed with the member for possible donor approval.
End-of-period financial procedures

All transactions for the reporting period must be posted before hard close. Accruals and severance provisions must be identified and recorded. Approved sub-award reports must be posted using Dr 5201 Partner payments – expenses and Cr 5200 Partner payments – advances.
Sub-award and GiK adjustments

GiK related to sub-awards must be adjusted and posted in Agresso. Partner advances and expenses must be fully reconciled before reporting. Supporting documentation must be retained for audit purposes.
Exchange rate verification

Country offices must confirm that correct donor exchange rates are set in Agresso. Donor-specified rates or methods must be applied where required. Exchange rate changes may cause minor over- or under-spend in donor currency.
Donor-currency expenditure review

Reports of expenditure versus budget in donor currency must be run for awards and match funds. Reviews must identify miscoding, missing accruals and variances. Regular monitoring ensures variances remain manageable.
Programmatic alignment verification

Country offices must confirm that programmatic deliverables funded by the award have been achieved. Financial claims must align with programmatic performance obligations. Off-system match contributions must be separately reviewed and authorised.
Final review and authorisation

The final donor report figures must be reviewed and authorised by the country director and country finance director. This authorisation must occur before hard close of the reporting period. No further adjustments are permitted after hard close without escalation.
Performance reporting linkage

Programme staff must prepare performance reports alongside financial reporting. Reports should compare actual achievements to planned objectives. Explanations must be provided for unmet goals, cost overruns, or high unit costs.
Adjustments identified after hard close

Manual changes to centrally generated donor reports are not possible after hard close. If an adjustment is identified, the country director must notify the member and regional finance director. The member determines whether to disclose, manually adjust, or take no action.
Post-close reconciliation requirements

If a member manually adjusts a donor report, the adjustment must also be entered into Agresso. This ensures donor reports reconcile to accounting records. All such adjustments must be fully documented for audit purposes.
Locally submitted donor reports

For locally received awards, members may delegate report preparation and submission to country offices. The country finance team must prepare reports in donor format and reconcile them to Agresso. An independent review must be performed by someone other than the preparer.
Approval & submission controls

The country director must authorise locally prepared donor reports. Member approval must be obtained before submission to the donor. Evidence of approval must be retained for audit.
Documentation & audit trail

All donor reporting records, adjustments, approvals and reconciliations must be retained. Documentation must support a clear audit trail from Agresso to donor submission. Records must be retained in line with donor and organisational retention requirements.
Practical compliance checklist (actionable items)

1) Run standard donor report by member from Agresso after hard close and use it as authoritative base.
2) Verify all transactions posted for the period before hard close (including accruals, severance, partner reports).
3) Confirm donor exchange rates in Agresso and apply donor-specified rates/methods where required.
4) Reconcile sub-award items: ensure Dr 5201/Cr 5200 postings and reconcile advances/expenses.
5) Identify miscoding and recode before hard close.
6) Check GiK & match are recorded, evidenced and authorised.
7) Prepare member-level adjustments (ICR, bank interest) but ensure they reconcile to Agresso SCI.
8) Country director + country finance director sign-off before hard close.
9) Retain documentation and approvals for audit retention period.
10) If post-close adjustments arise, notify member/regional finance director immediately and ensure any accepted manual adjustment is reproduced in Agresso.
Controls & segregation of duties

Ensure the preparer of locally produced reports is not the reviewer/approver. Ensure member adjustments reconcile to Agresso SCI lines. Maintain separation between programme staff preparing performance reports and finance staff preparing financial reports to preserve independence in verification.
Reporting escalation & decision points

If material variances, unresolvable miscoding, unreconciled partner items, or exchange-rate driven variances occur at or after hard close, escalate to member and regional finance director. Member decides disclosure/adjustment approach and documents rationale. All decisions and supporting evidence must be retained.
Audit preparedness

Keep supporting schedules that show reconciliation from Agresso SCI report to donor submission (including member adjustments). Retain sub-award supporting documentation, partner reports, GiK evidence and match documentation. Ensure accruals and provisions have appropriate authorisation and backup.

Country offices: implement programmes, post and code expenditure accurately and promptly, prepare Agresso donor-currency reports, reconcile and provide supporting documentation, prepare locally-delegated donor reports where applicable, obtain independent review, secure CD & CFD sign-off.

Chapter 19 — Effort Reporting (Personal Activity Reports)
Context & donor compliance background

Save the Children works with numerous institutional and government donors worldwide. Over time, many donors have introduced increasingly strict regulations requiring organisations to charge staff costs based only on the actual time worked (effort) on a specific donor-funded award. These requirements are designed to ensure that personnel costs claimed are accurate, reasonable, and directly attributable to donor-funded activities.

The United States Government has long enforced these requirements through OMB Circular A-122, while other donors—most notably the European Commission—have adopted similar effort reporting and time certification standards. Failure to comply with these donor requirements exposes Save the Children to a high risk of salary costs being declared ineligible, leading to disallowed expenditure, repayment to donors, audit qualifications, and reputational damage.
Overall purpose of effort reporting

Effort reporting is the formal mechanism used to demonstrate that staff costs charged to donor awards are valid and compliant with donor regulations. It provides assurance that payroll costs recorded in the accounting system accurately reflect the actual work performed by employees. This is achieved through reconciliation of payroll allocations to after-the-fact documentation of staff time and activities. Effort reporting is therefore a critical compliance, audit, and internal control requirement for donor-funded programmes.
Key points (mandatory requirements)

• Where a country office (CO) requires the recording of staff time against awards, effort reporting must be completed by all staff in that country programme, within the required timelines.
• Effort reporting is the method by which SCI demonstrates that staff costs charged to a donor award are valid and allowable.
• SCI demonstrates validity by reconciling the following for each staff member charged to a donor award:
– the allocation of salary costs across the awards on which the staff member has worked; and
– the actual activity of the employee, based on after-the-fact reporting of time spent on each award as recorded in the staff member’s timesheet.
• A standard reconciliation tool (“EFRP template”) is used for all staff subject to effort reporting.
• All SCI employees (national or international) working in a CO that is fully or partially funded by a donor requiring effort reporting must submit accurate timesheets that reflect total actual hours worked, not budgeted or planned hours.
• The country finance director must prepare effort reporting reconciliations on a quarterly basis for all relevant staff.
• Where reconciliations identify differences between salary cost allocation and actual time spent, correcting adjustments are mandatory to ensure payroll costs align with effort.
• Completed reconciliations and related adjustments must be submitted to the regional finance director for review no later than the end of the month following the end of each quarter.
Scope of application

Effort reporting applies to all SCI staff—national and international—whose salaries are charged wholly or partially to awards funded by donors that require effort reporting. This applies regardless of job function, seniority, or contract type, unless specific donor rules permit an alternative treatment.
Roles and responsibilities — country finance director (CFD)

The country finance director is responsible for:
• effectively implementing the effort reporting and timesheet system within the country office;
• ensuring staff and supervisors are trained on accurate completion and timely submission of timesheets;
• preparing quarterly effort reporting reconciliations using the approved EFRP template;
• submitting reconciliations to the country director for approval and to the regional finance director for review;
• identifying, documenting, and processing correcting payroll adjustments arising from reconciliations;
• ensuring all adjustments are posted accurately and promptly in the accounting system;
• maintaining complete supporting documentation and audit trails.
Roles and responsibilities — employees

All employees funded wholly or partially by donor awards requiring effort reporting are responsible for completing and submitting accurate, complete, and timely timesheets. Timesheets must reflect the actual work performed and account for the total time for which the employee is compensated.
Roles and responsibilities — supervisors

Supervisors are responsible for ensuring that employees complete timesheets accurately and submit them when due. Supervisors must review timesheets for reasonableness and accuracy and certify them based on first-hand knowledge of the employee’s activities.
Definition of the effort reporting system

The effort reporting system is:
• the prescribed method of certifying to certain donors that staff costs charged to their awards are valid;
• a listing of actual staff costs incurred on specific awards;
• a formal method of documenting employees’ work time and allocation to funding sources;
• the basis for billing donor and other funding sources for personnel costs.
Policy and documentation requirements

Effort reporting must be implemented using employee timesheets (Personal Activity Reports) and any required supplementary records. The system and procedures must be formally documented within SCI policies and procedures and must be capable of withstanding donor and external audit scrutiny.
Non-thematic staff treatment

For non-thematic staff (such as senior management or shared support roles), each country office must calculate an appropriate basis for charging staff costs to donor awards, in line with donor regulations and Award Management guidance. Timesheets must still be completed; however, they may not be used directly for charging purposes. The allocation methodology must be reasonable, documented, consistently applied, and defensible during audit.
Timesheets (Personal Activity Reports) — overview

Separate detailed guidance has been issued on the completion of timesheets and is available on OneNet. Country offices must ensure that only the most up-to-date approved version of the timesheet template is used.
Timesheet frequency and payroll alignment

Timesheets must align with the employee’s payroll period. Staff paid monthly must complete monthly timesheets; staff paid weekly must complete weekly timesheets; and staff paid on other cycles must complete timesheets corresponding to those cycles.
Timesheet content requirements

Each timesheet must account for the total activity the employee is required to perform under the terms of their employment contract and for which they are compensated. This includes programme, administrative, management, and supportive activities, ensuring that total effort equals total compensated time.
Timesheet certification and approval

Timesheets must be signed either by the employee or by a responsible supervisory official who has first-hand knowledge of the activities performed. The signature certifies that the distribution of activity represents a reasonable estimate of the actual work performed during the reporting period.
Timesheet submission timelines

Timesheets should be submitted to the finance department by month-end to allow timely and accurate salary allocation and quarterly effort reporting reconciliations. Late submission undermines donor compliance and internal controls.
Misrepresentation and disciplinary consequences

Misrepresentations or omissions on timesheets are considered serious violations and may result in disciplinary action. Supervisors are accountable for ensuring accuracy and timely submission of timesheets by their teams.
Effort reporting reconciliation (EFRP)

The effort reporting reconciliation template (EFRP) and detailed guidance for its completion are available in the finance section of OneNet. The template provides a structured, auditable method to reconcile payroll allocations to recorded effort for each staff member and award.
Quarterly reconciliation process

Quarterly reconciliations compare payroll cost allocations posted in the accounting system with the actual distribution of time recorded on approved timesheets for the quarter. Any discrepancies must be investigated, explained, and corrected through payroll reallocations.
Correcting salary allocation adjustments

Where reconciliations identify misalignment between salary costs and effort, correcting adjustments must be made to ensure that salary costs charged to awards accurately reflect time spent. Adjustments must be timely, justified, and compliant with donor rules.
Submission and review deadlines

Completed quarterly effort reporting reconciliations and all resulting salary apportionment adjustments must be submitted to the regional finance director for review no later than the end of the month following the end of each quarter.
Review of the effort reporting system

The regional finance team undertakes periodic reviews of the effectiveness and integrity of each country office’s effort reporting system. These reviews assess compliance, reliability, and control effectiveness.
Indicators that the system requires improvement

The following issues indicate weaknesses in the effort reporting system and may trigger corrective action:
• late, unsigned, or missing timesheets;
• timesheets appearing to have been signed in batches rather than individually reviewed;
• payroll adjustments or transfers affecting prior periods that are unsupported by justification or made more than 90 days after the fact;
• corrected timesheets and unusually high volumes of payroll cost transfers;
• corrected timesheets near the end of a contract that shift costs to unused funds;
• very small effort percentages spread across many awards without reasonable justification;
• effort certifications that fail to account for administrative or supportive effort as part of total effort.
Compliance risk and escalation

Where systemic weaknesses or non-compliance are identified, the regional finance team may require corrective action plans, additional training, enhanced oversight, or escalation to senior management. Persistent non-compliance increases donor audit risk and may affect future funding.

Chapter 20 — Audit and Control
Purpose & objective
Audit and control provide independent and systematic assurance that Save the Children International (SCI) resources are safeguarded and managed responsibly, transparently, and in full compliance with statutory law, donor requirements, SCI policies, and recognised standards of good financial governance. Effective audit and control frameworks strengthen organisational accountability, protect assets, deter and detect fraud and error, enhance the reliability and integrity of financial and operational reporting, and reinforce donor, regulator, and public confidence in SCI.

At the organisational level, SCI’s external auditor—appointed by the SCI Board—provides an annual independent opinion on the accuracy, completeness, and fairness of SCI’s consolidated financial statements and confirms compliance with applicable UK law, accounting standards, and regulatory frameworks.

At the country level, audit and control systems ensure that all financial transactions and balances are properly authorised, recorded, classified, supported by adequate documentation, accurately reflected in Agresso/GLACOS and related systems, and retained in accordance with statutory, donor, and SCI retention requirements. Country-level controls ensure that donor and statutory audit requirements are met in full; that risks of fraud, misappropriation, error, or misstatement are minimised; that internal control weaknesses are identified and addressed promptly; and that management responds effectively and transparently to audit findings, recommendations, and management letters.
Scope
This chapter applies to all SCI country offices, sub-offices, field offices, and partner-managed activities where SCI funds or assets are involved. It covers all financial and related operational transactions, records, systems, reports, and controls; all audits and reviews (statutory audits, donor audits, member audits, internal audits, management reviews, special investigations, spot checks, and agreed-upon procedures); and all financial documentation, whether physical or electronic, including emails, system-generated records, scanned documents, cloud-based files, and backups.

This chapter must be read in conjunction with, and does not replace, the Fraud and Dishonesty Policy; Treasury and Banking Controls; Procurement and Payments Policy; HR and Payroll Policies; Award Management and Donor Compliance manuals; Information Security and Data Protection Policies; and the Document Retention, Storage, Disposal and Data Protection Policy. Where donor or statutory requirements exceed SCI minimum standards, the stricter requirement applies.
Key principles
1. Auditability: Every transaction must be fully traceable from source documentation through approval, recording, payment, and reporting, and back again.
2. Accountability: Management at all levels is responsible for maintaining accurate records, effective controls, and full cooperation with auditors and reviewers.
3. Independence: Audit and assurance activities must be conducted independently of management influence, with objectivity and professional scepticism.
4. Transparency: Audit findings, reports, and management letters must be shared with regional and centre finance leadership and addressed openly.
5. Compliance: All statutory, donor, and SCI audit requirements must be met in full and on time.
6. Record integrity: Financial records must be complete, accurate, accessible, securely stored, and retained for required periods.
7. Segregation of duties: Key processes must separate initiation, authorisation, execution, and review.
8. Continuous improvement: Audit findings must be translated into corrective actions, tracked to completion, and used to strengthen systems and controls.
Governance & responsibilities (RACI)
Country Director (CD): Overall accountability for the governance and control environment; approves audit terms of reference; ensures unrestricted auditor access; provides leadership during audits; ensures regular and secure system backups; reviews and signs off significant audit findings and management responses; and ensures corrective actions are implemented and sustained.

Country Finance Director (CFD): Operational owner of audit readiness; ensures the completeness and quality of accounting records and audit trails; coordinates audits and reviews; ensures effective filing, document retention, and backups; prepares management responses and action plans; and tracks implementation of audit recommendations.

Regional Finance Director (RFD): Must be notified immediately of donor-specified audits, member audit requests, investigations, and receipt of audit reports or management letters; reviews all audit outputs; challenges the adequacy of management responses; escalates significant issues to the Director of IP Finance and Director of Financial Governance; and supports country offices during audits and remediation.

Director of IP Finance (Centre): Provides strategic oversight of audit and assurance matters; approves investigations; coordinates member audits; and receives escalations of high-risk or systemic issues.

Director of Financial Governance (Centre): Oversees serious control breaches, fraud trends, and governance failures; receives escalations; and determines further action.

Global Assurance (Internal Audit): Independent function reporting to the CFO; plans and conducts internal audits, reviews, and investigations; reports findings independently; and escalates serious issues as required.

All staff: Responsible for complying with policies, maintaining records, safeguarding assets, and cooperating fully with audits and reviews.
20.1 External audit — overview
Country offices may be required to appoint local external auditors to satisfy statutory registration requirements, donor conditions, or member agreements. All external audits must be properly scoped, procured, contracted, managed, and reported in line with SCI standards, donor requirements, and applicable law. Country offices remain responsible for audit quality, timely completion, and the implementation of recommendations.
20.1.1 Types of audit and assurance engagements
Statutory financial audit: Provides an opinion on annual financial statements in accordance with local law and applicable accounting standards.
Donor or award audit: Provides assurance on the use of donor funds against specific award agreements and budgets.
Member audit: Requested by SCI members funding country programmes.
Agreed-upon procedures (AUP): Performs specific tests defined by donors or partners without providing an audit opinion.
Management review: Assesses the effectiveness, efficiency, and adequacy of controls or operations; results in recommendations rather than an opinion.
Special investigations: Conducted in cases of suspected fraud, losses, or serious irregularities; must be agreed in advance with the Director of IP Finance and handled under the Fraud and Dishonesty Policy.
20.1.2 Selection of external auditors
Auditors must be professionally qualified, licensed, and independent; demonstrate experience with NGOs and donor-funded environments; understand local statutory requirements; and be acceptable to relevant donors. Selection must follow SCI procurement and due diligence procedures, including conflict-of-interest declarations, reference checks, and assessment of independence, technical competence, capacity, reputation, and value for money.
20.1.3 Engagement letter and contract
A written engagement letter or contract is mandatory and must specify: scope and objectives; applicable standards; detailed terms of reference; audit locations and coverage; responsibilities of management and auditors; fees (preferably fixed); payment terms; audit timetable; reporting format; deadlines for draft and final reports; and ownership and confidentiality of working papers. Audit fees should normally be paid only after satisfactory receipt of a signed final report. Audit costs should be budgeted within donor awards where allowable.
20.1.4 Terms of reference (ToR)
The ToR must clearly define objectives, scope, period under review, locations (country office, sub-offices, partners), sampling expectations, reporting requirements, timelines, and language. Donor-specified ToRs take precedence. Management should review and agree the audit approach with auditors before contract signature.
20.1.5 Audit approach and conduct
Auditors may review policies and procedures; test transactions and balances; interview staff and management; visit sub-offices and partners; and assess governance and risk management. Chapter 21 — Thematic, Cross-Thematic and Non-Thematic Costs
Purpose & objective
Provide a single, auditable framework for classifying, coding, allocating and reporting all costs between thematic, cross-thematic and non-thematic project types used by Save the Children International (SCI) country offices. The purpose is to ensure consistent internal cost reporting that:
• accurately reflects what the country office spends on programmatic themes and corporate support;
• enables transparent calculation of member contributions to country support costs that cannot be recovered from award budgets;
• supports budgeting, donor proposal development (full cost recovery guidance), internal management reporting, and auditability; and
• preserves donor charging practice (costs should still be charged to awards where appropriate), while keeping internal thematic classification separate from donor cost classifications.
Scope
Applies to all country office costs (main office and sub-offices), regardless of funding source, including: staff costs, operating costs, capital purchases, goods-in-kind, partner costs, travel, vehicle & fleet costs, IT, training, consultants, and procurement. Excludes supplier/vendor payments that are explicitly budgeted and managed by programmes as contractual external vendors and are recorded in AP where contract terms require.
Core principle
Classify every cost to one of: thematic project (specific theme & sub-theme), cross-thematic project (multi-theme programme management/M&E/advocacy where allocation to themes is not practical), or non-thematic project (country office support functions). The project code used on source documents and journal entries determines thematic classification and supports internal reporting. Classification must reflect the economic substance of the transaction and the primary beneficiary of the cost.
Key definitions & classification principles — Definitions
• Project code: unique ledger/project reference linked to theme & sub-theme; primary mechanism for classification.
• Thematic project: project code that maps to a single programme theme and sub-theme (e.g., Education, Health, Nutrition). Used when costs are directly attributable to delivery of that theme.
• Cross-thematic project: project/sub-theme used when costs relate to programme management, M&E or advocacy across multiple themes and the split by theme cannot be easily or reasonably identified.
• Non-thematic project: project/sub-theme representing country office support functions (e.g., Finance, HR, Logistics). Non-thematic regardless of location (main office/sub-office) or theoretical apportionability.
• Apportionment: systematic allocation of an item of cost across multiple project codes based on a transparent basis (FTE, time, usage, headcount, floor area, trips, vehicle kms).
• Full cost recovery: inclusion of all relevant thematic and non-thematic costs (direct and apportioned indirect) in budgets/proposals to seek adequate funding.
• Donor charging vs internal classification: donor accounting and internal thematic classification are separate; costs must be charged to awards as permitted by donor rules but still classified internally to theme/cross/non-theme for SCI reporting.
Classification rules (principles)
1. Direct attribution first: allocate costs directly to a thematic project/sub-theme when the cost is clearly for that theme (staff delivering the theme, programme supplies, partner payments for theme activities, programme travel).
2. Apportionment when identifiable & practical: where costs benefit multiple thematic projects and the attributable amounts can be identified without excessive effort, apportion across those thematic project codes using an auditable basis. Document methodology and calculations.
3. Cross-thematic where allocation impractical: where costs relate to multi-theme programme functions (M&E, advocacy, programme management) and splitting by theme is impractical or too onerous, post to a cross-thematic project/sub-theme. Use cross-thematic sparingly and justify in the payroll/project pack.
4. Non-thematic for support functions: costs required to run the country office and not program delivery should be coded to the appropriate non-thematic sub-theme (51–66 as listed below). Do not split non-thematic classification just because costs could theoretically be apportioned to programmes.
5. Consistency & documentation: maintain consistent conventions per cost type and document any apportionment rules in the cost allocation methodology file. Retain source documents with project code, rationale and approver.
6. Donor compliance: always ensure donor-chargeable items are posted to donor awards where allowable even if internal classification is non-thematic or cross-thematic; include donor references in the narrative for audit traceability.

Apportionment methodology — principles & examples
When to apportion:
• Where a cost benefits multiple thematic projects and individual consumption can be identified/reasonably estimated (e.g., vehicle exclusively used by two programmes for extended period; shared training for two themes), apportion across thematic projects.
• Apportion only where the administrative burden is proportionate to the benefit and not “too laborious” in practice. If it is too complex, consider cross-thematic coding with justification.
Acceptable apportionment bases (ordered by preference):
1. Direct usage measures: time sheets, travel logs, vehicle kms by programme, number of beneficiaries per programme, quantity distribution by project.
2. FTE / staff time: documented timesheets or agreed FTE split certified by line managers.
3. Cost drivers: headcount, number of active project months, percentage of programme budget, floor area usage.
4. Standardised proxies: where above are not available, use standard proxies approved by CFD and documented (e.g., 60/40 split for dual finance/grants function).
Apportionment rules & documentation:
• All apportionments must be recorded in an Apportionment Schedule showing: cost item; period; total amount; drivers; calculation; project codes and amounts; responsible approver; date.
• Apportionment schedules become source documents and must be kept in payroll/project packs and linked to GL postings/journals.
• Recurrent apportionments (monthly rent split) should use a standing schedule with version control; one-off apportionments require narrative and sign-off.
• Materiality threshold: define a local materiality threshold (e.g., >0.5% of monthly gross payroll or >USD 500) above which timesheet or formal apportionment evidence is required. Below threshold, administrative simplification may apply, documented in policy.
Example apportionments:
• Office rent (shared): apportion by programme headcount or by floor area occupied by programme teams. Journal: Dr Rent Expense (Project A) / Dr Rent Expense (Project B) — Cr Rent payable / Bank.
• HR staff who split time 70/30 between programmes: record salary charge to Project A (70%) and Project B (30%) with timesheet/manager confirmation.
• Vehicle used 40% for Programme X and 60% for Programme Y: apportion fuel, maintenance & depreciation by usage logbook percentages.
