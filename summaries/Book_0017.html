Chapter 1 — International GAAP
Overview
• Scope: governance, structure and due process of the IFRS Foundation; role, composition and remit of the IASB, IFRS Interpretations Committee (IFRS IC) and new International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB); IASB technical agenda and relationship with US GAAP; global adoption/endorsement approaches and enforcement/consistency issues.
• Key messages: IFRS accounting standards are now one of two globally recognised frameworks (with US GAAP); adoption methods vary (full adoption, convergence, endorsement); consistent application, audit and enforcement in jurisdictions are required to realise comparability benefits; Foundation remit extended to sustainability disclosure standards via the ISSB.
• Quick facts from chapter text: IFRS required for all or most publicly accountable entities in c.144 jurisdictions; Foundation Constitution revised 2021 to establish ISSB; Foundation funding largely voluntary and diverse; Foundation maintains jurisdictional profiles (168 jurisdictions completed at time of writing).
1 Why international financial reporting standards matter
• Economic rationale: globalisation of goods/services/capital increases need for comparable financial reporting to facilitate cross-border capital raising, investor decision-making and lower cost of capital.
• Historical milestone: EU’s June 2000 announcement (apply IAS/IFRS for listed EU companies by 2005) accelerated global adoption; by the chapter’s writing IFRS and US GAAP are the principal global frameworks.
• Practical implication: comparability is only achieved if standards are applied, audited and enforced consistently; adoption alone is insufficient.
2 The IFRS Foundation and the IASB
2.1 The standard-setting structure
• Structure components: Trustees (governance), Monitoring Board (public authority link), IASB (technical standard-setting), IFRS IC (interpretation/consistent application), Advisory Council, Accounting Standards Advisory Forum (ASAF), other consultative/advisory bodies, ISSB, DPOC (Due Process Oversight Committee).
• “IFRS accounting standards” defined to include: IFRS (IASB), International Accounting Standards (IAS) (IASC legacy), Interpretations (IFRS IC / SIC) and IFRS for SMEs.
2.2 The IFRS Foundation
• Constitution & review: Foundation Constitution governs Trustees; five-year structure/effectiveness review required (last revised Sep 2021 to establish ISSB and align interconnectivity).
• Trustee composition requirements: geographic balance — six Asia/Oceania, six Europe, six Americas, one Africa, plus three from any area subject to balance. Typical Trustee term: three years, renewable once; appointments by existing Trustees subject to Monitoring Board approval.
• Trustee selection criteria: collective balance of professional backgrounds (regulators, investors, auditors, preparers, users, academics), global experience, financial literacy and time commitment. Procedures exist to solicit nominations (consultation with public interest bodies).
• Trustee duties (selected, not exhaustive): appoint IASB/ISSB/IFRS IC/Advisory Council/ASAF members; appoint Executive Director (with Chairs’ consultation) and set performance criteria; review strategy annually for Foundation/IASB/ISSB (but not determine IASB technical agenda); establish/maintain financing arrangements and approve annual budget; establish/amend operating procedures and consultative arrangements; oversee due process and the DPOC; foster educational programmes; publish annual report and audited financials.
• Funding: mainly voluntary contributions (jurisdictions, seed funding for ISSB, philanthropic grants, firms including accounting firms); many jurisdictions use levies or public financing; earned revenue from licensing intellectual property; major 2022 funders included the European Commission, international accounting firms, Canada and Germany (per chapter).
2.3 The Monitoring Board
• Purpose: formal public-authority oversight link to Trustees, replicating national oversight relationships; preserve IASB/ISSB independence while reinforcing public interest oversight.
• Mission summary: promote IFRS accounting & sustainability disclosure standards; monitor/reinforce Foundation public oversight; participate in Trustee selection/approval; advise Trustees on regulatory/legal/policy matters and funding; discuss regulatory/market developments relevant to IFRS and ISSB work.
• Responsibilities: participate in Trustee appointment process and approve appointments; review/advise on Trustee performance (Trustees report annually); meet Trustees at least annually and may request additional meetings with Trustees or IASB/ISSB Chairs.
• Membership snapshot and criteria: membership drawn from major capital market authorities (examples given: IOSCO Board; IOSCO Growth & Emerging Markets Committee; Japan Financial Services Agency (chair); European Commission; US SEC; Brazil CVM; Korea Financial Services Commission; China Ministry of Finance; UK FCA; Basel Committee observer). Membership criteria include being a capital markets authority, commitment to IFRS adoption or convergence, market significance, enforcement capacity, funding contribution and national standard-setter engagement.
2.4 The International Accounting Standards Board (IASB)
• Membership & composition: Trustees appoint IASB members; IASB normally comprises 14 members (chapter reference) chosen for technical competence and diverse international business/market experience (auditors, preparers, users, academics, regulators). Expected geographic mix: 4 Asia/Oceania, 4 Europe, 4 Americas, 1 Africa, 1 any area (subject to balance). No individual should simultaneously be a Trustee and IASB member.
• Powers & responsibilities: full responsibility for IASB technical matters — prepare/issue IFRS accounting standards and exposure drafts (must include dissenting opinions), approve IFRS IC interpretations; set technical agenda (discretionary but must consult Trustees and Advisory Council on major projects); undertake public agenda consultation every five years; ensure IASB standards are compatible and avoid inconsistency with ISSB standards; establish liaison with national standard-setters.
• Voting thresholds & meeting practice: approvals for exposure drafts and standards require approval by at least eight members if fewer than 14, or nine if 14 members; other decisions (e.g., discussion papers) require simple majority with at least 60% member attendance; IASB meets monthly (not in August), meetings open to public with materials posted online.
2.4.1 Agenda consultation
• Five-yearly public consultation required to gather views on strategic direction, balance of IASB activities and priority-setting criteria; latest consultation began March 2021 with a July 2022 feedback statement and adopted priorities for 2022–2026 including: progress current work plan projects; complete PIRs of IFRS 9 and conduct PIRs for IFRS 15 and IFRS 16; initiate research/projects on intangible assets and statement of cash flows; maintain a reserve list (operating segments, pollutant pricing mechanisms); coordinate with ISSB (management commentary, intangible assets research); start a narrow-scope project on climate-related accounting where needed; complete second comprehensive review of IFRS for SMEs and publish educational material.
2.5 The IFRS Interpretations Committee (IFRS IC)
• Role and remit: supports consistent application by timely assessment, discussion and resolution of application issues within the IFRS framework; tackles issues with a widespread or material effect, that reduce diversity in practice, that can be resolved within existing IFRS/Conceptual Framework, and are sufficiently narrow yet cost-effective to address.
• Process for issues: national standard-setters/regional bodies often consulted; IFRS IC may add items to its work programme, issue tentative agenda decisions if not adding items (normally ≥60-day comment period), then confirm agenda decision, revise/re-expose, add to work programme or refer to IASB.
• Interaction with IASB: before publishing an agenda decision, the IASB is asked at its first practical public meeting whether it objects; if four or more IASB members object the agenda decision is not published and IASB decides next steps.
• Composition & governance: IFRS IC has 14 voting members; chair appointed by Trustees (may be IASB member or qualified individual) but chair does not vote; regulatory representatives may attend as non-voting participants; observers include Basel Committee, European Commission and IOSCO; quorum is 10 members; approval of draft/final interpretations requires that not more than four voting members vote against.
• Meetings & ratification: IFRS IC meets about six times a year in public sessions; interpretations developed by IFRS IC must be ratified by IASB.
2.5.1 Agenda decisions
• Purpose: explain why a standard-setting project has not been added and provide explanatory material on application of existing IFRS to described fact patterns; cannot change or add requirements to IFRS standards but explanatory material derives authority from the standards and entities must apply standards reflecting such explanatory material.
• Implementation timing guidance: IASB recognises agenda decisions can introduce persuasive new information; entities are not automatically in error for prior applications inconsistent with an agenda decision; IASB expects companies to have “sufficient time” (judgement; IASB suggested “a matter of months rather than years”) to implement accounting policy changes resulting from agenda decisions; companies should implement changes promptly and consider disclosure about implementation if material. The Due Process Handbook amended Aug 2020 to reflect these positions.
2.6 The Due Process Handbook
• Oversight: DPOC (Trustees’ Due Process Oversight Committee) oversees due process for IASB and IFRS IC across development stages including agenda-setting and PIRs.
• August 2020 revisions highlights: clarified authority/role of IFRS IC agenda decisions and formal inclusion of IASB in finalisation; emphasised the role of effect analyses at all stages of standard-setting; streamlined consultation requirements for adding major projects to IASB work plan; enhanced minimum review for Foundation educational material; clarified DPOC’s oversight of IFRS Taxonomy due process.
• Core due process principles: Transparency; Full and fair consultation; Accountability (including analysis of effects and rationale in Basis for Conclusions).
• Mandatory steps required before issuing standards/interpretations: debate proposals in public meetings; publish exposure drafts with minimum comment periods; consider comment letters timely; consider re-exposure where needed; consult Advisory Council and ASAF on work plan/major projects; ratify interpretations in public IASB meetings.
• Non-mandatory (comply-or-explain) steps: publishing discussion papers prior to exposure drafts; establishing consultative or specialist advisory groups; holding public hearings; undertaking fieldwork. If non-mandatory steps are omitted the IASB must notify DPOC and publish reasons in decision summaries and Basis for Conclusions.
• Comment periods: typical exposure draft comment period is 120 days; for narrow/urgent matters comment period can be not less than 30 days after DPOC consultation/approval; in exceptional ‘fast track’ urgent cases, with 75% Trustee approval, comment period may be reduced below 30 days but cannot be eliminated.
2.7 The IFRS Advisory Council
• Purpose: forum for geographically and functionally diverse organisations/individuals to provide input on IASB/ISSB agenda, priorities, project timetables, and application/implementation issues; flag matters for IFRS IC attention.
• Composition & meetings: 30+ members with diverse backgrounds; chair appointed by Trustees (cannot be IASB/ISSB member); normally meets at least twice a year in public; members serve initial three-year terms with possible extensions up to three additional years.
2.8 Accounting Standards Advisory Forum (ASAF)
• Purpose: advisory forum of national standard-setters/regional bodies to provide technical advice/feedback to IASB and to coordinate national/regional views on major technical issues.
• Membership & meetings: 12 non-voting members (appointed by Trustees) representing national standard-setters/regions, plus IASB chair/vice-chair as chair; geographical spread aims for 1 Africa, 3 Americas, 3 Asia/Oceania, 3 Europe, 2 any area; meets about 4 times a year (some videoconference meetings); meetings are public.
2.9 Other advisory bodies
• Formal advisory/consultative groups listed: Integrated Reporting & Connectivity Council; Capital Markets Advisory Committee; Emerging Economies Group; Global Preparers Forum; IFRS Taxonomy Consultative Group; Islamic Finance Consultative Group; SME Implementation Group; Consultative Group for Rate Regulation; Management Commentary Consultative Group. These groups provide project-specific input and assist IASB outreach.
2.10 The International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB)
• Background & establishment: Trustees formed task force (Oct 2019), public consultation (Sept 2020) and amended Constitution (Nov 2021) to create ISSB within Foundation governance to set IFRS sustainability disclosure standards and provide a global baseline for sustainability-related financial disclosures for investors.
• Consolidation & collaboration: Foundation consolidated Climate Disclosure Standards Board (CDSB) and Value Reporting Foundation (VRF — integrated reporting + SASB) into ISSB; collaboration agreement signed with Global Reporting Initiative (GRI) in Mar 2022 to coordinate standard-setting/activity where appropriate.
• Structure & outputs: ISSB has multi-location presence (Americas, EMEA, Asia), comprises 14 members plus advisory groups; May 2023 issued Request for Information on Agenda Priorities (comment period to Sept 2023); June 2023 ISSB issued inaugural standards IFRS S1 (General Requirements) and IFRS S2 (Climate-related Disclosures), effective for annual reporting periods beginning on or after 1 Jan 2024, with transition reliefs; mandatory application depends on jurisdictional endorsement/regulatory decisions and is not linked to an entity’s use of IASB IFRS accounting standards.
3 The IASB’s technical agenda and convergence with US GAAP
3.1 The IASB’s current priorities and future agenda
• 2023 focus areas (selected): exposure draft on annual improvements; urgent SME amendment aligned with IAS 12 temporary measures (OECD international tax reform relief); project direction set on business combinations under common control; research on climate-related effects on financial statements and potential accounting responses; exploration & evaluation disclosures project; exposure draft to clarify IAS 32 equity vs liability classification challenges; continuing implementation of projects into 2024+.
3.2 IFRS accounting standards/US GAAP convergence
• Convergence history: intensive IASB–FASB convergence activities 2002–2013; US SEC actions (permitting foreign private issuers to file IFRS without reconciliation from 2007; proposed roadmap 2008; 2011 staff work plan) produced mixed outcomes; by 2013 convergence ceased being a major agenda driver and IASB refocused on single-set high-quality global standards. The Handbook revised in 2013 removed convergence as an influential agenda factor.
4 The adoption of IFRS accounting standards around the world
4.1 Worldwide adoption overview
• Adoption modes: full adoption of IASB-issued IFRS; endorsement/adoption process (e.g., EU/UK); convergence (local GAAP aligned to IFRS but may contain mandatory or optional departures); voluntary adoption for certain issuers; IFRS for SMEs adoption by smaller/private entities in some jurisdictions.
• IFRS 1 First-time Adoption: entities asserting compliance with IFRS must apply IFRS 1 when first asserting compliance; local amendments or deviations from IASB text preclude an entity from claiming compliance with IASB-issued IFRS.
• Jurisdiction profiles: Foundation provides profiles for jurisdictions (168 profiles at time of writing).
Selected jurisdiction summaries (detailed)
• Australia — Australian Accounting Standards (AAS) are IFRS-equivalent word-for-word with Australian-specific paragraphs for NFP/public sector; required for publicly accountable for-profit private sector entities; non-publicly accountable entities can apply AAS recognition/measurement with the Simplified Disclosure Standard (SDS) option; SDS mirrors IFRS for SMEs disclosures with Australian overlays; AAS compliance allows explicit statements of compliance with IASB IFRS for for-profit entities.
• Brazil — Local CPCs converged with IFRS since 2010; regulated public companies required to comply (with some sectoral exceptions for banks/real estate historically); CPCs used for non-public companies; industry-specific local policies (e.g., homebuilding revenue recognition) remain.
• Canada — Publicly accountable enterprises required to apply IFRS as Canadian GAAP from 1 Jan 2011 (some temporary deferrals expired); AcSB adopts IASB standards as Canadian GAAP and may issue wraparound exposure drafts and stakeholder outreach; non-public entities may use other bases derived from Canadian standards or voluntarily use IFRS.
• Mainland China — ASBE substantially converged with IFRS but with specific differences (e.g., common-control business combinations, impairment reversal prohibition for long-lived assets, fair value model restrictions for investment property); MOF Road Map (Apr 2010) requires ASBE for listed companies, selected financial enterprises and large/medium enterprises; ongoing cooperation with IASB (2005/2015 Beijing joint statements) to maintain convergence; MOF issued interpretations in 2022–23 to align certain items (deferred tax single-transaction exemption, income tax on dividends on equity-classified instruments, share-based payment reclassification) with IASB amendments.
• Hong Kong — HKFRS (including HKAS and Interpretations) fully converged with IFRS since 1 Jan 2005 subject to limited exceptions; HKICPA may add disclosures or, in rare cases, deviate; HKFRS applied by Hong Kong incorporated entities (listed & non-listed); a SME/local small-entity framework exists.
• Japan — Voluntary adoption permitted for eligible listed companies that meet governance conditions; standalone financials remain Japanese GAAP; endorsement mechanism used to designate IASB IFRS for voluntary adoption; Japan’s Modified International Standards (JMIS) were developed (goodwill amortisation and OCI recycling) but currently unused; ~270 companies had adopted IFRS voluntarily by July 2023 (representing >45% of market cap).
• India — Ind AS (converged IFRS) phased in from financial years beginning on/after 1 Apr 2016 with mandatory and optional departures; central bodies (ICAI, NFRA, MCA) and sectoral regulators (RBI, IRDA) involved; companies not in roadmap may voluntary adopt Ind AS irrevocably (except NBFCs); SEBI required mutual fund AMCs to prepare financial statements in Ind AS from 1 Apr 2023 (first Ind AS MF statements year ending 31 Mar 2024).
• Korea — K-IFRS (IFRS as adopted in Korea) required for listed entities, unlisted financial institutions and state-owned entities; permitted for non-listed entities.
• Mainland China — see above (ASBE).
• Saudi Arabia — IFRS as adopted by local regulators required for banks, insurers and listed entities; other frameworks (IPSAS, AAOIFI) may apply to government or Islamic institutions; non-listed entities may choose IFRS or IFRS for SMEs.
• Singapore — SFRS(I) converged with IFRS required for Singapore incorporated listed entities; foreign entities listed in Singapore required to file SFRS(I), IFRS or US GAAP depending on status; Singapore incorporated entities may file IFRS with approval.
• South Africa — JSE required IFRS for all listed companies from 1 Jan 2005; Companies Act (effective 1 May 2011) allows frameworks by public interest score; listed companies required to use IFRS; other entities may apply IFRS, IFRS for SMEs or entity-specific accounting for micro-entities; local Financial Reporting Guides supplement IFRS application.
• Spain, France, Germany, Italy — follow EU-adopted IFRS rules per EU Regulation; many EU members permit EU IFRS application in non-listed consolidated and separate statements; Italy extends EU IFRS use in some separate financial statements by exchange rules (subject to exceptions).
• Switzerland — Listed issuers incorporated in Switzerland and listed under SIX International Reporting Standard must apply IFRS or US GAAP; other listed entities may apply IFRS, US GAAP or Swiss GAAP-FER; IFRS permitted in consolidated statutory financial statements for listed/non-listed entities.
• Taiwan — Taiwan-IFRS (endorsed local standards) effective dates generally aligned with global dates (effective from 1 Jan 2023 for standards/interpretations listed); IFRS permitted for foreign issuers with reconciliation to Taiwan-IFRS.
• Thailand — Thai Accounting Standards substantially converged with IFRS; effective dates often one year later than IASB; IFRS permitted for foreign companies.
• UAE — Companies generally required by law to apply IFRS; government and Islamic entities may use IPSAS or AAOIFI respectively.
4.2 Europe
4.2.1 EU
• Regulation No 1606/2002 required consolidated financial statements of listed EU companies to use EU-adopted IFRS by 2005; Regulation includes an endorsement mechanism to adopt IASB standards into EU law to ensure public oversight and to assess conformity with EU public policy.
• Endorsement process: European Commission (EC) decides after advice from EFRAG and vote by Accounting Regulatory Committee (ARC) of member states; EFRAG plays a central advisory role, conducting proactive outreach, field tests and advice; endorsement is completed only when published in the Official Journal of the EU.
4.2.1.A Endorsed IFRS accounting standards
• Endorsement record: most IASB standards effective have been endorsed for EU use except certain carve-outs and non-endorsements (IAS 39 carve-out for macro fair value hedge accounting; decision not to endorse IFRS 14; pending endorsement of IAS 12 amendments re Pillar Two Model Rules).
• Specific EU carve-out example: IAS 39 carve-out permitting macro fair value hedge accounting in EU where IASB removal of carve-out in IFRS 9 did not address EU concerns; EU also provided an optional exemption when endorsing IFRS 17 to allow opt-out from annual cohort requirement for intergenerationally mutualised and cash-flow-matched contracts (article requires disclosure if exemption used; review clause set for 31 Dec 2027).
4.2.1.B Evaluation of the Regulation
• 2014 EC evaluation and 2015 report: concluded IFRS created a common accounting language and remains dominant; identified areas for improvement.
• 2018 HLEG on Sustainable Finance recommended that adoption consider European public good and sustainability objectives; EC’s 2018 Fitness Check consulted widely and most respondents supported status quo and cautioned against EU-specific carve-ins that risked fragmentation.
4.2.2 United Kingdom
• Post-Brexit regime: from financial years beginning on or after 1 Jan 2021 UK incorporated companies not applying UK GAAP use UK-adopted international accounting standards (UK-adopted IAS) rather than EU IFRS; UK Endorsement Board has authority to endorse new/amended standards; entities listed on non-UK regulated markets must ensure they follow the appropriate applicable framework (EU-adopted IFRS or IASB IFRS) as required.
4.3 Americas
4.3.1 US
• Convergence note: see 3.2; US SEC permits foreign private issuers to file IFRS financial statements without reconciliation to US GAAP; there is no US endorsement mechanism for IASB IFRS for domestic issuers.
4.3.2 Canada
• IFRS mandatory for publicly accountable enterprises from 1 Jan 2011; AcSB integrates IASB standards into Canadian GAAP for those entities, issues wraparound EDs for Canadian input and may perform outreach; AcSB may modify standards but intends to avoid changing IASB requirements when adopting them as Canadian GAAP wherever possible.
4.3.3 Brazil
• CPCs converged with IFRS since 2010; CVM-regulated public companies must make formal IFRS compliance statements for consolidated financial statements; banking regulators may require additional local rules; insurance sector adopted CPCs/IFRS in 2011.
4.4 Asia
4.4.1 Mainland China
• ASBE convergence path: MOF/CASC-led ASBE series introduced since 2006 with Road Map (Apr 2010) mandating ASBE for listed companies and many large entities; ASBE substantially converged with IFRS with China-specific rules retained where necessary; 2015/2015 joint meetings with Trustees reaffirmed convergence objective and established further cooperation; MOF issued interpretations during July 2022–June 2023 to align certain tax/share-based payment rules with IASB amendments. Notable divergences: absence of IFRS-equivalent for common-control business combinations and prohibition on reversing impairment for long-lived assets in ASBE.
4.4.1.B Hong Kong
• HKFRS fully converged with IFRS since 2005; HKICPA may introduce additional disclosure requirements or, in exceptional cases, deviate (transitional exceptions noted); a small/SME reporting framework exists for eligible small groups.
4.4.2 Japan
• Voluntary adoption regime with endorsement/designation of IASB standards for voluntary use; standalone financials remain Japanese GAAP; JMIS exists as an alternative modified IFRS (amortise goodwill; recycle OCI) but is unused; voluntary adopters represent a minority of listed companies but a significant portion of market capitalisation (~270 companies by July 2023).
4.4.3 India
• Ind AS introduced via MCA notifications (Feb 2015) with phased application from Apr 2016 for many entities; central regulator/standard-setter framework involves ICAI, NFRA and MCA; sectoral regulators (RBI, IRDA) set implementation timetables for banks/insurers with some deferrals; voluntary adoption option exists but irrevocable; SEBI’s 2022 amendment mandates Ind AS for mutual fund AMCs from 1 Apr 2023 (first Ind AS mutual fund financials year ending 31 Mar 2024).
4.5 Australia
• AAS regime: Australian Accounting Standards are IFRS-equivalent for for-profit private sector entities, with Australian paragraphs for NFP and public sector; compliance with AAS generally equates to compliance with IASB IFRS for entities that are publicly accountable and do not elect SDS.
• Simplified Disclosure Standard (SDS): available to non-publicly accountable for-profit private sector entities (and certain not-for-profit/public sector reporting entities) that are required to prepare statements that comply with accounting standards; SDS applies IFRS recognition/measurement but reduced disclosures closely based on IFRS for SMEs; SDS financial statements are general purpose but cannot claim compliance with IASB IFRS.
4.6 South Africa
• Adoption: JSE required IFRS for listed companies from 1 Jan 2005; Companies Act (effective 1 May 2011) allows application of different accounting frameworks depending on an entity’s public interest score (listed companies required to use IFRS; others may use IFRS, IFRS for SMEs or entity-specific accounting for micro-entities).
• Local guidance: prior standard-setter Accounting Practices Board issued Financial Reporting Guides used by IFRS reporters in South Africa; additional disclosure/requirements from JSE and Companies Act overlay IFRS requirements.
5 Consistency in application of IFRS accounting standards
• Core point: adoption is necessary but not sufficient; consistent application, auditing and enforcement are required to achieve comparability benefits such as lower cost of capital and improved market functioning (evidence stronger where enforcement is robust).
• Enforcement mechanisms and cooperation: ESMA developed common enforcement priorities and guidelines (effective Dec 2014) to harmonise EU enforcement; Foundation has Statements of Protocols with ESMA and IOSCO to support consistent application and cooperation; US SEC uses filing reviews and bilateral/multilateral cooperation to enforce IFRS application in foreign private issuers’ filings.
• Tools for enhancing consistency: post-implementation reviews (PIRs) of major standards (IFRS 9 completed; IFRS 15 and IFRS 16 PIRs planned/ongoing); IFRS IC agenda decisions to reduce diversity; ASAF and national standard-setters’ liaison; educational material and Foundation outreach.
• Practical note for preparers: if jurisdiction endorses or modifies an IFRS standard locally, that local version is the applicable financial reporting basis — modifications preclude claiming compliance with IASB-issued IFRS.
6 Summary
• Status: IFRS accounting standards and US GAAP remain the two primary global frameworks. While a single worldwide accounting regime has not been fully achieved, widespread adoption, convergence and institutional governance have established IFRS accounting standards as “International GAAP” in practice.
• Governance & future orientation: IASB and ISSB operate independently but with high interconnectivity; coordination is required to ensure compatibility between financial reporting standards and sustainability disclosure standards; agenda-setting emphasises high-quality standard-setting, PIRs, effect analyses and stakeholder consultation.
Appendices, procedural details and practical checks
Due process minimum steps
• Mandatory: public debates/meetings; exposure drafts with minimum comment periods (normally 120 days); timely consideration of comments; consult Advisory Council and ASAF on major projects; ratify interpretations in public IASB meeting.
• Non-mandatory (comply-or-explain): publish discussion papers before EDs; set up consultative/advisory groups; hold public hearings; fieldwork. IASB must notify DPOC and explain omission.
IFRS IC agenda decision mechanics
• Tentative agenda decision published with at least ~60-day comment period; after comment review IFRS IC may confirm, revise/re-expose, add to work programme or refer to IASB; IASB may object (4+ IASB member objections block publication). Agenda decisions explain application of existing standards and cannot introduce new requirements but have persuasive authority and require timely company consideration and implementation as appropriate.
Voting thresholds & quorums
• IASB: approval of exposure drafts and standards normally requires at least 8 votes if fewer than 14 members (9 votes if 14 members); other decisions require simple majority with at least 60% member attendance.
• IFRS IC: 14 voting members; quorum 10; approval of draft/final interpretations allowed if not more than four voting members vote against. Chair does not vote; observers may attend without voting rights.
ISSB transition & application
• ISSB inaugural standards IFRS S1 and S2 effective for annual periods beginning on/after 1 Jan 2024; transition reliefs provided; mandatory adoption dependent on jurisdictional endorsement/regulation; application of ISSB standards is independent from application of IASB financial reporting standards (entities may apply one without the other, depending on jurisdiction).
Jurisdictional endorsement and carve-outs
• EU endorsement mechanism (Regulation 1606/2002) uses EFRAG advice and ARC votes; endorsement ends with publication in Official Journal. EU has used carve-outs (IAS 39 macro hedge carve-out) and optional exemptions (IFRS 17 annual cohort exemption for certain life-insurance contracts) subject to disclosures and review clauses (e.g., review by 31 Dec 2027). UK now uses UK-adopted IAS post-2021 with its own endorsement arrangements.
Practical actions for preparers and auditors
• When asserting compliance with IFRS ensure you apply IASB-issued standards in full (use IFRS 1 on first assertion). If local endorsement or convergence produced deviations, do not claim compliance with IASB IFRS unless full alignment is achieved.
• Monitor IFRS IC agenda decisions, IASB PIRs and national endorsement developments; allow sufficient time for implementation of agenda-decision-driven accounting policy changes; disclose implementation progress if material.
• Maintain close liaison with national regulators and enforcers, apply the local endorsed standard text where applicable, and document sources and calculation conventions to support consistent application.

Chapter 2 — The IASB’s Conceptual Framework
Overview
• Finalised Conceptual Framework for Financial Reporting published March 2018; broadly effective for preparers from 1 January 2020 where references updated.
• Replaces carried-forward material from the 1989 Framework and amends parts of 2010 Framework; some standards still reference earlier versions where the Board decided not to update references immediately.
• Purpose: provide coherent concepts to assist the IASB in developing IFRSs, to help preparers develop accounting policies where no specific IFRS applies (or where a choice exists), and to assist all parties to understand and interpret IFRS.
• Not a standard and does not override any specific IFRS; the Board may specify requirements that depart from the Framework but will explain departures in the basis for conclusions.
What you need to know
• The Framework sets the objective of general purpose financial reporting and the concepts (qualitative characteristics, elements, recognition, measurement, presentation and disclosure, capital concepts).
• Use when no standard applies or when a standard permits policy choice; preparers must consider definitions, recognition criteria and measurement concepts from the Framework.
• Emphasis on relevance and faithful representation; enhancing characteristics: comparability, verifiability, timeliness, understandability; pervasive cost constraint.
1 Introduction
What is a conceptual framework
• A conceptual framework is a statement of generally accepted theoretical principles forming the frame of reference for standard-setting and accounting practice—answers which events to account for, how to measure them and how to communicate them.
Historical development
• Early academic work (Paton & Littleton 1940; AAA committees) influenced initial frameworks.
• IASB–FASB joint project (2004→2010 first phase), restart as IASB-only project (2012), DP July 2013, ED May 2015, final Framework March 2018.
1.2 Why a conceptual framework is necessary
• Provides a theory for testing practical accounting solutions and guides consistent standard-setting; reduces ad hoc and mutually inconsistent rules.
• Addresses recurring theoretical tensions (substance vs form, neutrality vs prudence, balance-sheet vs matching).
• Recognises it is not a panacea—judgement remains necessary; standards may diverge from Framework (examples: IAS 38 goodwill treatment; IAS 12 deferred tax recognition rules).
2 Development and effective date
2010, 2013, 2015, 2018 timeline
• 2010 Framework partly joint with FASB; 2018 is full revision replacing earlier carried-forward material.
Use by Board and IFRS IC
• IASB and IFRS Interpretations Committee began using 2018 Framework on issuance; IFRS IC will refer issues to the Board if inconsistency between a standard and 2018 Framework arises.
Amendments to references
• Board issued Amendments to References to the Conceptual Framework (Mar 2018) updating references in Standards where appropriate; effective for periods beginning on or after 1 Jan 2020.
Exceptions and transitional references
• Some pronouncements continue to refer to the 1989 or 2010 Framework (examples listed in standards: IFRIC 12, IFRIC 19 reference 1989; IFRS 14, IFRIC 20, IFRIC 22 reference 2010; IFRS 3 transitional reference to 2010 until at latest 1 Jan 2022).
3 Contents, purpose and scope
Structure
• Introductory section on status and purpose, 8 chapters, Appendix of defined terms (editorial extraction of definitions).
Chapters list
• Chapter 1 Objective; Chapter 2 Qualitative characteristics; Chapter 3 Financial statements & reporting entity; Chapter 4 Elements; Chapter 5 Recognition & derecognition; Chapter 6 Measurement; Chapter 7 Presentation & disclosure; Chapter 8 Concepts of capital & capital maintenance.
Status and purpose
• Assist Board in developing IFRSs, preparers in developing policies where no standard applies or choice exists, and all parties to interpret IFRS.
• Framework is not authoritative over standards; revisions do not automatically amend standards—standards change only via normal due process.
• Framework frames Board’s public-interest mission: transparency, accountability, economic efficiency.
4 Chapter 1 Objective of general purpose financial reporting
Core objective
• To provide financial information about the reporting entity that is useful to existing and potential investors, lenders and other creditors in decisions about providing resources to the entity (buy/sell/hold equity or debt; lending or settling loans; exercising governance rights). [CF 1.2]
Primary users
• Existing/potential investors, lenders and other creditors are primary users; reports are prepared for users with reasonable knowledge who will perform diligent analysis. [CF 1.5, CF 2.36]
Scope of information
• Users need info on amount, timing and uncertainty of prospective net cash inflows and on stewardship (management’s use of resources). [CF 1.3, CF 1.4]
4.1 Objective usefulness and limitations
Usefulness
• Enables assessment of returns (dividends, principal, interest, market value increases) via forecast of net cash inflows and stewardship evaluation.
Limitations
• Reports do not supply all information needed by providers of capital (macro, industry or non-financial forward-looking intel); not designed to show entity value but to help users estimate it.
Estimates and judgement
• Financial statements rely on estimates/judgements and models—Framework sets concepts to guide these but perfection is unlikely; transparency and disclosure needed. [CF 1.11]
4.2 Information about resources claims and changes
Economic resources and claims
• Reporting should convey nature and amounts of resources (assets) and claims (liabilities/equity) to assess liquidity, solvency, priority of claims and stewardship. [CF 1.13-1.14]
Changes in resources and claims
• Financial performance and other events (issuances, repayments) change resources/claims; balance-sheet approach derives performance from movements in position; accrual accounting underpins period recognition. [CF 1.15-1.18]
Stewardship
• Information showing how efficiently and effectively management used economic resources helps forecast future use and hold management accountable. [CF 1.22-1.23]
5 Chapter 2 Qualitative characteristics of useful financial information
Framework view
• Fundamental characteristics: Relevance and Faithful representation.
• Enhancing characteristics: Comparability, Verifiability, Timeliness, Understandability.
• Pervasive cost constraint limits the provision of information. [CF 2.1, CF 2.23, CF 2.39]
5.1 Fundamental qualitative characteristics
Relevance
• Information capable of making a difference via predictive value and/or confirmatory value; materiality is entity-specific and depends on nature/magnitude in context—no uniform quantitative threshold. [CF 2.6-2.11]
Faithful representation
• Depiction must be complete, neutral and free from error (practicable); represent substance over legal form where substance differs. [CF 2.12-2.15]
Prudence
• Prudence = caution in judgements under uncertainty to support neutrality; does not permit biasing information to systematically under/overstate. [CF 2.16-2.17]
Measurement uncertainty
• Reasonable estimates are acceptable; high uncertainty does not automatically preclude usefulness if transparently described. [CF 2.19]
5.1.3 Applying the fundamental characteristics
• Process: identify phenomenon → choose most relevant information type → assess if it can be faithfully represented → if not, iterate to next best. [CF 2.21]
• Trade-offs possible: highly relevant information with high uncertainty may still be presented with disclosures; where measures lack faithfulness, choose less relevant but more faithful alternatives. [CF 2.22]
5.2 Enhancing qualitative characteristics
Comparability
• Enables identification of similarities/differences across entities/periods; consistency supports comparability but is not the same thing; uniformity is not required and making unlike things look alike is undesirable. [CF 2.24-2.29]
Verifiability
• Information is verifiable when independent observers can reach consensus that depiction is faithful; includes direct (observe) and indirect (check inputs & recalculation) verification. [CF 2.30-2.32]
Timeliness
• Information must be available early enough to influence decisions; older information may still be timely for trend analysis. [CF 2.33]
Understandability
• Clear classification, characterisation and concise presentation; prepared for users with reasonable business knowledge; complex items should not be excluded merely because they are complex—disclosure required. [CF 2.34-2.36]
Applying enhancing characteristics
• Iterative application; may be necessary to sacrifice one enhancing characteristic to improve another (e.g., prospective change to accounting reduces short-term comparability); use disclosures to mitigate. [CF 2.37-2.38]
5.3 The cost constraint
• Benefits of information must justify costs to preparer and users; cost ultimately borne by users via reduced returns.
• Board assesses costs/benefits via outreach; requirements may differ by entity size, capital-raising method and users’ needs. [CF 2.39-2.43]
6 Chapter 3 Financial statements and the reporting entity
Role of financial statements
• Provide information about assets, liabilities, equity, income, expenses, unrecognised items, cash flows, equity contributions/distributions, and methods/judgements used. Recognised items must meet element definitions. [CF 3.1, CF 5.1]
Reporting period and comparatives
• Prepared for a specified reporting period; comparative information for at least one preceding period to identify trends. [CF 3.4-3.5]
Forward-looking information and subsequent events
• Forward-looking info included if it relates to assets/liabilities or income/expenses for the period and is useful; post-period events included when necessary to meet objectives. [CF 3.6-3.7]
Perspective and going concern
• Reports present entity-level perspective; normally prepared on going concern unless entity intends or needs to liquidate/cease trading, in which case different basis disclosed. [CF 3.8-3.9]
6.2 The reporting entity
Definition and boundaries
• A reporting entity is any entity required or choosing to prepare financial statements; can be single legal entity, portion, consolidated group, combined entity; not necessarily a legal entity. [CF 3.10]
Consolidated versus unconsolidated
• Consolidated statements include parent and subsidiaries as a single reporting entity (useful for parent’s investors because distributions from subsidiaries affect parent cash flows). Unconsolidated show parent alone and may be supplementary to consolidated statements but cannot substitute when consolidation is required. [CF 3.11, CF 3.15-3.18]
Combined and carve-out statements
• Combined financial statements acknowledged as potentially useful; Framework does not specify preparation rules—Board may develop future standard. Carve-out discussion is silent but similar considerations apply. [CF BC3.21, CF 3.12-3.14]
7 Chapter 4 Elements of financial statements
Elements defined
• Assets: present economic resources controlled by entity from past events.
• Liabilities: present obligations to transfer economic resources as a result of past events.
• Equity: residual interest in assets after deducting liabilities.
• Income: increases in assets or decreases in liabilities that increase equity, other than owner contributions.
• Expenses: decreases in assets or increases in liabilities that decrease equity, other than distributions to owners. [CF 4.1-4.4, CF 4.68-4.71]
Other changes
• Exchanges that do not change equity, and owner contributions/distributions, are other changes distinct from income/expenses.
7.1 Matters concerning both assets and liabilities
Unit of account
• The right/group of rights or obligation(s) to which recognition and measurement apply; selection aims to provide relevant and faithful information while considering cost. May differ by recognition versus measurement. Examples: individual right, single contract, subgroup, portfolio of similar items, portfolio of dissimilar items, risk exposure within portfolio. [CF 4.48-4.55]
Executory contracts
• Contracts equally unperformed create combined right/obligation—treated as single asset if favourable, single liability if unfavourable; recognition depends on recognition criteria and measurement basis, including onerous contract test. [CF 4.56-4.58]
Substance of contractual rights and obligations
• Report substance over legal form; consider explicit and implicit terms unless they have no substance (no effect on economics); group/series of contracts may require combined accounting to reflect commercial effect. [CF 4.59-4.62]
7.2 Definition of assets
Rights
• Rights may arise from contracts, legislation, constructive obligations, or control of non-public know-how; rights include receivables, rights over physical objects, IP, options, forward contracts. Not all rights are assets (public goods, public domain know-how). Treasury shares and intra-group holdings within a reporting entity are not assets of the reporting entity. [CF 4.5-4.13]
Potential to produce economic benefits
• A right is an economic resource if it has potential to produce economic benefits in at least one circumstance; low probability does not preclude asset classification but affects recognition and measurement. [CF 4.14-4.18]
Control
• Control = present ability to direct use and obtain benefits and to prevent others from doing so; arises from legal rights or other means (e.g., ability to keep know-how secret); principal-agent distinctions apply (agent custody does not equal agent asset). [CF 4.19-4.25]
7.3 Definition of liabilities
Obligation
• A duty or responsibility the entity has no practical ability to avoid; may be legal or constructive; obligation is owed to another party even if identity unknown. [CF 4.26-4.31]
Transfer of economic resource
• Obligation must potentially require transfer of economic resource—cash, goods/services, unfavourable exchange, contingent transfers, issuing a financial instrument that obliges transfer. Probability can be low; disclosure may substitute for recognition. [CF 4.36-4.41]
Present obligation from past events
• Obligation arises from past events or from benefits received or actions taken; enactment of legislation creates obligation only when entity has obtained benefits or taken actions to which it applies. Present obligation can exist even if payment/enforcement occurs in future. [CF 4.42-4.47]
7.4 Definition of equity
• Residual interest after liabilities deducted; equity claims do not meet liability definition; classes of equity carry different rights (dividends, liquidation proceeds); legal/regulatory constraints (distributable reserves) may require separate disclosure/presentation. [CF 4.63-4.67]
7.5 Definition of income and expenses
• Income = increases in assets or decreases in liabilities increasing equity other than owner contributions.
• Expenses = decreases in assets or increases in liabilities decreasing equity other than distributions to owners.
• Contributions from owners are not income; distributions to owners are not expenses. Different transactions produce income/expenses with differing characteristics—presentation of components can aid user understanding. [CF 4.68-4.72]
8 Chapter 5 Recognition and derecognition
Recognition process
• Capturing an item that meets element definition into the statement(s) with a monetary amount (carrying amount). Recognition links opening/closing statements and performance via double-entry relationships; recognition decisions affect totals and reconciliation between position and performance. [CF 5.1-5.4]
Recognition criteria
• Only items meeting the definitions may be recognised, and only if recognition provides relevant information and a faithful representation (and costs justified). Not all definitional matches are recognised; judgement required. [CF 5.6-5.9]
Consideration of non-recognition
• If non-recognition, present note disclosure to compensate and ensure visibility—assess whether expense recognition might provide useful trend info. [CF 5.10-5.11]
8.2 Recognition criteria — relevance
Existence uncertainty
• When existence is uncertain and combined with low probability or wide outcome ranges, single-amount recognition may not be relevant; disclose uncertainties. [CF 5.12-5.16]
Low probability of inflow/outflow
• Low probability items may be assets/liabilities but the most relevant info may be magnitude/timing/probability in notes rather than recognition; however, exchange transaction costs/consideration may warrant recognition. [CF 5.15-5.17]
8.2 Recognition criteria — faithful representation
Measurement uncertainty
• Measurement estimates are acceptable; high measurement uncertainty may prevent faithful representation and thus recognition; provide explanatory disclosures where appropriate. [CF 5.18-5.23]
Other factors
• Faithful representation requires appropriate measurement, presentation and disclosure; consider accounting mismatches, related items, and whether presentation/disclosure can achieve faithful depiction. [CF 5.24-5.25]
8.3 Derecognition
• Derecognition = removal of all/part of recognised asset/liability when item no longer meets element definition (loss of control or extinguishment). [CF 5.26]
• Aims: faithfully represent retained components and effect of transaction; usually derecognise transferred/expired components, continue recognising retained components (become separate units of account) and present/ disclose retained/transferred components separately where necessary. [CF 5.27-5.28]
• Do not derecognise if entity retains control or exposure to significant variations in benefits; if derecognition would misrepresent change, consider continuing recognition of transferred component and treat proceeds as loan. [CF 5.29-5.32]
• Contract modifications: evaluate whether modification eliminates rights/obligations (derecognise), adds new ones (recognise as separate), or both (may need derecognition and recognition of new asset/liability when modification effectively replaces original). [CF 5.33]
9 Chapter 6 Measurement
Measurement overview
• Measurement basis = an identified feature (historical cost, fair value, fulfilment value, value in use, current cost) applied to an asset/liability to create a measure; selection guided by relevance, faithful representation, enhancing characteristics and cost constraint. [CF 6.1-6.3]
Framework stance
• No single basis suits all items; standards must choose or guide basis and implementation techniques; may specify techniques or simplified approaches; may use deemed cost at initial recognition in some cases. [CF 6.2-6.3]
9.1 Measurement bases
Historical cost
• Based at least partly on transaction price; updated for consumption (depr/amort), impairment and accruals (amortised cost for financial instruments). Historical cost entry = consideration + transaction costs; liabilities measured as consideration received minus transaction costs. [CF 6.4-6.9]
Current value
• Updated to measurement date reflecting current conditions; includes fair value (market participant exit value), value in use/fulfilment value (entity-specific PV of expected cash flows) and current cost (entry value of equivalent at measurement date). [CF 6.10-6.11]
9.1.2.A Fair value
• Price to sell asset or transfer liability in orderly transaction between market participants at measurement date; reflects market participant assumptions, time value, risk premium, liquidity and credit/own credit risk where applicable; excludes transaction costs on acquisition/disposal. Can be observed or estimated via valuation techniques (CF factors a–e). [CF 6.12-6.16]
9.1.2.B Value in use and fulfilment value
• Value in use = PV of expected cash flows from asset use and disposal (entity-specific). Fulfilment value = PV of cash/resources expected to be transferred to fulfil liability (includes costs to enable fulfilment). Determined via cash-flow techniques and reflect entity assumptions. [CF 6.17-6.20]
9.1.2.C Current cost
• Cost of equivalent asset at measurement date (entry value including transaction costs) or consideration for equivalent liability; different from fair value (exit value); may require adjustment for used asset age/condition. [CF 6.21-6.22]
9.2 Information provided by measurement bases
• Framework summarises how each basis populates statement of financial position and statement(s) of financial performance (see Figure 9-1): which elements appear where (carrying amount, initial recognition effects, consumption/fulfilment treatment, interest, impairment, value changes, holding gains/losses, onerous liabilities).
• Historical cost tends to support margin analysis, amortised interest and impairment recognition. Current values support timely recognition of changes in market expectations and risk pricing.
9.2.1 Historical cost implications
• Relevant when asset/liability acquired on market terms—price reflects expected recoverable benefits/fulfilment obligations; consumption and impairment produce expenses that help analyze margins and management stewardship. Amortised cost for financial instruments gives predictive/confirmatory value. [CF 6.24-6.31]
9.2.2 Current value implications
Fair value
• Reflects market expectations and risk pricing → predictive/confirmatory value; changes may be decomposed into components (interest, credit, liquidity, price changes) for user insight; initial recognition differences may arise if market used differs from transaction market. [CF 6.32-6.36]
Value in use / fulfilment value
• Provide entity-specific present value info useful for forecasting and impairment/testing; fulfilment value particularly useful where liability will be fulfilled rather than transferred. [CF 6.37-6.39]
Current cost
• Useful for predicting margins where prices change materially; requires splitting consumption vs price change (holding gains/losses). [CF 6.40-6.42]
9.3 Factors selecting measurement bases
• Consider relevance (asset/liability characteristics, contribution to future cash flows), faithful representation (measurement uncertainty, accounting mismatches), enhancing characteristics (comparability, verifiability, understandability) and cost constraint; also consider initial measurement alignment with subsequent measurement and the practicality of more than one measurement basis. [CF 6.43-6.48]
9.3.1 Relevance
• Sensitive value items (market sensitive, derivatives) likely need current values; assets used in combination often suited to historical/current cost because margins derived from consumption are useful. For directly saleable assets, current value often most relevant. [CF 6.49-6.57]
9.3.2 Faithful representation
• Measurement uncertainty may make some bases less faithful; using same basis for related assets/liabilities may avoid accounting mismatches. High measurement uncertainty does not automatically preclude a basis, but where all available measures are too uncertain recognition may be inappropriate. Distinguish measurement uncertainty from outcome/existence uncertainty. [CF 6.58-6.62]
9.3.3 Enhancing characteristics & cost
• Fair value enhances comparability when observable in active markets; valuation techniques may be costly and subjective, reducing verifiability and comparability. Historical cost often simpler/cheaper but may be less relevant for market-sensitive items. Using many different bases reduces understandability. [CF 6.63-6.76]
9.3.4 Factors specific to initial measurement
• Initial measurement often aligns with subsequent basis to avoid spurious recognition of income/expense at first remeasurement; in non-market transactions, deemed cost may be used and differences recognized on initial recognition; identify all relevant aspects of non-market transactions (consider recognising additional assets/liabilities or owner contributions/distributions). [CF 6.78-6.82]
9.3.5 More than one measurement basis
• Sometimes use one basis in the statement of financial position (e.g., current value) and a different basis for profit or loss components; then decompose total change and present remainder in OCI so accumulated OCI equals difference between position carrying amount and carrying amount under measurement basis for profit or loss. [CF 6.83-6.86]
9.4 Measurement of equity
• Total equity is residual = recognised assets less recognised liabilities; not equal to market value or sale proceeds; may be measured directly for some classes/components but residual nature means at least one class/component cannot be directly measured. Total equity may be negative depending on recognition/measurement choices. [CF 6.87-6.90]
9.5 Cash-flow-based measurement techniques
• Techniques are not measurement bases; used to implement bases when direct observation not possible (e.g., fair value via DCF).
• Central estimates include expected value (probability-weighted mean), median (more likely than not), and mode (most likely single outcome); each conveys different information and range/distribution disclosure often necessary.
• Outcome uncertainty and measurement uncertainty must be considered; disclosure of ranges and sensitivity is important. [CF 6.91-6.95]
10 Chapter 7 Presentation and disclosure
Presentation as communication
• Financial statements are communication tools—structure, classification and aggregation should enhance understandability, comparability and faithful representation. Primary statements summarise; notes provide detail. [CF 7.1, CF 7.22]
Objectives and principles
• Balance flexibility for entity-specific relevant disclosure with comparability across entities and periods; prefer entity-specific over boilerplate; avoid unnecessary duplication that impairs understandability. [CF 7.4-7.6]
10.2 Classification
Classification of assets and liabilities
• Applied to unit of account; may split into components when characteristics differ (e.g., current/non-current); classification should group like with like and separate unlike items to avoid obscuring information. [CF 7.7-7.9]
Offsetting
• Offsetting (net presentation) groups dissimilar items and is generally inappropriate. Distinct from treating rights/obligations as a single unit of account. [CF 7.10-7.11]
Classification of equity
• Present classes/components separately where characteristics or legal/regulatory constraints differ (e.g., distributable reserves). [CF 7.12-7.13]
10.2.3 Classification of income and expenses
• Classify income/expenses by unit of account or components; separate components (interest, changes in value, impairment) when they have different characteristics and disclosure enhances user understanding. [CF 7.14]
Profit or loss and other comprehensive income
• Statement of profit or loss is primary source of performance; default location for income/expenses is profit or loss. Board may exceptionally require certain current-value changes to be recognised in OCI when that provides more relevant/faithful presentation. Items recognised in OCI may be reclassified to profit or loss in future when reclassification makes profit or loss more relevant; Board may restrict reclassification where no clear period/amount to reclassify exists. [CF 7.15-7.19, CF BC7.24, CF BC7.29]
10.3 Aggregation
• Aggregation reduces detail to summarise information but must not obscure material information; use appropriate levels of aggregation across primary statements and provide detailed notes as needed. [CF 7.20-7.22]
11 Chapter 8 Concepts of capital and capital maintenance
Purpose
• Capital maintenance defines what capital the entity seeks to maintain and distinguishes profit (return on capital) from a return of capital; choice influences accounting model and measurement. [CF 8.4-8.6]
11.1 Financial capital maintenance
• Capital defined as invested money (nominal) or invested purchasing power (constant units). Profit is increase in financial capital excluding owner contributions/distributions. Measurement basis choice depends on capital definition (nominal vs constant purchasing power). Holding gains may be profit under nominal money concept; under purchasing power concept, only gains above inflation are profit (rest treated as capital maintenance adjustment). [CF 8.1-8.7]
11.2 Physical capital maintenance
• Capital defined as physical productive capacity; profit arises only if physical capacity increases, requires current cost measurement; price changes treated as capital maintenance adjustments within equity not profit. [CF 8.3-8.10]

IFRS Practice Statement
• 2010 Practice Statement set non-binding guidance; management commentary is narrative that supplements financial statements with historical explanations, prospects, strategies, significant resources/risks and KPIs—prepared for users and consistent with Framework concepts. [MC.IN2, MC.Appendix, MC.12]
Elements of commentary
• Nature of business; management objectives & strategies; significant resources/risks/relationships; results of operations and prospects; critical performance measures and indicators. [MC.24]

Framework expects closer linkage with sustainability-related disclosures and investor needs.

Practical application guidance and implications
Framework’s role in practice
• Primary guide where IFRS silent or choice permitted—assist in selecting policies, measurement bases, unit of account and presentation.
Recognition decisions
• Apply definitions → relevance & faithful representation tests → cost-benefit constraint → decide recognition vs disclosure.
Measurement decisions
• Consider asset/liability nature, contribution to cash flows, measurement uncertainty, comparability and costs; align initial & subsequent measures where practicable; consider combining measurement bases with OCI if justified.
Presentation and disclosure
• Classify by nature/function/role/measurement; avoid offsetting; use notes to explain methods, assumptions, judgements, unrecognised items, risks and sensitivity; avoid boilerplate.
Common interpretive issues and examples
• Instances where standards diverge from Framework: IAS 38 (goodwill capitalisation), IAS 12 (deferred tax recognition) highlighted as examples where standards require or permit treatments not fully consistent with Framework definitions.
• Combined and carve-out financial statements conceptually acknowledged but detailed guidance absent—Board may issue future standard.
• Management commentary update expected to address investor needs and interaction with sustainability reporting (ISSB).
Executive checklist drawn from Framework
1. Identify primary users and their decision needs.
2. Determine reporting-entity boundary and unit(s) of account.
3. Identify items meeting element definitions (asset, liability, equity, income, expense).
4. Apply recognition criteria: relevance, faithful representation, cost-benefit.
5. Assess measurement alternatives and select basis for initial and subsequent measurement; consider consistency, comparability and verifiability.
6. Evaluate derecognition consequences for transferred/retained components.
7. Classify and present items (statement of financial position, profit or loss vs OCI, notes).
8. Prepare disclosures: measurement methods, judgements, assumptions, sensitivity, unrecognised assets/liabilities, and reconciliation where multiple bases used.
Final recommendations for preparers and standard-setters
• Use Framework as principled starting point where IFRS silent; document and disclose judgements, unit-of-account choices, measurement bases and rationale.
• Prefer entity-specific, informative disclosure over boilerplate; ensure material information is prominent and understandable.
• Maintain version control and basis-for-conclusions documentation when diverging from Framework or when a standard requires departures.

Chapter 3 — Presentation of financial statements and accounting policies
Overview & Purpose
• Scope and aim: Interpret and apply IAS 1 with IAS 8 and related guidance to ensure financial statements present fairly the financial position, performance and cash flows; emphasise comparability, faithful representation and user needs.
• Key themes: presentation format, components of primary statements, statement of changes in equity, disclosure of accounting policies, judgements and estimation uncertainty, selection and change of accounting policies, accounting estimates and corrections of errors, impracticability rules, capital management disclosures, covenant-related disclosures, governance and audit trail.
1 Introduction
Objective and scope of IAS 1
• Sets minimum presentation and disclosure requirements for general purpose financial statements prepared under IFRS; applying IFRS is presumed to achieve fair presentation but additional disclosure may be necessary to achieve faithful representation. [IAS 1.15, IAS 1.17]
Objective and scope of IAS 8
• Provides rules on selecting and applying accounting policies, changes in policies, accounting estimates and correction of prior period errors; sets hierarchy for authoritative guidance when no IFRS specifically applies.
2 The purpose and composition of financial statements
Primary financial statements required
• Statement of financial position, statement(s) of profit or loss and other comprehensive income (single or two-statement approach), statement of changes in equity, statement of cash flows, notes to the financial statements.
Presentation choices and minimum items
• Minimum line items and additional line items required by IAS 1 and other IFRSs; entities provide additional line items when relevant to understanding.
Statement of changes in equity — required content
• Present total comprehensive income, transactions with owners in their capacity as owners (equity contributions, dividends, buybacks), reconciliation between opening and closing balances for each component of equity, including each class of contributed equity, each class of OCI, retained earnings and non-controlling interests. [IAS 1.107-110]
Statement of changes in equity — dividends
• Disclose amounts of dividends distributed to owners and dividends per share on the face of the statement or in the notes. [IAS 1.107]
Statement of changes in equity — retrospective adjustments
• Disclose adjustments to each component of equity separately resulting from changes in accounting policy and from corrections of errors for each prior period and at the beginning of the period; retrospective adjustments are generally to retained earnings unless another component is specified by an IFRS. [IAS 1.110, IAS 8]
Illustration 3-9: Statement of changes in equity
• Worked example explaining retained earnings movements, OCI components net of tax and non-controlling interest, revaluation surplus entries, and detailed footnote calculations showing gross amounts, tax, and NCI allocations. [IAS 1.IG Part I]
3.3.1 Transfers between components of equity
• Transfers (for example, share premium to retained earnings) are permissible if legal steps are taken under local law; may be shown as reduction of one component and increase of another or in a separate component; explanatory disclosures of policy choice required. Consider jurisdictional prohibitions or constraints when drafting policy.
3.4 The notes to the financial statements
Purpose of the notes
• Present basis of preparation, specific accounting policies used, required IFRS disclosures not on face of statements, and additional information necessary for understanding financial statements. [IAS 1.112]
Organisation and presentation
• Present notes systematically to enhance understandability and comparability; cross-reference primary statement line items to notes; ordering may follow entity-specific prominence, grouping by nature (e.g., fair-value-measured items), or follow the order of line items in the primary statements. [IAS 1.113-114, IAS 1.BC76D]
Examples of note order
• (i) Statement of compliance with IFRS, (ii) Material accounting policies, (iii) Supporting information for items on the face of primary statements, (iv) Other disclosures such as contingencies, commitments and financial risk management. [IAS 1.114]
Notes — separate accounting-policy section
• IAS 1 permits the basis-of-preparation and accounting-policy disclosures to be presented as a separate section of the financial statements. [IAS 1.116]
4 Accounting policies — general principles
Why accounting policies matter
• They determine recognition, measurement, presentation and disclosure; IFRSs largely prescribe policies but many standards permit choices requiring judgement and disclosure. IAS 1 provides overarching principles; IAS 8 provides detailed mechanics for selection, changes and errors.
4.1 General principles
• Key principles discussed by IAS 1 and IAS 8: fair presentation and compliance with IFRS; going concern; accrual basis of accounting; consistency; materiality and aggregation; offsetting; profit or loss for the period.
4.1.1 Fair presentation
Fair presentation and compliance with IFRS
• Financial statements should faithfully represent transactions as defined in the Conceptual Framework; applying IFRS with additional disclosure normally achieves fair presentation. [IAS 1.15-17]
Situations requiring additional disclosure
• When compliance with IFRS is insufficient to enable users to understand specific transactions, additional disclosure is required; implementation guidance does not form part of IFRS but is expected to be followed in practice. [IAS 1.15, IAS 8.9]
4.1.1.B The fair presentation override
When a departure from IFRS is permitted
• Exception: in extremely rare circumstances management may depart from an IFRS requirement if complying would be so misleading that it conflicts with the objective of financial statements and the relevant regulatory framework permits the departure. [IAS 1.19, IAS 1.24]
Required disclosures when departing and permitted by regulator
• (a) Management’s conclusion that the financial statements present fairly; (b) statement of compliance with IFRS except for the departure; (c) title of IFRS departed from and nature of the departure, including required treatment vs adopted treatment and reasons why complying would be misleading; (d) financial impact by period presented; (e) if departure occurred in a prior period and affects current amounts, disclose (c) and (d). [IAS 1.20-22]
If regulator forbids departure
• Entity cannot depart — must disclose the IFRS title and requirement, explain why compliance is misleading and disclose adjustments that would be necessary to achieve fair presentation to the maximum extent possible. [IAS 1.23]
4.1.2 Going concern
Assessment
• Management must assess ability to continue as a going concern using all available information at least 12 months from the reporting date; depth of assessment depends on entity’s facts and circumstances. Disclose material uncertainties that may cast significant doubt. [IAS 1.26]
When not a going concern
• Prepare financial statements on an alternative basis (e.g., liquidation basis); disclose basis used, reasons for non-going concern conclusion, and any specific accounting policies developed for the alternative basis. Change from going concern to non-going concern is a change in circumstances and therefore applied prospectively. [IAS 1.25, IAS 8.12]
4.1.3 The accrual basis of accounting
• Financial statements (except cash flows) must be prepared using the accrual basis; recognise elements when definitions and recognition criteria in the Conceptual Framework are met. Accrual accounting depicts effects when they occur, not when cash flows occur. [IAS 1.27-28; CF 1.17]
4.1.4 Consistency
• Maintain presentation and classification from period to period unless a significant change in operations or an IFRS requires change; a change is allowed only if the new presentation is more reliable and relevant and likely to continue; reclassify comparatives when presentation is changed. [IAS 1.45-46]
4.1.5 Materiality and aggregation
• Present each material class separately and avoid aggregation that obscures material information. Materiality judged by nature and magnitude in the context of the financial statements as a whole; primary users are investors, lenders and creditors with reasonable knowledge. Examples of obscuring include vague language, scattered disclosures, inappropriate aggregation or hiding material info with immaterial info. [IAS 1.29-31, IAS 1.7]
4.1.6 Offset
• Do not offset assets and liabilities or income and expenses unless required or permitted by an IFRS or when offsetting reflects substance (e.g., disposal proceeds less carrying amount). Measuring assets net of valuation allowances is not offsetting. Report gains/losses from similar transactions net if appropriate and materiality considered. [IAS 1.32-35]
4.1.7 Profit or loss for the period
• Include all items of income and expense in profit or loss unless an IFRS requires or permits otherwise (items may be presented in OCI). The Conceptual Framework definitions of income and expense are increases/decreases in assets/liabilities other than owner contributions/distributions. [IAS 1.88-89; CF 4.68, CF 4.49]
4.1.8 Practice Statement 2 — Making Materiality Judgements
Nature and scope
• Non-mandatory guidance on making materiality judgements: characteristics of materiality, a four-step materiality process, and guidance on specific areas (accounting policies disclosure, prior period info, errors, covenants, interim reporting). [PS 2]
Four-step materiality process
• Step 1 Identify: find information with potential to be material; Step 2 Assess: evaluate quantitative and qualitative factors in context; Step 3 Organise: structure material information to communicate clearly; Step 4 Review: final check for completeness and aggregation effects. [PS 2.29-65]
4.2 Definition and distinction between accounting policies and accounting estimates
Accounting policy definition
• Specific principles, bases, conventions, rules and practices applied in preparing and presenting financial statements. [IAS 8.5]
Accounting estimate definition
• Monetary amounts subject to estimation uncertainty; outputs of measurement techniques and inputs used to make those estimates (e.g., valuation techniques, expected credit loss models). Changes in measurement techniques/inputs are generally changes in estimates unless they correct an error. When differentiation is difficult, treat the change as a change in accounting estimate. [IAS 8.32-35, IAS 8.32A-B]
Illustrations applying the estimate definition
• Illustration 4-1: Changing valuation technique for investment property is a change in accounting estimate because the accounting policy (fair value model) is unchanged.
• Illustration 4-2: Changing expected volatility input for Black-Scholes measurement of a cash-settled share-based payment liability is a change in accounting estimate.
4.3 Selection and application of accounting policies
When an IFRS specifically applies
• Apply the IFRS and its implementation guidance; do not ignore it because guidance is not part of IFRS — consider it in judgement. Materiality exceptions apply but do not permit deliberate immaterial departures to achieve a presentation. [IAS 8.7-8]
Hierarchy when no specific IFRS applies
• Management shall use judgement to develop a policy that results in relevant and reliable information, considering in descending order: (a) requirements/guidance in IFRSs dealing with similar and related issues; (b) definitions, recognition and measurement concepts in the Conceptual Framework. May also consider recent pronouncements of other standard-setters, other accounting literature and accepted industry practice, provided they do not conflict with (a) or (b). [IAS 8.10-12]
How to apply analogy and avoid cherry-picking
• When developing policies by analogy, apply all aspects of the analogous IFRS that are relevant; do not selectively adopt parts of other bodies’ pronouncements. IFRS IC has emphasised consistent application of analogies.
4.4 Changes in accounting policies
When changes are permitted
• Only if required by an IFRS or if the change results in financial statements that are more reliable and relevant. [IAS 8.14]
Sources of policy change
• (a) Initial application of IFRS with specific transitional provisions (apply those provisions); (b) Initial application of IFRS without specific transitional provisions (apply retrospectively unless impracticable); (c) Voluntary changes (apply retrospectively unless impracticable). [IAS 8.19-20]
Retrospective application mechanics
• Adjust opening balance of each affected component of equity for the earliest prior period presented and restate comparatives as if policy always applied; disclose amounts and explanation. [IAS 8.22-26]
4.4.2 Revaluation exception
• Change to a revaluation policy for intangible assets or PPE is treated under IAS 38 / IAS 16 as a revaluation, not as an IAS 8 change in accounting policy — do not restate prior periods’ carrying value and depreciation under IAS 8. [IAS 8.17-18]
4.5 Changes in accounting estimates
• Estimates arise from measurement uncertainty and are updated when circumstances or new information warrant. Changes are not corrections of prior period errors and are accounted for prospectively: adjust carrying amounts in the period of change and recognise effects in profit or loss in the period of change and future periods as applicable. Examples include ECL allowances, depreciation useful lives, warranty provisions, fair value inputs. [IAS 8.32-38]
4.6 Correction of errors
Definition of prior period error
• Omissions or misstatements in prior period financial statements arising from mathematical mistakes, wrong application of policies, oversights or fraud, using information that was available and could reasonably have been obtained when the prior statements were authorised. [IAS 8.5]
When errors are material or intentionally immaterial
• Financial statements containing material errors, or immaterial misstatements made intentionally to achieve a presentation, do not comply with IFRS. Correct potential current period errors before authorisation for issue. [IAS 8.41]
Correction required
• Material prior period errors must be corrected in the first set of financial statements prepared after discovery by retrospective restatement: restate comparatives or adjust opening balances for earliest period presented. Disclose nature and amounts of corrections and effect on equity. [IAS 8.42-46]

• Beta Co inventory error: restatement adjustments to COGS, profit, income tax and retained earnings; present restated statement of comprehensive income and statement of changes in equity with explanatory notes showing effects and calculations.
4.7 Impracticability of restatement
Definition and tests for impracticability
• Applying a requirement is impracticable when an entity cannot apply it after making every reasonable effort. Impracticability exists if: (a) effects of retrospective application/restatement are not determinable; (b) retrospective application requires assumptions about management’s intent in the prior period; (c) retrospective application requires significant estimates and it is impossible to distinguish information that existed at the prior reporting date from information developed later. [IAS 8.5, IAS 8.50-53]
If impracticable for period-specific effects
• Apply the new policy to carrying amounts as at beginning of the earliest period for which retrospective application is practicable and adjust opening balances of affected equity components for that period. [IAS 8.24]
If impracticable to determine cumulative effect
• Apply the new policy prospectively from the earliest practicable date and disregard earlier cumulative adjustments; disclose circumstances and from when the policy is applied. [IAS 8.25-27, IAS 8.43-47]

Chapter 4 — Non-current assets held for sale and discontinued operations
Overview
• IFRS 5 addresses (a) measurement, presentation and disclosure of non-current assets (and disposal groups) expected to be recovered principally through sale rather than continuing use and (b) identification, presentation and disclosure of discontinued operations.
• Core requirements: classify assets/disposal groups as held for sale when strict criteria are met; measure such items at the lower of carrying amount and fair value less costs to sell (or fair value less costs to distribute for distributions to owners); cease depreciation/amortisation for items classified as held for sale; present held-for-sale assets and the assets and liabilities of disposal groups separately in the statement of financial position; present discontinued operations separately in the statement of comprehensive income and re-present prior periods’ discontinued disclosures.
• Principal judgements and recurring issues: timing of reclassification, whether sale is “highly probable”, definition and scope of a disposal group, which liabilities transfer to purchaser (including deferred tax), measurement of fair value less costs to sell, allocation of impairment within disposal groups (including treatment of goodwill), treatment of intragroup transactions between continuing and discontinued operations, the one-year rule and its exceptions, and retrospective adjustments when classification ceases.
• Users and practical implication: preparers, auditors and users need transparent disclosures of policies, significant judgements, segment disclosures and effects on statement of cash flows and equity (OCI reclassifications). Entities should adopt consistent policies and disclose choices where IFRS 5 is silent.
1 Objective and scope of IFRS 5
Objective
• Prescribe accounting for non-current assets (and disposal groups) held for sale and the presentation and disclosure of discontinued operations.
• Key outcomes required by the standard: measurement at lower of carrying amount and fair value less costs to sell; cessation of depreciation/amortisation for held-for-sale items; separate presentation in statement of financial position and separate presentation of discontinued operations’ results in profit or loss/statement of comprehensive income.
Scope and exceptions
• Classification and presentation requirements apply to all recognised non-current assets and disposal groups. Measurement requirements do not apply to certain items, which remain measured under their specific standards: deferred tax assets (IAS 12), assets arising from employee benefits (IAS 19), financial assets within IFRS 9, investment property accounted for at fair value under IAS 40, biological assets at fair value less costs to sell under IAS 41, and (for entities applying it) insurance contract groups within IFRS 17.
2 Non-current assets and disposal groups held for sale or held for distribution to owners
2.1 Classification of non-current assets and disposal groups held for sale or distribution to owners
Definitions: current and non-current asset
• Current asset: expected to be realised, sold or consumed in entity’s normal operating cycle; held primarily for trading; expected to be realised within 12 months after the reporting period; or cash/cash equivalent unless restricted for at least 12 months.
• Non-current asset: asset that does not meet the definition of current asset. These definitions align with IAS 1 and determine which items fall in scope of IFRS 5 rules.
2.1.1 The concept of a disposal group
• Disposal group = group of assets to be disposed of together in a single transaction and liabilities directly associated with those assets that will be transferred in the transaction. The group may include goodwill allocated to a CGU or an operation within a CGU.
• Only liabilities assumed by purchaser are included; liabilities to be repaid from sale proceeds typically excluded.
• A disposal group may be a single CGU, multiple CGUs or part of a CGU; once recovery becomes principally by sale, that group’s cash flows are less dependent on other assets and may be treated as separate CGU(s).
2.1.1.A Deferred tax assets and liabilities in a disposal group
• Whether deferred tax balances are included in the disposal group depends on whether they transfer to the buyer (common in share deals where tax bases remain with the legal entity) or will be realised/settled by the seller (common in asset deals). Entities must assess domestic tax treatment and transaction form; include only those deferred tax balances expected to be assumed by buyer. Reassess deferred tax measurement where manner of recovery is expected to change from use to sale. Disclose judgements.
2.1.2 Classification as held for sale or held for distribution to owners
Core criteria
• To classify an asset or disposal group as held for sale an entity must demonstrate:
(a) asset/disposal group is available for immediate sale in its present condition, subject only to usual and customary terms for such sales;
(b) sale is highly probable (significantly more likely than probable); and
(c) sale is expected to qualify for recognition as a completed sale within 12 months from the date of classification and the sale is not abandoned (the “one-year rule”), subject to narrow exceptions.
• If criteria cease to be met the asset/disposal group must be declassified and remeasured as required. Classification timing matters: IFRS 5 applies from the date all criteria are met. If criteria are met after reporting date but before authorisation, disclosure is required. The same classification, presentation and measurement rules apply to items held for distribution to owners, with costs to distribute replacing costs to sell.
2.1.2.A Meaning of available for immediate sale
• “Available for immediate sale” means entity has intention and ability to transfer the asset/disposal group in its present condition; delays imposed by seller (e.g., vacating, completing renovations or winding down operations) indicate asset is not available for immediate sale. Examples: headquarters vacating timing, sale with/without operations, renovation or remediation needs — these facts affect availability.
2.1.2.B Meaning of highly probable
Definition and indicators
• Highly probable = “significantly more likely than probable” (with probable = more likely than not). Indicators that must be present (IFRS 5 guidance and IFRS IC commentary): commitment by appropriate level of management to a sale plan; an active programme to locate buyer and complete plan; active marketing at a price reasonable relative to current fair value; expectation that sale will qualify as completed sale within one year (with possible extensions in narrowly defined circumstances); actions indicating unlikely that plan will be significantly changed or withdrawn.
Shareholder approval and evidence
• If shareholder approval is required, assess likelihood of approval. Written consent from majority shareholder before reporting date is strong evidence; dispersed shareholding or controversial rationale reduces likelihood and may mean highly probable criterion not met.
One-year rule and exceptions
• Standard rule: sale expected to complete within 12 months. Exceptions where delay beyond one year does not preclude held-for-sale classification include:
(a) delay due to conditions imposed by others (e.g., regulatory approvals) that cannot be acted upon until after a firm purchase commitment and a firm purchase commitment is highly probable within one year;
(b) buyer unexpectedly imposes conditions after firm purchase commitment and seller takes timely actions with favourable outcome expected;
(c) market deterioration after classification makes sale extend beyond one year but entity actively markets at reasonable price and sale remains highly probable and available for immediate sale.
• No quantitative maximum extension specified; judgement needed and longer expected extensions reduce likelihood of meeting “highly probable”.
Firm purchase commitment
• Defined as a binding agreement with an unrelated party specifying all significant terms (price, timing) and containing a disincentive for non-performance large enough to make performance highly probable. Regulatory contingencies may still allow a firm purchase commitment to exist if other criteria are met.
2.1.2.C Abandonment
• Assets/disposal groups to be abandoned (used to the end of their economic life or closed rather than sold) must not be classified as held for sale because recovery is principally through continuing use. If a disposal group to be abandoned meets discontinued operation criteria, treat as discontinued in the period when it ceases to be used. Temporary suspension of use is not abandonment — apply impairment tests and reassess useful lives under IAS 36, IAS 16/IAS 38 as needed.
2.1.3 Partial disposals of operations
2.1.3.A Loss of control of a subsidiary
• If a plan will result in loss of control of a subsidiary, classify all assets and liabilities of that subsidiary as held for sale when criteria are met regardless of retained interests (e.g., retained associate or JV).
• Debate exists for cases where retained interest is a joint operation (arguable that only disposed parts should be classified as held for sale). Pending any change to standard, entities may have a policy choice whether IFRS 5 applies to loss of control that results from partial disposals or other deemed disposals, but must apply consistently and disclose policy. If subsidiary meets discontinued operation definition, apply discontinued presentation/disclosure requirements.
2.1.3.B Partial disposal of an associate or joint venture
• IAS 28 requires that if a portion of an investment in an associate or JV meets held for sale criteria and is classified as held for sale, account for the retained portion using equity method until disposal of the portion classified as held for sale; upon disposal, subsequent accounting depends on nature of retained interest (IFRS 9 or continued equity method). If classification ceases, equity method applies retrospectively from original classification date.
2.1.4 Deemed disposals
• Deemed loss of control (dilution, contractual expiry, exercise/expiry of potential voting rights, etc.) is not explicitly addressed by IFRS 5. IASB Basis for Conclusions suggests loss of control is a significant economic event and could trigger held-for-sale treatment. IFRS IC has considered deemed disposals and recommended broader consideration; in practice entities need a consistent policy and should disclose.
2.2 Measurement of non-current assets and disposal groups held for sale
2.2.1 Scope of measurement requirements
• Measurement provisions (lower of carrying amount and fair value less costs to sell) apply to all recognised non-current assets and disposal groups except items measured under other standards: deferred tax assets (IAS 12), employee benefit assets (IAS 19), financial assets (IFRS 9), investment property at fair value (IAS 40 fair value model), biological assets at fair value less costs to sell (IAS 41), and insurance contract groups (IFRS 4/IFRS 17). Those excluded items continue to be measured under their applicable standards even when included in a disposal group.
2.2.2 Measurement of non-current assets and disposal groups held for sale
2.2.2.A Measurement on initial classification as held for sale
• Immediately before classification, measure assets and liabilities in accordance with applicable IFRS accounting policies (i.e., the usual measurement basis up to classification date). After classification, measure the asset or disposal group at the lower of carrying amount and fair value less costs to sell.
• Fair value defined by IFRS 13. Costs to sell are incremental costs directly attributable to disposal excluding finance costs and income tax. If sale is expected beyond one year, measure costs to sell at present value and recognise unwinding of discount as finance cost.
• For disposal groups the measurement is applied to the group as a whole; any write-down is allocated among the non-current assets within the scope of IFRS 5 (no allocation to items outside scope). The fair value less costs to sell of a disposal group should reflect market-participant assumptions and may include unrecognised assets or liabilities that market participants would price. Items held for distribution to owners use fair value less costs to distribute.
• Newly acquired assets or disposal groups acquired exclusively with a view to disposal and meeting held-for-sale criteria on acquisition are measured at the lower of their carrying amount had they not been so classified (e.g., cost) and fair value less costs to sell — a business combination may therefore result in measurement at fair value less costs to sell on acquisition.
2.2.2.B Subsequent remeasurement
• Assets classified as held for sale are no longer depreciated or amortised. Interest and other financing costs attributable to liabilities of a disposal group continue to be recognised.
• When remeasuring a disposal group, first remeasure items outside IFRS 5 measurement scope under their respective standards. OCI balances (e.g., revaluation surplus, foreign currency translation reserves, cash flow hedge reserves) related to items in the disposal group will be reclassified on disposal and may produce additional gains or losses on disposal; these are not anticipated in IFRS 5 measurement but are recognised at derecognition in accordance with applicable standards.
2.2.3 Impairments and reversals of impairment
• Initial recognition of asset/disposal group at lower of carrying amount and fair value less costs to sell may create an impairment loss when carrying amount exceeds fair value less costs to sell; recognise impairment under IFRS 5.
• Subsequent increases in fair value less costs to sell are recognised as a gain but only up to the cumulative impairment previously recognised under IFRS 5 (or earlier under IAS 36) in respect of the assets within scope.
• For disposal groups any impairment loss (or subsequent gain) should be allocated to the non-current assets in the group that are within the scope of IFRS 5’s measurement rules. Allocation order: first reduce goodwill in the group (if any), then allocate remaining loss pro rata to other non-current assets within scope based on carrying amounts. Items outside measurement scope (e.g., certain financial assets, deferred tax) are not adjusted by IFRS 5 allocation.
• IFRS 5 is silent on the situation where required impairment exceeds carrying value of non-current assets within its measurement scope. Practice has followed alternative approaches (policy choices): (a) apply impairment to current assets in group, (b) apply impairment to non-current assets outside IFRS 5 scope, (c) recognise a separate liability, or (d) restrict impairment allocation to carrying value of assets within IFRS 5 scope. Entities must choose a policy, apply it consistently and disclose it. The IASB considered this issue but (as of the chapter’s drafting) did not amend IFRS 5.
2.2.4 Presentation in the statement of financial position of non-current assets and disposal groups held for sale
Presentation requirements
• Non-current assets classified as held for sale and assets of disposal groups classified as held for sale must be presented separately from other assets in the statement of financial position.
• Liabilities of a disposal group classified as held for sale must be presented separately from other liabilities in the statement of financial position.
• Assets and liabilities held for sale must not be offset and presented as single net amount. Major classes of assets and liabilities classified as held for sale should generally be separately disclosed either on the face of the statement or in the notes, except where the disposal group is a newly acquired subsidiary that met held-for-sale on acquisition (in which case some illustrations exempt detailed separate disclosure).
• Any cumulative income or expense recognised directly in other comprehensive income relating to a non-current asset or disposal group classified as held for sale should be presented separately (either on the face or in notes) to allow users to identify amounts that will be recognised upon disposal. IFRS 5 implementation guidance illustrates a caption for "Amounts recognised in OCI and accumulated in equity relating to non-current assets held for sale".
Practical presentation approaches
• Common practice: show held-for-sale non-current assets within current assets, on dedicated separate lines or groups, rather than only in notes. Simply noting an asset as held for sale in the notes without separate presentation on the face is not compliant. When classification first occurs, comparative statement of financial position is not re-presented.
2.2.5 Changes to a plan of sale or to a plan of distribution
2.2.5.A Assets or disposal groups to be retained by the entity
• If criteria for held-for-sale cease to be met, the asset/disposal group ceases to be classified as held for sale. If an individual asset/liability is removed from a disposal group, reassess whether the remaining group still meets held-for-sale criteria. If not, measure remaining items individually and reclassify as appropriate.
• Measurement on ceasing classification: measure the asset at the lower of (a) its carrying amount before classification as held for sale adjusted for any depreciation/amortisation and revaluations that would have been recognised had it not been classified as held for sale, and (b) its recoverable amount at the date of the decision not to sell (recoverable amount = higher of fair value less costs to sell and value in use). Any adjustment is recognised in profit or loss from continuing operations in the period criteria cease to be met, except where prior revaluation treatment applies (in which case revaluation accounting applies). If the asset was a subsidiary, joint operation, joint venture or associate, financial statements for periods since classification must be amended accordingly—IAS 28 requires retrospective amendment for associates and joint ventures from original classification date. Entities must disclose effects on results and prior period adjustments.
2.2.5.B Change in method of disposal
• Changing disposal method from sale to distribution to owners or vice versa is treated as continuation of original disposal plan. Do not reverse IFRS 5 accounting at that date; instead apply presentation and measurement requirements applicable to the new disposal method. Date of initial classification remains unchanged, and exceptions to the one-year rule may still be relevant. Recognise changes in fair value less costs to sell or distribute per impairment and reversal rules.
3 Discontinued operations
3.1 Definition of a discontinued operation
• Discontinued operation = a component of an entity that has been disposed of or is classified as held for sale and that either:
(a) represents a separate major line of business or geographical area of operations, or
(b) is part of a single co-ordinated plan to dispose of a separate major line of business or geographical area of operations, or
(c) is a subsidiary acquired exclusively with a view to resale.
• A "component of an entity" is operations and cash flows that can be clearly distinguished, operationally and for financial reporting purposes, from the rest of the entity (i.e., a CGU or group of CGUs when held for use). The adjective “major” applies to both line of business and geographical area; judgment, rationale and disclosures are required under IAS 1 when determining "major". A single co-ordinated plan can permit piecemeal disposals; parts sold within 12 months will be presented as discontinued for the period in which they meet held-for-sale criteria. Abandonment triggers discontinued operation treatment only when use actually ceases.
3.2 Presentation of discontinued operations
Face-of-statement presentation
• Present a single amount on the face of the statement of comprehensive income that comprises:
(a) the post-tax profit or loss of discontinued operations, and
(b) the post-tax gain or loss recognised on the measurement to fair value less costs to sell or on disposal of the assets or disposal group(s) comprising the discontinued operation.
• Provide further analysis (either on face or in notes) into: (i) revenue, expenses and pre-tax profit or loss of discontinued operations; (ii) the gain or loss on measurement to fair value less costs to sell or on disposal; and (iii) the related income tax expense amounts for each of (i) and (ii). The detailed analysis is not required for disposal groups that are newly acquired subsidiaries that met held-for-sale on acquisition.
Attribution and comparative presentation
• Disclose amounts of income from continuing operations and discontinued operations attributable to owners of the parent. IFRS 5 requires re-presentation of prior periods’ disclosures so that the financial statements reflect all operations that have been discontinued by the reporting date for the latest period presented. This means adjustments to comparative periods’ presentation of discontinued operations.
Adjustments related to prior discontinued disposals
• Current period adjustments directly related to disposal of a discontinued operation in a prior period (e.g., purchase price adjustments, indemnities, settlement of retained obligations such as warranties or employee benefit liabilities) should be classified separately in discontinued operations with nature and amount disclosed.
3.2.1 Cash flows attributable to discontinued operations
• Disclose net cash flows attributable to operating, investing and financing activities of discontinued operations either on the face of the cash flow statement or in the notes. Disposal groups that are newly acquired subsidiaries held for sale on acquisition are exempt from this disclosure.
• IFRS 5 does not explicitly state whether proceeds from disposal are attributable to discontinued or continuing operations; entities have a policy choice and must disclose it. Consider separate line for disposal proceeds in the cash flow statement if material.
3.3 Trading between continuing and discontinued operations
Consolidation and elimination requirements
• Discontinued operations remain consolidated until disposal; intragroup transactions between continuing and discontinued operations are eliminated in consolidation per IFRS 10 (IFRS 5 does not override IFRS 10). IFRS IC agenda decision (Jan 2016) emphasised that eliminating intragroup income and expenses is required by consolidation rules and that IFRS 5’s disclosure objective may require additional information when eliminations mask economic effects.
Presentation alternatives and policy choices
• Two practical approaches to elimination of intragroup transactions between continuing and discontinued operations:
(1) eliminate all intragroup transactions without adjustments (strict consolidation elimination), resulting in continuing operations showing only transactions with external parties; or
(2) eliminate intragroup transactions but make adjustments to present how transactions will be reflected in continuing operations going forward (i.e., adjust eliminated balances to show expected future continuing impact).
• IFRS IC noted that failure to eliminate intragroup transactions is inconsistent with IFRS 10; entities may choose approach (2) if it provides more useful information, but must apply chosen policy consistently and provide additional disclosures if intra-group transactions are significant. Illustrative example (Group X with Entity A selling components to Entity B) demonstrates different profit splits under alternatives.
4 Comparative information
4.1 Treatment of comparative information on initial classification as held for sale
4.1.1 Statement of comprehensive income
• For assets/disposal groups not qualifying as discontinued operations there is no special requirement to restate comparative income statement amounts. When a component becomes a discontinued operation, present a separate disclosure of its total results and re-present prior periods presented so that discontinued operation disclosures relate to all operations discontinued by the reporting date for the latest period (IFRS 5 requires re-presentation of prior-period discontinued disclosures).
4.1.2 Statement of financial position
• Do not reclassify or re-present amounts in prior periods’ statements of financial position to reflect held-for-sale classification in the latest period; comparatives remain as originally presented.
4.2 Treatment of comparative information on the cessation of classification as held for sale
• When classification as held for sale ceases, remeasure the asset at the lower of: (a) the carrying amount before classification, adjusted for depreciation/amortisation and revaluations that would have been recognised had the asset not been classified as held for sale, and (b) its recoverable amount at the date of the decision not to sell (recoverable amount = higher of fair value less costs to sell and value in use). Recognise the adjustment in profit or loss from continuing operations in the period the criteria cease to be met, unless the asset was revalued prior to classification (apply revaluation accounting in that case).
• Financial statements for periods since classification must be amended where the asset/disposal group that ceased to be held for sale was a subsidiary, joint operation, joint venture, associate, or portion of an interest in JV/associate. IAS 28 clarifies that for associates and joint ventures amendment is retrospective from the date of original classification as held for sale; practice and IFRS 5 language indicate retrospective amendment for measurement and (in many cases) presentation where the ceased-to-be-held-for-sale item was an investee. When a component ceases to be classified as held for sale but was presented as discontinued, results previously presented as discontinued should be reclassified back into continuing operations for all periods presented.
5 Disclosure requirements
5.1 Requirements of IFRS 5
• Overarching disclosure objective: present and disclose information enabling users to evaluate financial effects of discontinued operations and disposals of non-current assets or disposal groups.
• In the period in which an asset/disposal group is classified as held for sale or sold, disclose in the notes: a description of the non-current asset or disposal group; facts and circumstances leading to the sale or expected disposal; expected manner and timing of disposal; the gain or loss recognised on measuring the asset/disposal group at fair value less costs to sell and, if not separately presented on the face of the statement of comprehensive income, the caption that includes that gain or loss; and, if applicable, the operating segment per IFRS 8 in which the asset/disposal group is reported.
• If classification occurs after the reporting date but before authorisation for issue, disclose the items above. If classification ceases or an individual asset/liability is removed from a disposal group, disclose facts and circumstances and the effect on results for the period and any prior periods presented. Disclose net cash flows attributable to discontinued operations’ operating, investing and financing activities (face or notes).
Practical examples
• The chapter reproduces practical disclosure examples from real companies (e.g., Siemens 2021 and ENGIE 2021 extracts) illustrating how entities disclose descriptions, results, segment presentation and cash flows for discontinued operations.
5.2 Disclosures required by standards other than IFRS 5
• Disclosures required by other IFRS standards do not apply to assets/disposal groups classified as held for sale or to discontinued operations unless that standard specifically requires disclosures in respect of held for sale/discontinued operations, or requires disclosures about measurement of items in a disposal group that are outside IFRS 5 measurement scope.
• IFRS 12 (Disclosure of Interests in Other Entities) applies to interests classified as held for sale, except where IFRS 12 explicitly exempts particular summarised information. IAS 1 requirements on fair presentation and estimation uncertainty (including IAS 1.117–122) may require disclosure of significant judgements and accounting policy choices made when applying IFRS 5 — examples: whether IFRS 5 is applied to all losses of control, how excess impairment is allocated, elimination policy for intragroup balances, and treatment of deferred taxes in disposal groups. Entities should ensure cross-reference to other-standard disclosures where relevant (e.g., IAS 36, IAS 16, IAS 28).
6 Possible future developments
Items considered by IASB and IFRS IC
• Topics raised and considered for potential standard-level actions include: scope of held-for-sale classification (including deemed disposals and loss of control nuances), accounting for disposal groups consisting mainly of financial instruments, the “excess impairment” issue (impairment allocation where required loss exceeds carrying amounts within IFRS 5 scope), reversal of goodwill impairments in disposal groups, allocation of impairments within disposal groups, definition and disclosures for discontinued operations including the meaning of “major”, presentation of OCI related to disposal groups, rules for intragroup transactions between continuing and discontinued operations, applicability of IFRS 12 to subsidiaries classified held for sale, and presentation where a disposal group includes a subsidiary plus other assets.
Board activity and status
• The IASB/IFRS IC discussed multiple IFRS 5 issues; some were referred to IFRS IC (e.g., intragroup transactions). The IASB considered but did not prioritise some amendments in December 2009 and subsequently sought broader feedback. Post-implementation reviews of IFRS 13 (completed Dec 2018) and IFRS 10–12 (completed Jun 2022) were completed; the IASB’s 2015 consultation and the 2021 third agenda consultation gathered mixed feedback on IFRS 5 matters and, as of the chapter, a dedicated IFRS 5 project had not been added to the immediate work plan. Future revision remains possible but timing and scope are uncertain; entities should monitor IASB/IFRS IC developments and disclose significant judgements in the interim.

Core IFRS 1 requirements and implementation interpretation
Date of transition
• Definition — beginning of the earliest period for which full comparative IFRS information is presented. The opening IFRS statement of financial position is prepared at this date.
• Practical point — confirm comparatives required by jurisdictions and stakeholder needs before fixing the date; if company elects to present fewer comparatives under other standards (e.g., IAS 1/IFRS 1 relief in severe hyperinflation cases), document rationale.
Accounting policies
• Consistent IFRS policies — opening IFRS statement must use accounting policies that comply with IFRS and be applied consistently to all periods presented in the first IFRS financial statements unless an IFRS 1 exemption permits otherwise.
Estimates and no hindsight
• Retain previous GAAP estimates — carry forward estimates made under previous GAAP at the date of transition unless objectively incorrect. New estimates required by IFRS at transition should reflect conditions at the date of transition and not later information.
• Evidence requirement — maintain source documents that support prior GAAP estimates and demonstrate why they are not objectively incorrect.
Presentation and comparatives
• Statements required — opening statement of financial position at transition + at least two statements of financial position (most recent year-end and prior year), two statements of profit or loss and OCI, two statements of cash flows, two statements of changes in equity, and full related notes and reconciliations. If any standard permits shorter comparatives document the exception and rationale.
Reconciliations and disclosures
• Mandatory reconciliations — reconciliation of equity under previous GAAP to equity under IFRS at the date of transition and at the end of the latest period presented under previous GAAP; reconciliation of total comprehensive income for the latest period presented under previous GAAP to IFRS. Provide explanations for material adjustments and any cash flow effects. Disclose exemptions used and qualitative effects where required.
Mandatory exceptions from retrospective application
Estimates exception
• Rule — IFRS 1 prohibits using hindsight to revise estimates at the date of transition; retain previous GAAP estimates unless objectively incorrect.
• Implementation control — prepare a schedule listing each estimate (contract provision, warranty, inventory obsolescence, useful lives, fair value estimates, actuarial assumptions), source evidence, and a judgment memorandum explaining acceptability.
Derecognition of financial assets and liabilities
• Rule — avoid reconstructing past derecognition transactions where impracticable; some retrospective derecognition adjustments are restricted.
• Practical step — where evidence for earlier derecognitions is missing, apply the IFRS 1 relief and document limitation. Keep a derecognition evidence log (contracts, settlements, legal documents).
Hedge accounting exception
• Rule — retrospective application of hedge accounting is restricted where it is impracticable to recreate prior documentation, hedge designations, or effectiveness assessments.
• Practical step — collect hedge documentation and effectiveness support; if incomplete, apply relief and disclose the consequential prospective approach to hedge accounting under IFRS.
Foreign currency transactions and advance consideration (IFRIC 22) exception
• Rule — need not apply IFRIC 22 to assets, expenses and income recognised before the date of transition.
• Practical step — document transactions involving advance consideration and the exchange rates used under previous GAAP. For transactions after transition apply IFRIC 22 rules as normal.
Other mandatory exceptions
• Rule — apply other IFRS 1 mandatory exceptions where explicit (see IFRS 1 text D-paragraphs). Maintain a master exceptions register with rationale, supporting evidence and cross-reference to IFRS 1 paragraph numbers.
Exemptions and practical expedients — catalog, decision criteria and templates
General principles for choosing exemptions
• Consistency — apply each chosen exemption consistently across similar items and periods.
• Disclosure — disclose which exemptions are used and estimate qualitative/quantitative effects to the extent reasonably possible.
• Cost-benefit analysis — balance the cost and feasibility of retrospective application against the impact on comparability. Document the analysis in the decision file.
Decision checklist for each elective exemption
• Item — name the exemption.
• Description — short summary of what it permits.
• Quantitative effect — estimate on opening equity and first IFRS P&L where material.
• Pros/cons — practical trade-offs (e.g., audit cost vs comparability).
• Supporting evidence — valuations, contracts, actuarial reports.
• Sign-off — accounting owner, tax, CFO and auditor sign-off required for material elections. Store in evidence pack.
Business combinations and related items
Business combinations exemption
• What it permits — an entity may elect not to restate business combinations that occurred before the date of transition; carry forward goodwill and intangible assets recognised under previous GAAP subject to IFRS measurement rules at transition.
Goodwill at transition
• Impairment requirement — where the exemption is used, test all goodwill for impairment at the date of transition irrespective of indicators; recognise impairment losses in retained earnings if appropriate.
Acquisitions under common control
• Practical guidance — previous GAAP treatments for common control transactions differ widely; IFRS 1 does not prescribe a universal method — disclose policy choice and the effect on opening balances. Maintain legal and transaction documentation.
Provisional amounts and measurement period
• Measurement period adjustments — where provisional amounts were used for business combinations pre-transition, apply measurement-period guidance in IFRS 3 to finalise amounts, documenting changes and their effects on retained earnings at transition date as needed.
Worked illustrations and reconciliations

Deemed cost options for non-current assets
Fair value as deemed cost
• Scope — PPE, investment property (if using cost model), ROU assets, and selected intangibles may be measured at fair value at date of transition and adopted as deemed cost.
• Requirements — obtain appropriate valuation evidence (independent valuations where material) and disclose aggregate fair values and total adjustments from previous GAAP carrying amounts.
• Accounting consequences — deemed cost becomes carrying amount; depreciation/amortisation bases reset; deferred tax consequences must be recognised. Maintain valuation reports and sign-off.

Common pitfalls, controls and corrective actions
Typical pitfalls
• Using year-end instead of average balances for turnover ratios — fix by using (opening + closing) / 2 or rolling averages for monthly forecasting.
• Mixing cash vs accrual sales for DSO — choose credit sales or adjust formula and document approach.
• Ignoring seasonality — perform monthly modelling and use seasonal indices.
• Omitting deferred tax on transition adjustments — always map tax bases and compute deferred tax. Get tax sign-off.
Corrective actions and controls
• Perform reconciliation checks and variance analysis by line item versus prior GAAP. Maintain a validation panel that must be green before distribution.
• Use named ranges and structured references in Excel; avoid hard-coded addresses to reduce formula breakage. Protect formula sheets and lock assumptions.

Worked numeric walkthroughs (detailed calculations using chapter example)
Example assumptions
• Current assets turnover = 196%
• Debtors turnover = 400%
• Creditors turnover = 308%
• Inventory turnover = 385%
• DayBase = 365
Compute DSO
• AR turnover = 400% → 4.00 times per year.
• DSO = 365 / 4.00 = 91.25 → 91 days (round as appropriate for reporting).
Compute DPO
• AP turnover = 3.08 times per year.
• DPO = 365 / 3.08 = 118.51 → 119 days.
Compute DIO
• Inventory turnover = 3.85 times per year.
• DIO = 365 / 3.85 = 94.81 → 95 days.
Cash conversion cycle (CCC)
• CCC = DSO + DIO − DPO = 91 + 95 − 119 = 67 days.
Interpretation
• CCC = 67 days implies cash tied up ~9.5 weeks; quantify funding gap and stress test with seasonal peak liquidity scenarios. Document assumptions and rounding conventions.

How we see it — practical observations, governance and common pitfalls
Strategic considerations
• Exemptions vs full retrospective application — using exemptions reduces implementation cost but may reduce comparability and create future work if a later standard requires full retrospective restatement. Perform cost/benefit analysis and seek auditor/regulator input for material items.
Judgement and documentation
• Document everything — contemporaneous documentation for judgements (completed contracts, discount rates, asset valuations) is crucial for auditability and regulatory review. Maintain a sign-off trail.
Tax complexities
• Deferred tax mapping — transition adjustments often create complex tax consequences. Early involvement of tax specialists is essential to avoid late surprises. Document tax bases and provide illustrative journal entries for tax effects.
Systems and controls
• Operational readiness — IFRS adoption often requires ERP changes, new control points (lease registers, contract repositories) and training for accounting teams. Build a post-adoption control plan.
Disclosure depth and stakeholder needs
• Be pragmatic but transparent — IFRS 1 does not require exhaustive listing of all exemptions used, but best practice is to disclose the major elections and their quantitative effects. Stakeholders and regulators typically expect clear, well-documented reconciliations.
