Audience — workshop design & facilitation guide
Purpose: Help organisers choose and design sessions that fit the right people, produce usable outcomes, and surface practical commitments that participants can implement.
Audience (who & why):
- Not all goals suit all audiences — match goal → audience before you design content.
- Peer-to-peer learning works best when participants have diverse perspectives but a shared goal (e.g., improving project close-out, or data-sharing practice).
- Possible audience mixes: (a) people in similar roles across departments (e.g., finance officers from multiple faculties), (b) people from different institutions in a region, (c) cross-functional project teams (finance, contracts, IP, governance, communications).
- If you are not clear on the audience, potential participants cannot decide whether the workshop is for them. Provide an explicit “Who should attend” line.
- Example promo line: “This workshop will interest research support staff: finance, admin, contracts & IP, governance and communications.”
Goal (define success up-front):
- State a single primary goal (e.g., “Produce an agreed SOW checklist and 3 site-ready templates”).
- List 2–3 measurable outcomes (e.g., templates completed, owners named, follow-up date).
- Set success criteria (e.g., 80% of attendees commit to trialling one template within 2 weeks).
- Map audience → goal: if goal = capacity building, include junior staff as required beneficiaries; if goal = policy harmonisation, include decision-makers.
Hierarchies & participation (design norms):
- Acknowledge status differences explicitly: list roles in participant pack and decide whether meetings are safe for candid input.
- Use facilitation rules to protect junior voices: rotate who speaks first, run anonymous polls, or allocate a "junior voice" slot.
- Balance technical authority with lived knowledge: pair technical leads with operational implementers in exercises.
- If senior leaders attend, set their role (listener/advisor vs decision-maker) and communicate it before the session to avoid dominance.
Structure (session architecture):
- Typical 2.5–4 hr workshop structure: Framing (10–15 min) → Principles & short input (20–30 min) → Hands-on practice (3 × 20–25 min cases) → Synthesis & commitments (20–30 min).
- Use mixed formats: micro-lectures (10–12 min), small-group practicals (10–20 min), paired peer review (6–8 min), plenary debriefs (5–8 min).
- Keep the cognitive load low: one principle per micro-lecture, 1–2 clear outputs per exercise.
- Timeboxing example: Intro (10) → Norms & charter (10) → Exercise 1 write (12) + peer review (6) + plenary (8) → Break (10) → Exercise 2 (as above) → Commitments (15) → Close (9).
Practicalities (logistics & materials):
- Pre-work: short survey (roles, pain points, example documents). Ask for 1 anonymised example of a real problem.
- Room & tech: whiteboard or shared doc, projector, breakout rooms, stable Wi-Fi, and recording for absent partners.
- Handouts: one-page principles, templates, one-line status sticky notes. Provide layered materials: 1-line summary → 1-page templates → annex.
- Supplies: sticky notes, pens, printed templates, name badges showing role (e.g., “Finance — Payments”).
- Accessibility: captions, translated one-pagers if needed, recorded sessions and transcripts.
Facilitation skills (what the facilitator must do):
- Frame outcomes clearly and early: "By the end you will have X, Y, Z."
- Model psychological safety: admit limits, invite dissent, and thank people for flagged issues.
- Manage status: call out silent voices, use round-robin, and handle interruptions with a neutral script.
- Time discipline: visible timer, clear handover to next activity, and tidy recaps.
- Rapid synthesis: convert group outputs into one-line commitments and record owners before moving on.
- Live-editing: capture outputs in a shared doc (editable) so groups see synthesis in real time.
Developing exercises (practical templates):
- Case rewrite (email/template): 6–10 min individual write → 4–6 min peer edit → plenary 5 min. Outcome: one improved template.
- Role-play escalation: 10 min scenario → 10 min role-play in triads → 8 min debrief. Outcome: short escalation script.
- What’s-in/what’s-out: 8 min group list of items required for decision → 5 min consensus → outcome: decision checklist.
- Lightning clinics: participants bring one live example and get 10 minutes peer advice + 5-minute action commitment.
- Equity micro-check: anonymous 3-question pulse after exercise to surface access or credit concerns.
Be prepared to share (deliverables & follow-up):
- At close: one-page summary with templates, decisions, owners, and follow-up date.
- Share pack within 24–48 hours: templates (editable), recording/transcript, action tracker link.
- Set a short review: 2-week sample audit or peer-check meeting scheduled and owners assigned.
- Encourage participants to upload one example of their changed practice to a shared tracker for peer review.
Audience selection checklist (use before inviting):
- Is the primary goal clearly stated? (Yes/No)
- Will the invited group be able to act on outputs? (Yes/No)
- Do you have decision-makers and implementers represented? (Yes/No)
- Is there enough diversity of perspective to enable peer learning? (Yes/No)
- Are accessibility and language needs addressed? (Yes/No)
Sample 90-min programme (copy-ready):
- 0–10: Framing & norms (one-line outcomes, safety rule).
- 10–25: Principles + examples (3 short inputs).
- 25–45: Exercise A — live rewrite (12 write + 8 peer + 5 share).
- 45–55: Break / informal peer clinic.
- 55–75: Exercise B — role-play escalation (15 + 5 debrief).
- 75–85: Commitments (each participant states 1 habit & owner).
- 85–90: Close & share next steps.
Facilitator micro-scripts (copy-ready):
- Opening: "Today we aim to leave with one template you can use tomorrow and one commitment to improve a process."
- Invite input: "Quick round — name one pain you want solved (30s each)."
- Handle silence: "I’ll call on three people to share a quick view — start with [junior name]."
- Close exercise: "Please post your final draft to the shared folder now; we’ll capture owners."
Risks & mitigations specific to audience design:
- Risk: senior attendees dominate → Mitigation: pre-brief seniors on role; use breakout groups without seniors for candid input.
- Risk: mixed technical levels → Mitigation: provide primers and pair high/low expertise for peer teaching.
- Risk: no implementers present → Mitigation: require at least one implementer or decision-owner per table.
Quick templates to include in invite:
- Purpose line: one sentence.
- Who should attend: roles and decision authority.
- Prep required: 5-minute pre-read or one example to bring.
- Outcomes: 3 deliverables and follow-up date.
One-line aide-memo for organisers:
"Choose the audience to match the goal; protect junior voices; design short practice-led exercises that produce tangible outputs; share editable deliverables and schedule a short review."

Table 2
Goal — design, pitch, success criteria & follow-up
Core question: What is your single primary goal for the session? (Skill uplift / sharing practice / networking / responsiveness / proactive relationship-building). State it in one sentence.
Common specific goals (pick one primary + up to two secondary):
- Improve skills: participants complete a hands-on task and leave with an immediately usable template.
- Share practice: surface 6 real examples and a short how-to for each.
- Network: create 8 new named connections per table and a follow-up buddy system.
- Be responsive: solve live problems brought by participants and assign owners.
- Be proactive: create a 6-month collaboration pipeline (seed ideas + owners).
If you met me in an elevator — the 1-minute pitch (templates):
Skill-focused: "This workshop helps research support staff learn one practical template and one short workflow they can use tomorrow to speed grant closeouts."
Practice-share: "We’re sharing three tested approaches for funder reporting from peer institutions so you can adopt what fits your team."
Network: "This session connects finance, contracts and admin staff across universities to solve common grant-management bottlenecks and form peer support pairs."
Responsive / problem clinic: "Bring one real problem; we’ll triage it in a peer clinic and leave with a clear next step and owner."
Why clarity matters:
If the core goal is not explicit participants will judge the session against their expectations and feel disappointed. Always open with a one-line goal and repeat it at the start of each exercise.
Example workshop aim (copy-ready):
"The aims of the workshop are for participants to develop useful professional networks, gain skills and knowledge that will empower them in their job roles, and share their expertise with colleagues from other institutions."
Success — define measurable outcomes (choose 3 metrics):
1) Immediate satisfaction: % participants who say the workshop met expectations (target e.g., ≥80%).
2) Behaviour change: % participants who report implementation within 3 months (target e.g., ≥25%).
3) Tangible outputs: # templates completed, # new cross-institutional collaborations formed, # policies drafted.
4) Network density: average new named contacts per participant.
5) Practical adoption: number of trainings/policies delivered as a result (target e.g., 10–20 within 3 months).
Sample success statement (copy-ready):
"Success = 80% immediate satisfaction; 20% of participants implementing a new policy or training within 3 months; 50 new cross-institution connections recorded."
Use the Tanzania evidence (how to cite in comms):
- Immediate feedback example: "90% said they got what they hoped; many got more than expected."
- Three-month impact example: "20 participants implemented changes: trainings, grant management policies, increased research support in proposals."
- Use these numbers as plausible targets or benchmarks for similar workshops.
Goal → format mapping (practical):
- Improve skills → short demos + hands-on template work + peer review (write 12 min / edit 6 min).
- Share practice → 3× short case clinics + synthesis sheet.
- Network → structured speed-dating rounds + committed follow-up buddy pairs.
- Responsive → problem clinic format with owners and timelines.
- Pro-active → idea pipeline session with seed notes and responsible leads.
Hierarchy & participation rules tied to goal:
- If goal = candid problem-solving, protect junior voices (anonymous submission or breakout without senior leaders).
- If goal = policy alignment, include decision-makers and budget owners.
- State role expectations in invite ("decision-maker / implementer / practitioner").
Success measurement plan (immediate + 3-month):
- Immediate: 3-question exit poll (Did workshop meet expectations? One practical thing you will implement? Who will you contact next week?).
- 1-week: send one-page summary + ask each participant to post one commitment in shared tracker.
- 3-month: short survey + sample audit (ask for one evidence item: training slide, policy draft, procurement change).
- Reporting: produce a 1-page impact brief and share with participants and funder.
If your goal includes networking — make it measurable:
- Target: each participant names 3 new contacts and schedules at least 1 follow-up call within 30 days.
- Track: shared directory entries + confirmation check at 30 days.
Practical tips to align participants to the goal (on invites & start):
- Invite text must state: one-line goal, who should attend, what to bring (one example), and expected outcomes.
- Start script: restate the one-line goal, success criteria, and what "winning" looks like for the room.
- Mid-session check: after each exercise ask "Does this advance our stated goal?" and adjust.
Common goal-failure modes and mitigations:
- Failure: too many goals → cognitive overload. Mitigate: prioritize one core goal and label others as secondary.
- Failure: missing implementers → outputs not adopted. Mitigate: require at least one implementer/decision-maker per table.
- Failure: senior domination → juniors disengage. Mitigate: set talking-order rules and anonymous input.
Short scripts (copy-ready) to state the goal:
- Opening line: "Today’s goal: leave with one usable template and one committed owner to adapt it in your team within two weeks."
- Mid-session reminder: "Quick check — are we on track to meet that goal? If not, what should we change now?"
Be realistic: timeframe vs ambition:
- Short workshops (90–150 min): realistic outputs = 1–2 templates, a few commitments, networking seeds.
- Half-day/full-day: scope for deeper skill practice and a 6-week action plan.
- Multi-day or funded programmes: aim for pilot implementations and policy drafts within 3 months.
Follow-up & accountability design:
- Use an action tracker with columns: Owner | Commitment | Evidence required | Due date | Check date.
- Schedule a 30–60 minute peer-review check-in at 2 weeks and a 60-minute reflection at 12 weeks.
- Ask participants to upload one sample of implemented change (slide, policy excerpt, screenshot) as proof of adoption.
Quick templates to measure success (copy-ready):
- Exit poll Qs: 1) "Did the workshop meet your expectations?" (Yes/No; 1-line why). 2) "Name one action you will take in 2 weeks." 3) "Who will you contact first?"
- 3-month survey Qs: 1) "Did you implement the action?" (Yes/No) 2) "Attach evidence (slide/policy link)" 3) "Rate impact (1–5)".
Final one-line checklist before you run the session:
- Can you state the core goal in one sentence on the invite? (Yes/No)
- Have you chosen 1–2 measurable success indicators? (Yes/No)
- Are implementers/decision-makers present? (Yes/No)
- Is there a 2-week and 3-month follow-up plan with owners? (Yes/No)
