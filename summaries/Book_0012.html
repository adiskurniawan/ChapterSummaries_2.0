Corporate Income Tax — Extracted Topics (v1.3)
Tax rates.
Generally a flat rate of 22% applies, with public companies meeting listing and public-share tests eligible for a 3% cut to an effective 19% rate; small enterprises and certain micro-turnover taxpayers receive special final-tax or discounted regimes.
- Public company cut: 3% off standard rate (→ 19%).
- Small enterprises: 50% discount on standard rate for turnover ≤ IDR 50 billion and special final tax at 0.5% for some ≤ IDR 4.8 billion turnover.
Tax residence.
A company is tax resident if incorporated or domiciled in Indonesia; foreign entities with a Permanent Establishment (PE) are taxed like residents for Indonesian-sourced income.
- Residence by incorporation or domicile in Indonesia.
- Foreign companies with a PE assume resident tax obligations.
Tax payments & instalments.
Resident taxpayers and Indonesian PEs prepay CIT via monthly Article 25 instalments and prepayments from Articles 22, 23, and withholding; shortfalls are settled as Article 29 at filing.
- Monthly Article 25 instalments use the latest CIT Return as basis.
- Withholding (Article 22/23/4(2)/26) counts as prepayment; Article 29 covers year-end shortfalls.
Business profits (taxable base).
Taxable profit starts from financial accounting profit adjusted by tax rules; deductions allowed for expenditures incurred to obtain, collect and maintain taxable income, subject to timing and disallowance rules.
- Normal accounting basis with statutory tax adjustments.
- Timing differences where accounting expense is not immediately deductible for tax.
Taxation on certain offshore income.
Indonesian residents are generally taxed on worldwide income but specific offshore receipts (PE abroad, dividends, active business income) may be exempt if reinvested or used domestically within prescribed reinvestment thresholds (e.g., 30% minimum for some cases).
- Reinvestment thresholds and time limits apply; shortfalls become taxable.
- Distinctions between PE income, dividends, and active foreign income are material.
Controlled Foreign Companies (CFCs).
CFC rules attribute certain passive and related-party income (dividends, interest, rentals, royalties, gains) to Indonesian owners when ownership ≥ 50% (direct/indirect or collective thresholds), with listed-share exceptions.
- Ownership tested at fiscal year end on paid-up capital or voting rights.
- Listed foreign entities are generally excluded from CFC scope.
Capital allowances — Depreciation.
Tangible assets are depreciated from acquisition month using straight-line or declining-balance; asset categories determine useful lives (Category 1–4, buildings, with specific rates e.g., Category 1 = 4 years / 25% SL).
- Building: permanent 20 years (5% SL) or taxpayer’s actual useful life if >20 years with DGT approval.
- Special industry rules (oil & gas, forestry, plantations) may override generic classes.
Capital allowances — Amortisation & intangibles.
Intangibles and certain pre-commercial costs are amortised using category rates or production-unit method for extractive rights; amortisation may be capped (generally ≤20% p.a.) unless longer useful life is documented.
- Production-unit method for oil, gas, mining, forestry concessions.
- Intangibles with >20-year life may use 20 years or actual life with bookkeeping evidence.
Asset transfers & revaluation.
Sales of non-land assets produce capital gains/losses measured against tax written-down value; revaluation (DGT approval) permitted once every five years with a 10% final tax on revaluation gain and retention/anti-abuse rules for subsequent disposals.
- Revaluation requires Government-approved appraiser and DGT sign-off.
- Early disposal within minimum holding triggers additional tax equal to difference between CIT rate and initially paid 10% final tax.
Disallowed deductions.
Typical disallowances include non-business expenses, tax payments, penalties, profit distributions, certain provisions, and costs tied to income subject to final or exempt regimes; specific exceptions exist for regulated provisions.
- Examples: tax penalties and profit distributions are non-deductible.
- Permitted provisions are narrowly defined (e.g., banking doubtful accounts, insurance provisions).
Limitation on interest deduction / Debt-to-Equity.
Interest deductibility is limited by internationally accepted mechanisms (e.g., percentage of EBITDA, debt-to-equity tests) or other statutory methods to prevent excessive interest stripping.
- Common tests: Percentage of EBITDA, D/E ratios; exact method depends on taxpayer profile.
- Anti-avoidance focus on related-party financing.
Losses.
Tax losses can be carried forward for up to five years, with limited extensions (up to 10 years) for certain regional or concessionary businesses; loss carry-back is not permitted and group consolidation is unavailable.
- Carry-forward period: standard 5 years; extensions for qualifying activities/regions.
- No carry-back; no tax group relief/consolidation.
Profit distributions (dividends).
Dividends to resident recipients can be non-taxable if reinvested under conditions, otherwise subject to final tax (resident individuals up to 10% final rate); dividends to non-residents generally attract 20% final withholding (may be reduced by treaty).
- Resident individual reinvestment conditions can avoid tax; corporate recipients often exempt.
- Treaty relief may lower non-resident withholding below 20%.
Transfer Pricing & documentation.
Related-party transactions must meet the arm’s-length principle; taxpayers meeting thresholds must prepare Master File, Local File and Country-by-Country Report (CbCR), with disclosures required in the CIT Return and potential DGT audits.
- APA and MAP mechanisms available; APAs up to five years and may have limited rollback effects.
- Detailed transfer pricing disclosures (transaction values, methods, rationale) are mandatory in returns.
Sovereign Wealth Fund (LPI) tax treatment.
LPI is treated as a domestic corporate taxpayer with general CIT treatment but receives specific tax reliefs (e.g., deductibility of certain provisions, BPHTB treatment, withholding exemptions on certain loan interest) to support public investment management.
- Third-party partners cooperating with LPI may qualify for dividend or withholding concessions subject to conditions.
- Special deductibility and BPHTB rules apply per statute.

Individual Income Tax — Extract (v1.3)
Normal tax rates.
Most income for resident individuals is taxed progressively under five brackets with rates 5%, 15%, 25%, 30% and 35%.
- Up to IDR 60,000,000 — 5%.
- Above IDR 60,000,000 up to IDR 250,000,000 — 15%.
- Above IDR 250,000,000 up to IDR 500,000,000 — 25%.
- Above IDR 500,000,000 up to IDR 5,000,000,000 — 30%.
- Above IDR 5,000,000,000 — 35%.
Concessional tax rates.
Certain lump-sum or terminal payments are subject to final/concessional rates depending on type and timing (e.g., severance, pensions).
- Severance paid within two years: up to IDR 50,000,000 — Nil; above IDR 50,000,000–100,000,000 — 5%; above IDR 100,000,000–500,000,000 — 15%; above IDR 500,000,000 — 25%.
- Lump-sum pension/old-age payments have analogous final-rate buckets with Nil up to IDR 50,000,000 and 5% above that (year 1–2).
Main Personal Relief.
Annual non-taxable income (PTKP) components and fixed reliefs reduce taxable income for resident individuals. PTKP amounts are specified and must be applied before bracket rates.
- Taxpayer — IDR 54,000,000.
- Spouse — IDR 4,500,000.
- Each dependant (max. 3) — IDR 4,500,000 per dependant.
- Occupational expenses cap: 5% of gross income, max IDR 500,000/month (IDR 6,000,000/year).
Tax residence.
An individual is a tax resident if they reside in Indonesia, are present >183 days in any 12-month period, or are present in a fiscal year with intent to reside; residents taxed on worldwide income.
- Foreign citizens may be taxed only on Indonesian-sourced income for the first four years if conditions met.
- Non-residents: Article 26 withholding at 20% on Indonesian-sourced income (subject to treaty relief).
Registration and filing.
Resident individuals with annual income above PTKP must register (NPWP) and file an Annual Income Tax Return (Form 1770/AITR) reporting global income, assets and liabilities where required.
- Families typically report under a single NPWP in the head-of-family’s name; CFC exposures must be disclosed (shareholder deemed dividend rules).
Tax payments.
Most individual tax is collected by withholding (Article 21/26) with employers or payers remitting tax monthly; non-withheld liabilities are settled at filing.
- Resident employees: withholding based on normal tax rates; non-residents: withholding at 20% (subject to treaty rates).
- Other payments subject to withholding include pensions, severance, fees, prizes and certain interest.
Benefits-in-kind (BIKs).
BIKs are generally taxable to employees unless specifically exempted (job-required items, certain area allowances, food/drink provided to all employees, government-financed BIKs, and types below thresholds).
- Tax treatment: include fair market value of BIKs in taxable compensation unless a statutory exclusion applies.
Social security system.
Employers must ensure payroll deductions and employer contributions to BPJS Kesehatan and BPJS Ketenagakerjaan; contributions are remitted by employers together with their share.
- Example contribution structure (percentages of salary): Old age savings — employer 3.7% / employee 2%; Health care — employer 4% / employee 1%; Pension — employer 2% / employee 1%.
- Maximum calculation bases and annual updates apply (e.g., health max base IDR 12,000,000/month).
Saving Management — Tapera & contributions.
Tapera (Tabungan Perumahan Rakyat) is a housing-savings scheme for workers; contributions apply (typical rates cited in guidance) and aim to support affordable housing finance.
- Deposits/contributions examples: employer 0.5% and employee 2.5% (or 3% for freelancers) as described for programme participation.
- Government window for employer registration deadlines and detailed mechanics apply.

Withholding Taxes — Extracted (v1.3)
Withholding Taxes.
Indonesian income tax is primarily collected through a system of withholding where the payer is generally responsible for withholding tax on specified payments.
- Withholding constitutes prepayment of income tax for recipients except where expressly stated as final tax.
Article 21 income tax (PPh 21).
Employers must withhold PPh 21 from salaries and other compensation for employees and remit it monthly to the State Treasury.
- Resident employees: withholding based on normal rates; non-residents: withheld at 20% of gross amount.
Article 22 income tax (PPh 22).
PPh 22 applies to certain import and domestic purchase events where government, BUMN, specified collectors or importers act as collectors of tax at source.
- Import CIF-based rates include 10%, 7.5%, 0.5%, and 2.5% depending on goods; many other event-specific rates apply as listed in the source tables.
PPh 22 events & rates (summary).[PARAPHRASED]
Key PPh 22 events include imports, government purchases, BUMN purchases, selected distributor purchases (oil, cement, paper, automotive), exports of minerals, and sales of luxury goods.
- Example: import of certain end-customer goods = 10% of CIF value.
- Example: sale of very luxurious goods = 5% of selling price.
Article 4(2) — final income tax (PPh Final).
Certain gross payments to resident taxpayers or PEs are subject to final withholding tax under Article 4(2) and that withholding constitutes final settlement for that income.
- Rental of land/buildings: 10%.
- Proceeds from transfer of land/building rights: 2.5%.
Article 23 income tax (PPh 23).
Certain payments to residents are subject to PPh 23 withholding at rates typically 15% or 2% depending on income type.
- 15% applies to dividends, interest, royalties, prizes and awards.
- 2% applies to rents (other than land/building) and fees for technical/management/consulting services where not withheld under Article 21.
Article 26 income tax (PPh 26).
Payers in Indonesia must withhold 20% from payments to non-residents on specified gross amounts unless reduced by treaty.
- Covered items include dividends, interest, royalties, service fees, rents, pensions, swap premiums, gains from debt write-offs, and after-tax profits of a branch.
Article 26 ENI (Estimated Net Income) rules.
Certain payments to non-residents may be taxed on an Estimated Net Income (ENI) basis where a specified percentage of gross is deemed taxable and an effective tax rate results.
- Example: sale of non-listed Indonesian company shares: ENI 25% → effective tax rate 5%.
Withholding tax and treaties (rates table).
Indonesia’s tax treaties reduce or modify domestic withholding rates for dividends, interest, royalties and branch profit tax; treaty relief requires a Certificate of Domicile (CoD).
- Typical treaty dividend/interest/royalty caps in the table range from 5% to 20% depending on partner country (verbatim country rates preserved in source).
Permanent establishment time test (treaties).
- Examples preserved: Algeria/3 months; Australia/120 days; China/6 months; United States/120 days.
Withholding as prepayment and final tax distinctions.[PARAPHRASED]
Withholding commonly serves as prepayment of the recipient’s year-end tax liability, except where legislation explicitly declares the withholding to be final tax (e.g., certain Article 4(2) and Article 26 items).
- Prepayments (Articles 22, 23, 25) reduce corporate CIT liability; shortfalls are Article 29 income tax.
Taxpayers without an NPWP surcharge.
Where payees lack an NPWP, additional surtaxes apply to withholding — notably an extra surcharge of 20% for Article 21 payees and a 100% surcharge for certain other taxpayers as stated in the source.
- Preserve: "surcharge of 100%" verbatim where applicable.
Withholding tax collectors & compliance mechanics.[PARAPHRASED]
Certain parties act as designated tax collectors (e.g., State Treasury, BUMN, PSC companies); collectors must remit and report withheld amounts and provide required documentation to beneficiaries and the tax office.
- Withholding constitutes input for recipient’s tax returns and must be reflected in periodic reporting.

International Tax Agreements — Extracted Topics (v1.3)
Double Taxation Agreements.
Indonesia’s DTAs provide withholding tax relief and exemptions on service fees, dividends, interest, royalties and branch profits for residents of treaty partners, subject to Certificate of Domicile and anti-abuse/beneficial-ownership tests.
- Foreign party must present a Certificate of Domicile (CoD) to claim reduced rates; without it Indonesian withholding may apply at 20%.
- Anti-treaty-abuse and beneficial-ownership tests require economic substance, independent management, sufficient assets and personnel, and that the entity is not a conduit; each condition is assessed to prevent treaty misuse.
Tax Information Exchange Agreements (TIEAs).
Indonesia has TIEAs with specified jurisdictions (e.g., San Marino pending ratification, Bermuda, Guernsey, Isle of Man, Jersey, the Bahamas) to enable exchange of taxpayer information for enforcement and transparency.
- These agreements support tax transparency and may be pending exchange of ratification documents for full effect.
Mutual Administrative Assistance in Tax Matters.
Indonesia ratified the Convention on Mutual Administrative Assistance in Tax Matters (signed 3 Nov 2011, ratified 17 Oct 2014) to enable broad cooperation including automatic exchange of financial account information and transfer-pricing documentation (CbCR).
- Indonesia participates in automatic exchanges under the Common Reporting Standard and CbCR frameworks.
Multilateral Instrument (MLI) — BEPS implementation.
Indonesia signed and ratified the MLI (signed 7 Jun 2017; ratified 13 Nov 2019) and notified the OECD of completion of internal procedures on 28 Apr 2020; the MLI entered into force for Indonesia on 1 Aug 2020, covering selected bilateral treaties as notified by Indonesia and counterparties.
- By 27 Nov 2023 Indonesia had indicated 60 treaties to be covered by the MLI, subject to counterparties’ notifications.
Pillar Two implementation — GloBE & QDMTT (Domestic measures).
Indonesia issued domestic regulation to implement Global Anti-Base Erosion (GloBE) rules on 31 Dec 2024, with the Income Inclusion Rule (IIR) and Qualified Domestic Minimum Top-up Tax (QDMTT) applicable on or after 1 Jan 2025 and the Undertaxed Profits Rule (UTPR) on or after 1 Jan 2026.
- Domestic top-up may apply to taxpayers holding Tax Holiday facilities where multinational groups are subject to Global Minimum Tax under Pillar Two.
Subject-to-Tax Rule (STTR).
Indonesia signed the MLI on 19 Sep 2024 to facilitate implementation of the Pillar Two STTR and has a provisional list of 29 treaties it wishes covered; applicability depends on deposit of instrument of ratification and counterparts’ positions.
- STTR coverage and treaty inclusion are subject to the deposit and mutual notifications process.
US FATCA (Intergovernmental Agreement).
Indonesia has principally agreed to sign an IGA for FATCA compliance to facilitate exchange of US-related financial account information and align domestic reporting with FATCA requirements.
- The IGA enables reciprocal cooperation between Indonesian financial institutions and the US IRS under agreed procedures.
Withholding tax treaty rates and PE time tests (summary).
The source provides a treaty-rate matrix for dividends, interest, royalties, branch profit and portfolio/substantial holdings rates for numerous jurisdictions and tabulates PE-creation time thresholds for specified activities (e.g., building site, construction, installation, supervisory activities, other services) that commonly range from 90 days to 183 days or specified months, depending on treaty partner.
- Examples preserved verbatim: “MLI entered into force on 1 August 2020.”; PE tests include explicit country entries such as “Australia 120 days”, “Belgium 6 months”, “Hong Kong 183 days”.
Permanent establishment (PE) time-test details.
The document lists activity-specific PE time thresholds by treaty partner (e.g., building site, construction, installation, assembly, supervisory activities, other services) which are used to determine whether activities create a PE in Indonesia under bilateral treaties; thresholds vary by country and activity and must be checked against the relevant treaty text for precise application.
- The tabulated country entries and day/month thresholds are preserved verbatim in the source tables and must be used when applying PE tests.

Value Added Tax — Extracted Topics (v1.3)
General.
VAT is due on transfers of taxable goods and provision of taxable services within the Indonesian Customs Area and arises where annual deliveries exceed IDR 4.8 billion. VAT also applies to use/consumption of foreign intangible goods or services in Indonesia and to exports by PKP at 0%.
Taxable events (scope).
Taxable events include: deliveries of taxable goods by an enterprise; import of taxable goods; deliveries of taxable services; use/consumption of foreign intangible goods or services; and export of taxable goods or services by a PKP. Exports are zero-rated (0%).
Non-taxable goods and services.
By default goods and services are taxable unless listed as non-taxable under the legal negative list. Key non-taxable items include certain food/drink in hospitality (regional tax object), money, gold bars for government forex reserves and securities; certain services such as religious and specified public services are also non-taxable.
Tax rates and effective rate rule.
The statutory VAT rate is 12%, but the government applies an Other Value tax base (DPP Nilai Lain) that makes the effective VAT for most goods/services 11% (12% × 11/12), while exports remain 0% and certain luxury items use different treatments. Changes to statutory rates (5%–15%) require GR and parliamentary consultation.
Special “Certain Amount” regimes (examples).
Certain supplies use a preset DPP formula (Certain Amount) instead of transaction value; examples include: 1.1% for specified agricultural products, used motor vehicles, packaged delivery services and some services; 0.825× retail price for some LPG; 9.9% of retail price for tobacco products. Each regime disallows crediting of Input VAT related to that DPP.
VAT base (DPP Nilai Lain) rules.
Where DPP Nilai Lain applies the VAT base is typically 11/12 of a designated amount (e.g., 11/12 of cost of sales for own-use gifts, 11/12 of market value for remaining inventory at dissolution, 11/12 of auction price, etc.), and those formulae must be applied verbatim to compute output VAT.
VAT reporting and registration.
Taxable persons designated as PKP must register and file monthly VAT returns and settle VAT liabilities monthly; centralisation of VAT reporting across business units is possible with DGT approval, otherwise each unit registers with its local KPP.
Input–output mechanism and crediting Input VAT.
Output VAT charged by vendors is creditable against a buyer’s output VAT to the extent the input is necessary for the buyer’s business; monthly net positions are settled by the following month and unpaid input VAT may be carried forward or claimed as an annual refund subject to rules and time limits.
VAT invoices (electronic Faktur Pajak / e-FP).
To claim Input VAT as a credit taxpayers need valid tax invoices containing minimum information (supplier and purchaser names/NPWP, description, VAT amount, invoice serial/date, authorised signatory). PKPs must issue e-FP; failure to meet minimum requirements invalidates the input VAT claim.
Input VAT timing and pre-production limits.
Input VAT must generally be claimed in the same tax period, or within three months if not yet expensed; if a taxpayer remains in pre-production beyond prescribed cut-offs (generally three years; five years for manufacturing; six years for National Strategic Projects) credited Input VAT may be disallowed.
Import VAT and reverse charge (self-assessed VAT).
Import VAT is payable on goods entering Indonesia (CIF basis). Self-assessed VAT (reverse charge) applies for use/consumption of foreign taxable services or intangible goods and is treated within the standard input-output mechanism; non-resident sellers may be appointed as VAT Collectors to avoid reverse-charging the Indonesian buyer.
VAT Collectors and special collecting regimes.
Certain parties (State Treasury, BUMN and some subsidiaries, PSC companies, specified mining companies, and appointed foreign/digital sellers) are designated VAT Collectors who must collect VAT from PKPs and remit directly to government; appointed collectors must file reports and remit VAT monthly.
VAT refunds and timings.
VAT refund applications are typically resolved after a VAT audit and DGT must decide within 12 months of a complete application (no decision = deemed approved). Supporting documents must be provided within one month of application; early refunds are available for qualifying taxpayers (Golden Taxpayers, low refund values, and certain low-risk PKPs).
VAT facilities — exemptions and non-collection.
Certain imports, strategic goods/services and public-interest supplies are exempted or non-collected (examples: polio/COVID-19 vaccines; select educational and religious books; factory machinery for manufacturing; medical services; electricity and clean water in specified cases). Exemption/non-collection is by GR within defined scopes and may interact with import duty and LST reliefs.
VAT on e-commerce (digital economy rules).
Overseas suppliers, foreign service providers, foreign e-marketplaces and domestic marketplaces may be appointed as VAT Collectors if either: transaction value with Indonesian customers > IDR 600 million/year or IDR 50 million/month; or platform access from Indonesia > 12,000 users/12 months or > 1,000 users/month. Appointed collectors must collect VAT from customers and report/pay via DGT electronic channels.
Practical compliance and risk highlights.
Key practical risks: invalid tax invoices (loss of Input VAT credit); incorrect application of DPP Nilai Lain or Certain Amounts; late supporting documents for refund claims; mis-appointment or misclassification of VAT Collectors; and cross-border seller thresholds for e-commerce. Controls should include invoice validation, accurate DPP application, documented centralisation approvals, and monitoring of platform thresholds.
Key rate and threshold summary (verbatim values preserved).
- Statutory VAT rate: 12%.
- Effective standard VAT (using DPP Nilai Lain 11/12): 11%.
- Export VAT rate: 0%.
- E-commerce thresholds: IDR 600 million/year or IDR 50 million/month; access thresholds 12,000 users/12 months or 1,000 users/month.
- Pre-production cut-off: 3 years (general), 5 years (manufacturing), 6 years (National Strategic Projects).
Practical checklist for a VAT-clean compliance file.
- Ensure PKP registration and monthly VAT returns are filed.
- Validate every tax invoice against e-FP minimum fields before crediting Input VAT.
- Apply DPP Nilai Lain formulas (11/12) and Certain Amount rates exactly where prescribed.
- Track and document digital sales and platform access against e-commerce thresholds.
- Maintain ready refund support and expect DGT audit within the 12-month refund decision window.

Luxury-goods Sales Tax — Extract (v1.3)
Luxury-goods Sales Tax.
The Luxury-goods Sales Tax (LST) is an excise-style levy applied in addition to VAT on specified manufactured goods deemed luxurious and is charged once either on importation or on domestic supply by the manufacturer.
- LST is collected monthly together with VAT and administered through the VAT/LST reporting cycle.
Scope & Taxable Items.
LST applies to designated manufactured goods identified by Customs HS codes and regulatory lists, with applicability depending on characteristics such as capacity, size or price rather than solely on product description.
- Determination of LST applicability requires reference to Customs Harmonised System (HS) codes and MoF/GR guidance.
Rates & Calculation.
LST rates vary by product grouping and currently range broadly (indicative rates from 10% up to 95% are published), with a statutory ability to increase up to 200% by regulation; the exact rate is determined by item classification and regulatory criteria.
- A particular item attracts LST only once (on importation or on manufacturer’s supply).
- Example: published indicative group rates include multiple tiers from 10% to 95%.
Base & Interaction with VAT.
The LST base is determined under the Customs/VAT/LST rules and is distinct from VAT; where both taxes apply the LST is calculated on the LST base and accounted alongside VAT but is not creditable as input VAT.
- LST is imposed in addition to VAT and may affect the effective consumer price beyond VAT-inclusive pricing.
Administration, Filing & Payment.
Importers or resident manufacturers are responsible for LST settlement and monthly reporting in tandem with VAT returns; LST must be declared and paid within the same monthly filing cycle that applies to VAT for PKP taxpayers.
- Importers remit LST at customs clearance; domestic manufacturers account for LST in monthly returns.
Concessions, Exemptions & Special Rules.
Certain items or circumstances may benefit from reduced LST treatment or base reductions (for example “green” motor vehicles may receive an LST base reduction to 0% to 931/3% of the LST base as provided by regulation), and classification exceptions apply for policy-driven incentives or public-interest supplies.
- Whether an item is subject to LST depends on combined criteria: HS code, capacity/price thresholds, and any sector-specific exemptions or regional facilities.
Compliance Risks & Practical Controls.
Key compliance risks include misclassification of HS codes, double charging (import + domestic) and incorrect rate application; robust controls should include HS-code validation, documented rulings for borderline items, and reconciliation of LST with VAT and customs declarations.
- Retain customs documentation and tax invoices that evidence whether LST was charged at import or by the manufacturer.
- Seek binding classification/rulings for material-value products before large-scale procurement or import programs.
Implementation checklist (practical steps).
1. Map product portfolio to HS codes and published LST group lists.
2. Determine whether supply is import- or manufacture-triggered for LST.
3. Confirm applicable LST rate per item and document basis of classification.
4. Update tax systems to collect LST at invoice/clearance and to report monthly with VAT returns.
- Retain documentary backup for audits and refund or exemption claims.

Carbon Tax — Extracted Topics (v1.3)
Carbon Tax.
Carbon Tax is imposed on carbon emissions that negatively impact the environment and may apply to individuals or legal bodies that either emit above a threshold or purchase carbon-content goods. The regime is designed to price emissions and incentivise reduction.
- Applies to emitters and purchasers of carbon content goods.
- Designed to be implemented via market and regulatory mechanisms.

Carbon Emission.
Emissions above a defined threshold are taxed at the lower of the domestic carbon credit price per kg CO2e or a fixed floor rate. This creates a pricing floor while allowing market signals to operate.
- Pricing rule: lowest of carbon credit price (domestic market) or IDR 30/kg CO2e.
IDR 30/kg CO2e

Cap and trade.
The Carbon Tax framework explicitly uses a “cap and trade” mechanism as its core settlement route, enabling emission trading and deduction of tax liabilities through traded carbon credits. This combines quantity control with price discovery.
- Emission trading can reduce net tax via credit offsetting.
“cap and trade”

Coal-Fired Power Plant.
The Coal-Fired Power Plant sector is identified as the first (and currently sole) industry to adopt the cap-and-trade mechanism and thus is the pilot scope for Carbon Tax collection, monitoring and reporting. Implementation experience here will guide expansion.
- Pilot sector for cap-and-trade implementation.
- Expansion contingent on industry readiness and regulatory decisions.

Purchase of Carbon Content Goods.
Purchases of goods containing embedded carbon may be subject to Carbon Tax at the point of sale; this element is noted as not yet implemented but is planned as a complementary collection channel to production-side taxation.
- Seller may be appointed as Carbon Tax collector when implemented.
- Aimed at capturing upstream embedded emissions in goods.

Carbon emitter is required to record.
Carbon emitters must record emission activities, self-remit tax liabilities, and report Carbon Tax on an annual basis; sellers appointed as collectors must record sales, remit, and report monthly. These reporting cadences differentiate emitter versus collector obligations.
- Emitters: record, self-remit, annual reporting.
- Collectors (sellers): record sales, remit, report monthly.

Customs & Excise — Focused Extract (v1.3)
Customs and Excise (Overview).
Import duty, export duty and excise are applied within a comprehensive tariff and relief framework and are calculated on customs value (CIF) or specific ad valorem / specific bases to achieve revenue, protection and trade policy objectives.
- Import duty range: 0% - 150% on customs value (CIF).
- Customs value basis: CIF (Cost, Insurance and Freight).
Import Duty.
Import duty is levied on the customs value of imported goods with rates set by tariff line and may include ad valorem or specific amounts and additional measures (anti-dumping, safeguard, compensation).
- Example group rates: Automobiles (Passenger & commercial) 5 to 50%.
- Electronic goods 0 to 20 or IDR 21,450/minute.
- Textile goods 0 to 35%.
Import duty relief / exemption / deferral.
Import duty reliefs are available for investors and qualifying government projects and can be granted as exemptions, non-collection of VAT/LST, Article 22 suspension or postponement of import duty on capital goods, often subject to conditions and pro rata treatment for partial foreign funding.
- Common facilities: import duty exemption; VAT/LST non-collection; Article 22 suspension; postponement of import duty on capital goods.
ASEAN duty rates.
Under the ASEAN Trade In Goods Agreement (ATIGA) qualifying intra-ASEAN goods that meet origin criteria and direct shipment rules may benefit from preferential 0% import duty to promote regional trade liberalisation.
- Origin requirement: wholly obtained or meeting regional value/content and direct shipment between ASEAN countries.
Agreements on preferential duty rates.
Indonesia applies preferential tariffs under bilateral and regional trade agreements beyond ASEAN; specific relief depends on treaty text, HS code classification and documentary proof of origin.
- Relief types: tariff reductions/exemptions under trade/economic partnership agreements.
- Practical note: verify HS code mapping and certification requirements for each agreement.
Export Duty.
Export duties are applied ad valorem or specifically to certain natural resources and commodities to manage resource conservation and value capture; rates vary by product, grade and policy settings.
- Examples: Cocoa beans 0% to 15%.
- Palm fruit / CPO and derivatives USD 0 to 431 / Metrics Ton.
- Certain minerals (e.g., nickel, bauxite) may attract specific rates (e.g., 10%).
Excise (scope and objective).
Excise targets goods with social or externality concerns (notably tobacco and alcoholic products) and is imposed per unit or quantity to control consumption and raise targeted revenue, coordinated with customs for imports and domestic production.
- Policy: control distribution and externalities; fund health and social programmes.
Excise rates (selected).
Excise amounts are prescribed per unit or weight in legislation and vary by product category to reflect policy aims and product characteristics.
- Ethyl alcohol IDR 20,000/litre.
- Alcoholic drink IDR 16,500/litre to IDR 152,000/litre.

Regional Taxes
Regional Taxes.
Regional Taxes are governed by the Financial Relationships between the Central Government and the Regional Government (HKPD) Law and cover a set of locally administered taxes and duties collected by regional governments. The regime delegates rate ceilings and implementation details to regional regulations (Peraturan Daerah/PERDA) while preserving central-law limits and national negative lists.
- Key national framework: HKPD Law (central guidance); local implementation via PERDA.
Regional Tax/Duty rates and “Opsen”.
HKPD sets maximum rates for regional taxes and an “Opsen” (optional additional share) mechanism that regions may apply within statutory ceilings. Examples: PBB max 0.5%; BPHTB max 5%; Motor Vehicle Tax first-ownership up to 1.2% (higher ceilings for special regions).
- Opsen examples: PKB opsen 66% of PKB; BBNKB opsen 66% of BBNKB.
Land and Building Tax (PBB).
PBB is an annual regional tax on land and/or buildings computed from NJOP (tax object sale value) subject to a regional-determined taxable percentage (20%–100%) less a non-taxable NJOP threshold (minimum IDR 10,000,000). PBB administration, exemptions, and exact rates are set by each regional PERDA within HKPD ceilings.
- Exempt categories include state property, public-interest religious/social uses, cemeteries, protected forests, diplomatic properties.
Tax on land and building transfer.
Transfers of land/building rights produce a final income tax on the transferor generally set at 2.5% of the gross transfer value, with a reduced 1% rate for transfers of simple houses/apartments by property developers. The tax base is usually the higher of the declared transaction value or the NJOP/official valuation.
- Timing: payable at transfer and treated as final tax.
Duty on the acquisition of land and building rights (BPHTB).
BPHTB is a regional duty payable by the acquirer and calculated by applying the BPHTB rate (max 5%) to the Tax Object Acquisition Value (NPOP), typically the higher of market/transaction value or NJOP, minus a non-taxable threshold (regional; minimum IDR 80,000,000; inheritance threshold often IDR 300,000,000).
- Due date: usually on signing of the transfer deed before a notary; final payment must be made to complete transfer.
Administration, assessment and collection (SPPT, payment & penalties).
PBB is assessed annually by regional authorities via a Tax Due Notification Letter (SPPT); taxpayers must pay as indicated. Late or non-payment triggers administrative procedures under HKPD, and central rules prescribe interest/penalty regimes for late filing/payment consistent with national tax administration norms.
- SPPT issuance triggers the annual PBB obligation; regional procedures determine payment windows and enforcement steps.
Exemptions, negative list, and PERDA role.
The HKPD negative list excludes specified land/building categories from PBB (e.g., government properties, public-interest uses); regions enact PERDAs to implement exemptions, non-taxable thresholds, and finer rate structures within national ceilings. Regional PERDAs also set procedures for valuation (NJOP) adjustments and special local reliefs.
- Practical implication: consult the local PERDA for exact rates, NJOP calculation and applicable exemptions.
Practical compliance notes for taxpayers.
Property owners must monitor SPPT issuance, regional PERDAs for rate or threshold changes, and transactional timing (deed signing) because tax and duty liabilities arise at discrete administrative events. For transfers, retain records proving transaction value vs NJOP to support correct NPOP determination.
- Keep transfer deeds, sale-purchase agreements, and notarial records as evidence for BPHTB/NPOP and transfer tax calculations.

Stamp Duty
Stamp Duty (overview).
Stamp duty (Bea Meterai) in Indonesia is a nominal, fixed tax imposed on certain legal documents and becomes payable when a document is signed, completed, or delivered as evidence; the statutory flat rate is IDR 10,000 per document. .
Key point: physical or electronic stamp evidence (e-stamp) shows the duty has been paid and is required for the document to have evidentiary effect in many civil matters. .
Documents subject to duty.
Stamp duty applies to documents made as civil evidence or used as court exhibits, including agreements, notarial deeds, certificates, receipts over certain values, auction documents, and securities transaction documents. .
- Agreements, certificates, and similar written instruments. .
- Notarial deeds, land-deed documents, and securities transaction papers. .
Fixed nominal rate and scope threshold.
The statutory nominal rate is Rp 10,000 per document effective 1 Jan 2021 under the updated stamp duty law; the law also clarifies thresholds for documents that record sums of money (e.g., amount thresholds referenced in regulation). .
- Historical note: prior rates (Rp3,000 / Rp6,000) were replaced by the single Rp10,000 tariff. .
Exemptions and limited exceptions.
Certain documents are exempted from stamp duty by law or implementing regulation, for example specific government, social, or charitable documents and other limited statutory exceptions; always verify against the current DGT/MoF listings for precise exclusions. .
- Example: some government programme documents, religious/non-commercial transfers and other items may be exempt. .
Timing, affixation and legal effect.
Stamp duty is due at the time of signing or when a single-party document is completed/delivered; a physical stamp must be affixed or an e-stamp applied before the document is relied upon as evidence to avoid enforceability or evidentiary issues. .
- Practical rule: attach or record the stamp BEFORE presenting the document in court or transferring rights whenever feasible. .
Penalties, risk and enforcement.
Failure to apply the required stamp may lead to evidentiary objections, administrative fines or remedial requirements under tax rules; separate civil consequences (e.g., enforceability disputes) may arise if a required stamp is missing. .
- Remediation: some remedies or late-payment procedures exist but vary by document type and regulatory guidance — confirm with DGT or legal counsel before relying on after-the-fact fixes. .
Practical examples (common cases).
- Sale/purchase agreements, rental contracts and employment letters that record obligations or sums and are signed by parties. .
- Receipts or documents recording money above statutory thresholds and not otherwise excluded. .
- Mutual fund transaction confirmations, which have been specifically addressed recently and may attract a Rp10,000 stamp depending on regulatory treatment. .

Monthly obligations & key due dates.
Withholding taxes are generally payable and reportable monthly; withholding payments are typically due by the 15th and returns by the 20th of the following month.
VAT and LST are reported monthly and must be paid prior to filing the monthly VAT return (reporting deadline: end of the following month).

- VAT/LST: settle the tax liability by the payment-before-filing rule to avoid interest.
Annual obligations and statutory deadlines.
Corporate Income Tax (CIT) payment and filing: payment and filing deadlines are the end of the fourth month after the fiscal year end.
Individual Annual Income Tax Return (AITR) payment and filing: payment and filing deadlines are the end of the third month after year end.
- PBB (property tax): due within six months after SPPT receipt.
Permitted payment methods, electronic filing and bookkeeping links.
Tax payments are required to be made electronically through designated tax-payment banks and e-filing channels; taxpayers may use a tax deposit and settle by overbooking (pemindahbukuan).
Bookkeeping is normally in IDR and Indonesian; selected taxpayers may use USD/English with DGT approval — tax liabilities however are settled in IDR.
- Use authorised e-filing channels for AITR and e-tax returns; keep remittance proof for audits.
- Tax deposits (overbooking) should be reconciled before return submission.

Late filing and late payment attract interest at the monthly MoF Interest Rate plus surcharges, typically up to a maximum of 24 months; part of a month counts as a full month.
Administrative fixed penalties (examples): VAT return IDR 500,000; other monthly returns IDR 100,000; Individual income tax return IDR 100,000; CIT return IDR 1,000,000.
- Ignoring a DGT warning may lead to SKP issuance with 20% surcharge and further collection action.
- An SKPKBT (additional underpaid assessment) may add a 100% surcharge unless voluntarily disclosed earlier.

Early tax refunds are available for qualifying taxpayers (Golden Taxpayers), low-refund value filers and certain low-risk PKPs; refunds trigger documentary checks and typically a DGT decision within 12 months of a complete application.
Golden Taxpayer criteria include timely filing, no arrears (exceptions apply), audited financial statements with Unqualified Opinion for three consecutive years, and no tax-crime conviction in the last five years.
- Low-refund thresholds: Individuals (non-business) and individuals with business/freelance refund ≤ IDR 100 million; companies with refund ≤ IDR 1 billion; PKP VAT refund ≤ IDR 5 billion.
- Request early refund by ticking the refund box in the return; be prepared for a formal/material examination.
Common audit triggers, one-month rule and dispute linkage.
Refund applications (except certain early-refund eligibles) commonly trigger a tax audit; taxpayers must produce requested documents within one month or face deemed-profit adjustments.
Objections to SKPs must be filed within three months of issue; the DGT has 12 months to decide (silence = deemed approved).
- Keep a centralised, indexed documentary pack for any refund or audit: remittances, invoices, contracts, tax invoices (e-FP), NPWP records, and transfer pricing files where applicable.
- If you disagree with SKP, file Objection promptly and preserve closing conference records to limit immediate collection exposure.
Practical compliance checklist (operational best practices).
Adopt the following controls to reduce risk of underpayment, penalties, and disruptive audits.
- 1. Centralise payroll withholding and VAT processing; reconcile monthly before filing.
- 2. Maintain tax deposits (pemindahbukuan) and document overbooking transactions.
- 3. Issue and archive compliant electronic tax invoices (e-FP) with required fields.
- 4. Pre-validate refund applications against available documentary evidence.
- 5. Track statute of limitation windows (5-year DGT assessment rule) and novum exposures.
- 6. Keep transfer-pricing Master/Local/CbCR documentation ready if cross-border related-party transactions exist.

Tax Audits and Tax Assessments — Focused Extraction (v1.3)
Tax audits.
Tax audits are formal examinations by the Directorate General of Taxes (DGT) that test a taxpayer’s self-assessed liabilities across one or more taxes for specific tax periods; audits may be on-site, at DGT offices, or both.
- Scope may cover a single tax period or multiple taxes and years.
- Audits may be routine, refund-triggered, or risk-selected.
Conditions triggering a tax audit.
Common triggers include refund claims, unusual tax positions, risk-based selection, or other DGT criteria; a refund request normally prompts a full audit and may broaden scope to other taxes.
- Refund applications frequently lead to complete CIT audits.
- Special audits use different timelines/procedures.
Special tax audit.
Special tax audits are conducted for specified purposes and follow timelines and procedures that differ from general audits; taxpayers should expect distinct rules and possibly expedited handling.
- May be applied for targeted reviews or complex cases.
- Procedures and deadlines vary by audit type.
One-month rule.
When auditors request documents or information, taxpayers must provide them within one month; failure to comply may lead the DGT to determine tax liabilities on a deemed-profit basis and preclude later use of those documents in defence.
- Noncompliance increases risk of adverse deemed assessments.
- Timely submission preserves evidentiary rights.
Closing conference.
Auditors issue temporary findings and discuss them with the taxpayer; a closing conference documents agreed and disagreed corrections and forms the basis for subsequent tax assessment and collection instruments.
- Taxpayer must state Agree or Disagree to each proposed correction in writing.
- Disagreed items remain payable only after final Objection/appeal processes conclude.
Products of a tax audit.
Main audit outputs are Tax Assessment Letters (SKP) and Tax Collection Letters (STP); SKPs set the tax balance while STPs may be used to collect administrative sanctions or current-year amounts.
- SKP types reflect underpayment, overpayment, or nil positions.
- STP is a legal instrument for certain collections.
Tax assessments.
An SKP applies to a specific tax and period and records the tax due, credits, resulting balance, and administrative penalties where applicable; assessments derive from audit findings, refunds, or automated reviews.
- SKPs form the statutory basis for collection and disputes.
- Assessment content must specify tax, period, and penalties.
Types of tax assessment letters.
There are three principal SKP types: Overpaid Tax Assessment Letter (SKPLB), Underpaid Tax Assessment Letter (SKPKB), and Nil Tax Assessment Letter (SKPN), each reflecting the balance between tax due and credits for the period.
- SKPLB → refund/credit position.
- SKPKB → taxpayer underpayment and potential interest/surcharges.
Payments of tax assessment.
Tax due under an SKP must generally be paid within one month of issuance; failure to pay without filing an Objection may lead to Distress Warrant steps and administrative penalties based on MoF interest rates plus surcharges.
- Timely payment avoids escalation to collection steps.
- Partial agreements from closing conference allow staged payment where an Objection is filed.
Statute of limitation.
The DGT may issue an SKPKB within five years after the tax liability arose (end of month/period or tax year); additional SKPKBs can follow within five years if new data (novum) emerges, and tax crime exceptions extend exposure.
- Five-year bar applies subject to novum or criminal findings.
- Tax certainty arises if no SKP within five years.
Additional Underpaid Tax Assessment Letter (SKPKBT).
An SKPKBT (additional underpaid assessment) carries a 100% surcharge on the tax due, though the surcharge may be avoided if the liability results from a taxpayer’s voluntary disclosure prior to a DGT audit.
- Voluntary disclosure can eliminate the 100% surcharge.
- SKPKBT signals material new findings after an initial SKP.
Taxpayer remedies: Objections & Appeals.
A taxpayer may file an Objection to an SKP within three months of issuance; the DGT must decide within 12 months or the Objection is deemed approved — further remedies include administrative Appeals to the Tax Court and judicial review to the Supreme Court.
- Filing an Objection suspends immediate full collection only to the extent agreed in the closing conference.
- Deadlines and procedural steps are strict and must be met to preserve rights.

Tax Collection Using Distress Warrant — Structured Summary (Selected section)
Tax Collection Using Distress Warrant.
If a legal tax-collection instrument is not paid within the required time, the Directorate General of Taxes (DGT) may issue a Distress Warrant (Surat Paksa) to enforce payment. The Distress Warrant is the statutory escalation for unpaid tax-collection instruments and triggers a defined sequence of enforcement steps. Each enforcement step has legislated timelines and potential consequences.
Instruments subject to enforcement — STP; SKPKB; SKPKBT; Objection/Tax Court/Correction Decisions.
The instruments that may lead to a Distress Warrant include STP, SKPKB, SKPKBT, Tax Objection Decision Letters, Tax Court Decisions, and Correction Decision Letters. These instruments are legal tax-collection instruments that create a payable tax liability if not settled within the prescribed time. Where such an instrument exists, the taxpayer becomes liable to pay the underpaid tax within one month of the instrument date.
Payment timeframe and interest penalties.
The taxpayer is required to pay the underpaid tax stated in the tax-collection instrument within one month of the instrument date. Late payment triggers an interest penalty calculated using the applicable monthly Ministry of Finance (MoF) Interest Rate and may apply for a maximum of 24 months. The interest calculation and potential surcharge exposure materially increase the taxpayer’s payable amount if the debt is not resolved promptly.
Effect of Objection or Appeal on collection.
Under current tax-administration rules, if a taxpayer files an Objection (or later an Appeal) concerning an SKP, they remain bound to pay at least the minimum amount agreed at the tax-audit closing conference. The remainder of the disputed assessment becomes due only after the DGT issues an Objection decision or the Tax Court issues an adverse Appeal decision. This mechanism preserves a balance between administration collection and taxpayer procedural rights.
Execution steps for Distress Warrant (sequence and deadlines).
The DGT follows a defined escalation sequence before and after issuing a Distress Warrant:
- Issue a Warning Letter (Surat Teguran) if the underpaid tax is not settled within 7 days of the due date.
- Issue a Distress Warrant if the underpaid tax is not settled within 21 days after the Warning Letter is issued.
- Issue a Confiscation Order (Surat Sita) if the underpaid tax is not settled within 48 hours of issuing the Distress Warrant.
- Publish an auction announcement for the confiscated assets if the tax is not settled within 14 days of the Confiscation Order.
- Conduct a public auction if the tax remains unpaid 14 days after the auction announcement.
Each step is a statutory trigger and must be observed carefully by both taxpayers and advisors.
Consequences and scope of enforcement remedies.
Confiscation and auction of assets are statutory remedies to recover unpaid tax; assets seized under a Confiscation Order may be publicly auctioned to satisfy the tax debt and associated administrative sanctions. Enforcement proceeds are applied against the tax liability, interest, and any applicable surcharges. The DGT’s power to escalate to seizure and auction means unresolved liabilities can result in material loss of assets and reputational consequence.
Practical compliance and risk-mitigation guidance.- Where an Objection or Appeal is being prepared, ensure the minimum agreed payment at closing conference is settled to prevent immediate enforcement action.
- Maintain documentary evidence and contemporaneous communications to support positions in Objection or Appeal procedures.
- Seek early engagement with the DGT (e.g., payment instalment requests where eligible) and consider professional advice to manage audit outcomes and execution risk.

Tax Audits and Tax Assessments.
The DGT undertakes audits to verify taxpayers’ self-assessments and may issue Tax Assessment Letters (SKP) that record overpaid, underpaid or nil positions; assessments include tax due, credits and administrative penalties. Audits may be scoped by tax type or period and can be onsite or at DGT offices.
- Audit triggers include refund claims and risk-based selection; scope may expand during the audit.
- Final products: SKPLB, SKPKB, and SKPN; payments follow prescribed deadlines.
Tax Collection Using Distress Warrant.
If assessed liabilities remain unpaid the DGT follows progressive enforcement: Warning Letter → Distress Warrant → Confiscation Order → auction announcement → public auction; interest and surcharges apply during enforcement. Collection instruments (STP, SKPKB, SKPKBT) become enforceable and may prompt asset seizure and sale.
- Taxpayers may limit cash-out to amounts agreed in closing conference while disputing remaining items.
- Procedural timings: warning (7 days), distress warrant (21 days after warning), confiscation (48 hours after distress warrant), auction (if unpaid).
Objections.- Objection must specify reasons and documentary support.

Appeals to the Tax Court.
A taxpayer dissatisfied with the DGT’s Objection decision may appeal to the Tax Court (administrative judicial review); the appeal process reviews legal and factual determinations and can suspend collection of contested amounts under limited conditions. The Tax Court’s decision can affirm, reduce or annul the tax assessment.
- Filing deadlines and procedural formalities are strict; legal representation commonly required.
- Concurrent MAP or APA processes may run alongside domestic appeals in treaty cases.
Judicial Review Requests to the Supreme Court.
Following an adverse Tax Court decision, taxpayers may seek judicial review (Kasasi) to the Supreme Court on points of law; the Supreme Court reviews legal errors rather than re-weighing facts. A final Supreme Court ruling establishes binding precedent for similar cases unless overturned.
- Judicial review focuses on legal questions and correct application of law.
- Supreme Court decisions may trigger re-assessments or refunds depending on outcome.
Alternative & International Mechanisms (MAP / APA / QAT).
For cross-border or treaty-related disputes, taxpayers may request Mutual Agreement Procedure (MAP) or use Advance Pricing Agreements (APA); domestically, Quality Assurance Teams (QAT) and mediation-type discussions can be used to resolve legal basis disagreements before escalation to courts. MAP/APA aim to eliminate double taxation and align positions between jurisdictions.
- MAP requires treaty partner involvement and Certificate of Domicile for treaty benefits.
- APAs set future transfer-pricing treatment (up to 5 years) and may be rolled back in limited cases.
Practical compliance & defensive measures.
To minimise dispute exposure, maintain contemporaneous books and transfer-pricing documentation (Master File, Local File, CbCR when applicable), respond to audit requests within the one-month rule, and document closing-conference agreements; good recordkeeping reduces risk of deemed-profit assessments and collection actions. Early, well-documented engagement often shortens dispute life-cycles and limits penalties.
- Keep requested documents within one month to avoid deemed profit adjustments.
- Use timely Objection, appeal, and documented settlement positions to manage cash exposure.
